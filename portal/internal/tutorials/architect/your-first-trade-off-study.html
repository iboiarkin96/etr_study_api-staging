Your first trade-off study

Pick a trivial decision with two plausible options, list 4–5 criteria, score each option, and write a one-page memo recommending one. The point is to feel the trade-off loop — options → criteria → score → recommendation — not to ratify a real decision. Real studies use the same shape on harder problems and feed straight into an ADR.

⚙️ 4 phases ⏱ 30–45 min 📚 First-time 📝 Paper-only

– Before you start

  • A pen and a sheet of paper, or any plain-text editor.
  • 30–45 minutes of uninterrupted thinking time.
  • You've skimmed ADR template once so the next step (turning the memo into an ADR) is familiar.
  • You know the difference between a reversible decision (cheap to undo) and a one-way door (expensive to undo). Trade-off studies pay off most on one-way doors.

0 Goal

  • Pick one decision with exactly two options — default candidates below.
  • List 4–5 criteria that matter for this decision, with weights.
  • Score each option against each criterion on a 1–3 scale.
  • Write a one-page memo at services/portal/internal/scratch/trade-off-<date>.md with the matrix, tie-breakers, and a one-paragraph recommendation.

Default candidate decisions (pick one — they're all trivial enough to fit in 30 min):

  • Caching strategy for GET /api/v1/hello — in-process LRU vs no caching at all.
  • Test framework for a new service — pytest vs unittest (stdlib).
  • Background task runner — APScheduler in-process vs a separate Celery worker.

1 Frame the decision

At the top of your memo, write three lines and nothing else yet:

# Trade-off study: <decision in one sentence>

**Forcing function**: <why now? what changes if we don't decide?>
**Reversibility**: reversible | one-way door
**Stakeholders**: <3 names or roles max>

The forcing function is the most important line. If you can't write a real one, the decision isn't ready — go back to gathering context, not to drafting options.

✓ Check your progress

Three lines on the page. The forcing-function line names a concrete event or deadline ("ADR 0029 lands next sprint", "a new endpoint ships Friday") — not "we should probably decide". If it's vague, rewrite it; vague framing produces vague conclusions.

2 List criteria and score

Pick 4–5 criteria — fewer than 4 hides trade-offs, more than 5 dilutes them. Each criterion gets a weight from 1 (nice to have) to 3 (deal-breaker).

Score each option against each criterion on a 1–3 scale — 1 (weak fit), 2 (acceptable), 3 (strong fit). Use the same matrix shape every time:

## Matrix

| Criterion              | Weight | Option A  | Option B  |
|------------------------|:------:|:---------:|:---------:|
| Time to first ship     |   3    |    3      |    1      |
| Operational complexity |   3    |    3      |    1      |
| Throughput headroom    |   2    |    1      |    3      |
| Observability fit      |   2    |    2      |    3      |
| Cost to revert         |   1    |    3      |    2      |
| **Weighted total**     |        | **24**    | **18**    |

Weighted total = sum of weight × score per row. Compute it by hand — the act of multiplying surfaces ties and forces you to defend each cell.

✓ Check your progress

Every criterion has both a weight and two scores. Weighted totals are written and you can defend each cell in one sentence — "Option B scores 1 on ops complexity because it adds a second long-running process to the deployment". If a cell can't be defended that briefly, replace it with a question for stakeholders, not a guess.

3 Stress-test the winner

The matrix produced a number. Before you trust it, walk three checks:

  • Flip one weight. Drop the heaviest criterion's weight from 3 to 1 — does the winner change? If yes, the result is fragile; call that out in the memo.
  • Hunt for missing criteria. What's the loudest objection your most-skeptical stakeholder would raise? If it's not in the matrix, you're scoring against the wrong axes.
  • Pre-mortem the winner. Imagine it's 6 months later and the winning option turned out to be a mistake. What broke? Write one sentence. If the answer is "nothing I could have known", you're fine; if it's "the throughput cliff at 1000 req/s", add it as a criterion and re-score.

✓ Check your progress

Three short paragraphs in your memo under "Stress test": weight-flip, missing-criteria, pre-mortem. Each is two or three sentences max. If the winner changed during stress-test, the matrix is the draft and the stress-test is the result — say so in the recommendation.

4 Recommendation + next step

Close the memo with one paragraph and a hand-off line:

## Recommendation

Adopt **<option>**. Stronger on the two highest-weighted criteria
(<A>, <B>); weaker on <C>, which we mitigate by <mitigation>.
Reversibility: <one-way door | reversible in N hours>.

**Next step**: open ADR <NNNN> — <short title> — using this memo as
the *Alternatives considered* section. Owner: <name>. Target ratify: <date>.

The hand-off line is what turns a study into a decision. Without an ADR owner and a target date, the memo dies in scratch/.

✓ You're done

  1. Memo file exists under scratch/ with framing, matrix, stress-test, recommendation.
  2. The recommendation names a single option and one mitigation.
  3. The hand-off line points to a real ADR number (next free under governance/adr/) and an owner.

That is the trade-off loop — frame → score → stress-test → recommend → hand off. Real studies widen the option set, deepen the stress-test, and may run as a small RFC instead of a one-pager; the shape is the same.
