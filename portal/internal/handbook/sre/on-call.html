SRE Explanation

Why on-call is a contract

On-call works when it is precise — a named person, an agreed time, a known escalation — and degrades fast when it is implicit. The point of formalising it is not bureaucracy; it is to protect both the user and the human being paged.

On-call is not “someone will probably look”

The naive version of on-call is an unstated norm: someone on the team will notice if something breaks and will deal with it. This works as long as the team is small, the alerts are rare, and the people are watching. The moment any of those conditions weaken, the norm fails silently — a page goes unattended for an hour because everyone assumed someone else had it.

An on-call contract turns the norm into a guarantee: one named person, at a known time, responds within a known budget. The contract is the difference between an alert reaching a human and an alert sitting in a channel.

This is a single-maintainer project today. The on-call person is Ivan Boyarkin. The structure on this page describes the shape on-call takes when there is more than one human in the rotation — and even today, treating it as a contract clarifies what the system is promising its users.

Why the roles form an escalation, not a committee

An on-call structure has three roles, and they activate in sequence rather than in parallel. Each one exists because the previous one has a failure mode it cannot cover for itself.

  1. Primary First responder

    Receives the page first; owns the response window.

    • Acknowledges within the agreed time.
    • Owns triage, mitigation, and external communication until handed off.
  2. primary unreachable
  3. Secondary Backup

    Catches the case the primary cannot.

    • Activates when the primary has not acknowledged inside the window.
    • Covers planned absences and ad-hoc unavailability of the primary.
  4. multi-person response
  5. Commander Incident commander

    Coordinates a real incident.

    • Worn as a separate hat the moment more than one person is responding.
    • May be the primary, or someone else if the primary is hands-on with the system.

On a one-person team the same human plays all three roles, but the labels still matter. They mark which hat is on at a given moment, which keeps decisions and actions separable in the timeline. They also make it possible to grow the rotation without rebuilding the structure: the second hire steps into a role that already has a definition.

Severity, response budget, communication cadence

Response targets are downstream of severity. Higher severity buys faster acknowledgement, faster start-of-mitigation, and a more frequent communication cadence; lower severity relaxes all three. The two ends of the ladder mean different things:

  • At SEV-1 and SEV-2, mitigation runs in parallel with communication. The user-visible problem needs to stop and the affected users need to be told that someone is on it; neither activity can wait for the other to finish.
  • At SEV-3 and SEV-4, the response stretches into ordinary working hours. Forcing a SEV-4 into a paged response is the most reliable way to destroy trust in the paging system, because the on-call person learns that the page does not actually mean what it says.

The precise minutes are an operational detail kept where the rest of the alert configuration lives. The conceptual point is that the contract is graded: severity does not only describe how loud the alarm is, it determines how the team has agreed to behave.

Why routing is mechanical, not social

A page that depends on the right human being awake and looking at the right channel is a page that will be missed. Routing turns the social question — who is on-call, who is the backup, when does the handoff happen — into a mechanical one that runs without human attention.

That is why routing config lives in the repository alongside the rest of the alerting rules: the answer to “who gets paged for this?” must be answerable by looking at the file, not by asking the team. The Alertmanager hop and the future PagerDuty integration are implementations of the same idea — make the routing decision a property of the system rather than a property of memory.

Reducing toil is part of the role

On-call should be quiet most of the time. A rotation that is noisy is not evidence of dedication; it is evidence of broken alerts, broken systems, or both. Two consequences follow.

First, the on-call person is allowed — and obliged — to fix the alert that woke them up for the wrong reason. The fix may be lowering the level, raising the threshold, adding context, or deleting the alert entirely. A page that fires every day and that nobody acts on is a page that should not exist.

Second, when the underlying system is the noisy thing, the on-call person files a ticket and the team treats it as work, not as a personal complaint. Toil reduction is the work; it is not a side project that gets done if there is time.

Without this discipline, the rotation slowly destroys itself: the page becomes background noise, the response time drifts, and the on-call contract stops being meaningful.

Why the handoff is the protection

A shift that ends silently is a shift whose context evaporates. The next person on rotation inherits the system without inheriting the knowledge of what fired this week, what is still fragile, what alerts have been silenced and why. The handoff is the artefact that prevents that.

The mechanical content of a handoff — which alerts fired, which remain open, what is fragile, what is silenced — is less important than the act itself. Writing it forces the outgoing on-call to reflect on the week and to surface things they would otherwise have absorbed without noticing. Reading it tells the incoming on-call what posture to adopt before the first alert lands.

Self-care is system safety, not soft skills

Tired engineers cause incidents. Sleep-deprived response correlates with mis-mitigation; multi-day fatigue correlates with errors of judgement that cost more than the page that caused them. Treating self-care as a personal preference is a category error.

The system-safety framing is what makes “take time off the next day” a rule rather than a kindness. It is what makes “tell the secondary before the alert fires, not after” a contract obligation rather than etiquette. The same logic applies to the recurring 3 a.m. alert: snoozing it is the engineer's problem; filing the ticket so it cannot wake the next person is the team's problem.

Tradeoffs

Rotation cadence, hand-off rituals, and follow-the-sun coverage each trade one form of pain for another. There is no rotation that is good for everyone.

Win

Short rotations spread the pain

A one-week rotation means nobody carries the pager for long; recovery time between shifts is real.

Cost

Short rotations fragment context

A multi-day investigation that crosses a hand-off is restarted by a new responder who lacks the thread. Long incidents pay the price.

Win

Explicit hand-off rituals catch silent silences

Forcing the outgoing on-call to articulate every muted alert means none of them survive the rotation by accident.

Cost

Hand-off meetings tax small teams

A 15-minute weekly hand-off on a 3-person team is 1% of every week's engineering time. We accept it; we never love it.

Common failure modes

  • “Someone will look.” No named owner, no acknowledgement budget. Eventually a page goes unattended and the team learns about it from a customer.
  • Severity-blind paging. Every alert pages at the same urgency; the on-call cannot tell SEV-1 from SEV-4 at a glance; trust in the paging system erodes.
  • Routing in human memory. The rules for who gets paged when live in the heads of two people on the team. One of them leaves; the rules leave with them.
  • Silent silences. Alerts muted “temporarily” that nobody ever un-mutes, with no record of why they were muted. A real failure later is hidden by a silenced version of itself.
  • Heroic on-call. The same alert wakes the same person every week; everyone agrees this is unfortunate; nothing changes because filing the fix-the-alert ticket has lower social status than enduring the page.
