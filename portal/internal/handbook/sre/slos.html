SRE Explanation

SLOs & error budget

Without numbers, “the service is fine” is just an opinion. SLOs give us a target; the error budget tells us when to slow down and fix instead of shipping more.

Why we need SLOs

An SLO (service-level objective) gives us a clear target. As long as we hit the target, we can focus on building features. When we miss it, we slow down and spend time on stability. The SLO is also what tells an alert “this is worth waking somebody up for.”

The contract for this service is defined in ADR 0011 — SLO, SLA, and error budget. This page explains what each term means and how we use them day to day.

The three terms

The three terms are nested, not parallel. Each outer layer wraps the layer below it and adds one more thing: a target on the measurement, then a contract on the target.

SLA

Service-Level Agreement

= SLO + money penalty

A contract with a paying customer. This solo project has no SLA today; the layer exists so we can plug one in later without renaming concepts.

SLO

Service-Level Objective

= SLI ≥ target, over a window

The target value of the SLI over a window. An internal promise — we set it ourselves and we measure ourselves. Example: 99.5% of API requests succeed over a rolling 30 days.

SLI

Service-Level Indicator

= raw measurement

A measurement we can read from the system. Examples:

  • Share of HTTP requests with status 2xx or 3xx.
  • p95 response time of GET /conspectuses/due.
  • Share of background jobs that finished without error.

Error budget — what it is and how to spend it

An error budget is “100% minus the SLO”. If the SLO is 99.5%, the budget is 0.5%. Over 30 days that is about 3.6 hours where we are allowed to be unhealthy without breaking our promise.

The budget is a tool, not a punishment. The simple rules:

  • Budget remaining: ship features. Risky migrations, new endpoints, dependency upgrades — go ahead.
  • Budget low (less than 25% left): slow down. Prefer reversible changes, add extra tests, postpone optional refactors.
  • Budget exhausted: feature work pauses. Only changes that improve reliability or fix the SLO miss are merged. Follow runbook 0009 — error budget exhaustion.

Burn-rate alerting

We do not wait for the budget to run out before we react. Burn-rate is the ratio between how fast budget is being spent and how fast it would be spent if we exactly hit the SLO. A burn rate of 1× means we're spending budget at the SLO's pace — sustainable. A burn rate of 10× means we'll exhaust the 30-day budget in three days; 40× exhausts it in eighteen hours; 100×+ is an active outage.

The trick is that one burn-rate window alone is either too jumpy or too slow. Multi-window alerting layers two windows on the same burn-rate threshold: a short window catches the «right now, this is bad» signal, and a longer window confirms it's a sustained pattern, not a 30-second spike. The two have to fire together to page.

How we review SLOs

  1. Once a month, look at the previous 30 days for each SLI.
  2. Record the result in the changelog (“met / missed / N/A”).
  3. If we missed an SLO, write a short note: was it one big incident, slow drift, or a measurement bug?
  4. If the same SLO is missed twice in a row, either fix the system or change the target through an ADR — never silently lower a number.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • “100% uptime” SLO. Impossible to hit and impossible to spend a budget against. Always pick a target less than 100%.
  • Vanity metrics. Picking an SLI that always looks green (e.g., “the homepage returns HTML”). Pick something that reflects user pain.
  • Silent SLO changes. If a number stops being realistic, change it through an ADR. Never quietly relax a target after a bad month.
  • No window. “99.5% availability” with no window can mean anything. Always state the window (7 / 30 days).
