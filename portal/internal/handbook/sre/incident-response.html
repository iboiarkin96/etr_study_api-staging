SRE Explanation

Why incident response is its own discipline

Under pressure, ordinary engineering judgement degrades. Incident response is the framework that keeps the team coordinated, the communication honest, and the record honest enough to learn from — built around the fact that none of those things happen by default.

An incident is a contract failure

An incident is any unplanned event that hurts users or breaks an important promise — an availability SLO, a latency target, a data-integrity guarantee. The framing matters: an incident is not “the system is bad”, it is “the system has fallen out of contract with its users”. Re-establishing the contract — restoring service — is the immediate goal. Understanding why the contract broke is the eventual one.

Treating an incident as a contract failure rather than a moral failing changes the response. The question stops being “who is to blame?” and becomes “what assumption did we hold that turned out to be false?”. The latter has answers that prevent recurrence; the former has answers that prevent honesty.

Why an unstructured response fails

Two engineers handling the same outage with the same information will reach different mitigations if they communicate informally. One disables a feature flag, the other rolls back a deploy, a third scales up; none of them know what the others did. Five minutes later the system is in a state nobody owns. Half an hour later a customer asks for an update and there is nobody to write it. The next day, writing the postmortem, the team cannot reconstruct the order of events.

These failure modes are not caused by lack of competence; they are caused by the cognitive load of a live incident, which is too high to handle the work and the coordination at the same time. Incident response is the discipline that separates those two jobs.

The four moves, and what each is for

An effective response has a shape: declare, mitigate, communicate, resolve. The shape exists because each move addresses a different failure mode of the unstructured response.

  1. Declare Name the incident, pick a severity, open a channel and a timeline. The point is to surface — “this is a thing now, here, owned by this person”. Without declaration the response is private.
  2. Mitigate Stop the user-visible pain. Root cause can wait. The temptation under pressure is to debug first; the disciplined response is to roll back, fail over, drain — anything that restores service, even if it leaves the cause unknown for an hour.
  3. Communicate Tell the affected users what is happening and when the next update will come. Silence reads as nobody is on it; predictable updates buy trust even when the news is bad.
  4. Resolve Confirm health with metrics, not with feelings. Note any workarounds still in place. Name an owner for the postmortem. Until those are true, the incident is suspended, not resolved.

The order matters more than the labels. Communicating before declaring is gossip. Resolving before mitigating is denial. Debugging before mitigating is choosing to be right over choosing to be useful.

Why severity exists, and why it is coarse

Severity is the response's first decision: how loud should this be, how fast must we move, and who has to be woken up. It is intentionally coarse — four buckets, not a continuous score — because finer gradations invite arguments at exactly the moment when arguing is most expensive.

The ladder below ranks scope of impact. Each step removes one ring from the alarm cone: SEV-1 is most users or data at risk; SEV-2 is one core feature seriously degraded; SEV-3 is noticeable but not blocking; SEV-4 is annoying. The point of putting them in a row is that a glance at the response tells you what posture you are taking.

  1. SEV-1
    Major outage

    The service is unusable for most users, or data is at risk.

    Shape
    Broad scope, immediate harm; one user-visible thing breaks for nearly everyone, or invariants of the data are threatened.
    Posture
    All hands, frequent updates, postmortem mandatory.
  2. SEV-2
    Partial outage

    A core feature is down or seriously slow for many users.

    Shape
    Narrower scope than SEV-1 but on a load-bearing feature; people who depend on this feature cannot work.
    Posture
    Dedicated responders, regular updates, postmortem mandatory.
  3. SEV-3
    Degraded

    Noticeable but not blocking — users can still get work done.

    Shape
    The system is in contract for its load-bearing promises but visibly worse than usual.
    Posture
    Working-hours response; postmortem recommended when patterns recur.
  4. SEV-4
    Minor

    Annoying — can wait until the next working day.

    Shape
    Cosmetic or edge-case; no SLO is at risk and no user is blocked.
    Posture
    Filed as a ticket, not paged.

The bias is to round up. Treating a real SEV-1 as a SEV-3 is the more dangerous mistake by an order of magnitude; downgrading an over-classified SEV-2 once the picture clarifies is cheap. Severity discipline lives in the bias more than in the criteria.

Why we separate the roles — even on a small team

Three roles surface in any non-trivial incident, because they are three different cognitive activities that interfere when one brain tries to do them all.

  • The incident commander decides what happens next, owns the timeline, and decides when the incident ends. They do not run commands; doing so puts them deep in the tactical context and removes the bird's-eye view.
  • Operations runs the commands, ships rollbacks, edits configs. Their job is to act and report facts back to the commander.
  • Communications writes the updates that go to users and to the rest of the company. They absorb questions from outside so the commander and ops can focus.

On a one-person team, the same human plays all three roles. The trick is to make the role explicit at each moment — “I am writing as IC” or “I am running this command as Ops” — because the timeline is the postmortem's raw material, and a postmortem that confuses commander decisions with operational actions cannot diagnose what went well or badly.

Why we communicate before we know

The instinct under pressure is to wait until you understand the problem before saying anything. Affected users read silence in only one way: nobody is working on this. By the time you have a satisfying technical answer to share, the trust damage is done.

The discipline is to communicate state, not cause: we are aware, we are working, here is the next time you will hear from us. The cadence of updates buys more trust than the content. A status update every thirty minutes that says “still investigating” is more reassuring than a single brilliant update two hours later, because the first signals that someone is awake and the incident is owned.

Why the timeline is more than a log

The timeline serves the live response — by letting the commander track what has been tried — and it serves the postmortem, by being the only honest record of what actually happened. The two purposes overlap but pull in different directions.

For the live response, brevity matters: short factual entries, UTC timestamps, no narrative reconstruction. For the postmortem, completeness matters: every action and every disproven hypothesis is data. Capturing facts as they happen — rather than at the end, when memory has already smoothed the rough edges — is what makes the postmortem honest. A clean timeline written after the fact is fiction in the same way a tidy lab notebook is fiction.

Tradeoffs

Severity thresholds and roleplay during a live incident are not free. Tightening them buys clarity; loosening them buys speed.

Win

Sharp severity thresholds reduce paging fatigue

Calling SEV-2 only when the criteria match keeps the page meaningful. Responders trust the alarm.

Cost

Borderline incidents under-trigger

A 30-second window of high error rate that misses the threshold by a few seconds gets no SEV — and no postmortem, and no learning.

Win

Naming roles separates decisions from actions

Incident Commander vs. Subject-Matter Expert keeps the timeline auditable. The postmortem can reconstruct who decided what.

Cost

Roles are overhead on small incidents

A two-person team during SEV-3 still has to declare an IC; the ritual feels heavy until you need it.

Common failure modes

  • Debugging before mitigating. The team understands what went wrong an hour into a customer-visible outage that a thirty-second rollback would have ended.
  • Silent response. No external updates because “we don't want to make it worse”. The absence of updates is the thing that makes it worse.
  • Severity by feeling. The first responder is calm, so SEV-2; another team would have called it SEV-1. Calibration drifts; downstream processes (postmortem requirement, paging policy) drift with it.
  • One person doing everything without naming the role. The timeline conflates decisions with actions; the postmortem cannot reconstruct who decided what when.
  • Resolved-but-with-a-workaround. The incident is marked over because the symptoms are gone, but a fragile manual step is silently keeping the system upright. The next failure will arrive without warning.
