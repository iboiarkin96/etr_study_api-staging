QA Explanation

What “test strategy” means

A strategy is not a list of test types. It is a stance on what we are willing to not test, which oracles we believe when they disagree, and where we are willing to be surprised. This page is the rationale; the per-feature plans and the inventory of test types live elsewhere.

Strategy, plan, and tactic — three things that are not the same

The vocabulary of “testing” collapses three different concepts in everyday speech, and most of the disagreements about testing trace back to which one a speaker means.

  1. Strategy Long-lived: what we believe about risk, what we choose not to test, which oracle wins when oracles disagree. Changes seasonally, at most.
  2. Plan Per-feature: which areas of this change carry which risks, what coverage depth we picked, what we left out. Reviewed before the feature ships.
  3. Tactic Per-test: the test types, frameworks, and concrete cases. Cheap to change; lives in the codebase.

A strategy that descends into tactics — listing pytest fixtures, naming conventions, framework choices — is doing the wrong job at the wrong altitude. Those belong on the test pyramid page and in the per-feature test plans. The strategy's job is to make the choices that those plans inherit.

The mission, in one sentence

QA exists to shorten the feedback loop between the team and reality. Every other principle on this page is downstream of that sentence.

Defects caught in review are cheaper than defects caught in CI; defects caught in CI are cheaper than defects caught in staging; defects caught in staging are cheaper than defects caught in production. The strategy is the set of bets we make about where to spend attention on that curve, and how to keep the curve from steepening.

The five principles, and what they cost

Most strategy documents list principles as if they were free. Each of ours is a trade-off; the cost is what makes the principle load-bearing.

  • Shift-left. Testing starts at the spec. The cost: QA's time on a feature spent before any code exists, when there is nothing to click on. The benefit: defects fixed in a sentence rather than in a refactor.
  • Risk-based, not coverage-based. We do not test everything equally. The cost: low-impact areas get less attention than they would under a uniform-coverage rule, and occasionally something embarrassing slips through there. The benefit: the high-impact areas — auth, persistent writes, payments — get the depth they need.
  • Automation first, manual where it pays. Repeated checks are automated; manual time is reserved for exploration. The cost: the upfront expense of writing the automation, and a discipline of refusing to repeat manual work. The benefit: human attention spent on the things only humans can do.
  • Evidence-driven. Every pass must be reproducible. The cost: more typing, more screenshots, more linked artefacts. The benefit: when something fails in production, the question “did we test that?” has an answer instead of an opinion.
  • One source of truth. The spec page is the contract. The cost: a feature blocked on a missing or wrong spec, even when the code “works”. The benefit: behaviour does not silently drift between code, tests, and documentation.

Test oracles — how we decide what counts as “correct”

An oracle is whatever a test compares its observation against to decide pass or fail. The strategic question is not which oracle to use — most tests use several — but which oracle wins when they disagree.

Our ordering is deliberate and worth stating in full. The spec page is the human contract: it is what callers were promised. The OpenAPI baseline is the machine contract: it is what the code currently says. Shared cross-cutting standards — error envelope, idempotency, versioning — are the rules that apply across all specs. Heuristics are last because they are local to the tester: useful for forming an opinion, weak as a tiebreaker.

The strategically important consequence is this: if two oracles disagree, you do not silently pick the one that makes the test pass. Either the spec is wrong, the code is wrong, or the test is wrong — exactly one of these, and figuring out which is the activity. “All three are right and they disagree” is a state we refuse to ship from.

Scope is a bet, not a budget

A common framing puts the scope of testing as a budget — a fixed amount of QA time, divided among areas. The framing that survives reality treats scope as a series of bets: for each area in or out of scope, the strategy is implicitly betting that defects in that area are tolerable or that the cost of finding them outweighs the cost of fielding them.

This is why an explicit out of scope list matters. Saying “we do not test third-party SaaS internals” is the bet that integration boundaries are sufficient. Saying “English only” is the bet that localisation is not a near-term concern. The bets may be right or wrong, but they are knowable: the next person to ask “why doesn't this work in Cyrillic?” has an answer, instead of a silent assumption.

When the strategy itself should change

A strategy that is never revised has stopped being a strategy and has become folklore. The signals that the strategy needs a fresh look:

  • An incident with a category we did not believe needed coverage. If a class of defect we explicitly chose not to chase shows up in production, the bet was wrong and the strategy needs to acknowledge it.
  • A pattern of late defects. Repeatedly catching defects at staging that should have been catchable in CI suggests the line between unit, integration, and contract tests is in the wrong place.
  • A pattern of test-induced delay. If shipping is slow because of test ceremony that has stopped earning its cost, the strategy is over-fitting and needs to drop something.
  • A change in product scope. A new external integration, a new compliance regime, a new release cadence — each may invalidate the cost/benefit on which the strategy rests.

Tradeoffs

A test strategy is a budget allocation, not a quality philosophy. Choosing what to test is, by definition, choosing what not to test — and the cost shows up where the strategy ignored.

Win

A risk-ranked surface focuses scarce testing time

"Test what matters most" is hard until you've actually named the risk surface. After you do, the same hours of testing catch more bugs because they hit the right places.

Cost

Risk-ranking ages badly

Last quarter's high-risk areas may be this quarter's stable ones. A strategy without a re-ranking cadence becomes a strategy aimed at yesterday's failure modes.

Win

Stating a coverage stop prevents endless testing

"We test until X is true" lets you ship; "we test until we feel good" never lets you. The stop criterion is what makes the strategy an actual contract.

Cost

Stop criteria invite gaming

"Coverage ≥ 80%" is met by a test that calls every method without asserting anything. Quantitative stops without qualitative review become metrics gaming, not quality.

Common failure modes

  • The strategy as a wish list. Principles with no cost stated. Everyone agrees; nobody changes their behaviour.
  • The strategy as a tactic catalogue. Pages of test types and tools and no statement of what they are for. A change of framework forces a strategy rewrite that should have been one section deep.
  • “Out of scope: none.” Either dishonest or expensive. A strategy that is in scope of everything is in scope of nothing.
  • Oracle silence. A strategy that does not say which source wins when sources disagree. Each tester picks one, inconsistently.
  • Strategy frozen in amber. Last revised eighteen months ago; refers to teams, products, and tools that no longer exist. The team works around it without admitting they do.
