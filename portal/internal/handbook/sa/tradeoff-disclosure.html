Tradeoff disclosure

The canonical shape for any section that names what a decision costs. Operationalises Substance Principle 02 — «Disclose tradeoffs honestly». Three section-templates (one for Explanation, one for ADR/RFC, one for normative Reference), four anti-patterns to refuse on review, four canonical exemplars already in the portal, and a five-gate retroactive audit checklist. Pages without disclosure read as marketing; pages with it read as «we knew what we were signing up for».

Substance gate · mandatory Explanation · ADR · RFC · Reference Owned by SA

TL;DR

Every non-procedural page carries a tradeoff block. Explanation uses § Tradeoffs with at least two named «we gain X, we lose Y, we accept Z». ADR / RFC uses § Alternatives considered with at least two rejected options + one-line «rejected because…». Reference uses § Limits naming load ceilings, supported versions, and failure modes. Anything else is form-compliant but not substantive.

Explain

01

Why we disclose

Architecture without trade-offs reads as marketing. A page that lists only the benefits signals one of two things, both bad: either the author did not think hard enough, or the author thought hard but chose to hide the cost. Senior reviewers detect both within 30 seconds. The Architect's Bar test for any non-procedural page is: «an experienced reader should be able to say within 30 seconds what this design gives up to be what it is.»

Disclosure also gates revision. An ADR that ships without alternatives is unauditable — six months later nobody remembers the constraint set, so the decision cannot be revisited without re-running the whole analysis. The «rejected because…» one-liner is what makes an ADR auditable, not just recorded.

Rules

02

The three mandatory shapes

Disclosure looks different depending on what kind of page you are writing. Three shapes cover the whole substance contract; each row of the table below is normative for the quadrant in its second column.

Block Required for Minimum content Failure mode
§ Tradeoffs Every Explanation page At least 2 named tradeoffs, each shaped as «we gain X, we lose Y, we accept it because Z». Page reads as a textbook chapter, not as «opinion based on this team's experience».
§ Alternatives considered Every ADR · every RFC At least 2 rejected options, each with one-line «rejected because…». Empty block ⇒ ADR fails review. Decision becomes unauditable after 6 months; reviewer cannot tell whether the constraint set still holds.
§ Limits Every Reference page that promises behaviour Where the promise breaks: load ceiling, supported versions, known failure modes. Downstream consumers assume the promise is unconditional and break at the edge.
§ Would we do this again? ADRs on 6-month revisit (optional but high-signal) One-line retroactive «still right», or «here's what we'd change today». Team's collective memory of the decision drifts; new joiners read confidence that the team no longer feels.

Rules

03

Template · Explanation page § Tradeoffs

Drop near the bottom of any Explanation page, just before the history footer. Two named tradeoffs minimum; three is better. Each tradeoff is one paragraph, shaped «we gain X, we lose Y, we accept it because Z». 

<h2 id="tradeoffs">Tradeoffs</h2>
<p>
  Three trade-offs this design accepts deliberately. Knowing them tells you
  where the model bends if you push it in the wrong direction.
</p>
<ol class="path-timeline" data-tone="rose">
  <li><div>
    <strong><Trade-off name>.</strong> We gain <benefit>; we lose
    <cost>; we accept the cost because <reason rooted in our context>.
  </div></li>
  <li><div>
    <strong><Trade-off name>.</strong> …
  </div></li>
      </ol>

Worked example — from handbook/qa/test-pyramid.html

  • Speed vs realism. We gain millisecond unit-test feedback at the cost of a coverage hole around real DB constraints; we accept it because integration tests close the hole at PR time and a flaky 30-minute suite blocks more PRs than a 5-minute suite that misses 5% of DB regressions.
  • Coverage vs coupling. We gain confidence by testing the whole stack end-to-end; we lose the ability to refactor freely (every E2E test pins a UI surface in place). We accept the coupling because shipping confidently in a small team is worth more than refactor-flexibility we rarely use.

Rules

04

Template · ADR / RFC § Alternatives considered

Mandatory for every ADR. The empty-block failure is the single most common ADR rot — flag it on review and block merge until at least two rejected options carry their one-line reason. 

<h2 id="alternatives">Alternatives considered</h2>
<p>
  Options we evaluated and the reason each lost. The chosen option (§ Decision)
  is <OPTION_X>; the others are below.
</p>
<ul class="rail-list" data-tone="rose">
  <li>
    <strong>Option A — <name>.</strong> <One-line description.>
    <em>Rejected because</em> <reason in 1-2 sentences, rooted in our
    constraints, not in industry generalities.>
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Option B — <name>.</strong> <One-line description.>
    <em>Rejected because</em> <reason.>
  </li>
</ul>
<p>
  Trade we accept by choosing <OPTION_X>: <the cost the chosen option imposes>.
  We accept it because <reason this cost is cheaper than the rejected alternatives'
  costs>.
      </p>

Worked example — from ADR 0008

  • Option A — keep ad-hoc scripts/*.sh wrappers. Each developer maintains their own shortcuts. Rejected because drift was the #1 onboarding complaint and PRs kept adding redundant scripts that did almost-the-same thing.
  • Option B — switch to just / task / nox. Modern alternatives with cleaner syntax. Rejected because Make is already installed on every dev machine and CI runner; adding a tool-install gate for «slightly nicer syntax» would burn budget we'd rather spend on docs-check.
  • Option C — single monolithic Makefile with no taxonomy. What we had. Rejected because make help returned 80+ lines with no grouping; new joiners could not predict the target name for a workflow they'd never run.

Trade we accept by choosing the taxonomy. Make's tab-vs-space sensitivity remains a footgun, and the taxonomy adds a small upfront cost when adding any new target. We accept this because the lookup cost on make help is the load-bearing newcomer interaction and it pays back within the first day.

Rules

05

Template · Reference § Limits

Required on every Reference page that promises behaviour (SLOs, API contracts, schema invariants). Name the load ceiling, supported versions, and known failure modes — downstream consumers assume the promise holds unless you state otherwise. 

<h2 id="limits">Limits</h2>
<ul class="rail-list" data-tone="warn">
  <li><strong>Load ceiling.</strong> Behaviour as documented holds up to
    <quantified threshold + horizon>. Beyond that, <observed degradation>.</li>
  <li><strong>Supported versions.</strong> Promise holds for <range>;
    earlier / later versions are <status: deprecated / untested / known-broken>.</li>
  <li><strong>Known failure modes.</strong> The promise breaks when
    <condition>. The break manifests as <observable signature>.</li>
      </ul>

Worked example — Reference · SLOs

  • Load ceiling. p95 < 400 ms holds up to 4 k RPS sustained over a 1-hour window (drill 2026-04-12). Beyond 4 k RPS, latency degrades non-linearly and the 6 h burn-rate alarm fires within 18 minutes.
  • Supported versions. Burn-rate maths assumes the 99.5 % monthly target. If the SLO is renegotiated, the multi-window thresholds in § Alerting must be re-derived; the formula in SRE Workbook ch. 5 p. 91, table 5-2 applies.
  • Known failure modes. Burn-rate alarms become noisy during the first 24 hours after deploy because the rolling-window denominator is small. Suppress for that period and rely on threshold alarms.

Anti-patterns

06

Anti-patterns — refuse these on review

Each pattern below is a review-block. If you see one on a PR, request changes and link to this page; do not merge until the page carries the right shape from § 02.

  • Empty «Alternatives considered». ADR ships with None considered. or no block at all. The single most common ADR rot. Block merge until at least two rejected options carry one-line «rejected because…».
  • «Alternatives» that are all strawmen. Two rejected options that nobody would seriously propose («Option A: do nothing. Rejected because nothing is bad.»). Signals the author skipped the analysis. Demand at least one rejected option that another team in the industry actually uses.
  • Tradeoffs without an accept clause. «We picked X because X is good.» «X is faster than Y.» These are claims, not trade-offs. A trade-off names the cost; without the cost there is no trade. Demand the «we lose Y» half.
  • Industry-pattern citation with no anchor. «We followed the SRE Book.» Two SRE books exist, ~500 pages each. Demand SRE Workbook ch. 5 «Alerting on SLOs», pp. 88-93 or similar pinpoint citation. See Substance Principle 04.
  • «Limits» replaced by hedging. «Behaviour may vary under load.» Refuse. Demand a number with a horizon: «p95 < 400 ms up to 4 k RPS sustained over 1 hour.»
