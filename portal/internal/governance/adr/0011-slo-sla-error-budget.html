SLOs, SLAs, Error Budget, and Monitoring Alerts

Explicit SLIs and 30-day SLOs (availability, latency p95, readiness), a 0.1% error budget with burn alerts, and Blackbox-probed readiness — all wired into Prometheus rules.

Context

Why this matters: Dashboards alone do not answer “are we allowed to ship today?” Service level objectives (SLOs) turn metrics into targets (for example “99.9% of requests succeed without 5xx”). An error budget is the allowed failure slice implied by that target; when it is exhausted, the team slows feature work and fixes reliability—an idea popularized by Google SRE and widely adopted in the industry.

ADR 0009 added metrics and probes. This ADR adds explicit SLIs/SLOs, budget-style burn signals, and alerts so on-call responds to customer-impacting risk, not arbitrary thresholds.

Decision

Service level indicators (SLIs)

  • HTTP availability (5xx) — fraction of requests that do not return a 5xx status, measured from http_requests_total (Prometheus job study-app-api).
  • API latency — 95th percentile of http_request_duration_seconds for routes whose path_template is not /metrics, /live, or /ready, so health and scrape traffic do not dominate the SLI.
  • Readiness — success of an HTTP GET to /ready expecting status 200, measured via the Prometheus Blackbox exporter (job study-app-blackbox, metric probe_success). This reflects dependency readiness; it is distinct from the HTTP error SLI on application routes.

Service level objectives (SLOs)

Targets are evaluated over a rolling 30-day window unless stated otherwise.

SLI Objective Notes
HTTP availability (non-5xx) 99.9% of requests succeed without 5xx Complementary view: 5xx ratio must stay within 0.1% of traffic over the window.
API latency (p95) 95% of in-scope requests complete in under 500 ms Measured as histogram p95 over 5 min rate windows for alerting; SLO is a monthly/rolling intent.
Readiness probe /ready returns HTTP 200 at least 99.9% of probe intervals Blackbox-based; indicates DB and dependency health from outside the process.

Error budget

For HTTP availability, the allowed bad event ratio is 1 − 0.999 = 0.001 (0.1% of requests may fail with 5xx over the rolling window). Prometheus recording rules expose job:study_app:http_5xx:burn30d: the ratio of actual 5xx share to the allowed 0.001 share. Values above 1 mean the error budget for the window is exhausted.

Fast burn (short windows) and sustained high 5xx rates are alerted separately so on-call can act before the full 30-day budget is consumed.

Optional external SLA

An SLA is a commitment to customers or internal consumers (support tickets, credits, page). This ADR defines internal SLOs only. If a public SLA is published, it should be equal to or stricter than these SLOs, with measurement clauses referencing Prometheus/Grafana and the error budget exhaustion runbook for breach handling.

Alerting

Prometheus alerting rules live under services/monitoring/prometheus/rules/. Alertmanager is not required for evaluation: firing alerts appear in the Prometheus UI (/alerts). Wiring Alertmanager for notifications is a follow-up.

Scope

  • In scope: the conventions, rules, and artefacts named in the Decision section above.
  • Out of scope: any system, process, or surface not explicitly listed here.

Alternatives considered

Three alternatives were evaluated before committing to the SLI/SLO/budget triple + burn-rate alerts. Each is real (another serious team in the industry uses it) and explicitly rejected with a reason rooted in our constraints. Anchored on Site Reliability Workbook ch. 5 «Alerting on SLOs», pp. 88-93, table 5-2.

Threshold alerts only (no SLO, no error budget)

rejected

Why considered: the «5xx rate > 1% for 5 min ⇒ page» pattern. Simpler to write, no recording rules. Most non-SRE teams start here.

Why rejected: threshold alerts are decoupled from customer impact — they fire on the same percentage during low and high traffic, which makes them either alarmist (during low-traffic nights) or silent (during high-traffic days). Error-budget burn is denominator-aware: 5xx during the day's traffic peak is treated as bigger than the same percentage at 3 a.m. The burn-rate model from SRE Workbook ch. 5 is the only one that makes alerts proportional to user pain.

Higher target (99.99% «four nines»)

rejected

Why considered: a stricter SLO signals «we care about uptime». Used by payment-rail and identity-provider services.

Why rejected: four-nines availability requires investment we have not made — multi-region active-active, no-downtime deploys, an on-call rotation, sub-minute recovery times. Publishing 99.99% without those is theatre; the moment we miss it (and we will, given a single instance) the SLO becomes meaningless. 99.9% with measurable infrastructure is a credible commitment; 99.99% with our current infrastructure is not.

External SLA from day one (with credits / refunds clause)

rejected

Why considered: the «commit publicly so the team has skin in the game» argument. Forces operational discipline. Used by SaaS vendors with revenue tied to uptime.

Why rejected: SLA is a customer contract, with legal and financial implications. We have no paying customers yet and no team to defend a breach in court; publishing an SLA before there is a commercial relationship is empty signalling. Internal SLO + the «SLA stricter than SLO» rule in § Optional external SLA keeps the door open for the day we ship for money.

Trade we accept by choosing SLO + budget + burn-rate alerts. We pay for the recording rules, the Blackbox container, and the operational overhead of explaining «what's the budget left?» to anyone new. We accept it because each rejected alternative either decouples alerts from customer impact (thresholds), commits us to infrastructure we have not built (99.99%), or makes promises we cannot defend (premature SLA). The SLI/SLO/budget triple is the smallest mechanism that ties reliability to a number we can actually move.

Consequences

Positive

wins
  • Shared language for reliability and explicit trade-offs between velocity and stability.
  • Alerts grounded in SLOs rather than ad hoc thresholds alone.

Trade-offs

costs

Two named tradeoffs accepted on purpose. Shape from Tradeoff disclosure.

  • 30-day rolling window with 99.9% target. We gain numbers that align with industry-standard SLO conventions (SRE Workbook ch. 4) and that the integrator can budget against in calendar quarters. We lose the precision a longer window would give in a low-traffic environment — the same single failure event has a larger ratio impact on a thin denominator. We accept it because 30 d is the smallest window where the budget arithmetic stays readable to humans; tuning the window for non-prod is allowed and called out in the dev-onboarding notes.
  • Blackbox-probed readiness as a separate SLI. We gain an outside-in view of /ready that catches «process is alive but the LB is not getting through» — the kind of incident a request-rate SLI alone would miss. We lose one extra container in the compose stack and the operational overhead of «Blackbox can scrape API host». We accept it because the external-probe perspective is irreplaceable for readiness; the container cost is paid once per environment.

Revisit — would we do this again?

Yes, with two SRE-Book moves we are still missing. The 99.9% target + 30-day window + Blackbox external probe has aged well; the budget arithmetic remains readable and the burn-rate alerts fire when something real is happening. Two refinements we would commit to on day one if starting today:

  • Wire Alertmanager to Slack / PagerDuty, not just Prometheus UI. Today firing alerts appear at /alerts; nobody watches that URL. The page-on-burn loop is broken until Alertmanager routes; this was explicitly flagged as a follow-up in the ADR and is still open.
  • Publish a one-page «what does 99.9% feel like» explainer in onboarding. The number is meaningless without translation: 99.9% ≈ 43 min / month of downtime allowance, ≈ 8.7 h / year. New joiners ask this question every time; the answer should be in the onboarding handbook, not in chat.

Compatibility and migration

  • Backward-compatibility impact: none — pure governance / convention change with no wire-level effect for existing consumers.
  • Migration plan: apply the rules to new work; bring existing artefacts into compliance during normal touch-ups.
  • Rollback strategy: revert the merge PR; no data or schema changes to undo.
