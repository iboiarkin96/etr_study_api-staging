Methodology Explanation

ETR — Extract, Transform, Retrieve

Working notes on the ETR (Extract–Transform–Retrieve) study pattern: how a person uses it to retain what they read or watch. Exploratory — no product surface here. Authoritative rules live in Service catalog · Architecture and the internal API docs.

Fixing weak retention — short overview

Information slips away quickly when intake is passive and nothing forces effortful retrieval. The ETR framework keeps three steps in the loop: capture lean cues while you read or watch, consolidate them in your own words straight after, and rehearse on a schedule with feedback.

A practical bundle that works well together:

  • During intake: a three-column cue sheet (anchors only, not full lecture notes).
  • Right after: one dense paragraph plus bullets, explained plainly.
  • After that: spaced active recall on a predictable schedule, with an error log that targets weak cues.

ETR at home (with notes)

The three steps in short: Extract is bounded consumption with capture; Transform is recall and compression without the source; Retrieve is scheduled rehearsal from the single master conspectus (cue, dense paragraph, and bullets).

Step What to do Goal
1. Extract
(~50 min consumption)		 Use a clean three-column cue sheet. Column 1: keywords (one to three words). Column 2: questions or gaps (for example, “What are the three steps of X?”). Column 3: one- to three-word hints or answers only. Aim for anchors only — conventional notes tend to be passive and lower the loop's yield. Pause every 5–10 minutes. Keep the page count low (one or two). Add one quick sketch per major idea where it helps. Lower cognitive load and surface gaps while reading or watching.
2. Transform
(10–20 min, immediately after)		 Close all sources. Work from Column 2: try to recall aloud or on scratch paper; use Column 3 only as a last resort. Then write one dense paragraph (at most five sentences) and turn it into five to seven bullets explaining the idea as if to a colleague. Use a Feynman-style check: simplify, connect to your work, teach it plainly. Force elaboration and integration, not copy-paste.
3. Retrieve
(spaced)		 Use the single master conspectus from Transform. Cover it completely: rewrite the bullets and speak the paragraph aloud. When you fail, peek, log the error, correct it, then re-cover and retry until you succeed twice in a row. Speak aloud every session. Spacing starts about an hour later, then on the next day, then weekly and beyond. See Retrieve: slots, tags, and cadence. Turn one-time study into durable memory through retrieval practice.

Retrieve: slots, tags, and cadence

A simple four-slot mental model (whether you track it in an app or on paper):

  • Slot A: today
  • Slot B: tomorrow
  • Slot C: in three days
  • Slot D: in seven days, then fourteen, thirty, and so on as you extend the ladder

Tag each conspectus easy, hard, or forgot. Easy moves it to the next slot; hard or forgot returns it to Slot A. With consistent practice, the daily review fits in about five to ten minutes.

Keep an error log for misses. Record the weak question, keyword, or explanation so the next Transform pass targets the actual failure mode.

Out of home — no access to notes

Without the cue sheet (for example, on a walk with headphones), a lighter ETR loop still works:

Phase What to do
During the walk Every 5–10 minutes, pause the audio and say aloud: “The last few minutes were about ___ . The one thing to remember is ___ .” That rehearses the gist in working memory. Optionally record 30-second voice memos for later.
Within an hour after Transcribe memos or write from memory, then feed the result straight into Transform (dense paragraph + bullets). Resist re-listening to the whole episode — retrieval is the work.
