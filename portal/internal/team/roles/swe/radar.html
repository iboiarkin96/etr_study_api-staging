Group A · 5 practices

Code craft & quality

How we write code that other engineers can change a year later without fear. When this group is weak, every change is risky, every refactor a multi-week project, and the codebase calcifies into a "legacy" nobody wants to touch. Strong code-craft practices keep the team moving at week 100 as fast as it moved at week 1: small steps, fast feedback, and ruthless attention to what makes code easy to delete.

01

Trunk-Based Development — short-lived branches, daily merges

3.0→8.5

What it is. Every engineer commits to the same main branch at least once a day. Long-lived feature branches are forbidden; anything that does not survive a 24-hour merge is hidden behind a feature flag instead. The DORA elite cohort lives on trunk-based development — it is the strongest single predictor of high delivery performance.

Why it matters. Long branches accumulate merge debt that nobody pays at the time of writing — it gets paid later as painful rebases, surprise conflicts, and "integration days" that drag the whole team off real work. Daily integration makes the cost visible at the moment a divergence appears, when it is still cheap to resolve.

How we will roll it out. Enforce a 24-hour max-branch-age on PRs (CI blocks PRs older than this). Wrap anything not finished in a feature flag and merge it dark. Track average PR-age weekly and post it in the engineering channel — make the number visible until it lives under 24h.

02

Test-Driven Development — red, green, refactor

2.5→7.5

What it is. The engineer writes a failing test first, then the smallest production change that makes it pass, then refactors with the safety net of the green test. TDD treats the test as the spec and the code as a consequence — it inverts the usual ordering and shrinks every step to something obviously correct.

Why it matters. Code written test-after tends to be hard to test because it was not designed to be testable; the result is tests that exercise implementation details and a codebase that is fragile to change. TDD shapes the code's public surface to be small, composable, and verifiable from the outside.

How we will roll it out. Pick one new module and write all its tests first for two weeks. Pair-program the first three TDD sessions so the rhythm is shared by example. Track ratio of test-first commits in CI; aim for > 60% of new logic written test-first within the quarter.

03

Small PRs — < 400 LOC, < 24h to review

4.0→8.5

What it is. Every change is sliced into a PR small enough for a reviewer to read, understand, and merge in under an hour. Google's research on code-review found defect-detection drops sharply above ~200 changed lines; the industry rule-of-thumb is < 400 LOC. Reviews finish in under 24h, so the author is never blocked over a working day.

Why it matters. Big PRs get rubber-stamped because the reviewer cannot hold the change in working memory. Small PRs make review meaningful: the reviewer actually catches bugs, the author actually rewrites bad ideas, and the codebase actually improves through review rather than degrading with each merge.

How we will roll it out. Configure danger.js to flag PRs > 400 LOC or older than 24h. Adopt the "stack of small PRs" workflow (Graphite / git-spice / Phabricator-style stacked diffs) so that one logical feature can be reviewed as a sequence of small reviewable units instead of one huge dump.

04

Pair & Mob Programming — knowledge as a shared asset

2.0→7.0

What it is. Two engineers (pair) or the whole team (mob) work on one keyboard, on one problem, in real time. Originated in XP (Kent Beck), formalised by Woody Zuill at Hunter Industries, and now a default mode at firms like Pivotal Labs and parts of Stripe / Shopify for the gnarliest work. The output is not just code — it is shared context, faster onboarding, and zero bus-factor.

Why it matters. Solo code review catches symptoms; pairing catches the bad idea before the keystroke. Mobbing on the hardest problems turns "the one person who knows X" into "the whole team knows X". The hidden cost of an expert silo — a 6-month rewrite when that person leaves — dwarfs any short-term productivity loss from pairing.

How we will roll it out. Schedule one 2-hour pair-programming block per engineer per week, on a real ticket. For any task touching the auth or payments paths, default to mobbing with the on-call. After four weeks, retro on which work benefited most and double down there.

05

Continuous Refactoring — Boy Scout rule, no rewrite weeks

3.5→8.0

What it is. Refactoring is a daily activity, not a quarterly project. Every PR leaves the code slightly better than it found it — the Boy Scout rule from Robert Martin. The catalogue of small, behaviour-preserving moves comes from Martin Fowler's Refactoring (1999, 2nd ed. 2018): Rename Variable, Extract Function, Inline Variable, Replace Conditional with Polymorphism, and so on.

Why it matters. The alternative is the "great rewrite": a 6-month engineering sink that produces a parallel codebase, no new features, and an exhausted team. Continuous refactoring keeps the code's maintenance cost approximately constant over time, instead of letting it grow exponentially until a rewrite becomes inevitable.

How we will roll it out. Add a "refactors included" section to the PR template. Pre-commit hook on staged files runs the IDE's safe-rename / extract-function checks. Track lines-of-comment-rot, cyclomatic complexity, and untested file count in CI — make the trend visible alongside coverage.

Delivery & automation

How a change goes from a commit to production safely, many times a day. When this group is weak, deploys are big-bang events scheduled for Fridays, rollbacks take an hour, and the team trades velocity for stability — and gets neither. Strong delivery practices make every commit a candidate for production, every release reversible in seconds, and every risky change a quiet, gradual exposure rather than a heart-stopping switch-flip.

06

CI & green-trunk discipline — every commit deployable

5.0→8.5

What it is. Continuous Integration as Kent Beck originally described it: every engineer integrates with main multiple times per day, every integration runs the full test suite, and if main is red, fixing it is the team's top priority. The DORA "Accelerate" research shows green-trunk discipline as one of the four predictors of elite software performance.

Why it matters. A long-running red main is a tax on every engineer on the team — nobody can confidently rebase, deploy, or merge. Teams that tolerate it slip from elite (deploy hourly) to medium (deploy weekly). Restoring main quickly is not "nice to have" — it is the basic precondition for everything downstream.

How we will roll it out. Sub-10-minute CI on every PR. Required green-main check on merge. Page the team if main is red for > 15 minutes. Track DORA's "time to restore service for failed deploy" and post it monthly.

07

Feature Flags — decouple deploy from release

3.0→8.0

What it is. Every risky change ships behind a runtime flag (LaunchDarkly, GrowthBook, Statsig, internal). The deploy installs the code; the release flips the flag for a percentage of users, then 100%. GitHub, Stripe, Atlassian, and Shopify all operate this way — typical large repos run with hundreds of active flags at any time, plus a lifecycle process that retires them after rollout.

Why it matters. Without flags, "rollback" means revert-and-redeploy, which on a big monorepo can take 30+ minutes — long enough to cost real money during an incident. Flags reduce rollback to a single config flip and let you separate "is the code safe?" from "is the change a good idea?" — two questions that deserve two different tests.

How we will roll it out. Adopt GrowthBook (OSS) or LaunchDarkly. Every new user-facing change ships flagged. Set up a flag-lifecycle policy: every flag has a default-on date; after that date, CI fails if the flag still has both branches in code.

08

Pre-commit hooks — fast feedback before push

5.0→8.5

What it is. A managed bundle of fast checks (formatters, linters, type-checks on changed files, secret scan, conventional-commit validator) runs locally before the commit lands. The toolchain — pre-commit, Husky, lefthook — is configured in-repo so every clone has the same gates. The same checks re-run in CI as the source of truth, but most issues never reach the PR.

Why it matters. Style-and-format comments waste reviewer time and crowd out comments about substance. Hooks turn "your indent is wrong" from a Tuesday afternoon PR comment into a fix on the engineer's own keyboard, before commit. They also catch secret commits before they leave the laptop — a strictly cheaper place to catch them than git-history rewriting after the fact.

How we will roll it out. Adopt pre-commit with: formatter, linter, type-check on staged files, gitleaks for secrets, conventional-commits check. CI re-runs the same hooks via pre-commit run --all-files. New repos get the hook bundle as a template.

09

Conventional Commits — machine-readable history

3.5→8.0

What it is. Every commit follows the Conventional Commits 1.0 grammar: type(scope): subject, with types feat / fix / docs / refactor / chore / test. A breaking change is marked with !. semantic-release, changesets, or release-please then auto-generate CHANGELOG and bump SemVer based on the commit log.

Why it matters. Unstructured commit messages mean changelog generation is manual, version bumps are guesses, and "what changed in 2.3.0?" requires re-reading the diff. Structured commits turn the git log itself into the source of truth for releases — automated, auditable, and impossible to forget.

How we will roll it out. Add commitlint to pre-commit and CI. Adopt semantic-release (Node) or python-semantic-release. Document the type allow-list and scope vocabulary in the contributor guide. Every PR title is also a conventional-commit, so the squash-merge default produces clean history.

10

Progressive Delivery — canary, blue-green, ramped

1.5→7.5

What it is. Every deploy is rolled out to a small slice first (1% → 5% → 25% → 100%) while automated health gates compare error rate, latency p99, and business KPIs between the new and old versions. The rollout pauses or rolls back automatically if any gate fails. The term was coined by James Governor (RedMonk, 2018); Argo Rollouts and Flagger are the standard tooling.

Why it matters. A "deploy to all users" change exposes 100% of your traffic to any latent bug — a 0.2% regression is invisible in a test environment but immediately visible in an SLO dashboard. Canary deploys catch the bug at 1% of users, where the cost is two orders of magnitude smaller. This is how Netflix, Google, and every elite team ship.

How we will roll it out. Wire Argo Rollouts (or Flagger) into the deploy pipeline. Define one health gate per service: error rate < baseline +2σ, p99 < baseline +20%. Start with two services on canary; expand to all critical services within the quarter.

Architecture & design

How we make design decisions that survive scaling from 10 to 10 million users. When this group is weak, every new feature requires a partial rewrite, contracts drift between services, and the team accumulates incidental complexity faster than essential complexity. Strong architecture practices push design decisions to the boundary (configs, contracts, types) so the code in the middle stays small, replaceable, and obvious.

11

Twelve-Factor App — portable, scalable, ops-friendly

5.5→8.5

What it is. The 12 design rules from Heroku's Adam Wiggins (2011): one codebase, declared dependencies, config in env, stateless processes, explicit port binding, dev/prod parity, logs as event streams, etc. Every cloud-native runtime (Kubernetes, Cloud Run, Fly.io, Render) assumes these rules; violating them shows up as ops pain, not compile errors.

Why it matters. The most common production failures — secrets baked into builds, stateful in-memory caches that break under autoscaling, dev "works on my machine" — are all 12-factor violations. Following the rules is cheap if you start there; retrofitting them onto a non-conforming codebase costs weeks per service.

How we will roll it out. Audit each service against the 12 rules. Move all secrets to a secret manager. Make every process stateless (sessions in Redis, no local files). Add a 12-factor checklist to the service-creation template so new services start conformant.

12

API Contract-First — OpenAPI / GraphQL SDL / Protobuf before code

4.0→8.5

What it is. The interface is written first, in a machine-readable schema language: OpenAPI for HTTP, GraphQL SDL for GraphQL, Protobuf for gRPC. Handlers, client SDKs, mocks, and tests are generated from that schema. Stripe and GitHub publicly run on this model — Stripe generates its 7 client libraries from a single OpenAPI source.

Why it matters. Code-first APIs drift: the docs lie, the SDK is a hand-written reimagination of the server, and breaking changes ship silently. Contract-first makes the schema the source of truth — clients and servers must agree on it before either is built, and CI fails if they don't.

How we will roll it out. Write OpenAPI for every new endpoint before the handler. Generate the server stub and the client SDK from it. Run schema-compatibility checks in CI (oasdiff, buf). Block PRs that introduce undocumented endpoints.

13

DDD tactical patterns — aggregates, value objects, services

2.5→7.5

What it is. Eric Evans' tactical Domain-Driven Design vocabulary, refined by Vaughn Vernon in "Implementing DDD". Every state-changing operation goes through an Aggregate root that owns a consistency boundary. Identifierless things become Value Objects (immutable). Cross-cutting operations become Domain Services. Persistence is hidden behind Repositories.

Why it matters. Without tactical DDD, business logic gets sprinkled across controllers, ORMs, and helpers — and every change touches all three. Aggregates concentrate the rules of one consistency unit in one place: the rules are obvious to readers, the test surface is small, and accidentally breaking an invariant becomes much harder.

How we will roll it out. Pick one bounded context and refactor it to an aggregate-rooted model. Use a code review checklist: every state change goes through the root; value objects are immutable; the repository never returns mutable internals. Spread the pattern outward as new bounded contexts are added.

14

Type safety as default — strict mode, no escape hatches

5.0→8.5

What it is. Every codebase runs its language's strictest type-checker as a required CI gate: TypeScript strict: true, Python mypy --strict, Rust (which has no choice), or Go (gradually with golangci-lint typecheck). Escape hatches (any, cast(), type: ignore) are reviewed individually and must be commented with a reason.

Why it matters. A 2017 Microsoft study at scale showed that switching JavaScript codebases to TypeScript prevented 15% of the bugs that would otherwise have reached production. The type system is a free, exhaustive, line-by-line code reviewer that runs in < 30 seconds — disabling it because "we don't have time" is a strictly worse trade than disabling tests.

How we will roll it out. Turn on strict mode everywhere. Block PRs that introduce new any / cast / ignore without a comment. Track the count of escape hatches in the repo; aim for monotonic decrease quarter-over-quarter.

15

Design Docs & RFCs — written before code

3.5→8.5

What it is. Any non-trivial change starts with a 1–4 page Design Doc (Google's "Design Doc" / Squarespace / GitLab format) or an RFC (Rust, Python, Ember). It states the problem, the proposed solution, considered alternatives, the trade-offs, and the open questions. Reviewers comment on the doc before any code is written.

Why it matters. A code review is a poor place to debate architecture: by the time the reviewer sees the PR, the engineer has invested days or weeks and is emotionally committed. A design doc moves the architecture conversation upstream by an order of magnitude — to the cheapest moment to change direction.

How we will roll it out. Adopt a 1-page template (title, context, decision, alternatives, trade-offs, open questions). Any work estimated at > 5 days requires one. Reviewers leave comments asynchronously over 48–72 hours; the doc author resolves them before coding starts. Final docs are stored in docs/design/ and survive as project memory.

Observability & operability

How the code we wrote yesterday tells us what it is doing in production today. When this group is weak, "is it broken?" requires SSHing into a box, dashboards lag the incident by half an hour, and the on-call engineer learns about each outage from an angry customer in support. 16

Structured Logging — JSON, context, severity

4.5→8.5

What it is. Logs are written as JSON objects, not free-text. Every log line carries the request_id (and trace_id where applicable), the user_id, the service name, and a severity. Tools (structlog, pino, zerolog, slog) make this nearly free at the call site, and Datadog / Grafana Loki / Elastic make it searchable in production.

Why it matters. Free-text logs are unsearchable, ungroupable, and unalertable — you grep them once during an incident and abandon them. Structured logs become a queryable database: "show me all 500s on this endpoint in the last hour from EU users" is a one-line query, not a 20-minute regex hunt.

How we will roll it out. Replace every print / console.log with the structured logger. Enforce a per-service logging contract (required fields, severity ladder). Lint for print in CI. Add a "log review" step to the deploy checklist for new services.

17

Distributed Tracing — OpenTelemetry spans on every call

2.0→7.5

What it is. OpenTelemetry instrumentation in every service emits spans for every inbound and outbound call. A trace_id propagates through HTTP headers, message queues, and gRPC metadata; the result is an end-to-end timeline showing exactly where any individual request spent its time. Datadog APM, Honeycomb, Jaeger, and Tempo all consume the same wire format.

Why it matters. In a microservice or serverless system, a slow request can be slow in any of five services and three databases. Logs and metrics alone make diagnosis a multi-hour scavenger hunt. A trace gives the answer in 30 seconds — the longest span is your problem. The cost is small (1–3% overhead with sampling); the diagnostic speedup is two orders of magnitude.

How we will roll it out. Add the OpenTelemetry SDK to every service. Auto-instrument the HTTP/database/queue clients (free). Add manual spans for non-trivial logic. Tail-sample errors at 100%, normal traffic at 5%. Build a "trace this request" affordance into the internal admin UI.

18

SLOs in code — error budgets that gate releases

1.5→7.5

What it is. Every user-facing service has a written Service-Level Objective (e.g., "99.9% of API responses < 250 ms"). The corresponding Service-Level Indicator is wired up as a Prometheus / Datadog metric, and the remaining error budget is a number the team can see. When the budget burns too fast, releases pause until reliability work pays it back. Originated in Google SRE; standard at Stripe, Slack, Shopify.

Why it matters. Without a budget, "ship features" and "fix reliability" are competing priorities decided by whoever shouts loudest. With a budget, both are deterministic: budget healthy → ship; budget burning → fix reliability first. The team's incentives become aligned with users' lived experience instead of with feature-velocity vanity.

How we will roll it out. Write one SLO per user-facing service. Implement the SLI as a Prometheus query (success-events / valid-events). Set the budget at (1 − SLO) × traffic. Add a multi-window burn-rate alert (Google SRE Workbook). Establish a "release freeze on burn" policy.

19

Error monitoring — every exception has an owner

4.0→8.5

What it is. Every uncaught exception in every environment lands in a centralised tool (Sentry, Datadog Error Tracking, Rollbar, Bugsnag), deduplicated by stack signature, owned by a code-owner team via CODEOWNERS, and tracked through a "regression / new / triaged / fixed" lifecycle. Sentry's public customer list (GitHub, Disney, Microsoft) is essentially the directory of every shop that takes errors seriously.

Why it matters. An error that nobody owns is an error nobody fixes. Without explicit routing, the same null-pointer fires 100,000 times a day in production and nobody touches it because the alert fatigue is too high. Ownership plus deduplication turns errors back into a tractable backlog.

How we will roll it out. Add Sentry (or equivalent) to every service. Map every code path to a CODEOWNERS team. Triage new errors weekly in a 30-minute meeting. Set an SLA: P1 error → 24h, P2 → 1 week. Track the "unowned errors" count and bring it to zero.

20

Performance budgets — measured, not assumed

2.0→7.5

What it is. Each surface has a written performance budget (e.g., Web: LCP < 2.5 s on 4G; API: p99 < 500 ms; bundle size < 150 KB gz). CI measures the relevant numbers on every PR (Lighthouse-CI for the web, k6 for the API, bundlesize). PRs that exceed the budget are blocked until either the regression is fixed or the budget is updated with a written justification.

Why it matters. Without budgets, performance regresses in 1 ms increments per PR and the team notices when the page is suddenly 5 seconds slower — at which point the cumulative debt takes a quarter to repay. Budgets catch regressions at the moment they're introduced, when the offending code is still fresh in the author's head.

How we will roll it out. Pick one surface (the home page or the most-used endpoint). Measure today's number. Add the CI check at today's number + 5%. Tighten the budget once the team is consistently green. Roll out to other surfaces one at a time.

21

Reproducible dev env — devcontainers / Nix / Compose

4.5→8.5

What it is. Cloning the repo and running one command (devcontainer up / nix develop / docker compose up) yields the exact same toolchain, database, and runtime versions on every machine. The dev environment is declared in code (.devcontainer.json, flake.nix, or compose.yml) and version-controlled alongside the application.

Why it matters. "Works on my machine" is the most expensive incantation in software. It absorbs a half-day per new hire, makes every Friday-afternoon debugging session twice as long, and lets subtle version drift between engineers introduce production-only bugs. Reproducible envs make onboarding 30 minutes instead of 3 days and make environment-related production bugs nearly extinct.

How we will roll it out. Adopt the VS Code Dev Containers spec (or Nix). Pin every tool version. Add a CI job that builds the devcontainer image and runs make setup && make test from a clean clone. Publish the "clone-to-first-PR" time and aim for < 60 minutes.

22

Docs-as-Code — docs live next to the code they describe

4.0→8.5

What it is. All documentation lives in the repo as plain text (Markdown, RST, MDX), is reviewed in the same PR as the code change, is built by CI, and is deployed alongside the application. Adopted at scale by Stripe (stripe.com/docs), Twilio, GitLab (the entire handbook), and HashiCorp. Vale, markdownlint, and Lychee link-check enforce style and accuracy.

Why it matters. Docs that live in a separate wiki rot the moment the code changes — and they always change last. Putting docs in the PR makes them part of the change, not an afterthought. Reviewers catch wrong docs at the same time they catch wrong code; the docs and the code stay in lockstep forever, instead of drifting apart over months.

How we will roll it out. Move existing docs into docs/. Add Vale + markdownlint + Lychee to CI. Block PRs that introduce broken links or undocumented public APIs. Adopt a single static site generator (Docusaurus, MkDocs Material, Astro Starlight) and deploy on every merge to main.

23

Tech-Debt Register — explicit, ranked, quarterly

2.5→7.5

What it is. A single tracked list (Linear / Jira project, or a markdown file under docs/tech-debt/) where every known piece of tech debt is recorded with: a one-line description, the cost of carrying it, the cost of fixing it, and the team that owns it. Reviewed at the end of each quarter; the team commits a fixed slice of the next quarter's capacity to paying it down.

Why it matters. Tech debt that is not written down does not exist for planning purposes — and the team keeps adding to it while pretending it isn't there. The DORA research shows teams who explicitly budget debt-repayment outperform teams who don't on both quality and velocity. The act of writing it down forces honesty about the cost of carrying it.

How we will roll it out. Spend one afternoon writing down the top 20 known debts. Score each on impact × frequency. Reserve 15–20% of next quarter's engineering capacity for debt work. Review the register at the end of each quarter and publish the trend in the engineering channel.

24

CODEOWNERS & inner-source — every file has an owner

3.0→7.5

What it is. A CODEOWNERS file (GitHub / GitLab / Gerrit native) maps every path in the repo to a team. The corresponding team is auto-requested as a reviewer on any PR touching its files. Combined with an inner-source contribution guide ("anyone can submit a PR; the owning team approves"), this lets a 1000-engineer organisation share a monorepo without political traffic-jams.

Why it matters. "Nobody owns this code" is how production fires start. Without explicit ownership, refactors stall in review purgatory, security patches go unmerged, and the most-touched files become orphaned. CODEOWNERS makes ownership routable and pageable; inner-source makes it open without making it ungoverned.

How we will roll it out. Generate an initial CODEOWNERS by inspecting recent git history; assign every top-level path to a team. Require CODEOWNERS approval for merge. Publish the inner-source contribution guide. Track "files without owners" in CI and bring it to zero.

25

Continuous Learning — book clubs, brown-bags, conferences

3.5→8.0

What it is. A budgeted, calendared learning practice: one engineering brown-bag per week, one book-club cycle per quarter (everyone reads the same engineering book in 4–6 weeks), one conference per engineer per year, and a personal learning budget for books / courses. Stripe, Shopify, GitLab, and most YC alumni run some version of this.

Why it matters. An engineer at a top-tier startup is shipping in 2026 with what they learned in 2024 — the discipline moves too fast for any other ratio to survive. Teams that don't invest in learning bleed senior engineers (who can find places that do) and accumulate juniors stuck on outdated patterns. Learning is not a perk; it is the highest-leverage retention and quality investment a team can make.

How we will roll it out. Block a 60-minute weekly slot for brown-bags; rotate the speaker. Buy the team three books to start the first book club. Approve one conference per engineer per year (CFP-first preferred). Track participation; it is a top-of-funnel signal for engagement and an early warning for attrition.
