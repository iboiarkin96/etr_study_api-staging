Trunk-based development — short branches, daily merges

Every engineer integrates with main at least once a day; nothing lives in a branch longer than 24 h.

TL;DR

Trunk-based development (TBD), codified at trunkbaseddevelopment.com by Paul Hammant and adopted as a DORA capability, is the practice of committing every change to main at least once a day. Long-lived feature branches forbidden; unfinished work hides behind feature flags (Practice 07). DORA's State of DevOps research shows TBD is among the strongest single predictors of elite delivery — teams deploy multiple times a day with low change-failure rates.

What it is

Trunk-based development is a working agreement around the shared trunk:

  • Every engineer integrates with main daily. A change started Monday morning ships to main by Monday evening at the latest.
  • Short-lived branches (< 24 h). Feature branches exist only for the duration of one PR. Multi-day branches are an antipattern.
  • Unfinished work hides behind feature flags. Code lands in main behind a flag, off; flipped on later via Practice 07.
  • Trunk is always green. CI gates every merge; merge queue catches semantic conflicts (Practice 06).

The mechanism produces continuous integration in the real sense: changes integrate hourly, not at "feature done". Conflicts surface immediately when they're cheap to resolve. The DORA Accelerate research shows TBD is among the top predictors of elite delivery — teams that practice it deploy multiple times a day at low change-failure rates.

Why we need it

Long-lived feature branches accumulate semantic conflicts no CI can catch until merge. A 3-week feature branch hits dozens of incompatible main changes; merge day is multi-hour rebase pain; bugs ship that the original commits didn't have. The team learns to avoid integration, which makes the next merge worse — a feedback loop that ends in "release trains" and quarterly disasters.

TBD inverts the loop. Hourly merges mean conflicts surface at minutes, not weeks. The team learns to integrate continuously because the cost is small. Feature flags hide unfinished work so daily merges don't require a feature to be done. Trunk green is the invariant; everything else is detail.

How others do it

Paul Hammant — trunkbaseddevelopment.com

Hammant codified TBD at trunkbaseddevelopment.com (2017) with the patterns the industry now references: scaled trunk, short-lived feature branches, release branches at cut time, feature flags for WIP. The site is the canonical reference; widely cited in DORA + State of DevOps reports.

What we take: Use the patterns named on the site. Don't invent — Hammant has classified what works at scale (Google, Facebook, Etsy) vs what doesn't (git-flow).

Source: trunkbaseddevelopment.com

DORA Accelerate — TBD as elite predictor

DORA's State of DevOps research finds TBD correlates strongly with elite performance on all four DORA metrics. Low performers either have no CI or run multi-week branches; elites deploy multiple times daily on trunk. The gap on change-failure rate is 5× or more.

What we take: TBD is one of the few practices the research treats as causal, not just correlated. Adopt it; the metrics follow.

Source: dora.dev/research

Google / Facebook — monorepo trunk at scale

Google's google3 monorepo and Facebook's www repo are the largest known TBD examples — tens of thousands of engineers, hundreds of commits per minute, all to one trunk. Achieved via fast hermetic builds, automated testing, and aggressive feature-flag use.

What we take: TBD scales arbitrarily with the right tooling. Build speed + CI speed + feature flags + merge queue are the four pillars.

Source: Fowler — Continuous Integration

GitHub merge queue + Bors

Merge queues (GitHub native, Bors, Mergify) close the "PR passed CI hours ago, main has since moved" gap. Re-run CI against latest main before merging; reject on regression. Critical for TBD at scale: trunk green stays a hard invariant.

What we take: Enable a merge queue. It's the simplest fix for the "green-PR-broken-merge" failure mode.

Source: GitHub merge queue docs

How to run it

  1. Branch protection on main. Required CI checks; admin bypass logged.
  2. Enable merge queue. Re-runs CI against latest main.
  3. Adopt feature flags. Practice 07. Flag every unfinished feature.
  4. Cap branch lifetime. Median < 24 h; CI warns over 48 h.
  5. Cap PR size. < 400 LOC (Practice 03); stack for bigger features.
  6. Trunk-green paging. Practice 06: red main > 15 min pages.
  7. Release branches at cut time only. Hotfix to main; cherry-pick to release branch.
  8. Track DORA + branch-lifetime metrics. Weekly trend in engineering channel.

Deep dive — what makes TBD work

Feature flags are the unlock. Without flags, "daily merge" means "ship every WIP to users". With flags, code lands in main behind flag=off; users see nothing; the team integrates anyway. The flag lifecycle (Practice 07) is the prerequisite for TBD-at-scale.

Branch lifetime is the leading indicator. A team median of < 24 h means TBD is working. 72 h means git-flow is creeping back; 1 week means feature-branch culture has returned. Track + post weekly.

Merge queue closes the semantic-conflict gap. "PR was green 4 h ago" doesn't mean "PR is green now" — main moved. Merge queue re-runs CI against latest main, then merges. Without it, trunk-green breaks despite the gates.

Release branches only at cut time. The TBD pattern says release branches exist briefly for production hotfix flows. Forked from main at cut; hotfixes go to main and cherry-pick down. Long-lived "develop" branches are git-flow and antipattern.

Small PRs are the human pace. Practice 03 (< 400 LOC) makes daily merging viable. Big PRs make every merge a ritual; small PRs make it a non-event. The two practices reinforce each other.

Trunk green is the invariant. Practice 06 covers the on-call discipline. Red main > 15 min pages; revert-first runbook; flake quarantine. Without green-trunk discipline, TBD becomes "merging into a broken main" — the worst of both worlds.

TBD at scale needs fast CI. If CI takes 30 min, 10 daily merges become a queue. Sub-10-min CI (Practice 06) keeps the queue moving; merge queue handles ordering.

Lifecycle of a TBD change

Branched

Short-lived branch from main. Target < 24 h life.

Built

Small PR. Feature flagged if unfinished. CI green.

Reviewed

CODEOWNERS routes; review < 24 h (Practice 03). Small + flagged = fast review.

Queued

Merge queue picks up; re-runs CI against latest main.

Merged

Lands in main. Trunk-green invariant preserved. Deployed automatically.

Flag released

When feature is done, flag flips on per Practice 07 lifecycle.

Quality bar

  • Branch lifetime median < 24 h. Tracked; weekly trend visible.
  • Feature flags for all WIP. Practice 07; no merge without a flag for unfinished work.
  • Merge queue enabled. Re-runs CI against latest main.
  • Branch protection + required checks. No admin bypass without audit.
  • Small PRs. < 400 LOC median (Practice 03).
  • Sub-10-min CI. Practice 06.
  • Release branches at cut time only. Hotfix-to-main + cherry-pick down.
  • DORA scorecard monthly. Deploy freq, lead time, change-failure rate, TTR.
