Manager onboarding — 60-day journey

Your first 60 days as a product manager on Study App, mapped to concrete reads and concrete deliverables. Each step has a time horizon, a short description, the canonical pages to read, and one outcome — the thing you will be able to do when you have finished the step.

Work the steps in order. The first three are compulsory; the rest are strongly encouraged. By the end you will have written and shipped a release brief independently — that is the bar.

The journey · six milestones

  1. 01
    Day 1 · access

    Get into the project

    Clone the monorepo, install the toolchain, get the right accounts (GitLab, Jira, analytics dashboards), and confirm you can reach the staging environment and the product analytics workspace. Nothing exciting — but the next five steps depend on this one working.

    • Local development
    • SDLC RACI matrix
    • Methodology overview

    Outcome. You can open staging, run analytics queries, and find your team in the SDLC RACI.

  2. 02
    Days 2–3 · foundations

    Read the why before the what

    Understand how Study App decides what to build, who owns which call, and where discovery ends and delivery begins. The methodology page is the doctrine; dual-track agile is the cadence; impact mapping is the lens we use to tie an idea to a measurable outcome.

    • Methodology
    • Dual-track agile
    • Impact mapping
    • Decision RACI

    Outcome. You can explain in five minutes how an idea moves from problem statement to a shipped feature, and who owns each decision along the way.

  3. 03
    Week 1 · the way around

    Learn how discovery flows

    Shadow a senior PM through one full discovery cycle and one prioritisation session. Read the radar carefully — Group A (discovery) and Group B (decision-making) are the practices you will live in for the next two months. Open three recent tickets and trace each from problem framing to acceptance criteria.

    • Continuous discovery
    • Definition of ready & done
    • Practices radar (browse Group A · discovery)

    Outcome. Given any open epic, you can name its discovery stage, its current decision owner, and the next gate it needs to clear.

  4. 04
    Week 2 · first ticket

    Write your first discovery brief

    Pick one low-risk problem from the backlog (your buddy will point you at one). Frame it as a one-page discovery brief: who has the problem, what evidence we have, what we'd learn from solving it, and the smallest experiment that would tell us yes or no. Run it past the buddy and refine.

    • HEART framework
    • Customer journey map
    • Funnel & cohort analytics

    Outcome. You have a written, reviewed discovery brief and one falsifiable experiment proposal on a real problem.

  5. 05
    Weeks 3–4 · first deliverable

    Own one full discovery → spec cycle

    Take a real upcoming feature and steer it from discovery to a specced, prioritised, RACI-clear delivery ticket. Run at least one user-facing validation step (A/B, prototype test, or qualitative interview) and write up the result on the ticket.

    • A/B testing
    • RICE prioritisation
    • North star metric

    Outcome. You have shipped one feature spec to dev with clear acceptance criteria, owner RACI, and a baseline metric to measure against.

  6. 06
    Day 60 · sign-off

    Own your first release brief

    You are now the named PM on a release cut. Write the release brief: what shipped, what changed for the user, what the metric impact is expected to be, what the go-to-market plan is. Run the go/no-go meeting. The buddy stays available but does not facilitate. When you are done — that is the end of onboarding.

    • Go-to-market
    • Product brief
    • Stakeholder alignment

    Outcome. You have written and shipped a release brief and run the go/no-go independently. You are now a fully ramped manager on Study App.
