01 Group A · Customer Discovery & Intelligence

Continuous discovery — weekly customer touchpoints

At least one customer interview per week, every week — feeding directly into the next product decision instead of into a research archive.

TL;DR

Continuous discovery (Teresa Torres, 2021) replaces sporadic research phases with a weekly habit: the PM and designer interview at least one customer every week, extract one opportunity, and map it onto an Opportunity Solution Tree. The rhythm creates a direct feedback loop between real customer problems and every product decision the team makes.

What it is

Continuous discovery is the practice of conducting at least one customer interview every week as a non-negotiable team habit. Popularised by Teresa Torres in her 2021 book, it reframes discovery not as a project phase but as an ongoing operational rhythm — as regular as sprint planning or retros.

Each weekly session has three deliverables: a recording with consent, a three-bullet insight summary shared with the team, and at least one opportunity card added to the Opportunity Solution Tree. The OST is the living artefact that connects weekly interviews to active roadmap decisions.

Each session is deliberately narrow — 30 to 45 minutes per interview — but broad over time. After 12 weeks, a team has spoken to 12+ customers and has a pattern-rich OST that no quarterly research sprint could match.

Why we need it

The default mode for most product teams is episodic discovery: research happens at the start of a project, findings inform the roadmap, and nobody talks to a customer again until the next project starts. By then, the market has shifted, the assumptions from the earlier research are stale, and the team is shipping to a customer they no longer understand.

Weekly touchpoints eliminate staleness. They also change the team's relationship to uncertainty — instead of treating unknown customer needs as a problem to be solved in a big research sprint, the team treats them as a stream of information arriving continuously, updated every week.

Teams that practise continuous discovery report faster decisions (evidence is always fresh), lower rework rates (problems caught early), and stronger product-market fit (solutions are tested before full build). The habit itself is the moat — it is very hard to replicate an organisation that has talked to 150 customers in the past three years.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Intercom

Every PM maintains a "customer advisory panel" of 20 recruited customers available for 30-minute calls. PMs book these slots as recurring weekly calendar blocks. Interview summaries are posted in a shared Slack channel within four hours of each session.

What we take: Pre-recruiting a standing panel removes the scheduling friction that kills the weekly habit. The summary-in-Slack norm makes discovery a team sport, not a PM silo.

Source: Intercom Blog — Customer Research

Figma

Product and design pairs share a joint interview calendar. Every quarter, each pair must complete at least 12 recorded interviews. The OST is reviewed in the monthly design critique — not just the product review — so both disciplines own the discovery output.

What we take: Making design a co-owner of the weekly interview closes the gap between research insight and design decision. Joint scheduling doubles capacity without doubling PM workload.

Source: Figma Blog — User Research

Amazon

Amazon's "Customer Obsession" leadership principle is operationalised partly through weekly customer listening sessions embedded in each product team's rhythm. Product reviews open with customer data — recorded calls, verbatim quotes, NPS comments — before any internal metrics are discussed.

What we take: Leading every meeting with the customer voice (not just research readouts) creates a norm where any decision that cannot be linked to a customer insight is visibly suspicious.

Source: Amazon Leadership Principles

Airbnb

During the early growth years, Brian Chesky personally visited hosts every week. This became institutionalised as a quarterly "host immersion" — every PM spends five days doing the job of the most important user (hosting or booking) to generate raw material for discovery sessions.

What we take: Immersive discovery (doing the job) surfaces pain points that interview questions never do. Weekly interviews and periodic immersion complement each other.

Source: HBR — Airbnb Customer Experience

Across companies the pattern is the same: discovery is a habit, not a project. The teams that ship the best products have the most current customer understanding — not the best research methodology.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Standing panel

15–25 pre-recruited customers available for calls. Refreshed monthly as new customers join and inactive ones retire.

Weekly calendar block

Recurring 60-minute slot, blocked before sprint planning. Missing it requires explicit team acknowledgment, not a quiet skip.

Interview guide

5–7 open-ended questions on the customer's experience, not feature reactions. Updated quarterly from the OST's open questions.

Insight summary

Three bullets posted to the team channel within four hours: one new thing learned, one assumption confirmed or rejected, one open question.

Opportunity Solution Tree

Updated weekly with new opportunity nodes. The living bridge between raw interviews and active roadmap items.

Monthly synthesis

90-minute session where the PM clusters the month's insight cards into themes and reprioritises the OST opportunity layer.

Consent & data hygiene

Each session opens with a recording-consent script; PII is stripped from shared notes; raw recordings are retained 12 months max.

How it works in this project

The discovery calendar block is every Thursday 10:00–11:00. The PM and designer rotate the interviewer / note-taker roles weekly. All sessions are recorded in Loom with participant consent. The insight summary is posted in #pm-discovery by 14:00 the same day.

The OST lives in Notion under PM / Discovery / OST. Each opportunity node links back to the interview session(s) that surfaced it. The OST is opened as the first tab in every product review meeting — before metrics, before roadmap.

A short ADR will lock the recruitment policy (panel size, refresh cadence, incentive amount), the consent script, and the data-retention rules so participation is auditable.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Interviews vs surveys — why the weekly habit is qualitative

Surveys answer "how many"; interviews answer "why". Continuous discovery is interview-first because the question that beats every other in a young product is what does the customer struggle with that we don't already know about? — and that is impossible to multiple-choice. A 30-minute open-ended conversation reliably surfaces 2–3 themes that no closed survey would have prompted.

Surveys still matter, but they are a second-stage instrument: once interviews reveal a hypothesis ("users feel lost between lessons"), a survey then quantifies it ("38% of weekly active users").

The story-vs-opinion rule

The single most important interview skill is asking for stories, not opinions. "Would you like X?" gets you politeness; "Tell me about the last time you tried to do Y" gets you a real datum. Past behaviour predicts; hypothetical preferences don't. Train the team to spot the difference and to redirect every "Would you…" into a "Tell me about the last time…".

Saturation — when to stop with one segment

Qualitative research has a well-studied saturation curve: after 5–7 interviews in a single segment, new sessions stop surfacing new themes — they only confirm. The implication is operational: after ~6 interviews in one segment, pivot to a new segment (lapsed users, power users, a different role). Otherwise the team is collecting confirmation, not signal.

Confirmation bias — the silent failure mode

PMs unconsciously steer conversations toward their roadmap and hear what fits. Three countermeasures: (1) the interviewer does not know which features are in next sprint; (2) every session is recorded and a second team member spot-checks one per month; (3) the insight summary must list one thing that contradicted a prior assumption — if it never does, the interviewer is leading.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • PM/designer time. ~4 hours per week (recruiting + session + synthesis + OST update). At small team scale that's a real chunk; it pays back via avoided rework, not via velocity.
  • Participant incentives. Cash or gift cards at $25–$75 per session. A standing panel of 20 customers @ 4 sessions/month is ~$1k/quarter — budget upfront, not after a participant ghost.
  • Privacy posture. Recordings include PII; storage and retention need a legal review. Tools like Loom support per-recording deletion; have a policy before you start.
  • Habit fragility. The practice fails silently — one missed week becomes two, two become a month. Track weeks-since-last-interview as a public team metric.

Lifecycle

recruit panel → book weekly slots → interview + record → 3-bullet summary → OST update → monthly synthesis → refresh panel

The first cycle takes a month to set up — recruit a 15-person panel, pick a guide, hold the first 4 interviews. From there the loop is weekly with a monthly synthesis. The panel refresh closes the long-term loop: every quarter, retire ghosted or saturated participants and recruit new segments.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Skipping weeks "when busy" Discovery feels optional when delivery pressure peaks. Treat the weekly slot as immovable as sprint planning. Track weeks-since-last-interview as a public metric — when it goes above 1, the team feels it.
Interviewing only happy users Scheduling convenience pulls toward the most responsive customers. Intentionally recruit churned and inactive users. Their answers are the most valuable — they explain why people leave, which active users can't.
Asking about features PMs want to validate the roadmap, not discover problems. Never mention the roadmap. Ask about recent behaviour, frustrations, and workarounds. "Tell me about the last time you…" beats "Would you use…" every time.
Hoarding insights in PM notes Synthesis feels like extra work after a long interview day. Mandate the three-bullet Slack post within four hours of the session. Shared notes are a team resource; private notes are an archive.
Interviewing the same segment forever The standing panel is convenient; nobody refreshes it. After 6 interviews in one segment, the team has reached saturation. Pivot to lapsed users, a different persona, or a different geography. Refresh the panel each quarter.

Worked example

Insight summary

2026-W18 · interview with Maya (lapsed user, week-3 dropout)

Interviewee:  Maya, 27, marketing manager, signed up 6 weeks ago, last active week 3
Segment:      lapsed user · single-course start · mobile-first
Recording:    loom.com/share/REDACTED (with consent)

🟢 New thing learned
   Lessons feel like a "checklist" once she finished the first 3 — she lost the
   sense of why she was learning. The "next lesson" CTA reinforced this; she
   wanted a "what am I building toward" view, not a "what's next" view.

🟡 Assumption confirmed / rejected
   REJECTED: we assumed lapsed users dropped because of difficulty. Maya rated
   the content as "easy enough"; the problem was motivation, not capability.

🔵 Open question
   How many lapsed users would re-engage if we showed a personal "skill map"
   instead of a next-lesson queue? — needs a survey across 200 lapsed users.

OST opportunity node added:
   "Lapsed users lose the why → introduce a goal-and-skill map on dashboard"
   parent: 'Retention of new users (weeks 1–4)'

Outcome. The insight surfaced a goal-vs-task framing problem that the team had not modelled. Two weeks later, a follow-up survey of 200 lapsed users confirmed the pattern (38% cited "didn't see the point" as the top reason). The team launched an A/B test on a goal-map dashboard variant — the kind of decision that would have taken a full quarterly research sprint without the weekly habit.
