Product vision & narrative — the north star

A written product vision that serves as the litmus test for every major roadmap decision — and the founding document a new hire reads on day one.

TL;DR

A product vision is 1–2 paragraphs describing the world 3–5 years out: who benefits, what changes for them, what unique role the product plays. Written, not presented; durable across reorgs and pivots. Amazon's Working Backwards (PR/FAQ) is the most rigorous method: if you can't write a coherent press release dated 3 years from now, your vision isn't clear enough to make roadmap decisions from.

What it is

A product vision is a short written statement (1–2 paragraphs) describing the future the product is working toward, who benefits, and what changes in their lives as a result. Plain language, readable by any stakeholder, durable enough to survive a reorg or a market pivot.

The most rigorous method is Amazon's Working Backwards (PR/FAQ): the PM writes a mock press release dated 3–5 years out, announcing the product's impact. If the PM can't write a coherent press release, the vision isn't clear enough to make roadmap decisions from.

A vision is not a mission statement ("we empower teams to collaborate") or a values list. It describes a specific future state: "In 2029, every engineering team at a mid-market SaaS company can trace every product decision back to a customer outcome, in real time, without a data analyst."

Why we need it

Without a written vision, roadmap debates are power contests. With a clear vision, every debate has a resolution mechanism: "does this bring us closer to the vision?" — a question that can be answered. "Should we build this?" — can't be answered without knowing what the product is trying to become.

The vision also doubles as a recruiting tool, an investor-alignment tool, and a new-hire orientation tool — without requiring the PM. A new engineer reads it on day one and understands trajectory. A candidate reads it before the interview and self-selects.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Amazon — Working Backwards (PR/FAQ)

Every significant initiative starts with a one-page mock press release describing the finished product as if it has shipped. Comprehensible to a non-technical customer. No slides, only prose. Leadership reviews the release, not a deck.

What we take: Prose clarity is harder to fake than slide clarity. If you can't write a coherent press release about what you're building, the idea isn't ready to build. Writing before building is the discipline.

Source: Amazon — Working Backwards

Netflix — uncomfortable vision

Netflix's 2007 streaming vision — "to become the world's best Internet TV network" — was set when DVD-by-mail was still the main business. That single sentence guided the next decade of decisions: original content, international expansion, recommendation algorithms.

What we take: A good vision is uncomfortable — it describes a future that doesn't exist yet and requires the company to fundamentally change to reach it. If it describes the present, it isn't a vision.

Source: SVPG — Inspired (vision excerpt)

Slack — "We Don't Sell Saddles Here"

Stewart Butterfield's pre-launch memo described Slack's vision in 1,500 words — "making your working life simpler, more pleasant, and more productive". Narrow enough to be actionable, broad enough to not constrain the feature set. Became the founding document for every product decision in the early years.

What we take: A vision memo is the PM's founding document. Writing it is an act of leadership, not just planning. Read by every person who joins the team.

Source: Stewart Butterfield — We Don't Sell Saddles Here

Stripe — "increase the GDP of the internet"

Stripe's vision is a single sentence: "increase the GDP of the internet". Far enough to inspire, abstract enough to not lock the product, specific enough that competitive moves can be tested against it (does this raise online-commerce volume?).

What we take: A vision can be a sentence if the sentence does real work — provides a clear test for major decisions. Length doesn't make a vision; force does.

Source: Stripe — About

Across teams: written, uncomfortable (describes a future, not the present), testable (can decide whether a decision moves toward or away from it), durable (years, not quarters). Length varies; force is constant.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Time horizon

3–5 years out. Ambitious enough to inspire, close enough to feel real.

Beneficiary

Specific customer segment, plain language. Not "users" — "mid-market SaaS engineering teams".

Change

What's different in their life/work because the product exists? The more concrete, the better.

Unique angle

Why this product, not a competitor? Vision must be defensible, not generic.

Written, not presented

Prose document, not a slide. Writing forces precision slides can hide.

PR / FAQ companion

Amazon-style press release + FAQ. The FAQ surfaces objections proactively.

Annual review

Reviewed once a year. Update only if market or evidence has shifted enough to invalidate the original.

How it works in this project

Study App's vision lives in docs/vision.md — version-controlled, public to the org, 1–2 paragraphs of prose. It is paired with a PR/FAQ at docs/vision-pr-faq.md dated 3 years out. Every product brief (BR-*) includes a "vision alignment" line — one sentence on how the brief moves toward the vision.

The vision is reviewed once a year (Q4 retro) — a written reflection: is the future we described still plausible? Has market evidence shifted enough to invalidate it? Mid-year updates require explicit leadership decision (not a Slack message).

A short ADR locks the writing process (Working Backwards), the annual cadence, and the rule that the vision can only be changed by leadership decision.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Working Backwards — why prose beats slides

Slides reward design and verbal performance; prose rewards clarity of thought. A press release dated 3 years from now forces the PM to answer: who is this for, what changes for them, what makes us the right team to build it? Vague answers produce a press release that sounds like every other tech announcement; sharp answers produce something specific enough to be wrong. The discipline is the act of writing the document: most teams fail Working Backwards on the first three drafts and learn something each time. That learning is the practice.

The vision-strategy-OKR-roadmap cascade

Vision is the only level that doesn't change quarter-to-quarter. Below it: strategy (2–3 year choices and bets — which markets, what differentiation, what we won't do); OKRs (quarterly outcomes that ladder to strategy); roadmap (what we build this sprint cycle to hit the KRs). Each level derives from the one above. When the cascade is clean, a sprint task can be traced 4 levels up to the vision; when it's broken, the team builds features that satisfy no level above the roadmap.

Vision vs mission vs values — distinctions that matter

ArtefactQuestion it answersExample
VisionWhere are we going? (specific future state)"In 2029, every learner has a daily habit they actually want."
MissionWhat are we doing? (current purpose)"Help adults build self-directed learning habits."
ValuesHow do we behave? (principles)"Customer-evidence over opinion; outcomes over outputs."

Confusing them is common. A "vision" that's actually a values list ("we empower teams to thrive") provides no decision support — you can't ask "does this bring us closer to thriving?". A specific future state lets you ask that question and get an answer.

The "uncomfortable" test

A good vision should make someone outside the target market slightly uncomfortable — "that's too specific; that won't work for me". That specificity is the value. A vision broad enough to please everyone provides no constraint. Test: read the vision to someone in an adjacent market; if they nod politely, it's too broad. If they push back ("I don't see myself in this"), it's the right level of specific.

Vision shelf life and refresh discipline

Visions are designed to last 3–5 years; reality varies. Refresh triggers: (a) market structure changed (new regulation, technology shift, new competitor category), (b) primary customer segment proved wrong, (c) the vision is achieved. If none of those apply, refresh is just discomfort with the current vision — push back; vision drift kills the cascade. Annual reviews answer "is the trigger present?"; mid-year reviews require leadership decision.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Writing time. A real vision draft is 8–20 hours over multiple iterations. Cheap relative to the years of decisions it shapes, but the cost is felt immediately and the benefit is felt later.
  • Politics. A specific vision excludes adjacent opportunities ("we won't serve that market"). Stakeholders attached to the excluded opportunities push back.
  • Misuse risk. Vague visions get used to justify anything; specific visions get used to block things some leaders want. Neither is comfortable; specific is more useful.
  • Inertia. A vision that turned out wrong is expensive to admit and replace. Annual review is the relief valve; without it, an outdated vision quietly accumulates compounding misalignment.

Lifecycle

draft PR/FAQ → internal review (3 rounds) → distill to 1–2 paragraphs → publish to org → brief-template alignment line → annual review → refresh or confirm

First cycle: 4–6 weeks. PR/FAQ drafts iterate through 3 internal reviews; the final 1–2 paragraph vision is distilled from the converged PR/FAQ. Steady state: published once, referenced from every brief, reviewed annually. Refresh is triggered by market/customer change, not by team mood.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Vision is a values list "We believe in transparency" is safe. Describe a specific future state: "In 2029, X happens because our product exists". Values belong in a separate values doc.
Too broad "Transform how people work" could describe any B2B SaaS. Vision should feel slightly wrong to someone outside the target market. That specificity is the point.
Never updated Set in year one and forgotten. Annual review with written reflection. If market shifted enough, update with the same rigour as the original; otherwise confirm.
Slides, not prose Easier to present than to write. Working Backwards mandate: prose only. Slides hide vagueness; prose forces precision.
Updated mid-year on impulse Team gets excited about a new framing. Vision changes are leadership decisions, not Slack messages. The cascade breaks if vision shifts every quarter.
No alignment in briefs Vision lives in a doc; briefs are written without referencing it. Brief template requires a "vision alignment" line: how does this brief move toward the vision? Forces every brief to defend itself against the cascade.

Worked example

Vision · prose

Study App vision (3-year horizon, 2029)

# Study App — Vision 2029
last reviewed: 2026-05-24 (annual)
owner: @ivan (PM)

## The future we're building
By 2029, a self-motivated adult learner who wants to pick up a new
skill — a language, a craft, a technical capability — can build a
daily 15-minute habit that visibly compounds, without needing a
teacher, a school, or a payment plan they regret.

The product is the bridge between "I'd like to learn X" and "I have
been learning X for 90 days and it's working". Today that bridge is
either too thin (free apps that abandon you in week 2) or too heavy
(structured courses that demand 5 hours a week). Study App makes
the bridge daily, light, and self-reinforcing.

Specifically: in 2029, a Study App learner who has used the product
for 30 days can show measurable progress on the goal they set on
day 1 — and tell the story of why it stuck.

## What we won't be
We won't be a course marketplace. We won't be a tutor matchmaker.
We won't be a children's platform. We won't be free at scale.
These exclusions are not "not yet" — they are "not us".

## What makes us the right team
Our edge is in the daily-habit machinery: streak design, lesson
shaping, adaptive difficulty, and the moment-of-frustration
interventions that decide whether a learner returns tomorrow.
Most learning products optimise the content; we optimise the
return.

## PR/FAQ
See docs/vision-pr-faq.md — mock press release dated 2029-09-01.

Outcome. The vision provides a clear decision filter for every brief. Three recent examples: a proposal to add a children's mode was rejected against the "we won't be a children's platform" exclusion; a proposal to add tutor matching was rejected against "won't be a matchmaker"; a proposal to invest in a richer streak-freeze mechanic was approved against "our edge is daily-habit machinery". Without the vision, all three would have been judged on their individual merits and likely all three would have been approved over time — leading to a generic, defocused product.
