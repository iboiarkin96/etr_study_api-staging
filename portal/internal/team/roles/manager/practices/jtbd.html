Jobs-to-be-done — outcome-driven requirements

Customers "hire" products to make progress in specific situations — understand the job, not the persona.

TL;DR

JTBD (Christensen, Ulwick) is a theory of customer motivation: people hire products to make progress in a situation. The unit of analysis is the job — stable, situation-dependent — not demographic persona. Job statements have three parts: When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]. The primary research method is the switch interview: a structured timeline of what triggered the change, what was hired, and (often more useful) what got fired.

What it is

Jobs-to-be-done shifts the unit of analysis from who the customer is to what progress they are trying to make. Developed by Clayton Christensen and Tony Ulwick, the framework treats products as things customers "hire" to perform a job, and notes that jobs persist for decades while the products hired against them change radically (people have been hiring something to "send a message" since the Pony Express).

A job statement: When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]. The situation defines the trigger and context; the motivation captures the progress; the outcome describes the success state. The job is the right unit because two demographically opposite customers can share a job, and one customer can have multiple jobs depending on the situation.

The primary research method is the switch interview: a structured conversation with someone who recently changed solutions, exploring the timeline (first thought → passive looking → active looking → decision → first use → ongoing use) plus the four forces (push from the old solution, pull of the new, anxiety about the new, habit of the old).

Why we need it

Persona-based requirements describe who the customer is. JTBD describes what they're trying to accomplish. A 35-year-old marketer and a 55-year-old retired teacher can share a job ("when I book accommodation in an unfamiliar city, I want to feel confident I'm not being scammed, so I can enjoy the trip without anxiety"). Persona segmentation puts them in different buckets; JTBD targets them together.

JTBD also reveals the real competition. Slack's competitors aren't just messaging apps — they're email, hallway conversations, project tools, and Post-its. Understanding what users actually hire for a job exposes the real market and the real switching cost.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Intercom

Intercom publicly credited JTBD as the framework behind their core product decisions in the early years. Switch interviews with customers who moved from email to Intercom revealed exactly what job email failed to do. Their positioning shifted from "live chat" to "customer communication" based on the insights.

What we take: JTBD reframes competition. Run switch interviews not just when you win customers but when you lose them — lost-sale interviews are more informative than won-sale interviews.

Source: Intercom Blog — JTBD

Bob Moesta — milkshake study

The canonical JTBD story: a fast-food chain wanted to grow milkshake sales. Switch interviews revealed the morning milkshake was hired for "make my commute less boring, give me something to do with one hand, fill me up till lunch" — competing with bananas, doughnuts, and bagels. The afternoon milkshake was hired for an entirely different job ("appease my kid"). Two jobs → two product variations → measurable lift.

What we take: The same product can be hired for multiple jobs depending on time, place, mood. Surface jobs separately; don't average across them.

Source: Christensen Institute — JTBD

Apple (iPod + iTunes)

The iPod won not because it was a better MP3 player but because Apple identified the job precisely: "when I want to listen to music on the go, I want my entire library with me, so I can play whatever I feel like right now". No existing product served that. iTunes served the adjacent job (manage that library frictionlessly).

What we take: Underserved jobs beat underserved segments. A job with no good product hired against it is the rarest and most valuable kind of opportunity.

Source: HBR — Know your customers' jobs to be done

Basecamp — JTBD as filter

Basecamp avoids feature bloat by asking whether each candidate feature serves a job customers actually hire for, or only a job they say they want but never act on. The result is a product notably simpler than competitors, by design.

What we take: JTBD is a roadmap filter, not just a discovery tool. Before building any feature: what job does this serve? Have we observed customers hiring something else for this job today?

Source: Basecamp — Rework

JTBD is most powerful not as a research framework but as a decision filter. Every roadmap item should answer: what job does this serve, what are customers hiring today for that job, and how do we know? Without those answers, the item is faith-based.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Job statement

When [situation] · I want to [motivation] · so I can [outcome]. All three parts; situation drives precision.

Switch interview corpus

≥ 5 interviews per job, with both hires and fires. Lost-customer interviews are mandatory, not optional.

Four forces map

Push (away from old) · Pull (toward new) · Anxiety (about new) · Habit (of old). Identify each per job; products that fail usually under-address Anxiety + Habit.

Real competition list

Every alternative customers hire — including non-product solutions ("doing nothing", spreadsheets, hallway). Not just direct competitors.

Functional + emotional + social layers

Each job has all three. Pure functional analysis misses why people switch ("status with peers", "anxiety reduction").

Roadmap traceability

Every roadmap item traces to a documented job. Items with no job are flagged at planning, not built.

How it works in this project

Study App documents the top 3–5 jobs in docs/discovery/jobs.md, each with statement, the four forces, the real-competition list, and the switch-interview IDs that produced it. The corpus is refreshed quarterly: ≥ 5 switch interviews per job, mixing recent sign-ups (hire interviews) and recent churners (fire interviews — typically the more revealing of the two).

JTBD plugs into the discovery flow: opportunities surfaced in continuous-discovery interviews map onto job statements; the OST uses jobs as the parent layer; every product brief declares which job the feature serves before listing requirements.

A short ADR will lock the job-statement format, the switch-interview script, and the rule that no feature ships without a named parent job.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Switch interviews — the actual structure

A switch interview is not a free-form conversation about the product. It is a structured timeline reconstruction. Six anchors, in order:

  1. First thought — when did you first think about changing? What was happening that day?
  2. Passive looking — what did you do with that thought initially (nothing, told a friend, googled once)?
  3. Active looking — when did it become serious? What options were on the list?
  4. Decision moment — what tipped you? Who else was involved (spouse, manager, peer)?
  5. First use — what happened the first time? Did it match expectation?
  6. Ongoing / fire — are you still using it? If not, when did it stop?

The art is timeline anchoring: don't ask "why did you choose X?" (rationalised). Ask "what was happening the day you first thought about changing?" (specific, recoverable memory). The recoverable memory is the data; the rationalisation is the noise.

The four forces — the model of change

Change happens when two forces (push + pull) overcome two opposing forces (anxiety + habit). The math:

  • Push — frustration with the current solution. ("My spreadsheet broke at 50k rows.")
  • Pull — attraction of the new solution. ("This tool can handle millions of rows.")
  • Anxiety — fear about the new. ("What if my team can't learn it? What if it loses data?")
  • Habit — comfort with the old. ("I've used Excel for 15 years.")

Most product teams over-invest in Pull (more features!) and under-invest in Anxiety reduction (migration tools, social proof, free trials). Switch interviews reveal the actual balance per job; the leverage is usually on the anxiety/habit side.

Functional + emotional + social layers

Every job has three layers. Functional: the task to be done ("get from A to B"). Emotional: how the customer wants to feel ("safe", "confident", "not foolish"). Social: how the customer wants to be perceived ("competent", "modern", "responsible"). A purely functional analysis misses why an Uber feels different from a taxi at the same price.

Jobs vs tasks — keeping the altitude right

A job is high-level: "I want to feel confident I'm not being ripped off when I book accommodation." A task is low-level: "I want to filter listings by price." Tasks are how a job gets done; they're not jobs themselves. The test: a job is solution-agnostic; you can describe it without naming any product. If your job statement names a feature, it's a task wearing a JTBD costume.

JTBD vs personas — they're not in conflict

Personas describe the customer; JTBD describes the goal. Mature teams use both: persona for empathy and qualitative research recruitment; job for scoping and roadmap filter. The mistake is letting one substitute for the other. Persona without job → features without purpose; job without persona → research without recruits.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Interview cost. Switch interviews are 45–60 min each and require recent switchers. Recruiting churners is harder than recruiting customers; budget extra incentive.
  • Skill cost. Timeline interviewing is a learned skill. The first 3–5 interviews from a new interviewer will be biased toward rationalisations; pair with an experienced interviewer for the first batch.
  • Cultural friction. Teams attached to personas can react to JTBD as "yet another framework". Pair the first JTBD adoption with a concrete win (one switch interview that changed a roadmap decision) — abstract advocacy doesn't move the team.
  • Job inflation. Teams generate 20+ jobs and lose focus. Cap at 5 primary jobs per product; everything else is a sub-job or a task.

Lifecycle

recruit recent switchers → switch interviews (≥5) → timeline + 4-forces synthesis → draft job statements → map real competition → use as roadmap filter → quarterly refresh

The first cycle takes 3–4 weeks: recruit + interview + synthesis. Subsequent quarters refresh: 5 new interviews per primary job, four-forces re-balanced if anxiety/habit shifted, competition list updated. Roadmap filter runs continuously: each new candidate feature gets reviewed against the job list before entering the discovery backlog.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Feature-level "jobs" "When I want to export a report…" — that's a task. Jobs are high-level progress: "When I need to present results to leadership and want them to believe me…". Solution-agnostic.
JTBD = user stories Syntax looks similar. User stories describe what to build; JTBD describes why it's needed. Write JTBD first, derive user stories from it. Never the reverse.
Surveys instead of switch interviews Surveys are faster. Surveys capture stated preferences; switch interviews capture actual behaviour. Run ≥ 5 switch interviews before any major decision; surveys can come after to size.
Only interviewing won customers Churned customers are harder to recruit. Fire interviews (why they left) are usually more revealing than hire interviews (why they came). Budget extra incentive and reach out within 30 days of churn.
Skipping anxiety + habit Teams focus on Push and Pull (Pull = feature build). Anxiety reduction (migration tools, free trial, social proof) and habit-breaking (workflow integration, defaults) are usually higher-leverage than more features.
Job inflation (20+ jobs) Every PM adds their pet job. Cap at 5 primary jobs per product; sub-jobs and tasks live one level down. Maps with 20 jobs serve nobody; they're brainstorm artefacts.

Worked example

Job + switch interview synthesis

Study App — Job 1: "Build a daily learning habit that visibly compounds"

JOB STATEMENT
  When I have a self-set learning goal (new language, technical skill,
  hobby) and 15–30 min/day of attention,
  I want to build a daily habit with visible progress,
  so I can see myself getting better at something I chose for myself.

REAL COMPETITION (what users hire today)
  ✓ YouTube (free, no commitment, no progress tracking)
  ✓ Books / e-books (own pace, no streak pressure)
  ✓ Duolingo (similar habit mechanic, language-specific)
  ✓ Doing nothing — "I'll start Monday"

FOUR FORCES (synthesised from 7 switch interviews · INT-04, 07, 09, 12, 14, 17, 22)

  PUSH (away from old):
    • "YouTube has no structure; I'd watch 6 hrs and remember nothing." (INT-07)
    • "Books on the shelf judging me." (INT-12)
    • "Duolingo only does languages; I want to learn UI design too." (INT-22)

  PULL (toward Study App):
    • Daily structure with streak
    • Multi-domain (not language-only)
    • Visible progress map

  ANXIETY (about the new):
    • "Another app I'll abandon in week 2." (4 of 7 interviews)
    • "I'll lose money on the subscription if I don't use it." (3 of 7)
    • "The lessons might be too easy or too hard." (5 of 7)

  HABIT (of the old):
    • "I always open YouTube on the commute — it's automatic."
    • "I'd have to remember to open this app."

LAYERS
  Functional:  build a daily habit; track progress; learn something useful
  Emotional:   feel competent; reduce guilt about wasted time; sense of momentum
  Social:      share streak with friends; "I'm learning Italian" as identity

ROADMAP IMPLICATIONS (filtered through this job)
  ✓ Streak mechanic                 — addresses PULL + emotional momentum
  ✓ Streak freeze                   — addresses ANXIETY (abandonment fear)
  ✓ Level-placement quiz            — addresses ANXIETY (too easy / too hard)
  ✓ Cross-domain catalogue          — addresses PUSH (Duolingo limitation)
  ✓ Free trial (no card) for week 1 — addresses ANXIETY (subscription fear)
  ✗ Social leaderboards             — wrong layer (some users want status, most don't)
  ✗ Admin dashboard for parents     — wrong job (parental control, not self-learning)

Outcome. Before this job synthesis the team was building feature parity with Duolingo (PULL-side optimisation). The switch interviews surfaced that anxiety, not pull, was the binding constraint — 5/7 interviews mentioned "another app I'll abandon". The roadmap pivoted: streak-freeze, level placement, and no-card free trial all addressed anxiety; activation lifted 18% → 31% within two quarters. The wins came from interventions the persona-based roadmap had deprioritised because they didn't add new capabilities.
