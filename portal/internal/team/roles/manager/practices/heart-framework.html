HEART framework — UX metrics that matter

Google's five-dimension framework for selecting UX metrics that reflect real user experience — not just activity signals that look good on a dashboard.

TL;DR

HEART (Google, Kerry Rodden, 2010) is a UX-metrics framework with five dimensions: Happiness, Engagement, Adoption, Retention, Task success. For each selected dimension the team runs a Goals → Signals → Metrics (GSM) drill-down. The framework stops teams measuring what's easy (page views, signups) and forces deliberate choice of which UX dimensions actually matter per feature. Select 2–4 per focus area, not all five.

What it is

HEART was developed at Google by Kerry Rodden, Hilary Hutchinson, and Xin Fu and published in 2010. It defines five dimensions of UX quality:

  • Happiness — attitudinal: how satisfied users are. Measured by CSAT, NPS, CES (Customer Effort Score). The only dimension that requires primary research (surveys), not log data.
  • Engagement — behavioural depth and frequency. Sessions per week, actions per session, content consumed.
  • Adoption — new-user uptake and feature discovery. New users per period, % of users trying a new feature, time-to-first-key-action.
  • Retention — whether users come back. 30/60/90-day curves, churn rate, reactivation rate. The most direct leading indicator of LTV.
  • Task success — efficiency and error rate on core tasks. Completion rate, time-on-task, error count. Usability lens.

For each selected dimension the team runs the Goals → Signals → Metrics (GSM) process. The goal names a user outcome ("users find a relevant lesson in < 30 s"); the signal names an observable behaviour ("user finds a lesson via search, not scroll"); the metric quantifies it ("% of lesson opens originating from a search query"). Each step is a distinct artefact; collapsing them is the root of most measurement failures.

Why we need it

Without a framework, teams default to measuring what's easiest: page views, signups, click-through rates. These aren't bad — they're incomplete. A feature can lift page views while simultaneously increasing user frustration: Engagement up, Happiness down, and the dashboard celebrates. HEART forces deliberate dimension selection so the team measures what matters, not what's lying around.

The GSM step prevents the most common metric error: writing a "metric" that's actually a goal. "User satisfaction" is a goal; "average CSAT after checkout" is a metric. The three-step translation eliminates ambiguity about what is being measured and why.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Google

Google uses HEART across Docs, Gmail, Maps, and more. For Docs the primary dimensions are E, R, T — docs edited per user per week (E), weekly retention (R), and docs successfully shared / co-edited (T). Happiness is measured quarterly via in-product survey; Adoption tracks new-feature uptake (comments, suggestions mode) per release.

What we take: Not all five dimensions are equally important for every product. Docs focuses on E/R/T — the dimensions most predictive of collaboration value. Selecting 2–4 dimensions per focus makes HEART tractable; using all five at once is analytically unmanageable.

Source: Google Research — HEART paper

Spotify

Spotify squads select the two HEART dimensions most relevant to their mission and define GSM for each. Discovery squads focus on Adoption (discovery of new music) and Happiness (satisfaction with recommendations). Listening squads focus on Engagement (minutes per session) and Retention (weekly return). Two squads in the same product legitimately measure different dimensions.

What we take: Different squads can target different HEART dimensions — what matters is that the selection is deliberate and connected to the squad's mission, not that every squad uses the same dimensions.

Source: Spotify Engineering Blog

Duolingo — happiness as a daily signal

Duolingo embeds lightweight happiness signals into the product itself: after every lesson, an optional 1-tap "how was that?" prompt. Aggregated, it produces a per-feature happiness signal updated daily — not annually. The lift over traditional NPS is granularity: when retention dips, Duolingo can trace which lessons' happiness dropped first.

What we take: Happiness doesn't have to be a heavy quarterly survey. A 1-tap micro-survey per session generates orders of magnitude more data and connects directly to the feature that produced it.

Source: Duolingo Engineering Blog

Atlassian — counter-metrics

Atlassian pairs every HEART metric with a counter-metric: optimising Engagement (more sessions) must not regress Task success (completion rate); optimising Adoption must not regress Happiness. The counter-metric is named explicitly and tracked on the same dashboard.

What we take: Counter-metrics aren't a "nice to have" — they're the firewall against local optimisation. A team optimising one HEART dimension without a named counter-metric will silently regress the others.

Source: Atlassian — Product metrics

The pattern across teams: select fewer than five dimensions per focus, run GSM rigorously per selected dimension, pair every primary metric with a named counter-metric, and prefer many small happiness signals over one big annual survey. None of this is HEART per se — it's the operational discipline that makes HEART actually useful.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Dimension selection

2–4 HEART dimensions chosen for the current focus. The not-selected dimensions are written down with the reason for exclusion — to prevent silent re-introduction later.

Goal per dimension

User-outcome statement, not a metric. "Users find a relevant lesson in < 30 s." Forces clarity about what success looks like.

Signal per goal

Observable user behaviour that indicates progress. "User opens a lesson via search rather than scroll." Not yet quantified — but specific enough that the team agrees on it.

Metric per signal

Quantified, computed from available data: "% of lesson opens originating from a search query, per user per week."

Counter-metric per metric

What must not regress when this metric is optimised. Pairs every primary metric with its firewall.

Lightweight happiness mechanism

In-product 1-tap or 1-click prompt per session/task. Annual surveys are not enough — granularity is the value.

Quarterly review

Dimensions, GSM, and counter-metrics re-examined each quarter. Stale GSM produces stale metrics.

How it works in this project

Study App v1 selects three HEART dimensions: Engagement, Retention, and Task success. Happiness is deferred — at v1 user volume, NPS / CSAT samples won't reach statistical significance and would mislead more than inform; instead a 1-tap "was this lesson useful?" prompt collects per-lesson happiness from week 4 onward as a lightweight pilot. Adoption is owned by the growth track separately (acquisition funnels in funnel & cohort analytics) and not duplicated in the product-team HEART set.

GSM artefacts live as a single markdown file in docs/metrics/heart-gsm.md, version-controlled. Each selected dimension has its Goal, two Signals, two Metrics, and one Counter-metric written out. The PM reviews the file at every quarterly retro; changes go through PR. The metrics are wired into the same warehouse pipeline as funnel/cohort analyses, so HEART and product-health dashboards read from the same canonical event schema.

A short ADR will lock the dimension-selection criteria (re-examined at quarterly retro, not in-quarter) and the rule that no HEART metric ships without a named counter-metric.

Deep dive — the theory you need

GSM — why three steps, not two

The temptation is to skip the Signal step: jump from Goal ("users find lessons quickly") straight to Metric ("avg time from open to first lesson"). The Signal step exists because Goal and Metric are at different abstraction levels, and the Signal is where the team disagrees in a productive way. "Users find lessons quickly" might mean: (a) users search instead of scroll, (b) users open the first result they see, (c) users return to the same lesson the next day. Each produces a different Metric. Writing the Signal explicitly forces the team to argue at the level of user behaviour, not at the level of SQL.

Happiness — CSAT vs NPS vs CES

Three different instruments measure three different things. CSAT (1–5 satisfaction) is task-specific and granular — good per-feature. NPS (0–10 recommend) is brand-level and noisy below ~500 responses; useless for small products. CES (Customer Effort Score) measures friction specifically ("how easy was that?") — best for support and onboarding flows. Picking the wrong instrument is the most common Happiness mistake; NPS at v1 scale will give a number that means nothing.

InstrumentAsksBest forMin sample for signal
CSAT"How satisfied?" 1–5Per-feature, post-task~100 responses per feature
NPS"Recommend?" 0–10Brand level, quarterly~500 responses
CES"How easy?" 1–7Onboarding, support flows~100 responses per flow

Counter-metrics — the firewall against local optimisation

Every optimisation has a shadow. Optimise Engagement (more sessions) → may regress Task success (rushed users, error rate up). Optimise Adoption (more new users) → may regress Happiness (poorly qualified users, low satisfaction). The counter-metric is the named "must not regress" alongside each primary metric. Without it, every win is suspect; with it, every win is honest.

HEART vs AARRR (Pirate metrics) — when each applies

HEART is a UX-quality framework; AARRR (Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Referral, Revenue) is a growth-funnel framework. They aren't competitors — they answer different questions. AARRR maps to the user lifecycle; HEART maps to the quality of experience inside it. A serious product needs both: AARRR to see where users are in the funnel, HEART to see whether the experience at each stage is any good.

Why "not all five"

The mathematical reason: five dimensions × 2 metrics × 1 counter-metric × 1 dashboard = 15 things to monitor. Teams that try this either (a) ignore most of the dashboard or (b) have no clear north-star. The practical guidance from Google's own paper: "Most teams will select 2–4 of the five." A team that selects all five is signalling indecision, not rigour.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Instrumentation cost. Every new metric requires events shipped, joins maintained, dashboards built. Plan a per-quarter instrumentation budget; without it, GSM produces aspirational metrics nobody computes.
  • Happiness is hard at small scale. Below ~500 monthly active users, NPS produces noise. Lightweight in-product CSAT (1-tap, per-task) is the practical alternative — but it still needs design and engineering work.
  • Quarterly cadence vs metric stability. If GSM changes every quarter, longitudinal comparisons break. Lock metrics for at least 2 quarters at a time; only re-baseline with explicit team decision.
  • The "vanity metric" temptation. Page views are easy and big; HEART metrics are smaller and harder. Leadership often prefers the big number. Defending HEART requires showing that page views moved while a HEART dimension regressed — and that costs political capital.

Lifecycle

pick 2–4 dimensions → Goals (user outcomes) → Signals (behaviours) → Metrics (SQL-able) → counter-metrics → instrument + dashboard → quarterly review

The first cycle takes 1–2 weeks: dimension selection in a 2-hour workshop, GSM writeup over the following week, counter-metrics paired in review. Instrumentation is a 1–3 sprint engineering item depending on what events already exist. After that, the steady state is quarterly: dimensions reviewed (rarely changed), GSM revised (sometimes), counter-metrics expanded (often, as new optimisation pressures surface). Metrics themselves are locked for ≥2 quarters to keep longitudinal comparisons honest.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Measuring all five dimensions equally Team doesn't want to exclude any dimension. Select 2–4 per product focus. Write down what was excluded and why. All five at equal weight is indecision, not rigour.
Goals written as metrics Jumping from intuition straight to SQL. Enforce the three-step GSM sequence. If you wrote a Metric first, reverse-engineer: "what user behaviour does this metric reflect?" → that's your Signal; "why does the user behave that way?" → your Goal.
NPS at v1 scale NPS is famous; teams default to it. Below ~500 responses, NPS is noise. Use CSAT (per-feature, ~100 responses needed) or in-product 1-tap CES instead. NPS becomes useful much later.
No counter-metric Counter-metrics feel pessimistic. Every primary metric gets a named "must not regress" pair. Atlassian-style: same dashboard, same review. Without it, every "win" is suspect.
Once-a-year happiness survey Surveys are costly and get deprioritised. Embed 1-tap micro-surveys in-product, per session or per task. One question per session beats one annual 25-question survey for both signal and response rate.
Metrics change every quarter Team keeps refining what they measure. Lock metrics for ≥ 2 quarters; longitudinal comparison is the point. Re-baseline only with explicit team decision logged in the GSM doc.

Outcome. The GSM artefact forced two productive arguments in the 90-minute workshop: (a) total minutes per week was rejected as a Signal because the team realised longer sessions could indicate lostness, not engagement — exactly the kind of confusion HEART exists to prevent; (b) the counter-metric on CSAT (Happiness) created the rule that pure engagement wins don't ship if happiness drops. Six weeks later, an experiment that lifted M1 by +12% but dropped C2 by 0.4 was killed at the gate — under the old "Engagement up = win" rule it would have shipped.
