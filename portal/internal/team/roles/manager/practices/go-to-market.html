Go-to-market playbook — the plan between "built" and "adopted"

A structured pre-launch plan that coordinates marketing, sales, support, and customer success — so the product reaches the right users in the right way on launch day, not three weeks after.

TL;DR

A Go-to-Market (GTM) playbook is the operational plan for taking a feature or product to market. It names the target segment, the positioning and one-sentence pitch, the launch sequencing, the readiness checklist with named DRIs, and the launch metrics measured at D+7 and D+30. It is not the product brief — the brief defines what to build; the GTM defines how to take the built thing to market. Without one, launches are under-communicated and under-adopted.

What it is

A go-to-market playbook is the operational document that bridges the gap between "the feature is built" and "the feature is adopted". It is written by the PM with input from marketing, sales, customer success, and support, and covers five things:

  • Target segment. Which customers get the feature first? If phased, what is the sequencing criteria (plan, geography, account size)?
  • Positioning & messaging. The value proposition, the proof points, and the one-sentence pitch every sales rep and support agent can repeat verbatim.
  • Launch sequencing & timing. GA on a specific date, or beta → GA with explicit graduation criteria. Go/no-go conditions documented before the calendar invite goes out.
  • Readiness checklist. Help docs live, support trained, sales demo delivered, changelog written, in-app announcement drafted — each item with a named DRI who signs off, not the PM.
  • Launch metrics. What does success look like at D+7 and D+30? Adoption rate, support-ticket volume, sentiment score from in-app surveys.

Why we need it

The most common product failure mode is not shipping the wrong thing — it is shipping the right thing to no one. A feature that took a quarter to build reaches 3% of the eligible user base in the first month because nobody outside engineering knew it existed, the support team learned about it from the first confused customer, and sales found out from a changelog two days late. The GTM closes this gap by making launch communication and readiness as deliberate as the engineering sprint that produced the feature.

The readiness checklist alone earns its keep. Without one, two failure modes are predictable: support agents discover the feature live with customers (eroding trust), and sales reps mention it in deals after they've already lost the prospect on the prior feature. Both undo the goodwill of shipping something genuinely useful.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Intercom

Intercom's PM team writes a "launch brief" for every significant feature. It includes a "what changed for customers" section (not "what we built"), a "what sales needs to know" section (competitive implications, pricing), a "what support needs to know" section (common questions, escalation), and a "launch risk" section — what could go wrong in the first 72 hours, and which PM is on-call to respond.

What we take: The 72-hour on-call PM is the cheapest insurance against a bad launch. Name one before the calendar invite goes out — someone with authority to roll back, restrict the rollout, or hold a press post until Monday.

Source: Intercom — Product launches

Stripe — three-stage launches

Stripe runs SWE-facing features through three stages: private beta (5 selected customers, PM-managed feedback), public beta (announced in changelog, opt-in), GA (docs, migration guides, deprecation timeline for old endpoints). Each stage has explicit graduation criteria; beta → GA is a deliberate PM decision, not automatic.

What we take: Phased launches surface edge cases and migration friction before all customers are affected. Define graduation criteria before the beta starts — otherwise beta never ends and "graduating" is just running out of patience.

Source: Stripe Engineering Blog

Figma — readiness review

Two weeks before GA, Figma runs a 30-minute "launch readiness review" with PM, marketing, support lead, and sales lead. Each function confirms readiness against a shared checklist. If any function is not ready, the launch date moves — not the readiness standard. The meeting is a checklist review, not a feature demo.

What we take: A readiness review should answer "are we ready?", not "what is this?". By T-2 weeks every attendee should already know the feature; the meeting confirms readiness, period.

Source: Figma Product Blog

Atlassian — launch tiers

Atlassian classifies every launch as Tier 1 (major — press release, dedicated landing page, exec sponsor, ~6-week GTM), Tier 2 (notable — blog post + changelog, 2-week GTM), or Tier 3 (silent — changelog only, no GTM). The tier is decided early and dictates the depth of every step.

What we take: Not every launch deserves a full GTM. Tiering up-front prevents the team from over-investing in a small feature or under-investing in a flagship. A weekly bug-fix release running a full Tier-1 GTM is the same wasted effort as a Tier-1 launch shipped silently.

Source: Atlassian — Inside Atlassian

The pattern across teams: tier the launch before investing GTM effort; run a real readiness review (slip the date, not the bar); name a 72-hour on-call PM; define beta graduation criteria before the beta. Each of these is cheap; skipping any one of them is the cause of most failed launches.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Launch tier

Tier 1 / 2 / 3 decided up front. Dictates the depth of every other step; prevents over- and under-investment.

Target segment & sequencing

Who first? Plan, geography, account size. If phased, the rule that promotes the next cohort. Documented before a launch date is chosen.

One-sentence pitch

Any team member can say it to a customer. PM-written, sales-tested. If sales can't use it in a call, it's not ready.

Readiness checklist with DRIs

Help docs live, support trained, sales demo delivered, changelog written, in-app announce drafted, analytics events shipped. Each line owned by a named DRI — not the PM.

Readiness review (T-2 W)

30-minute meeting, checklist-driven, all leads present. Any red item slips the date — never the bar.

Launch tier metrics & floors

D+7 and D+30 targets for adoption, ticket volume, sentiment. Below floor = post-launch review opens an investigation, not a celebration.

72-hour on-call PM

Named PM with rollback / restrict authority for the 72 hours after GA. Not a passive observer — a decision-maker.

D+30 post-launch review

Scheduled on the day the feature ships. Comparing measured launch metrics against the pre-set floors. Result feeds the next launch's playbook.

How it works in this project

Study App stores GTM playbooks as markdown under docs/launches/<launch-id>.md — one file per launch, version-controlled, opened by PR. The first row of every file is the launch tier (T1 / T2 / T3); the depth of the rest of the playbook is dictated by that. A template lives at docs/launches/_template.md and contains the full checklist; sections are deleted, not skipped, for lower tiers.

Default cadence for a Tier-1 launch: kickoff at T−4 weeks (segment + tier decision), messaging draft at T−3 W, readiness build at T−2 W ending in the readiness review, launch at T−0 with on-call PM rotation, post-launch review at T+30. A 5% safety ramp is the default for any launch that ships behind a flag — D+0 to D+2 on 5%, then full rollout if guardrails are green.

A short ADR will lock the three launch tiers (criteria, template depth, default metrics) and the rule that any unchecked readiness item at T-2 W slips the GA date by at least one week — no exceptions, no soft-overrides.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Tiering — the discipline that scales

A product team that runs the same GTM on a weekly bug-fix and a flagship launch is wasting effort both ways: heavy process on the small change, and (eventually, by burnout) light process on the big one. Tiering decides depth up-front. A typical scheme:

TierTriggerGTM depthLead time
Tier 1New product, pricing change, public-facing rebrand, or first feature for a new segment.Press, landing page, exec sponsor, sales enablement, partner outreach, full readiness review.4–6 weeks
Tier 2Notable new feature, integration, or workflow change customers will ask about.Blog post + changelog, in-app announce, sales demo, support training, light readiness review.2 weeks
Tier 3Bug fixes, performance, minor improvements, internal tooling.Changelog line only. No GTM. Doc updated if user-visible.None

Bid the tier honestly. The most common mistake is calling a Tier-1 launch a Tier-2 to skip work; the second-most-common is calling a Tier-3 a Tier-1 because the team is proud of it. Neither serves customers.

Positioning — the four questions

The positioning section is most useful as a forcing function for four questions, in order: (1) who is this for? (2) what alternative are they using today? (3) what's different about ours? (4) why does that difference matter to them, in their language? Skipping (2) is the most common error — teams describe their feature in isolation rather than against the alternative the customer would otherwise pick. The competitive alternative might be a competitor; it might be a spreadsheet; it might be "do nothing". All three are valid; "nothing" is the most common.

Beta graduation — define the exit, not the entry

Most failed betas fail at the exit, not the entry: nobody defined what "ready for GA" means, so beta either drags forever or graduates because the team got bored. Define before beta starts: how many customers must opt in, what error/support thresholds must hold, what documented bugs must close, what migration tooling must ship. The graduation review checks the list — and if any item is open, beta extends, not GA-ramps.

Time-to-value as a launch metric

Adoption is the headline metric; time-to-value (TTV) is the diagnostic. TTV is how long after enabling the feature until a user reaches the "value moment" (first lesson completed, first invoice sent, first integration installed). A launch with 60% D+7 adoption but a TTV of 5 days is in trouble — most adopters never reach value and will churn off the feature. A launch with 40% adoption and TTV of 5 minutes is healthy — adopters get to value fast and tell others.

The 72-hour on-call PM rule

Most launches go sideways on day 1 — error spike, ambiguous metric, bad press tweet, support overflow — and the team needs a decision-maker available. Name a single PM as on-call for 72 hours post-GA, with explicit authority to: (a) restrict the rollout to a smaller segment, (b) roll back behind the feature flag, (c) hold a planned press post, (d) escalate to leadership. Authority must be pre-agreed; "I'll check with leadership" at 11pm Friday is the opposite of authority.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Lead time. A Tier-1 GTM is 4–6 weeks of PM/PMM effort, parallel to the engineering build. Squeeze it and either the readiness checklist becomes aspirational or the launch slips.
  • Cross-functional dependency. Support, sales, and CS owe their parts of the checklist. If they're under-resourced, the launch quality is capped at their bandwidth. Plan against that, not around it.
  • Tier inflation. Teams gradually upgrade every launch to Tier 1 because "ours matters too". Establish a hard quota — e.g., max 4 Tier-1 launches per quarter — to keep tiering honest.
  • Theater risk. A GTM document everyone signs but nobody reads is worse than no GTM. The readiness review and the on-call PM rotation are the practices that turn the document from theater into an operational tool.

Lifecycle

tier decided → segment + positioning → messaging + pitch → readiness build (DRIs) → readiness review → launch + on-call PM → D+7 / D+30 review → archive + lessons

Tier is decided when the engineering brief is approved (not later). The four-week pre-launch clock starts there. The readiness review at T-2 W is the gate: any red item slips GA by at least a week. Launch fires with a named on-call PM for the next 72 hours. D+7 is a quick pulse-check; D+30 is the full post-launch review against pre-set floors. The playbook is archived with the actual numbers next to the targets — the next launch's template gets one cycle smarter.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
GTM written on launch day Treated as a post-engineering task, not a parallel workstream. Start the GTM when the product brief is approved. A real Tier-1 GTM is 4–6 weeks of cross-functional work — it cannot compress into the week before launch.
Readiness checklist marked done by the PM The PM owns the document and self-checks the items. Each line requires sign-off from its DRI — support lead confirms training, sales lead confirms demo done, docs owner confirms docs live. The PM is not the source of truth for those items.
No tier — same depth for every launch "All launches are important" feels democratic. Tier explicitly (T1/T2/T3). Cap T1 launches per quarter (e.g. 4) to prevent inflation. Honest tiering protects both small launches (no over-process) and big ones (real process).
Beta never ends No graduation criteria defined; beta drags until the team gets bored. Define graduation criteria before beta starts — customer count, error/support thresholds, must-close bugs, migration tooling shipped. Beta extends until all are met; "graduation by exhaustion" is forbidden.
No on-call PM after launch Team moves to the next feature on D+1. Name a PM on-call for 72 hours with explicit rollback / restrict authority. Schedule it before the launch invite goes out.
No D+30 review Calendar fills up; everyone's on the next thing. Book the D+30 review on the day the feature ships — booking later when everyone is busy never happens. The review compares actual numbers to pre-set floors; below-floor opens an investigation.
Positioning skips the alternative "We built X" instead of "X vs the spreadsheet / competitor / doing nothing". Force the team to answer: what is the customer doing today instead? Positioning that doesn't name the competitive alternative reads as marketing copy, not customer-language.

Worked example

GTM playbook

LAUNCH-042 — "Streak freeze" feature, Tier 2, GA 2026-W22

id:             LAUNCH-042
title:          Streak freeze — one free day per week
tier:           Tier 2 (notable feature, expected support questions)
launch date:    2026-05-25 (Mon) · GA at 10:00 UTC

╭─ Segment ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────╮
│ first cohort:  users with active streak ≥ 7 days                │
│ sequencing:    week 1 — streak ≥ 7  (≈ 18% of WAU)             │
│                week 2 — all paid users                          │
│                week 3 — all users                               │
╰──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────╯

╭─ Positioning ────────────────────────────────────────────────────╮
│ today they use:   nothing — they break the streak and feel bad  │
│ what's different: one "pause" per week, no streak penalty       │
│ why it matters:   protects motivation, not punishes a busy day  │
│ one-sentence:     "Pause your streak for a day without losing  │
│                    it — life happens."                          │
╰──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────╯

╭─ Readiness checklist (T-2 W review) ─────────────────────────────╮
│ ☑ help doc live              · DRI: @kate (docs)   · 2026-05-15│
│ ☑ support trained            · DRI: @dana (sup)    · 2026-05-18│
│ ☑ sales demo done            · DRI: @ivan (PM)     · n/a (T2)  │
│ ☑ changelog drafted          · DRI: @ivan (PM)     · 2026-05-20│
│ ☑ in-app announce drafted    · DRI: @maria (design)· 2026-05-21│
│ ☑ analytics events shipped   · DRI: @oleg (eng)    · 2026-05-19│
│ ☑ feature flag wired         · DRI: @oleg (eng)    · 2026-05-19│
│ verdict:                      READY FOR GA                       │
╰──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────╯

╭─ Launch metrics & floors ────────────────────────────────────────╮
│ D+7  adoption (of eligible cohort): floor 25%   stretch 40%    │
│ D+7  support tickets:                floor < 30  stretch < 15   │
│ D+30 retention of users who froze:   floor +0pp  stretch +5pp  │
│ D+30 sentiment (in-app survey):      floor 7/10  stretch 8.5/10│
╰──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────╯

on-call PM:    @ivan, 2026-05-25 10:00 → 2026-05-28 10:00 (72 h)
               authority: rollback flag, restrict to ≥ 14d streak,
                          hold any planned external comms.

D+30 review:   2026-06-24 booked in calendar (booked 2026-05-23).

Outcome. The 72-hour on-call PM caught a real issue at D+1: the in-app announce text overlapped with another modal for users on small screens, hiding the freeze CTA. The on-call PM held the in-app announce rollout to large screens only while design shipped a fix on D+2. D+7 adoption came in at 31% (above floor); D+30 retention of users who froze was +3 pp on week-2 cohort vs control (above floor). The launch was a documented success in the post-mortem — but the win wasn't the feature, it was the readiness checklist (caught the analytics events early) and the on-call PM (caught the modal overlap before all users saw it).
