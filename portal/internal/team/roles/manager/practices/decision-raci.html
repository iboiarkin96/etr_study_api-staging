Decision RACI — who decides, who does, who is consulted

A decision matrix that makes authority explicit — preventing the two most common decision failures: no one decides and everyone decides.

TL;DR

A Decision RACI (Responsible · Accountable · Consulted · Informed) maps recurring product decisions to clear roles. Accountable is the single person who owns the outcome — only one per decision. Responsible does the work. Consulted shape the call before it's made; Informed are told after. The matrix covers recurring decision types (scope change, pricing, launch go/no-go), so when the moment arrives the process is pre-agreed — not negotiated in the room.

What it is

A Decision RACI is a matrix that maps recurring product decision types to four roles:

  • Responsible (R) — the person who does the work: gathers the data, writes the proposal, runs the analysis. Often a different person from the Accountable.
  • Accountable (A) — the single person who owns the outcome. Exactly one A per decision. If nobody is accountable, nobody is; if two people are accountable, the decision will either delay or fork.
  • Consulted (C) — people whose input is required before the decision. Their concerns must be heard and addressed; they do not have veto.
  • Informed (I) — people who are told after. Being informed is not an invitation to relitigate.

A Decision RACI covers recurring types of decisions — scope change, pricing, launch go/no-go, technical architecture, UX direction — so when one of those moments arrives, the team does not negotiate authority in the room. It applies the pre-agreed process.

Why we need it

The two failure modes of product decision-making are mirror images: no one decides because no one has clear authority, and everyone decides because authority is unclear, so every stakeholder feels entitled to weigh in. Both produce delay, drift, and resentment. The RACI makes authority explicit and non-negotiable: if you are not the A, you can be a C, but you do not block the decision.

The matrix also distributes the "consulted" workload fairly. When consultation is ad hoc — the PM asks whoever's nearby — the same loud voices are heard repeatedly while quieter stakeholders are missed. A RACI makes consultation predictable, which reduces the PM's social tax and the frustration of stakeholders who feel bypassed.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Amazon — single-threaded owner

Amazon's "single-threaded owner" (STO) principle: every significant product initiative has exactly one named person whose primary job function is that initiative. The STO is the Accountable in RACI terms. The company's culture is explicit — an initiative without an STO will be eroded by competing priorities. The STO removes ambiguity by design.

What we take: Single-threading is the most important rule of RACI. For every significant decision, there is exactly one person measured on the outcome — and they know it. Make that explicit in the matrix.

Source: Amazon — Working backwards & STO

Spotify — decision log paired with RACI

Spotify squads maintain a living "decision log" — a document that records significant decisions with Accountable, date, options considered, and rationale. Every A-row in the RACI generates a log entry when a decision fires. The log is referenced in postmortems to trace which decisions led to which outcomes.

What we take: RACI tells you who; the decision log tells you what and why. Paired, they create an audit trail that closes the learning loop without requiring memory or blame.

Source: Spotify Engineering Blog

Intuit — DACI variant

Intuit uses DACI (Driver · Approver · Contributor · Informed) for cross-functional decisions. The "Driver" runs the decision process; the "Approver" makes the final call. Splitting RACI's Responsible into Driver + Contributor makes "who runs the meeting" vs "who supplies analysis" explicit.

What we take: For decisions where running the process is itself a heavy task (cross-functional, multi-week), prefer DACI. For the day-to-day product decisions in a squad, RACI is enough.

Source: Atlassian — DACI framework

Bain — RAPID

Bain's RAPID (Recommend · Agree · Perform · Input · Decide) explicitly separates the people who must agree (veto power) from those who give input (no veto). Designed for senior-leadership decisions in matrixed orgs where two parties must align (e.g. CPO and CFO on pricing).

What we take: When a decision genuinely requires two parties to agree (compliance, legal, finance), RACI's "C" is not strong enough — it has no veto. Mark those parties separately. For Study App's v1 scale, RACI is the right starting point; promote to RAPID if a veto party emerges.

Source: Bain — RAPID

All four frameworks share the same core insight: exactly one person owns the decision. The variations exist for richer environments (multi-week processes, veto parties, leadership) — but the one-A rule is constant. Pick the lightest framework that names a single owner and ship.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Decision types (rows)

8–15 recurring decision types: scope change, tech architecture, UX direction, launch go/no-go, pricing, hiring, partnership. Not one-off decisions — patterns.

One A per row

Every row has exactly one Accountable. Two A's = the matrix is wrong. Resolve before the next decision, not during it.

C vs I distinction

C means "their input shapes the decision". I means "they are told after". Misclassifying I as C creates wasted consultation and false expectations.

Escalation path

What happens if the A is unavailable, or two C's strongly disagree? The matrix names the escalation target plus the trigger condition.

Decision log

Every fired decision generates a log entry: A, date, options considered, rationale. The log is the audit trail the matrix points at.

Cited at the moment

The matrix is referenced explicitly when decisions are made: "Per our RACI, @ivan is A for launch — Ivan, what is your call?". Not referenced = not real.

Quarterly review

Authority changes as roles change and complexity grows. Review at each quarterly retro; a stale RACI is worse than no RACI.

How it works in this project

Study App's RACI is a 12-row matrix in Notion under PM / Decisions / RACI. Rows cover the recurring decisions a v1 product team actually faces: feature scope change, technical architecture, UX direction, pricing & packaging, launch go/no-go, hiring (eng/design), partnership / vendor selection, security & data-handling, public communication, post-incident response, OKR setting, and budget reallocation.

The decision log lives next to it under PM / Decisions / Log. Every entry links back to the RACI row that authorised it and to the Notion or Slack thread where the decision was discussed. Escalation paths are explicit: PM → CPO, Eng Lead → CTO, Director → CEO. The matrix is opened at the start of any leadership meeting that touches a row — it is not an offsite artefact, it is the meeting's agenda spine.

A short ADR will lock the row taxonomy, the escalation triggers, and the format of decision log entries so the audit trail stays comparable across quarters.

Deep dive — the theory you need

The one-A rule — why "shared accountability" is no accountability

When two people are "jointly accountable", three things happen: (a) each assumes the other is closer to the problem, (b) decisions are deferred to find consensus, and (c) when the outcome is bad, nobody owns it because "we both decided". Bezos's STO principle exists to prevent exactly this. A single A means a single phone to call, a single performance review to hold, and a single mind to commit. The discomfort of naming one A is the matrix surfacing an ambiguity that already existed — it is doing its job.

C vs I — the cost of misclassification

The fastest way to ruin a RACI is to mark everyone as C "to be inclusive". Each C adds a real cost: a meeting, a Slack thread, a Loom recording, a follow-up. A 4-C decision is a week-long conversation. A 1-C decision is an afternoon. The right rule: C if and only if their input could change the decision. If you know in advance their input would not change the outcome, they are I, not C. Being I is not a demotion; it's clarity.

The veto question — when RACI is not enough

RACI's C has no veto. For most product decisions this is correct: input shapes the call, but the A decides. But three categories of decision genuinely require a veto party: legal/compliance (lawyer can stop a launch), security (CISO can stop a launch), and finance (CFO can stop a pricing change). For those rows, either: (1) move to RAPID and add an "Agree" column, or (2) make those parties the A themselves for that row. Pretending they are merely C will surface the problem at the worst time.

Escalation design — the unavailability question

Two failure modes the escalation path must answer. A is unavailable (vacation, OOO, departed): the matrix names a deputy who can stand in for that row's A. Two C's strongly disagree: the A's call still stands, but the matrix names the escalation target if a C wants to formally object (typically the A's manager). Both triggers are listed in the row header, not in the room.

Disagree-and-commit — the cultural pair

RACI gives the A authority; "disagree and commit" (Amazon, again) gives the C an honest path when their input is heard but not adopted. The norm: "I disagree with this decision, and I will commit to executing it without reopening the discussion." Without this norm, every contested decision gets relitigated in the hallway. The RACI is the framework; disagree-and-commit is the culture that makes the framework actually work.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Up-front design cost. A real RACI takes 4–6 hours with the leads to negotiate. The negotiation is the value — it surfaces ambiguity that already existed but was hidden.
  • Discomfort. Naming a single A on contentious rows (pricing, hiring) feels political. The discomfort is precisely the work the matrix is doing — labelling reality.
  • Risk of bureaucracy. A 50-row RACI is a worse problem than no RACI. Cap at ~15 rows and only for recurring decision types; one-off decisions go to the decision log directly.
  • Cultural prerequisites. RACI needs disagree-and-commit to survive contact with real disagreement. Without it, the C's start escalating to feel heard and the A's authority erodes.

Lifecycle

enumerate decision types → assign single A → define C / I lists → name escalation → publish + cite → log every firing → quarterly review

The first matrix is drafted by the team leads in one session — 4–6 hours, deliberately uncomfortable. Each row gets exactly one A and a 2–4 person C list. Escalation targets are filled in. The matrix is published in the team workspace and is cited explicitly the first few times a covered decision fires; once it's a habit, the citation becomes shorthand. Every fired decision generates a decision-log entry. Every quarterly retro reviews the matrix: which rows had ambiguity, which A's want to delegate, which new decision types now recur.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Multiple A's on the same row Nobody wants to be left out; shared accountability feels fair. Shared accountability is no accountability. Force one A per row even when uncomfortable. The discomfort is the matrix doing its job — naming an ambiguity that already existed.
Consulted = everyone in the org PM wants to avoid complaints of being bypassed. Cap C at 2–4 per row. Rule: C if and only if their input could change the outcome. If their input wouldn't change anything, they are I, not C — and I is clarity, not demotion.
RACI built then filed away Created at offsite, never opened again. Cite it explicitly the next time a covered decision fires: "Per our RACI, @ivan is A for launch — Ivan, what's your call?" If you cite it the first three times, the fourth is automatic.
No escalation path The A's vacation, departure, or unavailability isn't planned for. Every row names a deputy (covers the A) and an escalation target (if a C wants to formally object). Listed in the row header, not negotiated in the moment.
RACI grew to 50 rows Every one-off decision got its own row. Cap at ~15 rows; only recurring decision types. One-off decisions go to the decision log directly, not into the matrix.
Veto party hidden as a C Legal / Security / Finance marked as Consulted on a row where they can actually stop the launch. If the party has a veto, RACI is too weak. Either promote that row to RAPID (with an "Agree" column) or make the veto party the A for that row. Pretending otherwise surfaces the problem at the worst time.

Worked example

Decision log entry

DEC-031 — Launch go / no-go for the Activate redesign (2026-W19)

id:          DEC-031
type:        Launch go/no-go
raci row:    "Launch go/no-go"
                A = PM (@ivan)
                R = PM (@ivan)
                C = Eng Lead (@oleg), Design (@maria)
                I = Director (@cpo), Support lead (@dana)
                escalate to: CPO (@cpo) if Eng Lead and Design disagree

trigger:     Activate redesign EXP-042 ready for 100% ramp
data:        - SRM check passed
             - All guardrails green (signup, p95, error rate)
             - Primary metric: +7.2% relative lift on 7d completion (p=0.008)
             - Support backlog: 0 redesign-related tickets in 5% safety ramp

C input:
  @oleg:     "Ship. Code is stable, no incident risk."
  @maria:    "Ship, but please track week-3 retention separately —
              redesign may have novelty effect that fades."

decision:    SHIP at 100%; week-3 retention added to dashboard guardrails.
decided by:  @ivan (A)
decided at:  2026-05-12 14:30 (Slack #pm-decisions thread)
informed:    @cpo, @dana via #pm-decisions and email summary

review:      EXP-042 outcome reviewed at 2026-W22 retro; week-3 retention held.

Outcome. The decision fired in < 30 minutes because the matrix had already answered "who decides". The PM (A) collected the two C inputs in a Slack thread, made the call, and the I-list was notified the same hour. Maria's concern about novelty effect was captured in the log and converted into a tracked guardrail — exactly what "Consulted, no veto" looks like in practice: input shaped the decision without blocking it. Six weeks later the postmortem could trace the call back to its rationale without anyone reconstructing memory.

Quality bar — what good looks like

A checklist of what a healthy implementation has. ✓ done ◐ partial ○ todo

  • ○

    Exactly one A per row

    A second A on any row is a bug to fix, not a compromise to accept.

  • ○

    C ≤ 4 per row

    Each C must be able to change the outcome — otherwise they are I.

  • ○

    Escalation path on every row

    Deputy (covers the A) and escalation target (for objections) named.

  • ○

    Decision log entry per firing

    Options considered, rationale, and the A signature on every entry.

  • ○

    Cited at the moment of decision

    "Per our RACI…" is verbal habit, not a printed artefact in a drawer.

  • ○

    Veto parties named honestly

    Legal / Security / Finance are A on their rows, or those rows are RAPID. Not hidden as C.

  • ○

    Quarterly review with row turnover

    New decision types added, dead ones retired, A's reviewed.
