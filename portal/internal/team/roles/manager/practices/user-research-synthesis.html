User research synthesis — from interviews to insights

A repeatable process for turning raw interview data into structured insights the whole team can act on — converting research from folklore into institutional knowledge.

TL;DR

Synthesis converts raw notes and recordings into tagged insight cards, affinity clusters, and actionable themes. Without it, research produces stories; with it, evidence. Cards within 24h of each session; affinity in days 1–3; monthly review reprioritises the insight backlog and links top themes to OST opportunities.

What it is

User research synthesis is the structured process of converting raw research data — interview recordings, transcripts, survey responses, session recordings — into insight cards that are tagged, clustered, and rated by severity. Output: a shared insight repository the team can read and act on without being present in the original session.

Three stages:

  1. Within 24 h — one insight card per significant observation; tag by theme, segment, severity.
  2. Days 1–3 — affinity session: cluster cards into thematic groups; name patterns.
  3. Monthly — full synthesis review: reprioritise the insight backlog; link top themes to OST opportunities; convert to "How might we…" prompts.

Canonical artefacts: insight-card database (source of truth for findings), empathy map (say/think/feel/do), affinity diagram (clusters showing themes across sessions).

Why we need it

Research without synthesis is folklore. The PM who conducted the interviews has the nuance in their head; everyone else has the summary they heard in a meeting. When the PM leaves or memory fades, the research value disappears entirely.

A shared insight repository converts individual memory into institutional knowledge. New team members read the database and absorb 6 months of customer pain in 2 hours. Stakeholders not in the room review evidence before challenging decisions. The PM walks into prioritisation debates with a severity-ranked list rather than "I remember a customer saying…".

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

IDEO — "How Might We" reframing

IDEO's design thinking makes synthesis a formal phase with specific tools: affinity diagrams on sticky notes (or Miro), empathy maps in four quadrants (say/think/do/feel), and "How Might We" reframing that converts observations into opportunity prompts.

What we take: The "How Might We" step — converting "users struggle with X" into "How might we make X effortless?" — is the moment research becomes product direction. Build it into every synthesis session.

Source: IDEO Design Thinking

Google Ventures — 60-min sprint synthesis

GV's Design Sprint includes "How Might We" note-taking during interviews + dot-voting on insights. The format distils 5 hours of research into a ranked design-challenge list within 60 min.

What we take: Time-box synthesis to avoid infinite clustering. 60 min is enough to get from raw notes to a prioritised list if the format is right.

Source: Sprint book — Jake Knapp

Airbnb — Experience Library

Airbnb's research team maintains a centralised insight database tagged by persona, journey stage, and sentiment. Product teams search the library before starting new discovery — often finding the question was already answered by a previous session.

What we take: A searchable database eliminates redundant research. Before booking a new interview, check whether the question has already been answered. The library pays for itself when teams stop re-researching solved problems.

Source: Airbnb Design — Research

Microsoft — research repository at scale

Microsoft's research orgs use tagged insight repositories (e.g. Dovetail, Aurelius) with controlled vocabularies for themes. Researchers tag insights with persona, severity, and "evidence weight" (number of sessions backing it). Product teams query by tag to find relevant prior work.

What we take: Controlled vocabulary > free-text tags. Without it, "Onboarding" and "onboarding" and "OB" become separate tags and the database becomes searchable in theory only.

Source: Microsoft — Research repository case study

Across teams: tagged insight cards, controlled vocabulary, severity rating, searchable database, "How Might We" output. Without these, synthesis becomes journaling — useful for the PM, invisible to everyone else.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Observation

What the user said or did, in their words or direct description. No interpretation yet.

Insight

What the observation means. One sentence. "Users struggle with X because Y."

Theme tag (controlled vocab)

From a fixed list: Onboarding / Navigation / Trust / Performance / Pricing / etc. No free-text tagging.

Severity

Critical (blocks task) / Moderate (causes friction) / Minor (nice to fix). Derived from user behaviour, not subjective.

Source link

Timestamp link to the session recording. Lets reviewers verify without trusting the PM's paraphrase.

Frequency

How many sessions surfaced this insight. Updated as new sessions complete; rises = real pattern, stays at 1 = anecdote.

"How Might We" prompt

Converts the insight into an opportunity statement; the bridge to OST.

How it works in this project

Study App's insight database lives at docs/research/insights/ as one markdown file per interview (front-matter tagged), aggregated into docs/research/INDEX.md for searchability. Within 24 h of every continuous-discovery interview, the PM writes 3+ insight cards with controlled-vocab theme tags, severity, timestamped source link, and "How Might We" framing.

Affinity clustering runs at the monthly product review (90 min) — recent cards are clustered, frequencies updated, top themes linked to OST opportunities. The output: 2–3 ranked themes carrying forward into next month's discovery and brief work.

A short ADR locks the controlled-vocab tag list, the within-24h card-writing rule, and the monthly affinity-review cadence.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Observation vs insight — keeping them separate

The single most important discipline is keeping observation and insight as distinct fields. Observation: what the user said or did (no interpretation). Insight: what it means (one sentence of interpretation). Collapsing them produces cards where the team can't tell what's evidence vs what's PM's read. Separating them lets reviewers challenge the interpretation while preserving the original data.

Severity rubric — derived, not subjective

"Severity" tempts subjective ratings ("I felt this was important"). The discipline: derive severity from observable behaviour during the session.

SeverityBehavioural anchorExample
CriticalUser abandoned the task or expressed strong negative emotion"closed the app and didn't come back"
ModerateUser completed the task but slowly / with workarounds"used Cmd+F because the search was broken"
MinorUser noticed the friction but completed without effect"hesitated then clicked the right button"

Controlled vocabulary — the searchability lock-in

Free-text tagging is the most common database-killer. Within 6 months, the same concept appears as "Onboarding", "onboarding", "OB", "First-time experience", "FTUE" — and querying by tag becomes unreliable. The fix: a fixed list of 12–20 themes maintained by the PM lead; new themes require explicit addition (and migration of old cards). Cheap discipline, big payoff.

Frequency vs single-session — the "is it real?" test

A finding from one session is an anecdote; the same finding from 3+ sessions is a pattern. The frequency field on each card is updated as new sessions surface the same insight. Cards stuck at frequency=1 after 6 months either (a) describe a single rare user (deprioritise) or (b) describe a real but hidden problem (worth a targeted recruit). Track frequency; let it drive priority.

"How Might We" — the bridge to OST

The synthesis output isn't the insight; it's the "How Might We" prompt. "Users struggle with X" is descriptive; "How might we make X effortless?" is productive — it invites multiple solution paths. This is the exact format OST consumes as opportunity nodes. Without the HMW step, the insight database stays disconnected from the roadmap.

Empathy map vs affinity diagram — when each applies

Two synthesis artefacts often confused. Empathy map (per persona): what the user says, thinks, feels, does. Useful for understanding a single persona deeply. Affinity diagram (across sessions): clusters of insights by theme, regardless of persona. Useful for spotting patterns across many interviews. Mature teams use both: empathy maps per primary persona; affinity diagrams per quarter to surface emerging themes.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Per-session tax. ~30–60 min synthesis per interview. Cheap, but added to the interview's hour-and-a-half, the total weekly ask is real.
  • Tooling. Dedicated tools (Dovetail, Aurelius, Notion) cost money; rolling your own in markdown is free but loses search affordances.
  • Discipline of controlled vocabulary. Tag-list maintenance is unglamorous; the PM lead must own it or it drifts.
  • Single-session bias. A vivid quote from one user is memorable; frequency tracking is the antidote. PMs need to override the recency-and-vividness bias every quarter.

Lifecycle

interview held → cards within 24h → controlled-vocab tagging → severity from behaviour → affinity at month-end → "How Might We" output → link to OST

Per interview: PM writes 3+ cards within 24h with observation, insight, theme, severity, source link. Monthly: affinity-cluster the month's cards, update frequencies, name 2–3 dominant themes, convert to HMW prompts, link to OST. Quarterly: review the controlled-vocab list and prune low-frequency tags.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Synthesis weeks after the interview PM is busy; synthesis gets deprioritised. Block 30–60 min the same day. Memory fades fast; quotes lose nuance after 48 h.
Insights written as solutions "Add a search bar" is a solution, not an insight. Write the observation first. Derive the insight. Solutions belong in OST, not insight cards.
Database never revisited Cards are added but never linked to product decisions. Monthly synthesis review: cluster, reprioritise, link top themes to OST. The review is the practice.
Free-text tagging Easiest at write time. Controlled vocab list, maintained by PM lead. Tag drift = unsearchable database within 6 months.
Subjective severity "I felt this was critical". Behavioural anchor: did the user abandon? slow down? complete fine? Severity derived from observation, not feeling.
Single-session anecdotes treated as patterns Vivid quotes are memorable. Track frequency. Cards at frequency=1 are anecdotes; ≥3 is a pattern worth acting on. Resist vividness bias.

Worked example

Insight card

INS-2026-W18-03 — "Streak makes me anxious, not motivated"

id:           INS-2026-W18-03
session:      INT-2026-W18-Maya (recording at 24:15)
persona:      lapsed user · week-3 dropout
date:         2026-05-04
author:       @ivan

OBSERVATION (verbatim or direct description, no interpretation)
  At 24:15, Maya said: "When I see my streak number going up, I feel
  pressure not to lose it. After day 12, I started avoiding the app
  on days I was tired because losing a 12-day streak felt worse than
  not learning that day."

INSIGHT (one sentence interpretation)
  For some users, the streak mechanic creates loss-aversion anxiety
  that becomes a reason to disengage rather than engage.

THEME (controlled vocab)
  Retention · Motivation · Streak-mechanic

SEVERITY
  Critical · user explicitly described abandoning the app to protect
  the streak number — the mechanic is causing the opposite of the
  intended behaviour.

FREQUENCY
  3 sessions to date: INT-2026-W14-Diego, INT-2026-W16-Sara, INT-2026-W18-Maya
  pattern emerging; warrants OST opportunity.

"HOW MIGHT WE" PROMPT
  How might we let users protect their streak without feeling
  punished for taking a day off?
  → linked OST opportunity: D-114 "streak freeze"

SOURCE LINK
  https://loom.com/share/REDACTED?t=24m15s

Outcome. The insight card surfaced a counterintuitive finding — the streak mechanic was producing disengagement, not engagement — which a single-session summary would have missed. Frequency tracking confirmed the pattern across 3 sessions, justifying the OST opportunity D-114 (streak freeze). The behavioural-anchored severity rating (user explicitly described abandoning) gave the team confidence to deprioritise other less-evidenced opportunities. The "How Might We" framing converted the negative observation into a productive opportunity statement that the OST and brief could act on directly.
