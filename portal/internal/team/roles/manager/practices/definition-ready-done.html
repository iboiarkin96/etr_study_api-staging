Definition of ready & done — the quality gates

Two checklists that prevent the two cheapest mistakes: premature sprint entry and premature sign-off. The team's contract on what "ready to build" and "actually done" mean.

TL;DR

Definition of Ready (DoR) is a checklist a story must pass before it enters a sprint. Definition of Done (DoD) is a checklist a story must pass before it is marked complete. Both are written and owned by the whole team — not by the PM alone. DoR stops mid-sprint ambiguity; DoD stops phantom progress where the board shows 80% done but nothing is shippable. Together they are the team's quality contract.

What it is

The Definition of Ready (DoR) is a team-agreed checklist a story must satisfy before it can be selected for a sprint. Typical criteria: the story has an approved product brief, acceptance criteria are written in testable form ("Given X, when Y, then Z"), engineering has estimated the effort, dependencies are identified, and no blocking questions remain.

The Definition of Done (DoD) is a team-agreed checklist a story must satisfy before it can be marked complete. Typical criteria: PM has verified every acceptance criterion against the built feature, automated tests pass in CI, code is reviewed and merged, documentation is updated, and the feature is deployed to production (or staging with a scheduled deployment).

Both definitions are owned by the whole team — PM, engineering, design, and QA — and reviewed at the quarterly retro. Neither is a PM document imposed on engineering. Each is signed off as a team contract; updates are voted on by the team, not edited unilaterally.

Why we need it

Without a DoR, sprint planning becomes a negotiation between the PM's optimism and engineering's uncertainty. Stories enter the sprint half-baked and the team discovers the ambiguity mid-sprint, when it is most expensive to resolve. A DoR moves the preparation work to the right place: before planning, not during it.

Without a DoD, "done" means different things to different people. An engineer marks a story done when tests pass locally; the PM considers it done when the feature ships; the customer considers it done when the docs are updated. The DoD creates one shared definition that prevents the phantom progress problem — the sprint board glows green while the product is unshippable.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Atlassian

Atlassian's engineering teams post the DoD physically on the wall and as a pinned message in the team Slack. Any team member can call a "DoD check" during sprint review — the presenter then walks the checklist for each story. Stories that fail DoD return to "in progress" and are not counted as velocity.

What we take: Making the DoD visible and invokable by any team member — not just the PM — distributes quality ownership. When only the PM checks DoD, it becomes a formality; when any engineer can call it, it becomes a cultural norm.

Source: Atlassian — Definition of Done

Spotify

Spotify squads maintain type-specific DoDs — separate checklists for feature stories, bug fixes, and tech-debt stories. A feature requires customer-facing docs and PM sign-off; a bug fix requires a regression test; a tech-debt story requires a measurable before/after metric. The type is set at creation, not retrofitted.

What we take: One DoD does not fit all story types. A blanket DoD either slows bug fixes (held to feature standards) or ships features without docs (held to bug standards). Match the DoD to the story shape.

Source: Spotify Engineering Blog

Basecamp / 37signals

Basecamp uses "Shape Up" — six-week cycles with two-week cooldowns. Their DoR equivalent is the "shaped pitch": a problem framed at the right level of detail (concrete enough to commit, loose enough to discover). Their DoD is "production live + post-launch review" — code in main is not done; usage data is.

What we take: "Done" is not when code merges; it's when the change is observed in production. Wire post-launch checks into the DoD: error rate, primary metric, support tickets — measured in the first 48 hours, then closed.

Source: Basecamp — Shape Up

GitLab

GitLab's handbook publishes the DoR/DoD and the rationale for every item. Each line links to the incident or postmortem that produced it — "added 2024-Q2 after INC-118 leaked PII through analytics events". The DoD is a living archaeology of the team's mistakes.

What we take: Every DoD item should have a reason documented next to it. Without the reason, future teams cut items they don't understand. Reasons make the DoD self-justifying.

Source: GitLab Engineering Handbook

The pattern across teams: gates are team-owned (not PM-imposed), type-specific (not one-size-fits-all), justified (each item points at a past pain), and invokable by anyone (not just the PM). Lower the bar on any one of those and the gate softens into theatre.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

DoR — brief approved

PM and engineering lead have signed off on the product brief. No brief = not ready. No exceptions.

DoR — AC in G/W/T form

Acceptance criteria written as "Given X, when Y, then Z". Vague AC is the single biggest predictor of a missed sprint.

DoR — effort estimated

Engineering has provided a story-point or t-shirt estimate. Stories without estimates cannot be committed.

DoR — deps + blockers identified

External calls, third-party access, design assets, legal review — listed and not blocking. "We'll handle it" is not a state.

DoD — AC verified by PM

PM has personally tested each AC against the built feature. Not delegated to QA, not assumed from a screenshot.

DoD — tests passing

Automated suite green in CI; manual test cases documented and run; no open P0/P1 bugs on the story.

DoD — production-ready

Deployed to production, or to staging with a named deployment slot. "Merged to main" is not done.

DoD — post-launch checks

Error rate, primary metric, and support backlog observed for 48 hours after launch. Reviewed at next standup before the story closes.

Each item has a "why"

Every line on the checklist links to the incident, regression, or pain that produced it. Reasons make items survive personnel turnover.

How it works in this project

Study App's DoR and DoD are stored as a single markdown file in docs/process/definition-ready-done.md, owned by the whole squad. Both are pinned in #dev-team and printed on the team wall (when there is one). Sprint planning opens with the PM running stories through the DoR live; anything that fails is dropped back to backlog and the team pulls a smaller ready story instead.

Type-specific DoDs apply: feature stories require user docs + PM sign-off + post-launch metric check; bug-fix stories require a regression test + 48-hour error-rate watch; tech-debt stories require a documented before/after metric. The story type is set when the story is created and cannot change retroactively.

A short ADR will lock the checklist format, the per-type DoDs, and the rule that any team member can call a DoR or DoD check at any time — including during sprint review.

Deep dive — the theory you need

DoR as the "pull system" boundary

The DoR is the formal boundary between push and pull in the team's flow. Outside the DoR (the backlog) is a push system — stakeholders push ideas in. Inside the DoR (sprint) is a pull system — the team pulls only what is ready. Without a DoR, push leaks into the sprint and the team works on whatever shouted loudest yesterday; with a DoR, push stops at the gate and the sprint stays predictable. This is the same insight as Kanban WIP limits, applied to story quality rather than story count.

The acceptance criteria format — why G/W/T matters

"As a user I want X so that Y" is a story format, not an AC format. Acceptance criteria need to be testable, which means they need a precondition, an action, and an observable outcome. Given the user has 3 lessons completed, when they open the dashboard, then a streak count of 3 is displayed. A QA can write a test against that. "User can see their streak" is unfalsifiable — does any pixel count? Is one streak enough? G/W/T forces the team to name the test before the build.

Per-type DoD — the matrix

One generic DoD over-applies to bugs (slow) and under-applies to features (no docs). A practical matrix:

Story typeRequired DoD itemsOptional / type-specific
Featuretests pass · PM verifies AC · code reviewed · in production+ user docs · + post-launch metric (48 h) · + experiment plan if behind a flag
Bug fixtests pass · root cause identified · in production+ regression test added · + 48-h error-rate watch · + postmortem if P0/P1
Tech debttests pass · code reviewed · in production+ before/after metric (build time, p95, bundle size) · + ADR if architecture changed
Spike (research)findings written up · review held(no "production" requirement — a spike produces a document, not code)

Why PM verifies AC, not QA

QA tests that the code works; PM verifies that the work was the right one. They overlap but the question is different. A feature can pass every test, hit every AC literally, and still not solve the user's problem because the AC was poorly written. The PM is the only person who notices that early, and only if they personally touch the built feature. Delegating AC verification to QA hides PM mistakes inside engineering quality numbers.

The retention rule — every item has a reason

DoD items decay over time as team members rotate. The protection: each line carries the incident or pain that produced it. "docs updated" alone gets cut for speed; "docs updated — INC-118 customers contacted support because release notes were silent" survives, because cutting it now requires defending the cut. GitLab's handbook is the gold standard here.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Up-front time. A real DoR adds 1–2 days of PM/design work before each sprint. The payoff is predictability — finished sprints — but the cost shows up immediately and the benefit shows up later, which makes it politically vulnerable.
  • Velocity reduction (temporarily). The first sprint after introducing a DoD usually reports lower velocity, because stories that would have been "done" under the old norm now bounce back. This is the velocity number becoming honest, not the team becoming slower.
  • Team-contract fragility. If leadership pressures the team to skip the gates "just this sprint", the gates die. Naming a senior sponsor who defends the gates is the only durable fix.
  • Bureaucracy risk. A 20-item DoD that nobody reads is worse than a 6-item one everyone follows. Cap each gate at ~6 lines; type-specific extras are not "additions", they replace lines.

Lifecycle

team-drafted gates → per-type DoDs → pinned + posted → cited at planning + review → fails bounce back → quarterly review → items pruned / added with reason

The first DoR/DoD is drafted by the squad in a 90-minute workshop. The PM facilitates but does not own it. Type-specific DoDs are derived from the generic one. The gates are pinned in the team channel and on the wall. They are invoked verbally — "let me run this through DoR" — until the habit takes. At quarterly retros, gates are reviewed: items with no real failures in 6 months are candidates for removal; new pain points generate new items, each with a documented reason.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
DoR bypassed under pressure "We'll figure out the details during the sprint." If a story fails DoR at planning, it does not enter. Pull a smaller ready story instead of lowering the bar. The first time the gate holds is the only time it matters.
DoD as formality, not check The engineer who built it also marks it done. PM verifies every AC personally on the live build. If "PM sign-off" is in the DoD, it gets enforced; if you can't enforce it, remove it — half-enforced items teach the team that gates are negotiable.
DoD never changes Written in the first sprint, never opened again. Review at each quarterly retro. Add items born from recent incidents (with the reason linked); remove items whose failure rate is zero (they aren't catching anything).
One DoD for all story types "Same standard for everyone" sounds fair. Maintain per-type DoDs. Bug fixes need regression tests, not user docs; features need user docs, not before/after metrics; tech debt needs before/after, not user docs. Same level of rigour, different items.
Only the PM invokes gates The team treats DoR/DoD as the PM's job. Any team member can call a DoR or DoD check. Make this an explicit rule; it distributes quality ownership and breaks the PM-as-policeman dynamic.
DoD without a "why" column The checklist was written without rationale. Every item links to an incident, regression, or pain that produced it. Items without a reason get cut at the next retro; items with a reason survive.

Worked example

DoR + DoD walk-through

STORY-218 — "Show learning streak on dashboard" (feature)

STORY-218 · Show learning streak on dashboard
type:        feature
priority:    P1 (linked to OST opportunity "lapsed users lose the why")

╭─ DoR check (at planning, 2026-W19 Mon) ──────────────────────────╮
│ ☑ brief approved          → PM @ivan, EngLead @oleg (2026-05-08)│
│ ☑ AC in G/W/T form        → 4 ACs written, see below            │
│ ☑ effort estimated        → M (2–3 days), 1 BE + 1 FE           │
│ ☑ deps identified         → none (data already in events table) │
│ ☑ no open blockers        → confirmed by team                   │
│ verdict: READY → enters Sprint 24                                │
╰──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────╯

Acceptance criteria (Given / When / Then)
  AC-1  Given user has ≥1 completed lesson in last 24h,
        when they open the dashboard,
        then a streak count ≥1 is displayed in the header.

  AC-2  Given user has missed yesterday and today,
        when they open the dashboard,
        then no streak is displayed (no "streak: 0").

  AC-3  Given user has a streak ≥7,
        when they open the dashboard,
        then a flame icon is displayed next to the count.

  AC-4  Given the streak query fails,
        when the dashboard renders,
        then the page renders without the streak block (no error UI).

╭─ DoD check (at end of sprint, 2026-W21 Fri) ─────────────────────╮
│ ☑ tests pass in CI            → 14 new unit, 3 integration     │
│ ☑ PM verified AC              → all 4 ACs personally walked    │
│ ☑ code reviewed               → 2 approvals on PR-1142          │
│ ☑ docs updated                → user-facing changelog entry    │
│ ☑ in production               → released 2026-05-22 14:00       │
│ ☑ post-launch (48h watch)     → error rate flat; +9% return    │
│ verdict: DONE → closed at Sprint 24 review                      │
╰──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────╯

Notes:
  - AC-4 failed first verification (page errored on query failure).
    Bounced back to "in progress"; fixed and re-verified on day 6.
  - Bounce did not count against velocity — DoD-failure stories
    stay open until they pass.

Outcome. The DoR caught nothing this sprint (the brief was already strong), but the DoD caught a real defect on AC-4: the streak block silently broke the whole header when the query failed. Under the old "engineer says it's done" norm, that would have shipped — the user would have seen a blank dashboard the first time the query timed out. The 48-hour post-launch check then confirmed the lift on the lapsed-user metric the story was hypothesised to move. Both gates did their job: DoR kept the sprint predictable, DoD kept production safe.
