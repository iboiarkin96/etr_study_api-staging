Opportunity solution tree — outcome to experiment

A living visual map that traces every experiment back to a customer opportunity and a desired outcome — no orphan solutions, no faith-based features.

TL;DR

The OST (Teresa Torres) has four layers: Outcome → Opportunities → Solutions → Experiments. Every branch is traceable. The structure makes it impossible to work on a solution without a customer opportunity behind it, and impossible to pursue an opportunity without an experiment validating that the solution actually addresses it. Built incrementally from weekly discovery interviews; updated within 24 h of each session.

What it is

The opportunity solution tree, introduced by Teresa Torres in Continuous Discovery Habits (2021), is a visual artefact with four layers:

  1. Desired Outcome — one metric the team is trying to move this quarter, typically a Key Result from the OKR.
  2. Opportunities — customer needs, pains, or desires surfaced through weekly interviews. Written in the customer's voice; each one a real problem, not a solution.
  3. Solutions — potential ways to address an opportunity. At least two per opportunity, to prevent premature commitment.
  4. Experiments — assumption tests that validate whether a solution will move the opportunity, before full build.

The tree is built incrementally from weekly interview data. It is not a workshop artefact frozen on day one — it is a living document updated continuously: new opportunities promoted, old ones deprioritised or invalidated, solutions added, experiments run.

Why we need it

Without an OST, product decisions travel through an invisible chain of assumptions. "We should add a guided onboarding wizard" sounds like a solution — to which problem? How do we know it addresses the most important opportunity? How confident that a wizard beats the two other solutions we didn't evaluate?

The OST makes every step in that chain explicit. When a stakeholder proposes a feature, the first question is "which opportunity node does this address?". If none exists, the conversation shifts to "should we add this opportunity based on customer evidence?" — a far more productive question than debating whether the feature itself is good.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Teresa Torres — Product Talk

The originator uses OST as the backbone of her consulting with product teams. Canonical advice: start with one OKR Key Result as the Outcome, add ≥ 3 opportunities from the first month of interviews, and never add a Solution without first placing it under an Opportunity.

What we take: The tree's value is in the constraint: no solution without an opportunity, no opportunity without customer evidence. Enforce the rule even when it's uncomfortable; bypassing once teaches the team it's optional.

Source: Product Talk — OST guide

Amplitude

Amplitude's product team maintains an OST-like "opportunity map" connecting the NSM to specific user-friction points surfaced via session recordings and interviews. Each friction point has ≥ 2 candidate solutions before one is selected for experiment.

What we take: ≥ 2 solutions per opportunity prevents falling-in-love-with-the-first. Gives the PM leverage to deprioritise solutions that look good but lack evidence.

Source: Amplitude — Product Discovery

Notion — navigable OST

Notion's PM team keeps the OST in Notion itself, as a database of linked pages. Opportunities link to interview recordings + insight cards; Solutions link to design explorations; Experiments link to analytics dashboards. The whole tree is browsable in 5 minutes by any team member.

What we take: The OST should be navigable without the PM as guide. If a new engineer needs the PM to explain the tree, it isn't doing its job — the artefact has become PM-knowledge instead of team-knowledge.

Source: Notion — OST template

Slack — experiment-first

Slack's product organisation pairs every Solution node with at least one cheap experiment before any engineering commitment — fake doors, concierge tests, prototype usability sessions. Solutions that pass experiment move to engineering; solutions that fail return to the tree as "tried" and the team moves to the next candidate.

What we take: An OST without experiments at the leaves is just a brainstorm. The experiment layer is the rigour; without it the team builds solutions on opinion.

Source: Slack Engineering Blog

Across teams: customer-language opportunities, ≥ 2 solutions per opportunity, cheap experiments at the leaves, and a navigable artefact that doesn't need the PM to explain. Cut any one and the OST degrades into a brainstorm with arrows.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Desired Outcome

One metric, tied to an OKR KR. Changes ~once per quarter.

Opportunity (customer voice)

Written as the user would say it: "I can't tell if I'm making progress" — not "lack of progress indicator".

Evidence link per opportunity

Each opportunity cites ≥ 1 interview ID; no opportunity without recorded customer source.

≥ 2 Solutions per opportunity

Forces the team to consider alternatives before committing.

Assumption map per Solution

2–3 explicit assumptions per solution; the riskiest is tested first.

Experiment per riskiest assumption

Cheap test before full build (smoke, fake door, concierge, prototype). Results linked back.

Update-within-24h rule

After every interview, opportunities are added / refined / linked. Stale tree → dead tree.

How it works in this project

Study App's OST lives in Notion at PM / Discovery / OST, mirrored as a markdown snapshot in docs/discovery/ost.md for version history. The Outcome is the current quarter's primary KR (typically tied to NSM-input movement). Opportunities are added from continuous-discovery interviews within 24 hours of each session.

Each Solution has ≥ 2 siblings considered (documented in the node), 2–3 explicit assumptions, and a cheap experiment plan before engineering effort. Experiments use the dual-track playbook (dual-track agile): smoke tests, fake doors, prototype usability, concierge tests. Results feed back into the tree as the solution moves to "validated → delivery" or "invalidated → tried".

A short ADR locks the update-within-24h rule, the ≥ 2-solutions rule, and the rule that no Solution enters the delivery backlog without a documented experiment + result.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Opportunity in customer voice — why it matters

"Improve onboarding" is a goal. "Onboarding has too many steps" is a PM observation. "I gave up because the form asked too many questions" is an opportunity. The third version is the only one that survives interpretation across time: it's the customer's actual language, recoverable from the interview transcript. The first two are PM paraphrase — they drift away from what users said, and over months they become "what we always thought" rather than "what we heard". Customer-voice opportunities anchor the tree in evidence; PM-voice opportunities float free of it.

Why ≥ 2 solutions — the divergence rule

Single-solution thinking is the most common product failure mode. The team identifies a problem, brainstorms one solution, builds it, ships it, and discovers it didn't address the problem (because the right solution was different). The "≥ 2 solutions" rule forces a fork before commitment: two paths, two assumption sets, the team picks the one with cheapest experiment. Sometimes neither works, which is also valuable — the team learns the problem is harder than expected before sinking sprints.

Assumption mapping — surfacing the bet

Every solution rests on assumptions. "1-tap social login will lift activation" assumes: (a) the slow signup form is the real barrier (not motivation or pricing); (b) users have social accounts they're willing to link; (c) the implementation cost is justifiable. Mapping these explicitly lets the team test the riskiest first. Often the riskiest assumption (a) is testable without building the solution at all — e.g., a switch interview asking churners "what made you stop signing up?" If signup-form-length isn't mentioned, the assumption fails and the team can skip building.

Experiments at the leaves — the rigour level

An OST without experiments at the leaves is a wall of arrows. Experiments make the difference between "we think this will work" and "we tested this". Five experiment types ordered by cost:

  1. Switch interview — ask churners / new users directly; cheapest, fastest.
  2. Smoke test / fake door — a CTA for a feature that doesn't exist; measures demand.
  3. Concierge test — deliver the outcome manually (PM-as-backend) before automating.
  4. Prototype usability — clickable Figma with 5–7 users.
  5. Engineering spike — minimal real build, behind a flag, on a small audience.

Pick the cheapest experiment that tests the riskiest assumption. Avoid the engineering-spike default — it's often the most expensive option for a question that a 15-minute switch interview could answer.

The 24-hour update rule

The OST works because it's fresh. An OST updated quarterly is a quarterly artefact, not a discovery tool. The rule: within 24 hours of every interview, the PM adds opportunities, refines existing ones, or notes evidence. Saturday morning is fine; what matters is that the gap from interview to tree-update is small enough that the team can act on insights while they're still raw.

OST vs Impact Mapping vs JTBD

Three trees that look similar but answer different questions. OST — bottom-up: starts from customer outcome, opportunities from interviews. Impact mapping — top-down: starts from business goal, decomposes into behaviours and deliverables. JTBD — a lens, not a tree: defines the customer's job that the outcome serves. The mature team uses all three: JTBD frames the user; Impact map cascades from OKR Why; OST emerges from weekly interviews. They converge but don't replace each other.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Discipline. Easy to slip into PM-voice opportunities and single-solution branches when the team is busy. The PM has to police it.
  • Maintenance. 24-hour update rule + weekly tree review is real time. Without it, the OST decays to a wall ornament.
  • Stakeholder politics. Leadership sometimes pushes solutions ("we need feature X") that don't have opportunities. The OST forces the conversation back to evidence, which can be uncomfortable.
  • Tree explosion. Without pruning, the tree grows to dozens of opportunities. Mandate a monthly prune: opportunities not progressed in 2 quarters are archived.

Lifecycle

outcome from OKR KR → interview-sourced opportunities → ≥ 2 solutions per opp → map assumptions → cheapest experiment → validate · invalidate · iterate → scale or archive

The Outcome is set at quarter start (from the OKR). Opportunities are added weekly from interviews. Solutions are added when an opportunity gets prioritised; assumptions are surfaced; the riskiest is tested via the cheapest experiment. Validated solutions go to the delivery backlog; invalidated solutions are logged as "tried" so future teams don't re-propose them.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Solutions without opportunities Stakeholders propose features that bypass the tree. "Which opportunity does this belong under?" If none, the conversation shifts to whether the opportunity should be added — evidence-based.
Opportunity written as a solution "We need a dashboard" is a solution. Rewrite in customer voice: "I can't see how my team is performing without navigating five different screens".
Tree frozen after workshop Treated as one-time deliverable. 24-hour update rule after every interview; staleness kills the tree's value.
One solution per opportunity The first solution feels obvious. Mandate ≥ 2 solutions before any moves to experiment. Forces the team to think divergently before converging.
Experiments skipped "We know it'll work" — straight to build. Even cheap experiments (switch interview, smoke test) prevent expensive misses. Make the experiment the gate, not a nice-to-have.
Tree explosion (30+ opportunities) Every interview adds an opportunity, nothing gets pruned. Monthly prune: opportunities not progressed in 2 quarters move to archive with reason. Active tree ≤ 12 opportunities at any time.

Worked example

OST branch

Study App 2026-Q3 — branch from activation Outcome

OUTCOME (Q3 KR2)
  Lift activation rate from 18% → 33% (first-lesson within 24h)

├── OPPORTUNITY A (customer voice · ev: INT-04, 07, 12 · score: high)
│   "There are too many steps before I can actually learn anything."
│   ├── SOLUTION A1 · 1-tap social login (Apple / Google)
│   │     assumptions:
│   │       a. signup form-length is the binding constraint (RISKIEST)
│   │       b. users have linkable social accounts
│   │       c. eng cost ≤ 3 days
│   │     experiment: switch interview, 5 churners
│   │       result (2026-W18): 4/5 cited "too many steps" → a CONFIRMED
│   │     → moved to delivery (Sprint 24)
│   ├── SOLUTION A2 · Progressive form (3 fields → expand later)
│   │     assumptions:
│   │       a. progressive disclosure feels less intimidating (RISKIEST)
│   │       b. users complete the full form post-signup
│   │     experiment: prototype usability, 5 users
│   │       result (2026-W19): 4/5 found the gradual reveal helpful
│   │     → kept on tree as backup if A1 underperforms

├── OPPORTUNITY B (customer voice · ev: INT-09, 14 · score: medium)
│   "I don't know what the lessons feel like before I sign up."
│   ├── SOLUTION B1 · First-lesson preview before signup
│   │     assumption: preview lifts intent → reduces abandonment
│   │     experiment: fake-door CTA "preview first lesson" on landing
│   │       result (2026-W17): 14% CTR — significant demand
│   │     → in design, moves to engineering next sprint
│   └── SOLUTION B2 · Demo video on landing page
│         assumption: video > preview for showing value
│         experiment: A/B test landing variants
│         → waiting on B1 results to decide

└── OPPORTUNITY C (customer voice · ev: INT-22 · score: low — single source)
    "I don't know what level I am, so I just guess."
    └── SOLUTION C1 · 2-question level placement quiz
          → NOT enough evidence to prioritise this quarter
          → monthly review: see if more interviews confirm
          → archived 2026-W20 (no new interview evidence in 6 weeks)

Outcome. The tree did three useful things this quarter: (1) opportunity B's fake-door experiment cost 4 hours and produced a strong demand signal that justified engineering investment; (2) solution A1 shipped after a 15-minute switch interview confirmed the riskiest assumption — saving ~3 weeks of building B-side fallback variants that the team had originally planned in parallel; (3) opportunity C was archived (not killed — preserved with reason) after a 6-week wait for additional interview evidence didn't surface any. Without the tree, all three branches would have proceeded to build; with it, A1 shipped, B is in flight, C was paused with reasoned closure.
