RICE prioritisation — scoring over gut feel

Score every candidate feature on Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort — then rank by the number, and debate the inputs, not the rank.

TL;DR

RICE (Intercom, 2015) is a scoring model: Reach (customers/quarter) × Impact (0.25–3) × Confidence (0–100%) ÷ Effort (person-weeks). Higher score = higher priority. The score is not an algorithm — it's a structured conversation starter. The value is making overrides explicit and documented, not following the ranked list blindly.

What it is

RICE is a prioritisation scoring model created by Intercom PM Sean McBride (2015). Four scored dimensions:

  • Reach — customers affected per quarter. Use product analytics where possible; not gut numbers.
  • Impact — discrete scale: 3 (massive) · 2 (high) · 1 (medium) · 0.5 (low) · 0.25 (minimal). Used consistently across items.
  • Confidence — 0–100% in the Reach and Impact estimates. Low confidence → low score.
  • Effort — person-weeks across all functions (PM + design + eng). Minimum 0.5 wk.

Score = (R × I × C) ÷ E. The ranked list is the starting point for discussion — teams debate the inputs (is Reach realistic? Is Confidence too high?), not the conclusion. Overrides are allowed; they must be written down with reason.

Why we need it

Without a scoring model, prioritisation defaults to whoever makes the most compelling argument — usually the most senior stakeholder or the loudest voice, rarely the person with the best evidence. RICE externalises the reasoning behind every decision and makes it debatable at the input level, where evidence lives, rather than at the conclusion level, where politics live.

The Confidence dimension is particularly load-bearing: it forces the PM to acknowledge what they don't know before committing resources. A high-RICE-score item with 40% Confidence is a candidate for a discovery experiment before full build — and that insight is invisible in gut-feel prioritisation.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Intercom (inventors)

Intercom RICE-scores every candidate before sprint planning. The score is not binding — the team can override — but overriding requires a written justification visible to everyone.

What we take: The override mechanism is as important as the scoring. RICE is a structured conversation starter, not an algorithm. The value is making overrides explicit and documented.

Source: Intercom — RICE scoring

Atlassian — evidence-based Confidence

Atlassian replaces Confidence with "Customer Evidence" — a 0–3 score from the count of customers who specifically requested or described the need. Operationalises continuous discovery: features cited by more customers score higher.

What we take: Tying Confidence to concrete evidence (interview count, ticket volume, OST citations) makes it measurable rather than a PM's subjective feeling. Rewards the teams that do the most customer discovery.

Source: Atlassian — Prioritization

HubSpot — cross-team visibility

HubSpot scores features monthly in a shared spreadsheet visible to all PMs. Cross-team ranked list reviewed quarterly; engineering capacity allocated on combined scores. The transparency prevents gaming.

What we take: Cross-team RICE visibility surfaces resource conflicts before sprint planning rather than during. When every team's ranked list is visible, allocation becomes a strategic conversation, not a political one.

Source: HubSpot Product Blog

Shopify — weighted RICE for strategy

Shopify adds a fifth dimension: strategic alignment (0–2), multiplied into the score. Items aligned to the current strategic pillar get a 2× boost, off-strategy items get 0.5×. Keeps the ranked list anchored to strategy, not just local impact.

What we take: Pure RICE can over-weight small high-confidence wins. A strategic-alignment multiplier (or an off-strategy de-prioritisation) prevents the team from optimising the metric and missing the mission.

Source: Shopify Engineering

Across teams: evidence-based Confidence, engineering-validated Effort, cross-team visibility, documented overrides. RICE without these is a spreadsheet ritual; with them, it's an honest priority engine.

Confidence with justification

Effort signed by engineering

Strategic-alignment multiplier (optional)

0.5× off-strategy, 1× neutral, 2× on-strategy. Prevents local-impact wins crowding out mission.

Documented overrides

Every deviation from the ranked list is written down with reason. Overrides are allowed; secrecy isn't.

Cross-team visibility

All RICE sheets in one place; readable by every PM and engineering lead.

How it works in this project

Study App's RICE sheet lives at docs/prioritisation/rice.md (rendered to a public Notion view). Every candidate for the Next column has a RICE row. Reach is sourced from PostHog analytics; Confidence requires written justification if > 70% (typically: ≥ 5 switch interviews, an A/B result, or an analytics-confirmed pattern); Effort is signed off by the engineering lead.

Sprint planning starts with the ranked list distributed 24h before. Overrides are allowed and tracked in rice-overrides.md with date + reason. A short ADR locks the Impact scale anchors, the Confidence justification rule, and the override-documentation requirement.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Why Confidence matters more than Impact

Most teams over-invest debate in Impact (everyone has an opinion) and under-invest in Confidence (less comfortable to admit you don't know). The mathematical reality: a feature with Impact 3 and Confidence 40% scores the same as one with Impact 2 and Confidence 60%. The team that's more honest about Confidence ranks better because their high-Confidence items are real. The team that inflates Confidence ships work that was less validated than the score suggested.

Impact anchors — calibration discipline

The Impact scale (3 / 2 / 1 / 0.5 / 0.25) only works if everyone uses it consistently. Maintain anchor examples:

ScoreMeaningExample anchor
3Massive — transforms the experience for affected users"Lesson catalogue redesign" — visible to every visit
2High — significant change in a high-frequency flow"Signup form 1-tap social" — major activation lift
1Medium — clear positive but bounded"Streak freeze" — useful, not game-changing
0.5Low — minor improvement"Settings page polish"
0.25Minimal — barely worth shipping"Footer link reorganisation"

Without anchors, Impact drifts upward — every PM scores their item a 2 or 3. With anchors, the conversation becomes "is this really a 2, given that we anchored 2 = 1-tap social login?" — productive.

Reach over a quarter — not lifetime

"Customers affected" is per-quarter, not lifetime. A feature reaching 100% of MAU each quarter has a Reach number equal to MAU; a feature reaching 5% of MAU once has Reach = 0.05 × MAU. The quarter unit makes items with different exposure timelines comparable. Without it, lifetime-impact features always beat short-term wins, even when the short-term win pays for next quarter's discovery.

Effort gotchas — the inclusive number

Effort is total person-weeks across PM + design + engineering + QA. Common mistakes: PM forgets to include their own time; design effort is ignored; integration testing isn't counted. A "3-week" build that needs another 1 week of design and 1 week of QA is a 5-week item. Underestimating Effort inflates RICE scores systematically; review the breakdown explicitly per item.

RICE vs WSJF, MoSCoW, value/effort matrix

RICE is one of many scoring methods. Brief comparison:

  • WSJF (SAFe) — similar formula, replaces Confidence with "Time Criticality" and "Risk Reduction". Works when deadlines are external; over-engineered for self-paced product teams.
  • MoSCoW — Must / Should / Could / Won't. Coarse; useful for scope-cuts within a single release, not for ranking 30 candidates.
  • Value/Effort 2×2 — quick visual, no math; fine for early-stage when you have 8 items. Doesn't scale to large backlogs.
  • RICE — best fit for 15–100 candidate items where Confidence varies; explicit math forces honest conversation.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Scoring time. 5–10 min per item, ~1–2 hrs per quarter for a 20-item backlog. Cheap, but visible.
  • Number worship risk. Teams treat the rank as definitive. Counter with the documented-override norm: the rank is a starting point, not the answer.
  • Confidence honesty culture. Inflated Confidence is the most common failure mode; requires PM lead vigilance and a "justify > 70%" rule.
  • Anchor drift. Impact-scale anchors drift over quarters. Re-anchor at each quarterly retro to keep cross-period comparisons honest.

Lifecycle

candidate from OST / discovery → PM scores R / I → eng signs off Effort → PM marks Confidence + justification → ranked list shared 24h pre-planning → planning discusses inputs → overrides documented

Items enter via OST / discovery. PM does first-pass R/I/C scoring; engineering lead provides Effort. Justification required for Confidence > 70%. The ranked list is distributed 24 h before sprint planning so the meeting itself focuses on input debate, not number arithmetic. Overrides are written into the override log; the list updates iteratively as new evidence arrives.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Inflated Confidence PMs default to 80% to rank their items higher. Require written justification for > 70%. "I feel confident" isn't justification — interviews / A/B / analytics are.
Effort unsigned by engineering PM estimates without asking. Engineering t-shirt estimate required before score finalises. No engineering sign-off = item not eligible for ranking.
Ranking treated as final answer Score becomes an algorithm. The ranked list opens the discussion; it doesn't end it. Document overrides with reasons. The override log is part of the practice.
Impact-scale drift Every item scores 2 or 3 over time. Re-anchor at quarterly retro. Maintain example items at each level; calibrate against them.
Reach = lifetime, not quarterly Easier to think in lifetime. Per-quarter Reach. Lifetime numbers always make long-term plays look bigger than they are in the next 3 months.
No cross-team visibility Each PM keeps their own sheet. All RICE sheets in one place. Resource conflicts surface before planning, not during.

CONFIDENCE JUSTIFICATIONS (required for > 70%)
  Goal-based dashboard 85%   : 7 switch interviews + Q1 quant survey (200 users)
  1-tap social login 90%     : A/B test prototype confirmed 22% CTR (SR-022)
  Streak freeze 80%          : smoke test SMK-022 + 5 interviews
  CSV export 95%             : 14 support tickets in last 60 days + 3 enterprise asks

IMPACT ANCHORS (re-confirmed 2026-W17)
  3 = Massive  · anchor: "Lesson catalogue redesign" — touches every visit
  2 = High     · anchor: "1-tap social login" — major activation lift
  1 = Medium   · anchor: "Streak freeze" — useful, not game-changing
  0.5 = Low    · anchor: "Settings polish" — small QoL

Outcome. Six items, six different conversations — but each conversation was about inputs, not rank. The Live tutoring item ranked #7 by score but had a 30% Confidence — the team deferred to discovery instead of building, saving 30 person-weeks on a bet that wasn't validated. The 1-tap social login override was documented in DEC-038 (an RACI row), so future PMs can read why rank-2 went into Now before rank-1. Without the explicit confidence column + override log, both decisions would have happened silently and the team would have learned nothing.
