Funnel & cohort analytics — where users leave and why

Two complementary lenses on the same dataset: funnels show where users drop off inside a flow; cohorts show which users come back over time.

TL;DR

Funnel analysis tracks sequential step-conversion rates to find where users abandon a critical flow (sign-up, checkout, onboarding). Cohort analysis groups users by a shared attribute (sign-up week, channel, plan) and tracks each group over time. Together they answer the two diagnostic questions every product team needs: where do users drop off? (funnel) and which users come back? (cohort). Optimising the wrong one wastes a sprint on the wrong solution.

What it is

Funnel analysis tracks the sequential step-by-step completion rate of a critical user flow. For each step, the analysis shows what percentage of users who completed the previous step also completed this one. The step with the lowest step conversion (not lowest absolute %) is the bottleneck — the place where motivation breaks down or friction spikes.

Cohort analysis groups users by a shared attribute at a point in time (the cohort definition) and tracks their behaviour over subsequent time periods. The most common definition is "week of first use", which creates weekly cohorts comparable at 1, 4, 8, 12, and 24 weeks. Cohorts with materially better retention than the baseline identify a period when something — a feature, a campaign, a fix — improved the product, and become candidates for understanding cause.

The two techniques are complementary: funnels identify where users leave a specific flow; cohorts identify which users come back over time and whether product changes are improving baseline retention.

Why we need it

Without funnel analysis, product teams invest in features that aren't the bottleneck. A squad spends a sprint redesigning the sign-up form when 70% of users actually drop off at email verification — a step they never inspected. Funnel analysis directs effort to the highest-impact step of the journey, which is rarely the one the team intuitively suspects.

Without cohort analysis, teams cannot tell a growing product from a healthy one. A flat weekly-active-user line could mean stable retention with no acquisition (healthy but stagnant) or rapid acquisition masking catastrophic churn (terminal). The two situations look identical on a DAU chart and demand opposite responses; the cohort heatmap is the only view that separates them.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Amplitude

Amplitude's own product is structured around funnels and cohorts as primary analysis primitives. Their standard onboarding for new enterprise customers begins with a sign-up-to-activation funnel and a W0–W8 cohort retention chart. Those two charts together tell whether the activation problem is "users don't finish sign-up" (funnel) or "users who activate don't come back" (cohort) — which require completely different solutions.

What we take: Diagnosis must precede design. A sign-up redesign solves a funnel problem; an onboarding sequence redesign solves a cohort problem. Conflating the two wastes a sprint on the wrong solution.

Source: Amplitude — Product analytics

Intercom

Intercom divides the funnel into three phases — awareness (landing → trial), activation (trial → first value), habit formation (first value → week-2 return) — with a separate squad owning each. Each phase has a minimum acceptable step conversion. Drop below it and that squad pauses other work to diagnose, no waiting for quarterly review.

What we take: Phase ownership creates accountability without forcing every squad to optimise the same metric. Auto-trigger thresholds turn analytics from "we'll look later" into a closed loop.

Source: Intercom — Product analytics

Mixpanel — the "L7" view

Mixpanel popularised the "L7" engagement view — for each cohort, how many of the last 7 days each user was active. A user with L7 = 7 is a daily user; L7 = 1 is a one-day-a-week user. Plotting the L7 distribution per cohort reveals not just whether users come back, but how deeply they engage — a smiling distribution (heavy daily + heavy weekly) is the goal.

What we take: Binary "active in W4" hides depth. L7 (or LX for daily products, LXM for monthly) tracks engagement intensity per cohort and catches "users come back but only barely" patterns that look fine on a standard retention curve.

Source: Mixpanel Engineering Blog

Reforge — smiling retention

Reforge's retention course classifies products by their long-term retention shape: a "smiling curve" stops dropping and rises slightly (Facebook, Slack), a "flattening curve" stops dropping at a plateau (Spotify, Netflix), a "decaying curve" trends to zero (most consumer apps). The shape, not the W4 number, predicts whether the product has product-market fit.

What we take: Don't optimise W4 retention in isolation. Inspect the shape at W12, W24, W52 — if the curve still goes down, no W4 fix saves you. A flattening curve with a low plateau is a better signal than a steep curve with a high W1.

Source: Reforge — Retention is the silent killer

The pattern across teams: the diagnosis (funnel vs cohort) drives the design; phase ownership turns analytics into responsibility; depth views (L7) and shape views (smiling vs decaying) protect against optimising the wrong number. None of these are tools — they're framings, and they're cheap.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Funnel steps

3–7 sequential steps in a critical flow. Each step is a distinct, instrumented user action. Non-sequential "steps" are a list, not a funnel.

Step conversion (not absolute %)

The % of users who finished step N that also finished N+1. The lowest step-conversion is the bottleneck — independent of absolute size.

Funnel time-window

Each step has a maximum elapsed time for the user to count as "converted" (e.g. 24h between sign-up and activation). Without a window, slow conversion masks dropouts.

Cohort definition

One shared attribute per analysis: sign-up week, acquisition channel, plan, country, feature adoption. Mixing two attributes ruins the comparison.

Retention curve

% of cohort members active at week N. A healthy product flattens; an unhealthy product trends to zero. The shape matters more than any single point.

North-star action

A single observed event that counts as "active" (completed-lesson, sent-message, played-track). Counting opens vs core actions gives different curves; pick one and document it.

Triangular cohort table

Rows = cohorts (Jan, Feb…); columns = weeks since join (W0, W1…). Recent cohorts have fewer columns of data; the triangle expands week by week.

Engagement-depth view (L-X)

L7 (days active in last 7) or LXM (months active in last X). Catches "users come back but only barely" — invisible on a binary-active retention curve.

How it works in this project

Study App instruments events into a single warehouse table (analytics.events) populated from the product via the event-streaming pipeline. PostHog (self-hosted, open-source) sits on top for funnel + cohort dashboards; dbt materialises the canonical cohort triangle nightly into marts.cohort_retention for slower-moving analyses.

Three named funnels are tracked weekly: acquisition (landing → sign-up → activation), engagement (dashboard → start lesson → complete lesson), conversion (free → paid). Each has a named DRI (PM, growth, monetisation) and a minimum step-conversion floor; falling below the floor auto-creates a Linear ticket via PostHog's alerting hook.

Three cohort cuts are tracked weekly: by sign-up week (the default), by acquisition channel (organic / paid / referral / direct), and by plan (free / paid). The north-star action is "lesson completed" — counted as the active event in every retention curve. A short ADR locks the north-star definition, the event names, and the funnel time-windows so analyses stay comparable across quarters.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Step conversion — why the smallest box is not the problem

The biggest absolute drop in a funnel is almost always at the top, simply because the top has the most users. What matters is step conversion: the % of users completing step N who also complete step N+1. A funnel of 100 → 62 → 41 → 18 has absolute drops of 38, 21, 23 — top of funnel "loses the most users". But the step conversions are 62%, 66%, 44% — step 3 → 4 is the worst step. Optimising step 1 (which "loses 38 users") yields a few extra absolute conversions; fixing step 3 → 4 (44% step conversion) can double absolute output even though it's the smallest box. The vocabulary matters; teams that say "where do we drop the most users" optimise the wrong step.

The time-window — a step without a deadline isn't a step

"% of users who eventually finish step N+1" is a different question from "% of users who finish step N+1 within 24 hours". The first number is always higher; the second is the one that drives product decisions. Pick a window per step (sign-up → activation: 24h is typical; activation → habit: 1 week) and bake it into the funnel definition. Without a window, sleeping users count as "converted-eventually" and the team optimises a number that doesn't reflect the real experience.

Cohort triangle — the canonical table

The cohort table is read as a triangle: rows are cohort start dates (most recent on top or bottom by convention), columns are weeks-since-start (W0, W1, W2…). Each cell shows % of that cohort active at that week. The triangle grows row by row as cohorts age. Two readings:

  • Read across a row (one cohort over time) — does this cohort decay or flatten?
  • Read down a column (multiple cohorts at the same age) — is the product improving over time? If W4 retention is 28% for the Jan cohort and 45% for the Apr cohort, something improved between January and April. That's the question worth investigating.

The shape of the curve — smiling, flattening, decaying

Long-term retention shape predicts product-market fit better than any single number. Three shapes:

  • Smiling — drops, then rises. Network products: more users → more value → returning users come back more often. Facebook, Slack, LinkedIn.
  • Flattening — drops, then plateaus. Utility / content products: retained users keep using at a stable rate. Spotify, Netflix.
  • Decaying — keeps dropping. Most consumer apps without PMF. The W1 number doesn't matter; without flattening the product dies.

Inspect at W12, W24, and W52 (if you have the data). A team optimising W4 on a decaying curve is rearranging deck chairs.

The "what changed" question — how to interpret a cohort lift

When the April cohort retains materially better than the January cohort, the question isn't "the product got better" — it's what specifically changed between cohorts?. A disciplined investigation:

  1. List every product release between the cohorts.
  2. List every marketing campaign change between the cohorts (channel mix shifts the user quality, often more than product changes).
  3. List external factors (seasonality, competitor moves).
  4. Decompose the cohort by channel and plan — if the April lift is only in one segment, the cause is segment-specific.

Most cohort lifts come from channel-mix changes, not product changes. That's not bad — it just means you learned about your channels, not your product.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Event-instrumentation tax. A funnel is only as good as its events. Adding/changing events is engineering work and gets deprioritised in delivery sprints; without explicit slot for instrumentation, the analysis runs on stale or missing events.
  • Definitional brittleness. The north-star action ("completed lesson") looks settled until someone redefines "completed" mid-quarter. Retention metrics jump; nobody can compare across the change. Document the definition + change history.
  • Anonymous vs identified. Funnels above sign-up are anonymous (no user_id); funnels below are identified. Joining the two requires session/device IDs that may be cookie-blocked. Plan the identity strategy before instrumenting.
  • Cohort size for confidence. Below ~300 users a cohort is noisy; a 12 pp gap at W4 may be sampling noise. For small products, group cohorts by month or quarter rather than week.

Lifecycle

define flow + north-star → instrument events → first funnel report → cohort triangle → identify worst step / shape → hypothesis + experiment → re-measure same window

The first cycle takes 3–4 weeks. Defining the flow and the north-star action is the most contentious step; once locked, instrumentation is mechanical. The first funnel typically surfaces a surprise — the team's intuition about the worst step is wrong about half the time. The cohort triangle reveals shape; a decaying curve is a stop-the-roadmap signal. Each subsequent cycle runs against the same definitions (changing them invalidates comparison) until a re-baseline is explicitly declared.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Optimising the biggest absolute drop "We lose the most users at step 1" is the intuitive read. Compare step conversion, not absolute %. The worst step-conversion is the bottleneck — even when its absolute box is the smallest. Always.
Mixing cohorts in one retention chart "All users" feels simpler than splitting. A mixed retention chart hides everything that matters. Stable-looking retention often means new users masking churn of older users. Always break out by cohort.
Funnel defined post-hoc PM sees a bad metric and tweaks step definitions until it improves. Define the funnel steps and time-windows before looking at the data. Post-hoc edits are data mining, not analysis. Document the definition + change history.
No time-window per step "Eventually completed" counts as conversion. Bake a window into every step (24h, 1 week). Without it, sleeping users count as success and the team optimises an inflated number.
Optimising W4 on a decaying curve W4 is the easy metric to move. Inspect the curve shape at W12, W24, W52 first. If it still decays, no W4 fix saves you — the product doesn't have PMF yet, and the answer is upstream of analytics.
Cohort lift attributed to product without checking channel "Our W4 went up" — celebrated as a product win. Decompose the cohort by channel and plan. Most lifts come from channel-mix changes, not product changes. Learning that is also valuable, but not the same thing.

Worked example

Diagnosis

2026-W18 — Acquisition funnel + sign-up cohort, study-app v1

Funnel:  Landing → Sign-up → Email-verify → First-lesson-complete
window:  24h between each step
period:  last 14 days, all channels combined

step                       abs%       step ✓     verdict
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Visit landing              100.0%       —
Start sign-up               62.0%      62%        baseline (typical 50-70%)
Verify email                41.0%      66%        baseline
First lesson complete       18.0%      44%        ← WORST · investigate

Cohort retention (north-star = lesson-completed, weekly):
                   W0     W1     W4     W8    W12    W24
Jan 2026 cohort   100%   68%    48%    34%    24%    14%
Feb 2026 cohort   100%   69%    47%    33%    23%      —
Mar 2026 cohort   100%   71%    49%    36%      —      —
Apr 2026 cohort   100%   78%    61%    45%      —      —    ← lift

Decomposition of Apr lift by channel:
                   W4 retention   ∆ vs Mar
  organic              52%         +3 pp
  paid (Meta)          63%        +14 pp  ← driver
  referral             68%         +4 pp
  direct               58%         +9 pp

Verdict:
  • Funnel: step 3 → 4 (verify → first lesson) is the worst step. The
    activation squad opens a discovery item: why do 56% of verified
    users not complete a lesson within 24h?
  • Cohort: Apr lift is not uniform — it's concentrated in Meta-paid
    traffic. The growth squad investigates the Meta creative change
    that rolled out 2026-W14; if the new creative produces higher-
    intent traffic, the retention lift may not generalise.

Outcome. The two diagnoses produced two different roadmap items, owned by two different squads — one product (activation), one growth (channel mix). Without the funnel/cohort split, the team would have read the headline "W4 went up to 49%" and called it a product win; the decomposition revealed the lift was a channel-quality effect, not a product change. Both items entered the discovery backlog; the activation step ended up being fixed by changing the post-verify CTA from "Continue" to "Start your first lesson", which lifted step conversion from 44% to 58% in three weeks.
