Product brief — the contract before you build

A one-to-three-page document that gives engineering the why, the who, the what, the success metric — and explicitly not the how. Written before sprint planning, approved before sprint commit.

TL;DR

The product brief (modern lightweight PRD) is the written contract between PM and engineering. It covers Problem, Outcome (success metric), Scope (in + explicit out), Riskiest assumptions, and Open questions. It is not a technical spec — outcomes, not implementation. Approved by the engineering lead before sprint entry.

What it is

The product brief is the modern lightweight successor to the traditional PRD. Where a 50-page PRD documented implementation, a 1–3 page brief answers only what engineering needs to build the right thing:

  • Problem — who has the pain, how big, what evidence makes it real.
  • Outcome — one measurable change that defines success; how and when measured.
  • Scope — what's in (3–8 acceptance criteria in G/W/T form) and what's explicitly out.
  • Riskiest assumptions — 3–5 beliefs that, if wrong, invalidate the approach. Tested before full build.
  • Open questions — unresolved decisions with named DRI + resolution deadline.

The brief is written by the PM, reviewed by the engineering lead, and approved before sprint entry. It is not a design document — UX and technical decisions belong to the team during sprint. The brief defines the problem and success bar; the sprint defines the solution.

Why we need it

Without a written brief, scope is defined by whoever speaks most confidently in sprint planning. PM thinks the feature solves one problem; engineer builds it to solve a slightly different one; customer needed something else entirely. All three interpretations are plausible from the Jira title. The brief eliminates the ambiguity before code is written.

The explicit "out of scope" section is particularly load-bearing. Under deadline pressure, "out of scope" means different things to different people. Writing it down creates a defensible boundary: when a stakeholder requests an addition mid-sprint, the PM points at the brief — "explicitly out of scope; we can create a new ticket".

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Stripe — riskiest assumptions

Stripe's brief format requires: customer problem, success metric, what the feature does not do, and a "riskiest assumptions" section — 3–5 beliefs that, if wrong, invalidate the whole approach. This section must be filled before approval.

What we take: The "riskiest assumptions" block forces the PM to articulate what they don't know before engineering invests. Any assumption risky enough to list is risky enough to validate in discovery before sprint entry.

Source: Stripe Product Team Blog

Linear — short specs

Linear uses a Notion spec linked from the GitHub issue: "Why we're doing this", "What success looks like", "What we're not doing", plus an engineering "technical concerns" block added during review. The spec is never longer than the issue — if it is, the feature is too big for one issue.

What we take: Length is self-enforcing scope discipline. A brief that needs more than one page probably describes two problems, not one. Split it before code.

Source: Linear Blog

Intercom — three-draft process

Intercom writes the brief in three drafts: draft 1 defines the problem only; draft 2 adds the success metric; draft 3 adds scope after a 30-minute feasibility session with engineering. No solutions appear in the brief at any stage.

What we take: Separating problem / metric / scope into stages prevents premature solutioning. When the problem and the solution are written in the same paragraph, the solution crowds out better alternatives.

Source: Intercom Engineering Blog

Amazon — narrative + FAQ

Amazon's "working backwards" PRD includes an internal FAQ — anticipated questions from customers, leadership, support, legal — answered in writing before the brief is approved. Reviewers must read the FAQ as carefully as the narrative.

What we take: The FAQ section forces the PM to think through edge cases that a happy-path narrative skips. Pair it with explicit out-of-scope; the FAQ catches "what about X?" questions a stakeholder will ask anyway, but proactively.

Source: Amazon — Working backwards

Across teams the pattern is the same: short (≤ 3 pages), problem-first (not solution-first), explicit out-of-scope, riskiest assumptions named, and an engineering-lead approval as the gate. None of these are heavy; skipping any one of them is where briefs become decoration.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Problem statement

One paragraph: who has the pain, what it is, what evidence proves it's real and large enough.

Parent job + opportunity

Links to the JTBD job and OST opportunity this brief serves. No orphan briefs.

Success metric (single)

One outcome with number + deadline. "Ship X" is not a metric.

NSM impact hypothesis

Which NSM input is this expected to move, and by how much? Required field.

Acceptance criteria (3–8)

G/W/T form, customer-observable. "Given …, when …, then …".

Explicit out-of-scope

Related things we are NOT building. Defensible mid-sprint boundary.

Riskiest assumptions (3–5)

Bets that, if wrong, invalidate the approach. Each named, each with a planned test.

Open questions

Unresolved decisions with DRI + deadline. Open at sprint planning = risk flag.

Engineering lead approval

Signed before sprint entry. PM + eng-lead signatures mark shared commitment.

How it works in this project

Study App briefs live as markdown files in docs/briefs/<brief-id>.md, version-controlled, opened by PR. The template at docs/briefs/_template.md includes the 9 anatomy fields above with explicit prompts. Engineering-lead approval is recorded as a PR approval; no brief enters the delivery backlog without it.

Briefs are written during the dual-track discovery phase, not at sprint planning. By the time sprint planning happens, the brief is approved, the riskiest assumptions are tested (or scheduled for test), and the open questions are resolved or explicitly flagged as risks. The DoR check at sprint planning verifies the brief is signed; missing signature blocks sprint entry.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Outcome vs deliverable — the single biggest mistake

"Ship the dashboard" is a deliverable. "Increase daily-active users of the dashboard from 12% → 30% within 6 weeks of launch" is an outcome. Outcomes survive multiple solution paths (the team can discover a better dashboard mid-sprint); deliverables lock in the solution before discovery. The brief must name an outcome — and the outcome must be measurable. If you can't name the metric, you don't understand the problem yet.

Acceptance criteria in G/W/T form

"User can see their streak" is unfalsifiable — does any pixel count? G/W/T: "Given the user has ≥ 1 completed lesson in the last 24h, when they open the dashboard, then a streak count ≥ 1 is displayed in the header." This is testable, unambiguous, and (importantly) implementation-agnostic. Engineering can build it three ways; QA can verify all three the same way. AC in prose form silently let solutions drift.

Riskiest assumptions — Stripe's contribution

Every brief rests on assumptions. Naming the 3–5 riskiest makes them testable. Patterns:

  • Value bet — "users will actually want this once it exists" (testable: smoke test, fake door).
  • Feasibility bet — "we can build this in 2 weeks with current stack" (testable: engineering spike).
  • Viability bet — "this won't trigger legal / compliance issues" (testable: walkthrough with legal).
  • Adjacent-effect bet — "this won't regress retention / support load" (testable: A/B test).

The brief's role is to surface them; the discovery cycle's role is to test them; the sprint commits when (and only when) the riskiest is validated.

The explicit out-of-scope boundary

Scope creep eats sprints. The fix is structural: name the related things you're NOT building, explicitly. "We are not adding social-leaderboard mechanics in this brief; that's brief B-051 next quarter." Now, mid-sprint, when a stakeholder requests leaderboards, the brief is the answer: "see line 27 — out of scope; let's open B-051 for that". The boundary is defensible because it's pre-agreed.

One brief, one problem

A brief covering two problems is two briefs. The test: can you describe the success of one half without referencing the other? If yes, split. If no, the work is genuinely one feature, but the brief shouldn't be twice as long — split a multi-month effort into multiple briefs that build on each other.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • 2–4 hours per brief. Real briefs take effort. Squashing it into a 30-minute write-up produces brief-shaped artefacts that don't actually constrain the sprint.
  • Engineering review tax. Eng-lead approval is real time, not rubber-stamp. Bake it into the engineering schedule; without it, briefs queue.
  • Brief inflation. Briefs creep toward 5+ pages over time. Mandate ≤ 3 pages; the constraint is the discipline.
  • Discovery dependency. A brief is only as good as the discovery that fed it. If discovery is weak, briefs become aspirational documents disconnected from evidence. Pair brief-writing with continuous-discovery cadence.

Lifecycle

opportunity from OST → draft 1: problem only → draft 2: outcome metric → feasibility session w/ eng → draft 3: scope + assumptions → eng lead approval → sprint entry

Draft-staged writing is the Intercom pattern: problem, metric, scope written in three passes so solutions don't crowd out problem definition. Each draft is reviewed before adding the next layer. Engineering lead approval is the gate to sprint entry; missing approval = no sprint commit.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Brief written after sprint starts PM pressured to start building. No sprint commit without an approved brief. 2–4 hrs of brief saves 2–4 days of mid-sprint ambiguity.
Out-of-scope is empty PM doesn't want to close options. Ask the team "what related things might someone expect to be in this?". Anything mentioned becomes an out-of-scope candidate.
Success metric is a delivery milestone "Feature shipped" is easier than measuring behaviour change. Delivery ≠ success. The metric must describe a customer behaviour change or business result that happens after shipping.
Riskiest assumptions missing PM is confident in the approach. If you can't name 3–5 risky assumptions, you haven't examined the approach. Name them, then design the cheapest test for the riskiest.
Solutions in the brief PM has the implementation in mind. Brief defines the problem and success bar; sprint defines the solution. Pre-committing solutions in the brief erases engineering's judgment.
5+ pages Brief grew to cover edge cases. ≤ 3 pages, hard cap. Beyond that, it's a spec, not a brief. Split into multiple briefs or move detail into an appendix the team doesn't have to read.

Worked example

Brief · markdown skeleton

BR-042 — Goal-based dashboard for self-motivated learners

# BR-042 · Goal-based dashboard

parent job:      JTBD-1 (build daily learning habit with visible progress)
parent opp:      OST · "lapsed users lose the why"
brief author:    @ivan
eng lead:        @oleg
status:          APPROVED 2026-05-19  ·  enters Sprint 25

## Problem
40% of lapsed users (week 2–4 dropouts) cite "didn't see the point" as the top
reason for leaving (200-user survey, 2026-W14). Current dashboard shows a
"next lesson" queue — a task-list framing — without surfacing why the user
chose to learn this topic. Switch interviews (INT-04, 07, 12) show users want
"a sense I'm building toward something", not "what's next on the list".

## Outcome (single metric)
Lift week-2 retention of new signups from 58% → 65% (committed) / 70% (aspirational)
measured 6 weeks after launch on the cohort exposed to v2 dashboard.

## NSM impact hypothesis
Moves NSM input I3 (weekly_return) by +3 pp on the exposed cohort.
Counter-metric: per-lesson CSAT (must hold within ±0.3 of baseline).

## Scope — IN (3–8 G/W/T acceptance criteria)
AC-1  Given user has a self-set learning goal, when they open the dashboard,
       then a "what you're building" header is visible above the lesson queue.
AC-2  Given user has completed ≥ 3 lessons, when they open the dashboard,
       then a visual skill-map shows their progress through the goal.
AC-3  Given user has no self-set goal, when they open the dashboard,
       then a 2-question goal-setting prompt is shown before lessons.
AC-4  Given user already had v1 dashboard, when they upgrade,
       then no learning history is lost; existing streaks display unchanged.

## Scope — OUT (explicit)
✗ Multi-goal dashboards (one goal per user in v1 — multi-goal is B-058 next Q)
✗ Social goal-sharing (next quarter brief)
✗ Goal-suggestion ML model (heuristic-only in v1)
✗ Mobile app — v1 is web-only; mobile is BR-049

## Riskiest assumptions (3–5)
A1 [VALUE]       Users prefer goal-map framing over next-lesson queue
                  test: prototype usability with 6 users (DONE — 5/6 prefer A1, see SR-022)
A2 [VALUE]       Goal-prompt for users without goal won't increase abandonment
                  test: smoke test on 5% safety ramp (PLANNED Sprint 25 W1)
A3 [FEASIBILITY] Skill-map rendering at p95 < 200 ms with current data model
                  test: engineering spike (DONE — feasible, see SPIKE-018)
A4 [VIABILITY]   No privacy concerns surfacing user-set goals back to them
                  test: legal walkthrough (DONE — clear, no GDPR concerns)

## Open questions
Q1  What's the right number of skill nodes to surface? (DRI: @maria · by Sprint 25 W1)
Q2  Should goal text be editable post-set or locked? (DRI: @ivan · by Sprint 25 W1)

Outcome. The brief took ~3 hours to write across 2 draft cycles. The riskiest-assumption section is where the value emerged: A2 (goal-prompt won't increase abandonment) hadn't been considered until the brief forced it; the team scheduled a smoke test for the first sprint week instead of building it blind. The explicit out-of-scope list reduced mid-sprint scope creep — when a stakeholder asked about multi-goal support during Sprint 25 standup, the PM pointed at the brief and opened B-058 for next quarter. The ship landed with week-2 retention at 64% (committed level achieved, aspirational missed by 1 pp).
