Impact mapping — deliverables tied to business goals

A four-level mind map (Why → Who → How → What) that makes the business goal visible at every step — so the team can drop scope without dropping value.

TL;DR

Impact mapping (Gojko Adzic, 2012) structures planning as a four-level mind map: Why (one measurable business goal), Who (actors who can cause or prevent it), How (observable behaviour changes those actors must make), What (deliverables that enable the changes). Every What traces back through How → Who → Why. Items that can't be traced are not in scope.

What it is

Impact mapping was developed by Gojko Adzic and published in his 2012 book. It is a visual planning technique that structures scope decisions as a four-level mind map:

  1. Why — the single measurable business goal. Not "launch feature X" but "increase trial-to-paid conversion by 15% by Q3 2026".
  2. Who — the actors who can help achieve or hinder that goal. Includes customers, internal users, partner systems, and sometimes adversaries (churned users, competitors' customers).
  3. How — the behaviour changes those actors need to make. Not "use the new dashboard" but "check their usage data weekly instead of monthly".
  4. What — the deliverables (features, content, integrations, process changes) that enable those behaviour changes.

The map is built collaboratively in a workshop with PM, engineering, design, and business stakeholders. Every What must trace back through How → Who → Why; items that can't trace are not in scope.

Why we need it

Most feature roadmaps are lists with no connection to business outcomes. When priorities shift or budget is cut, there's no principled way to decide what to drop — the decision becomes political. Impact mapping makes the connection between every feature and a specific business goal explicit and visual. When budget is cut, the team looks at the map and asks: which How branches are highest-impact? Which What items can we drop without eliminating an entire branch?

The technique also surfaces alignment failures early. When the PM says the goal is "double sign-ups" and the engineering lead lists "improve admin reporting" as a What, the disconnect is immediately visible. In a flat feature list, that misalignment only surfaces at sprint review.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Gojko Adzic (originator)

Adzic developed impact mapping while consulting on government and financial-services projects where scope kept expanding without clear connection to outcomes. His key finding: teams that built the Why level first — with a measurable, time-bound goal — were able to cut 40–60% of planned scope without reducing business impact, because most features simply weren't connected to the primary goal.

What we take: The Why level must be a single, measurable, time-bound goal — a number and a deadline. "Improve the product" is not a Why. "Reduce enterprise churn from 12% to 8% by Q4" is a Why.

Source: ImpactMapping.org — Gojko Adzic

BBC

BBC digital squads use impact mapping in quarterly planning around a shared audience Why expressed as "minutes of content consumed per week per active user". Each squad builds its own map against that shared goal before sprint planning.

What we take: A shared company-level Why means different squads can build independent impact maps that are still aligned. The north-star metric is the Why that every squad's map shares.

Source: BBC Internet Blog

Spotify — pairing with OKRs

Spotify squads run impact-mapping workshops at the start of each OKR cycle. The squad's Key Result becomes the Why. The squad then identifies which listener segments (Who) need to change behaviour (How) to produce that KR, and only then lists the product changes (What). The map is posted next to the OKR card.

What we take: Impact mapping + OKRs is the most effective combination: OKR provides the Why and the metric; the map provides the causal chain from feature to outcome. Together they close the accountability loop.

Source: Spotify Engineering Blog

ING — measurable deliverables

ING's "Bizdevops" model pairs each What in the map with a measurable shipping criterion that connects back to the Why. A What is not "done" when it ships — it's "done" when its Why-side metric moves measurably (or doesn't, in which case the branch is killed).

What we take: Don't stop the map at "feature shipped". Add a post-ship measurement step that ties back to Why. A What that ships but doesn't move the Why was wrong — and that learning is the value of the map.

Source: ING — Agile tribes and squads

Across teams the pattern is the same: a single measurable Why, behaviours (not features) at the How level, the map built collaboratively (not by the PM alone), and post-ship measurement closing the loop. Without those four, a map is a brainstorm; with them, it's a tool for cutting scope honestly.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Why — one measurable goal

A number and a deadline. One Why per map; if you have two goals, build two maps.

Who — all actors

Customers, internal users, partner systems, even adversaries. Including negative actors (churned, competitor users) prevents blind spots.

How — observable behaviour

"Checks usage weekly" not "uses dashboard". If it doesn't describe a behaviour, it's a What in disguise.

What — deliverables

Features, content, integrations, process changes. Only What items with a path to Why are in scope.

Priority slices

Horizontal slices across the map define release scopes. MVP = the thinnest slice that produces measurable Why movement.

Post-ship measurement

Each What is paired with the metric on Why side that it should move. "Done" = shipped + measured + branch confirmed or killed.

Collaborative workshop

PM + engineering + design + business build the map together. PM-only maps miss the Who and How insights non-PM roles bring.

How it works in this project

Study App builds one impact map per quarter, anchored to the quarterly OKR's Key Result as the Why. The map lives in Miro for the workshop and exports to a markdown file in docs/planning/impact-maps/YYYY-Q.md as the durable record. The workshop is 2 hours, attended by PM, engineering lead, designer, and growth lead.

Every What item is tagged with its parent How and Who in the markdown file. Pre-launch, the PM pairs each What with the specific metric on Why side it should move (e.g., signup-funnel step-conversion, week-2 retention). Post-launch, the metric is checked at D+30; if a What didn't move its predicted metric, the branch is killed at the next planning round — the learning is logged, not the feature.

A short ADR will lock the workshop cadence (quarterly), the Why-source rule (must be a current OKR KR), and the post-ship measurement rule.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Behaviour vs feature — the load-bearing distinction

The single most common mistake is writing the How level as features instead of behaviours. "Use the new dashboard" is a feature (a What with a Wh-question stuck on the front). "Check usage data weekly" is a behaviour — observable, attributable to an actor, independent of the deliverable. The discipline: every How must answer "what is the actor doing differently?" — without naming any product feature. If you can't articulate the How without mentioning a feature, the goal isn't well-defined enough.

Negative actors and adversarial Whos

Most maps only include positive actors (customers who'll buy). Adzic's original technique deliberately includes adversaries: churned users (who can come back and re-leave), competitor customers (who can switch), internal blockers (sales reps who undermine new features they don't understand). A map without negative Whos misses the prevention side: actions that stop the Why from being achieved are often higher-leverage than actions that promote it.

The "what does shipping mean" question

In a feature-list world, "done" means shipped. In an impact map, "done" means the What's behaviour change happened — the metric moved measurably. If the What ships but the metric doesn't move, the branch's hypothesis was wrong: either the How is incorrect (the behaviour change didn't happen) or the assumed link between behaviour and Why is incorrect. Both are valuable learnings; both should be logged. Without post-ship measurement, the map is brainstorm decoration.

Cutting scope with the map — the principled drop

When budget shrinks, the standard reaction is to drop "less important" features by feel. With the map, the team drops in order: (1) entire What items whose parent How has another What already shipping (redundant deliverables); (2) entire How branches that target lower-impact Who segments (smaller audience); (3) finally, entire Who segments if the Why permits (focus). Three rules, three drop steps — political pressure has less surface area to land on.

Impact mapping vs OST (Opportunity Solution Tree)

The two techniques look similar (both are trees) but answer different questions. Impact mapping starts from a business goal and decomposes downward; OST starts from a customer outcome and discovers opportunities upward. In practice they complement: impact maps for top-down quarterly planning anchored to OKRs; OSTs for bottom-up discovery anchored to interview insights. Some teams collapse them into one artefact, which usually means neither tool is being used rigorously.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Workshop time. A real impact map takes 2–4 hours with all stakeholders. The first map per team takes longer (5–8 h) because the team is also learning the technique.
  • Stakeholder coordination. The map needs business stakeholders (not just PM + eng + design). Booking calendars across functions is harder than the map itself; without business in the room the Why is a guess.
  • Post-ship discipline. Closing the loop requires the team to face wrong predictions. Cultures that punish failed bets quietly stop measuring; cultures that celebrate learnings continue.
  • Map staleness. A 6-month-old map describes a Why that's no longer current. Rebuild quarterly; older maps are archived, not consulted.

Lifecycle

name the Why (from OKR) → workshop: Who + How + What → cut untraceable Whats → slice MVP → pair each What with a metric → ship + measure D+30 → kill / iterate / scale per branch

The first cycle (quarter zero) is the longest — naming a sharp Why is hard and the team will iterate the wording. Subsequent quarters reuse the structure; only the Why and the lower levels refresh. The post-ship measurement gate prevents the team from drifting into "build everything on the map" — branches are killed openly when their hypothesis fails.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Multiple Why goals on one map Stakeholders each want their goal represented. Build a separate map per goal. A map with two Whys has no causal discipline — features can't trace to a single outcome.
How level lists features, not behaviours Teams default to thinking in deliverables. Rewrite each How as observable actor behaviour: "user checks X weekly". If it can't be written without naming a feature, the parent Why is too vague.
Map built by PM alone before the workshop PM wants to come prepared. Build it collaboratively in the room. Engineering and business know Whos and Hows the PM doesn't — pre-built maps miss those nodes silently.
No negative actors / adversaries The team focuses on the positive case. Include churned users, competitor users, internal blockers. Stopping bad behaviour is often higher-leverage than promoting good behaviour.
No post-ship measurement "Done" means shipped, not measured. Pair each What with its Why-side metric. D+30 measurement decides: scale, iterate, or kill the branch. Otherwise the map is decoration.
Map kept after the quarter ends "It's still useful as reference." Archive it. Build a new one against the new quarter's Why. Stale maps confidently mislead the team because the goal underneath has shifted.

Worked example

Impact map · markdown export

Study App 2026-Q3 — "Double organic sign-ups"

WHY  (one OKR Key Result · measurable · time-bound)
     Double weekly organic sign-ups · baseline 320/wk · target 640/wk · by 2026-09-30

├── WHO  New visitor (organic search)
│   ├── HOW  reach "first lesson complete" within 24 h of signup
│   │   ├── WHAT  one-step social login (Apple, Google)
│   │   │         metric: signup → first-lesson 24h conversion · floor +10 pp
│   │   ├── WHAT  remove email verification gate (move to first paid action)
│   │   │         metric: signup-complete rate · floor +6 pp
│   │   └── WHAT  pre-fill name+language from social profile
│   │             metric: signup form-completion time · floor −30%
│   ├── HOW  trust social proof on the landing page
│   │   ├── WHAT  3 rotating testimonials above the form
│   │   │         metric: landing → signup-start step ✓ · floor +4 pp
│   │   └── WHAT  visible "10k learners" counter
│   │             metric: landing scroll-depth past form · floor +5 pp
│   └── HOW  return next day to complete signup if abandoned
│       └── WHAT  email reminder at +24 h with progress bar
│                 metric: signup completion within 48 h · floor +8 pp

├── WHO  Referral user (existing customer's friend)
│   └── HOW  receive + click invite within 7 days
│       ├── WHAT  2-click share link (copy + share-to-X)
│       │         metric: referral-link → signup-start · floor 30%
│       └── WHAT  reward both sides ($5 credit each on first paid lesson)
│                 metric: referral → first-paid conversion · floor 20%

└── WHO  Lapsed visitor (churned in the last 90 d)
    └── HOW  return after a content update
        └── WHAT  reactivation email at 30 / 60 / 90 d with new content
                  metric: lapsed → signup reactivation · floor 5%

Cut from this map (untraceable to Why):
  ✗ admin analytics dashboard       — no traceable How
  ✗ teacher portal v2 polish        — wrong Who
  ✗ payment-method UI refresh       — no link to sign-ups (revenue goal, different map)

Priority slice (MVP, ships in first 4 weeks):
  • one-step social login           (Who 1, How 1)
  • testimonials on form            (Who 1, How 2)
  • 2-click referral link           (Who 2, How 1)

D+30 review gate per What:
  metric moved → scale; metric flat → iterate the How; metric down → kill the branch.

Outcome. The workshop produced 12 candidate Whats; 3 were cut at the map (no traceable path to Why) and another 3 were deferred (lower-impact Who segments). The team shipped the priority slice (3 items) in 4 weeks and measured at D+30: social login moved signup→first-lesson 24h conversion by +14 pp (above floor → scaled to A/B test); testimonials moved landing→signup-start by +1 pp (below floor → iterated wording, A/B again); referral link moved referral→signup by 8% (below 30% floor → killed the branch, learning logged). Without the map, the team would have shipped all 12, attributed any movement to "the package", and had no honest way to kill the referral idea.
