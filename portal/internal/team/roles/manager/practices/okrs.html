OKRs — aligned goal-setting

One qualitative objective, three to five measurable key results, scored weekly, retrospected quarterly.

TL;DR

OKRs (Andy Grove at Intel, John Doerr at Google) separate direction from measurement. An Objective is qualitative and inspiring; Key Results are measurable outcomes (not tasks). Scored 0.0–1.0 weekly; 0.7 at quarter end is success, consistent 1.0 means the goals were too easy. Three rules: outcomes-not-outputs, public visibility, decoupled-from-bonus.

What it is

OKRs originated at Intel under Andy Grove and were popularised at Google by John Doerr. They separate direction (Objective) from measurement (Key Results).

An Objective is qualitative, inspiring, achievable within the quarter. It answers "where do we want to go?". Examples: "Make onboarding feel effortless"; "Become the most-trusted learning product for self-motivated adults".

Key Results are quantitative, time-bound outcomes that define what success looks like. They are not tasks or deliverables: "Reduce time-to-first-value from 12 min to 4 min", "Lift week-4 retention from 28% to 40%". The discipline is: if there's no number, it's not a KR; if it's a shipping milestone, it's not a KR either.

KRs are scored 0.0–1.0 weekly. 0.0 = no progress, 1.0 = fully achieved. 0.7 is success; consistently hitting 1.0 means the targets were too conservative.

Why we need it

Without a goal framework, teams optimise for output — shipped features — because output is visible on a sprint board. OKRs force the team to define success in terms of customer outcomes before any work begins, making output the means and outcome the end.

OKRs also solve the alignment problem in multi-team orgs. When every team's OKRs roll up to a company-level OKR, cross-team trade-offs become "which outcome is more strategically important?" (decidable) instead of "which team has more political weight?" (undecidable).

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Google — public visibility

Google has used OKRs since 1999. Every Googler can see every other Googler's OKRs. ~60% of OKRs are bottom-up (set by teams, not handed down). Company OKRs are leadership-set and stable; team OKRs can change quarterly.

What we take: Public OKRs create natural alignment without a heavy top-down process. When adjacent teams can see what each is optimising for, conflicts surface in week 1, not at launch.

Source: Google re:Work — OKRs guide

LinkedIn — committed vs aspirational

LinkedIn pairs each KR with two targets: a committed (must-achieve) and an aspirational (stretch). Removes the sandbagging incentive: missing committed is a crisis, missing aspirational is expected. The team plans against committed; aspirational is the upside.

What we take: "0.7 = success" alone breeds sandbagging — teams set targets they're 70% sure to hit. Committed + aspirational makes the contract explicit and stops the gaming.

Source: What Matters — LinkedIn OKRs

Intel — weekly one-on-ones

Grove's original at Intel was simpler than modern variants: one Objective, three KRs, reviewed in weekly one-on-ones. Grove believed the Objective set direction but the weekly KR conversation was where management actually happened.

What we take: The weekly score check is not optional. An OKR set at quarter start and reviewed at quarter end is a reporting tool, not a management tool. Weekly scoring surfaces problems while there's still time to act.

Source: Grove — High Output Management

Spotify — squad OKRs ↔ tribe OKRs

Spotify cascades OKRs in two layers: tribe OKRs (multi-squad business unit) and squad OKRs. Squad OKRs ladder to tribe; tribe OKRs ladder to company. The bottom-up half of the contract: squads propose their own KRs that contribute to the tribe goal.

What we take: Cascading must be aligned, not derived. Squads propose how they'll contribute (bottom-up); leadership confirms it's enough to hit the tribe number (top-down). Pure derivation top-down makes squads feel like execution arms; pure bottom-up loses alignment.

Source: Spotify Engineering Blog

Across teams the pattern is the same: public visibility, weekly scoring, outcomes not outputs, decoupled from bonus. Quarterly-only scoring → reporting tool; bonus-coupled → sandbagging machine; private → coordination through gossip. Cut any one and the framework degrades.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Objective

Qualitative, inspiring, achievable this quarter. Passes the "makes people want to come to work" test.

Key Results (3–5)

Outcomes with numbers and deadlines. "Ship X" is an output; "X drives Y% of activations" is a KR.

Committed + aspirational targets

Two targets per KR — must-achieve and stretch. Solves the sandbagging problem.

Weekly score check-in

15 min, Monday. PM updates 0.0–1.0 per KR; surfaces problems while there's time.

Mid-quarter escalation

Week 6: if a KR is < 0.3, either pivot approach or mark at-risk and escalate. Don't let a doomed KR coast.

Public visibility

Every team can see every other team's OKRs. Alignment happens through reading, not meetings.

Decoupled from bonus

OKR scores never feed compensation directly. Money tied to OKRs = sandbagging guaranteed.

Quarter retrospective

Final score + written reflection: what drove the score, what to do differently, what we learned.

How it works in this project

Study App has one company-level OKR per quarter (set by leadership) and one squad-level OKR per squad (proposed bottom-up, confirmed for alignment). OKRs live in docs/okrs/YYYY-QN.md, version-controlled, public to all team members. The Monday Weekly Score meeting is a standing 15-minute slot where each PM updates their KR scores.

Every KR follows the LinkedIn committed/aspirational pattern: committed is the must-achieve floor; aspirational is the stretch. KRs map to NSM movement or input-metric movement (see north-star metric) — KRs that don't ladder to NSM need leadership approval. Bonuses and performance reviews never reference OKR scores directly; the loop stays clean.

A short ADR locks the cadence (weekly score, mid-quarter escalation, quarter retro), the rule that no KR can be a shipping milestone, and the decoupling-from-bonus principle.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Outputs vs outcomes — the load-bearing distinction

An output is something the team produces: "ship the new dashboard". An outcome is something that changes for the customer or business: "lift dashboard daily-active users by 30%". KRs must be outcomes. The test: can the team hit the KR without shipping anything? If yes, it's a true outcome (which means there could be multiple ways to hit it, and the team chooses the cheapest). If no, it's a disguised output — you've pre-committed to a solution before you've validated the problem. Outputs masquerading as KRs are the single most common OKR failure mode.

The 0.7 paradox and committed/aspirational fix

"0.7 = success" is meant to encourage stretch. In practice it breeds sandbagging: teams set targets they're 70% confident in, then hit them at 1.0, then explain why the next quarter's targets are even more conservative. Doerr's original intent was lost. The LinkedIn committed/aspirational fix is the cleanest patch: committed is the team's guarantee (high confidence, expected to hit 1.0); aspirational is the genuine stretch (genuinely uncertain, 0.7 is a win, 1.0 would be remarkable). The team plans against committed; aspirational is celebrated when achieved but not required.

Weekly scoring as management, not reporting

Grove's insight was simple: the weekly score check is where management actually happens. If you only score at quarter end, you find out you missed in week 12 — too late to do anything. Weekly scoring surfaces a slipping KR in week 3 or 4, when there's still time to pivot approach, escalate, or formally mark the KR at-risk. Without weekly cadence, OKRs become a quarterly performance review — interesting but not actionable.

Why decoupling from bonus is non-negotiable

When OKR scores feed bonuses, two things happen: (1) teams sandbag (set easy targets they'll hit), defeating the stretch intent; (2) teams lie about scores at the end (you can't fire someone for missing a KR that fed their bonus, so leadership "rounds up"). Both corrupt the data and erode trust. The solution is structural: OKR scores must never appear in compensation conversations. Performance reviews look at how the person worked, not what score their KR got. Companies that conflate the two destroy the OKR's diagnostic value within 2 cycles.

Cascading — aligned, not derived

Top-down cascading ("here's the company OKR, decompose it into your squad OKRs") feels efficient but makes squads feel like execution arms — and misses local knowledge about what's actually possible. Pure bottom-up loses alignment — every squad picks what they personally care about, total impact diffuses. The compromise: leadership sets company OKRs and the rough shape they need from each tribe/squad; squads propose their KRs in response, confirming both that the work is interesting AND that the math adds up. This conversation is the value, not the resulting document.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Setup overhead. A real OKR cycle is ~2 weeks of cross-functional negotiation at quarter start. Squashing it into a day produces OKRs nobody believes.
  • Discipline drag. Weekly scoring is 15 min × 13 weeks = 3.25 hrs/quarter per PM. Cheap, but it's the first thing dropped when things get busy — and dropping it is the death spiral.
  • Sandbagging vigilance. Even with committed/aspirational, teams drift toward easy commitments. Quarterly retros must explicitly inspect: "did we sandbag this KR?"
  • Coordination tax at scale. Public visibility helps small orgs; at 500+ people, reading everyone's OKRs is impractical. Group by org area, surface only adjacent OKRs.

Lifecycle

leadership Os → squad KR proposals → alignment review → committed + aspirational locked → weekly score → mid-quarter escalation → quarter retro

Two-week setup at quarter start: leadership drafts company Objectives, squads propose Key Results in response, alignment review confirms math adds up, OKRs are committed and locked. Weekly scoring runs through the quarter; mid-quarter (week 6) escalation surfaces at-risk KRs while there's still time. Quarter retro produces written reflection + carry-over to next quarter where relevant.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
KRs are tasks, not outcomes "Launch X" is easier to write than measuring it. Reject any KR without a number + deadline. "Launch X" → "X drives 20% of new activations by 2026-09-30".
Scored quarterly, not weekly Weekly check-ins feel like overhead. 15 min Monday is the minimum. Missing the rhythm = reporting tool, not management tool.
Too many OKRs Every initiative wants representation. Max 3 OKRs per team per quarter, max 5 KRs per Objective. If everything is a priority, nothing is.
KRs tied to bonus Compensation design demands measurable targets. Decouple. OKR scores never feed comp directly. Money attached to OKRs = sandbagging guaranteed within 2 cycles.
OKRs private to leadership Fear of accountability if a goal misses publicly. Public to the org. Alignment happens through reading, not coordination meetings. Missing a goal publicly is the point — it's where learning happens.

Study App 2026-Q3 — squad OKR (mid-quarter check)

OBJECTIVE  (qualitative · inspiring · achievable this quarter)
  Make Study App the daily 15-minute habit our learners can't skip.

KR 1  Lift weekly_active_days median from 2.8 → 4.0 days / 7
        committed:   3.4 / 7    (current week 6: 3.3)  score 0.78
        aspirational: 4.0 / 7
        owner: engagement squad · @maria
        linked to: NSM input I3 (weekly_return)

KR 2  Activate 50% of new signups (first-lesson within 24h)
        committed:   42%        (current week 6: 39%)  score 0.62
        aspirational: 50%
        owner: onboarding squad · @ivan
        linked to: NSM input I1 (activation_rate)

KR 3  Reduce week-2 lapse rate from 42% → 30%
        committed:   34%        (current week 6: 38%)  score 0.33  ← AT RISK
        aspirational: 30%
        owner: retention squad · @oleg
        linked to: NSM (retention) + counter-metric on CSAT
        escalation (week 6): streak-freeze experiment under-performed;
                              pivot to email re-engagement campaign,
                              reassess at week 10.

WEEKLY SCORE LOG
  W1  0.10 / 0.05 / 0.05   (baseline)
  W2  0.20 / 0.20 / 0.15
  W3  0.30 / 0.30 / 0.20
  W4  0.50 / 0.45 / 0.30
  W5  0.65 / 0.55 / 0.35
  W6  0.78 / 0.62 / 0.33  ← mid-quarter · KR3 at risk, escalated

RETRO QUESTIONS (for quarter end)
  • Did committed targets stretch us, or were they sandbagged?
  • Did aspirational targets even matter, or were they ignored?
  • Which input metrics actually predicted KR movement?
  • What carries to Q4?

Outcome. The committed/aspirational structure made the week-6 escalation honest: KR 3 hadn't slipped — it had revealed that the streak-freeze hypothesis was wrong, and the team could pivot to a different intervention with 6 weeks left in the quarter. Under a pure 0.7-is-success model, the team would have either quietly missed (and discovered the miss at quarter end) or padded the committed number from the start. The weekly score log also revealed something useful for next quarter: KR1 moved smoothly with consistent weekly gains, while KR3 was flat for 5 weeks — a pattern the team will watch for in Q4 planning ("if a KR is flat by week 4, escalate immediately rather than waiting for week 6").
