Now-next-later roadmap — outcome over timeline

Three columns, no dates in Later — communicate direction and priority without committing to timelines that almost always slip.

TL;DR

Now-next-later (Janna Bastow / ProdPad) replaces Gantt-style date roadmaps with three columns: Now (in progress, scoped, assigned), Next (validated, ready to start), Later (directional, no dates). Items are outcomes, not features. Honest about uncertainty further out; clear about priority right now.

What it is

The now-next-later roadmap is a three-column format that replaces date-driven timelines with proximity-based prioritisation. Created by Janna Bastow at ProdPad, it solves the central tension of product roadmaps: stakeholders want commitments, but engineering realities make precise date commitments almost always wrong.

  • Now — work actively in progress. Fully validated, scoped, assigned. Team is accountable to completing it this sprint/cycle.
  • Next — validated and ready to start as soon as Now items complete. Brief written, rough effort estimate, ordered by RICE. No date commitments; ordering reflects priority.
  • Later — directional: themes and outcomes the team expects to address eventually, no timeline or scope. May be invalidated, re-ordered, or removed as new customer data arrives. Honest and explicit.

Why we need it

Date-based roadmaps destroy trust systematically. The PM sets dates from current estimates; requirements shift, unknowns surface, dates slip. After the second slip, stakeholders stop believing; after the third, they build parallel internal timelines. Now-next-later eliminates slips by eliminating the precision that creates them.

The format also reshapes the roadmap conversation. Instead of "when will we get X?" the discussion becomes "should X be in Next or Later, and what would need to be true for it to move into Now?" — a productive question with concrete answers grounded in evidence and capacity.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

ProdPad (inventors)

ProdPad runs their own product on a public now-next-later roadmap, updated monthly, with each item linked to the customer problem that motivates it. Customers see their requests acknowledged in Later, which reduces support escalations and increases product trust without requiring date commitments.

What we take: A public now-next-later roadmap is a customer relationship tool. Customers who see their request in Later stop hammering support for status updates; the format buys patience without requiring promises.

Source: ProdPad — origin story

Basecamp — Shape Up

Basecamp commits only within a six-week cycle; everything beyond is directional. Their "Shape Up" methodology makes the Now column explicit (the active six-week cycle) while keeping a loose backlog of shaped ideas that is functionally Later.

What we take: The commitment horizon is the most important decision in roadmap format. Now should contain only work that will realistically complete in the current cycle. Anything further out belongs in Next or Later — regardless of stakeholder pressure.

Source: Shape Up — Basecamp

Roman Pichler — goal roadmap

Pichler's "GO Product Roadmap" is a goal-oriented variant: each column contains a goal (outcome) per time horizon, not a feature list. Forces the PM to define what success looks like in each horizon before listing what gets built.

What we take: Describing roadmap items as outcomes ("reduce churn in enterprise segment") rather than features ("add admin dashboard") keeps the conversation on why before what. Most important structural choice in the format.

Source: Roman Pichler — goal roadmap

Buffer — public roadmap

Buffer publishes a public now-next-later board on their website. They update it weekly, mark items "validated" when discovery passes, and keep a separate "considered + cut" archive so customers can see what was decided against (and why).

What we take: The "cut" archive is as valuable as the live board — it shows customers and the team that priorities are actually being made, not just deferred. Without it, Later silently accumulates everything anyone ever asked for.

Source: Buffer — Transparency

The pattern across teams: outcomes not features, strict caps on Now (≤3) and Next (≤5), evidence link on every item, public visibility, and a cut archive so Later doesn't bloat into a wishlist. Without those, the format is just a kanban with no dates.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Now column (≤3 items)

Scoped, brief written, AC, named DRI. Updated weekly.

Next column (≤5 items)

Brief and rough effort estimate; ordered by RICE.

Later column (themes)

Outcome-themed, evidence-linked. No dates. Reviewed monthly.

Outcome framing

"Reduce activation drop-off" — not "redesign signup form". Outcomes survive solution changes.

Customer evidence link

Each item links to OST opportunity, insight card, or interview ID that motivates it.

Cut archive

Items considered and decided-against, with reason. Prevents Later from becoming a wishlist.

Public visibility

Visible to all stakeholders (sales, support, customers). The format only works when read; private roadmaps default to date-based.

How it works in this project

Study App's roadmap lives at docs/roadmap.md in the repo and is mirrored to a Notion view for stakeholder readability. Now: max 3 items, scoped at sprint planning, DRI named. Next: max 5 items, ordered by RICE, brief in docs/briefs/. Later: themed outcomes linked to OST opportunities or interview synthesis IDs.

Items move right-to-left only when prerequisites are met (Next → Now requires capacity; Later → Next requires discovery validation + brief). Items never jump from Later straight to Now — that's a sign of stakeholder pressure overriding evidence. The cut archive is docs/roadmap-cuts.md, appended-only with date + reason.

The roadmap is opened at the start of every monthly product review and the quarterly planning. A short ADR locks the column caps, the outcome-framing rule, and the rule that all items must link to customer evidence before entering Next.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Why no dates in Later — the cost of false precision

Stakeholders want dates because dates feel like commitment. But in product, a date attached to an item three quarters out is a number generated from intuition, multiplied by optimism, divided by political pressure — and consistently wrong. Each missed date erodes trust geometrically (one miss is forgiven; two start a pattern; three teach stakeholders to ignore the roadmap entirely). The discipline of "no dates in Later" is a precommitment against the temptation to produce false precision; it trades short-term political comfort for long-term credibility.

The strict caps on Now and Next

Without caps, Now grows to "everything currently active" (10+ items, nothing finishing) and Next grows to "everything we'd like to start" (20+ items, no real ordering). Caps force priority. Max 3 in Now means a fourth item requires explicitly bumping one out; max 5 in Next means the sixth requires a decision. The caps are the format's load-bearing constraint; without them it's a wishlist with three columns.

Outcomes vs features — the discipline

Compare: "Add CSV export" (feature) vs "Let power users get their data out without engineering help" (outcome). The outcome survives multiple solution paths (CSV export, Zapier integration, API endpoint, scheduled email); the feature pre-commits to one. The outcome lets the team discover better solutions during build; the feature locks the solution before discovery. Every item on the board should answer "what changes for the customer when this is done?". If the answer is "we shipped X", it's a feature in disguise.

The flow direction — right-to-left only

Items should flow Later → Next → Now over time. They should never flow the other way (Later items jumping to Now under stakeholder pressure) and should rarely move backwards (Next item demoted to Later) unless the underlying customer evidence has changed. A well-functioning board has a steady right-to-left flow; a broken board has chaotic motion in both directions, signalling that the order is opinion-driven, not evidence-driven.

Public vs internal — and the trade-off

The strongest version is public — every stakeholder, including customers, can read the current state. This forces precision (you can't be vague to internal politics if customers will see it) and reduces "where is X?" support load. The trade-off is competitive disclosure: published roadmaps signal strategy to competitors. Most products underestimate the competitive risk and overestimate it: customer trust gained typically outweighs competitive intelligence given. Default to public unless there's a specific reason not to.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Stakeholder education. First quarter after switching, stakeholders will repeatedly ask for dates and feel destabilised by their absence. Plan explicit education: a 30-min "how to read the roadmap" session with each function.
  • Date-pressure from leadership / sales. Sales needs commit-able dates to close enterprise deals. The compromise: per-deal dates negotiated separately (with engineering input, time-boxed), not roadmap-published. Don't backflow sales dates into Later.
  • Item-volume drift. Later silently bloats to 30+ items as everyone adds their favourite. Mandatory monthly pruning + the cut archive are the discipline; without them the format degenerates into a wishlist.
  • No timeline visibility. Internal teams (finance, ops, marketing) need rough timelines for their own planning. The compromise: a separate "expected horizon" doc that's clearly marked as planning-only, not commitment.

Lifecycle

opportunity (OST + interviews) → themed Later item → discovery validation → brief written → move to Next (RICE-ordered) → capacity opens → move to Now (DRI assigned) → ship · measure · archive

An item enters as a Later theme from OST/discovery. It earns Next via discovery validation + brief. It earns Now when capacity opens (an item ships out of Now) and a DRI is assigned. After shipping, the item is archived with its outcome metric (did it move the NSM input?). Items that fail validation in discovery return to Later or get cut to the archive.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Dates appearing in Later Stakeholders pressure for committed timelines. Explain that dates in Later are false precision that destroys trust on the second miss. Offer "we'll revisit at the next quarterly review" as the commitment.
Now column with 15 items Everything feels urgent; nothing moves out. Cap Now at 3. Items not in this sprint move to Next. The cap is the load-bearing constraint of the format.
Items described as features Easier to write "Add search bar" than the outcome. Ban feature descriptions. Every item answers "what changes for the customer?". PM rejects feature-form items at review.
Later as wishlist (30+ items) Everyone adds favourites; nothing gets cut. Monthly pruning, cut-archive mandatory. Items not validated in 2 quarters get cut with reason. Default position is cut, not keep.
Items jumping Later → Now Stakeholder pressure or sales deal. Investigate every such jump as a process exception. Items must pass discovery + brief before Next; skipping signals discovery is broken, not that the roadmap is.
Private roadmap only Fear of competitive disclosure. Default to public; the customer-trust gain typically outweighs the competitive intel given. Reserve private for unreleased product directions, not the working roadmap.

Outcome. The board ran cleanly for 4 quarters: Now items completed on schedule (no slips because nothing was over-committed); Next items had briefs ready when Now slots opened; Later items either earned promotion through discovery or were cut to the archive with reasoned closure. The cut archive in particular reduced support escalations: when customers asked "where is gamified leaderboards?", support could point at the public cut entry showing why — and the conversation became "we have other things that might address your underlying motivation problem" rather than "we'll get to it eventually".
