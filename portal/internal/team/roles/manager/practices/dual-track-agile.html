Dual-track agile — discovery and delivery in parallel

Engineers build pre-validated work while PMs stay one sprint ahead discovering what to build next. Two tracks, one team, zero unvalidated stories in the sprint.

TL;DR

Dual-track agile (Marty Cagan, Jeff Patton) runs two concurrent tracks: a Discovery track where PM and design validate the four big risks (value, usability, feasibility, viability) before engineering begins, and a Delivery track where engineers build only what cleared the gate. The rule is strict: no unvalidated work enters the delivery sprint. Engineers are never idle waiting on requirements; PMs never throw unvalidated work over the wall.

What it is

Dual-track agile was articulated by Marty Cagan (SVPG) and Jeff Patton as a structured way to eliminate the two most expensive wastes in product development: engineers waiting on requirements and engineers building unvalidated features. Both wastes exist because in single-track teams, discovery and delivery compete for the same calendar.

The Discovery track is owned by PM and design. It runs continuously — before, during, and after delivery sprints. Discovery activities include customer interviews, prototyping, usability tests, assumption tests, smoke tests, fake-door tests, and writing the brief plus acceptance criteria. Work exits discovery only when the team can score the four big risks (value, usability, feasibility, business viability) as mitigated to ≥70% confidence.

The Delivery track is owned by engineering. It receives only work that has cleared the discovery gate. Engineering's job is to build what was validated, not to validate while building. This keeps the delivery track predictable and gives engineers the full why before they pick up the story.

Why we need it

In a single-track process, discovery and delivery compete for the same team's time. When delivery pressure peaks (a release approaches), discovery quietly stops. When discovery runs ahead (project kickoff), engineers idle on cleanup work. Dual-track resolves both by separating the two activities into independent tracks running in parallel — different artefacts, different cadence, same team.

The deeper benefit is cultural: engineers become collaborators in discovery (joining one user research session per month, contributing to feasibility scoring) without being blocked by it. They see the evidence behind every story before they touch it. Mid-sprint surprises, scope changes, and "wait, why are we building this?" conversations drop dramatically.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

SVPG — Marty Cagan

Cagan's framework names four big risks that discovery must address before delivery: value (will customers buy / use it?), usability (can they use it?), feasibility (can we build it in the time we have?), and business viability (does it work for the business — legal, finance, support, partners?). All four must be scored before the gate.

What we take: Without the four-risk taxonomy, discovery defaults to usability testing alone — and feasibility blows up mid-sprint. Score all four explicitly per item, even if one of them is trivially "yes".

Source: SVPG — Dual-track agile

Spotify

Spotify squads embed a designer who maintains a separate discovery backlog from the delivery board. The designer runs 2–3 usability sessions per sprint while the squad ships the previous sprint's validated work. The PM reviews both backlogs weekly; gate clearances move items from one backlog to the other.

What we take: Two physical backlogs — not one with a "discovery" label — protect discovery from delivery pressure. With a single board, discovery items are always second-priority and quietly starve.

Source: Spotify Engineering Blog

Intercom

Intercom rotates one engineer into the discovery track each sprint — the "engineer in discovery". The role pairs with the PM and designer on prototypes, feasibility scoring, and customer interviews. After one sprint they rotate back. Every engineer cycles through within ~6 sprints.

What we take: Engineer rotation is the cultural unlock. Engineers who have sat in a customer interview build differently than engineers who only see Jira tickets — and they make better feasibility calls in the gate.

Source: Intercom Blog

Basecamp / Shape Up

Shape Up calls discovery "shaping" — a longer, deliberately rough phase where senior PMs and designers shape a pitch at the right level of detail before any 6-week cycle starts. Unshaped work cannot enter a cycle. Cycles are 6 weeks of pure delivery on shaped pitches with a 2-week "cool-down" for shaping the next batch.

What we take: The exact ratio of discovery to delivery is less important than the principle: unshaped work cannot enter a build cycle. Whether that's a 1-sprint offset (Spotify) or a dedicated 2-week shaping phase (Basecamp), the gate is the load-bearing wall.

Source: Basecamp — Shape Up

Across teams the pattern converges: discovery and delivery are separate workflows with separate artefacts, the gate is non-negotiable, and engineers participate in discovery (not just receive its output). The detail varies — 1-sprint offset, 2-week cool-down, embedded designer — but those three rules are constants.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Discovery gate criteria

Written checklist: 4 SVPG risks scored, customer tested, confidence ≥70%, brief complete, AC written in G/W/T form. Signed by PM + Eng Lead + Designer.

Discovery backlog

Separate artefact from the delivery board. PM-owned. Contains items under investigation — not committed work.

Delivery backlog

Contains only items that cleared the discovery gate. Engineering pulls from here at sprint planning, never from "good ideas" outside the backlog.

Discovery buffer (≥2 sprints)

The delivery backlog always holds ≥2 sprints' worth of validated work. Below that threshold is a PM-level emergency, not a delivery problem.

Discovery velocity

How many items clear the gate per sprint. Tracked publicly. If < 2/sprint sustained, delivery will starve in 4 weeks.

Engineer rotation

One engineer joins discovery each sprint as "engineer in discovery". Everyone cycles through within ~6 sprints; nobody opts out.

Failure register

Items killed in discovery are recorded with the risk that killed them. A team that never kills items isn't running discovery — it's stamping approval.

How it works in this project

Study App's discovery backlog lives in Notion under PM / Discovery / Backlog; the delivery backlog is the Jira sprint board. The PM and designer run discovery continuously — interviews on Thursdays (from the continuous-discovery cadence), prototyping in Figma, smoke tests via landing pages, fake-door tests inside the product. One engineer rotates into discovery each sprint and pairs on feasibility scoring.

The discovery gate is checked at the Friday gate-review meeting before sprint planning. Each item is scored on the four SVPG risks (value / usability / feasibility / viability), each on a 0–3 scale. An item clears the gate when all four risks are ≥ 2 (≥70% confidence). Items that fail are returned to the discovery backlog with the failing risk(s) noted; they are not re-scored until new evidence is gathered.

A short ADR will lock the gate criteria, the 0–3 scoring rubric, the buffer threshold (≥2 sprints), and the engineer-rotation cadence so the two tracks stay genuinely separate.

Deep dive — the theory you need

The four risks — and why scoring all four is the load-bearing rule

Without an explicit taxonomy, "we tested it" usually means "we ran a usability session and users liked the prototype". That misses three of the four risks. The four-risk scoring sheet:

RiskQuestionTypical evidence
ValueWill customers buy or use it? Will it move the metric?Smoke test, fake door, willingness-to-pay test, OST opportunity scoring.
UsabilityCan they figure out how to use it?Prototype usability sessions (5–7 users), task-completion rate, time-on-task.
FeasibilityCan we build it in the time we have, with the stack we have?Engineer-in-discovery spike, technical proof-of-concept, dependency map.
Business viabilityDoes it work for the business — legal, finance, support, partners, brand?Walkthrough with legal/CS/finance leads; checklist of known constraints (GDPR, App Store rules, partner API contracts).

The fourth risk (viability) is the one teams skip and the one that blows up at the worst time — three days before launch, when legal flags a compliance issue or App Store rejects the build. Score all four, even when one is trivially "yes".

Confidence — what ≥70% actually means

"70% confidence" is not a precise number — it's a calibration. A team that's right 7 out of 10 times when they say "70% confident" is well-calibrated. Most teams that haven't tracked this are over-confident: their 70% is reality's 40%. Two-year-old programmes tend to drift to 80–90% well-calibrated; new teams should expect to over-call wins for the first quarter and adjust. Track gate-clearance outcomes (ship/kill in delivery) against the discovery confidence to learn the bias.

The 1-sprint offset — not 2, not 0

Cagan's classic framing says "two sprints ahead". In practice a 1-sprint offset is what most teams achieve and what's easier to defend. Less than that and discovery pressure leaks into delivery; more than that and discovery output goes stale before delivery picks it up (the world moves; the validation expires). Treat 1 sprint as the floor and 2 as the ceiling.

Discovery techniques — the toolbox

Discovery is not "interviews" — it's a toolbox of techniques chosen per risk:

  • Smoke test / fake door — for value risk. A button or landing page for a feature that doesn't exist; measure click-through to gauge demand.
  • Concierge test — for value + usability. Deliver the outcome manually (PM-as-backend) before building automation. If nobody wants the outcome when delivered by hand, automation won't help.
  • Wizard-of-Oz — for feasibility on AI/algorithm-heavy features. Real UI + human in the loop; the user thinks they're using the system.
  • Prototype usability test — for usability. 5–7 users on a clickable Figma; classic Nielsen rule of saturation applies.
  • Engineering spike — for feasibility on a tricky technical area. Time-boxed, output is a written feasibility note + estimate, not code.

Each technique targets a specific risk. Running only one (usually prototype usability) leaves the other three unscored.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Two tracks need PM bandwidth. A single PM running two tracks simultaneously hits a ceiling at ~6 weeks. Designer + engineer rotation share the load; a PM without that support eventually drops one track.
  • Initial backlog cold-start. The first 4–6 weeks have low discovery throughput (the gate criteria are new, scoring is slow). Delivery may starve during the ramp; plan a "carry-over" of partially-discovered items from the prior process to bridge the gap.
  • Engineer rotation tax. One engineer in discovery is one engineer not delivering. The math: 1/6 of engineering capacity. The math pays back via fewer mid-sprint surprises, but the cost is visible immediately and the savings show up later.
  • The gate can become theatre. If failed items always get re-scored after a "quick chat", the gate is gone. Failure register + explicit re-scoring rule keep the gate honest.

Lifecycle

opportunity from OST → discovery backlog → 4 risks scored → prototype / test → gate review → delivery backlog · or · failure register → sprint commit → ship

An opportunity surfaces from continuous discovery (interviews + journey-map dips) and enters the discovery backlog. The PM scores the four risks; the lowest-scoring risk drives the discovery technique chosen. After testing, the item is re-scored at the Friday gate review. Items with all-four ≥ 2 enter the delivery backlog; items below threshold return to the discovery backlog with the failing risk(s) recorded, or are killed and logged to the failure register. The buffer rule keeps ≥2 sprints of validated work in the delivery backlog at all times.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Gate bypassed under deadline pressure Delivery needs stories; nothing is validated. An empty delivery backlog is a PM-level failure, not a justification to wave items through. Pull a smaller validated item — or accept the sprint will be lighter and explain why to leadership.
Engineers excluded from discovery PM treats discovery as their private domain. Rotate one engineer into discovery each sprint. Everyone cycles within ~6 sprints. Shared context makes the delivery track faster, not slower.
Discovery backlog = ideas list Every feature request from a stakeholder lands in discovery. The discovery backlog contains only items the PM has committed to validating this quarter. Stakeholder ideas live in a separate "ideas" list and earn a spot via prioritisation.
Only usability is tested Prototyping is the most teachable technique; teams default to it. Score all four risks before clearing the gate. The riskiest gap drives the next discovery technique (smoke test for value, engineer spike for feasibility, legal walkthrough for viability).
Gate theatre — failed items get re-scored on a hallway chat "We talked about it, it's fine now." Failed items return to the discovery backlog and are only re-scored when new evidence is gathered (new test, new spike). A failure register tracks killed items so a team can see whether the gate actually kills anything.
Discovery buffer drains to zero Discovery velocity dropped and nobody noticed. Track discovery velocity publicly. If the buffer falls below 2 sprints of validated work, it's an emergency — the PM stops other work and focuses on discovery throughput.

verdict:   GATE PASSED (all 4 risks ≥ 2)
           → moved to delivery backlog
           → committed to Sprint 25
           → brief + AC written, signed by @ivan + @oleg + @maria

confidence at gate:  82%  (own calibration: 70–90% band)

Outcome. Without the four-risk scorecard, the team would have shipped on the usability win alone (the prototype tested well). Two risks would have been silent: the "freeze" vs "delete" confusion (caught by usability score 2, addressed before delivery), and the pricing question (caught by viability score 2, resolved by deferring monetisation to v2). Both became delivery items — labelled and decided — instead of mid-sprint surprises. The streak-freeze feature shipped one sprint later with retention +4 pp on week-2 cohort; the failure register stayed empty for this item.
