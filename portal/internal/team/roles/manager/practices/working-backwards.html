Working backwards — PR/FAQ before you build

Write the press release and the FAQ first. If you can't write them, the idea isn't ready to build.

TL;DR

Working Backwards is Amazon's pre-build mechanism. Before any design or engineering: write a 1-page Press Release (future-dated, customer-facing) + a 1–3 page FAQ (every hard question answered). Read silently at the start of leadership review. If the documents can't be written, the idea isn't ready. Clarity of writing reveals clarity of thinking — and surfaces fatal flaws while they're still cheap.

What it is

Working Backwards is Amazon's mechanism for defining products before building them. Two documents written before any design or engineering:

  1. Press Release (1 page) — written as if the product has already shipped and a journalist is covering the announcement. Headline, customer problem, solution description (3–4 paragraphs), customer quote, call to action. No internal metrics — only customer-facing impact.
  2. FAQ (1–3 pages) — anticipates every hard question leadership, engineering, or customers might ask. "How does it work offline? How do we handle data privacy? What if the experiment fails?" The FAQ is where difficult trade-offs are documented before they become surprises.

Together, PR + FAQ are the product's source of truth from day one. Subsequent design and engineering decisions are checked against them. If a design decision contradicts the PR, one of them is wrong — and the team decides which.

Why we need it

Most product failures are clarity failures, not engineering failures. The team builds for months, then discovers that different people had different mental models of what was being built, for whom, and why. Working Backwards surfaces those conflicts in document review at the start — when fixing them costs hours, not months.

Writing a press release is also a proxy test for whether the product idea is any good. If you can't write a coherent headline and a believable customer quote, the vision isn't clear enough to build toward. Amazon teams know the initiative is ready to build when the PR makes a senior leader want to use the product before it exists.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Amazon (originator)

Every significant Amazon product — Echo, AWS Lambda, Amazon Go — started with a PR/FAQ. Documents are read silently at the start of leadership review (no presentations). Leaders annotate questions in the margin. The PM updates and re-presents. Sometimes 5–6 cycles before build begins.

What we take: Silent reading at meeting start is the key ritual. It removes the PM's ability to paper over weak ideas with confident delivery. The document must stand alone.

Source: Amazon — Working Backwards

Microsoft Azure — Customer Promise

Azure teams adopted a variant: a 1-page "Customer Promise" that replaces the press release but serves the same purpose — describing the service from the customer's perspective before any architecture is defined. Engineering uses it to evaluate scope and interface decisions.

What we take: The Customer Promise (or PR) should be a living gate, not a one-time artefact. When engineering proposes a simplification that breaks a customer promise, the trade-off is explicit and decided consciously.

Source: Azure — Microsoft

Stripe — brief with riskiest assumptions

Stripe's product brief embeds Working Backwards elements: customer-facing description, explicitly out-of-scope list, FAQs capturing the riskiest assumptions. Shared with the entire product team before any sprint begins.

What we take: The out-of-scope section is as valuable as the in-scope description. Making explicit what you are NOT building prevents scope creep and aligns the team on what's acceptable to cut under pressure.

Source: Stripe Product Team Blog

Twilio — Customer Promise + checkpoint reviews

Twilio uses a "Customer Promise" doc (PR equivalent) reviewed at three checkpoints: initiative kickoff, mid-build (week 3 of a sprint cycle), and pre-launch. Each checkpoint re-tests whether the build still matches the promise. Drift = explicit conversation, not silent change.

What we take: The PR/FAQ isn't just a pre-build gate. Re-read it at mid-build and pre-launch checkpoints. Drift between document and build is the early-warning signal for misalignment.

Source: Twilio Blog

Across teams: customer-facing prose, silent-read review, FAQ surfaces hard questions, out-of-scope explicit, document as living gate. The discipline is the writing; the meeting is just the test.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Headline

One sentence, customer-language. If it needs explanation, it's not ready.

Customer problem

What specific struggle does this eliminate? Customer language, not PM language.

Solution description

2–3 paragraphs, no internal jargon. How the product solves the problem.

Customer quote

Fictional but believable; from a real customer persona. If you can't write the quote, you don't know the customer.

External FAQ (5+)

Customer questions: pricing, edge cases, what-ifs.

Internal FAQ (5+)

Engineering, legal, sales — the hard internal questions about trade-offs and risks.

Out-of-scope section

Explicit list of related things NOT in scope. Same load-bearing role as in a product brief.

Silent-read review ritual

15 min silent reading at meeting start; written annotations; PM re-presents after revisions.

How it works in this project

Study App requires PR/FAQ for any tier-1 launch (see GTM) and any new vision-level initiative. The doc lives at docs/working-backwards/<launch-id>.md, paired with the brief at docs/briefs/<launch-id>.md. Format: PR (1 page) → FAQ (1–2 pages) → out-of-scope.

Review meetings open with 15 min silent reading. Leadership annotates in the margin (Notion / Google Docs comments). The PM revises and re-circulates; up to 3 cycles before build approval. Re-read at mid-build (week 3) and pre-launch (T-1 W) to catch drift. A short ADR locks the silent-read ritual, the tier-1 trigger, and the 3-cycle revision rule.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Prose vs slides — why writing wins

Slides reward visual design and verbal performance; prose rewards clarity of thought. A confident presenter can sell a vague idea with bullets; the same vague idea written down sounds like the gibberish it is. Bezos's mandate: "narratives, not slides" is one of the most copied Amazon practices because it works mechanically — the structure of prose forces the writer to make claims that can be challenged. Slides hide the claims; prose surfaces them.

The customer quote test

The most diagnostic part of the PR is the customer quote. Try writing a sentence that a real customer (named) would say about the product, in their voice, that doesn't sound like marketing copy. If you can't, the product's value proposition isn't clear yet. Quotes that fail the test usually sound like "Now I can do X faster" — generic, could be any product. Quotes that pass sound like "I used to dread the part where I had to Y; now I just do it on the train". Specific, voiced, believable.

FAQ — the negative-space document

The PR describes what the product is; the FAQ describes what it isn't. Hard questions reveal the boundary. Sample internal FAQ:

  • What happens if our largest customer asks for X? — surfaces enterprise vs SMB priority
  • How does this work for users in regions with poor internet? — surfaces geographic constraints
  • What's our answer when [competitor] ships their version next quarter? — surfaces differentiation
  • How much data does this collect and where is it stored? — surfaces privacy/compliance
  • What's the rollback plan if v1 is broken? — surfaces operational readiness

Each question is an opportunity to make the trade-off explicit before it becomes a launch-day surprise.

Silent-read mechanics

The ritual: meeting opens with 15 min silent reading. No phones, no slides, no PM voiceover. Leaders annotate in the margin (digital or print). After reading, PM goes section by section answering annotations. The format does three things: (1) attendees process at their own speed (fast readers don't wait for slow speakers), (2) the document must stand alone (no rescuing weak prose with verbal explanation), (3) annotations become a record of what was challenged and what was resolved. Teams that adopt this format report ~30% fewer follow-up clarification meetings.

Tiering — when is PR/FAQ worth it?

PR/FAQ takes 8–20 hours of focused PM time. Not worth it for everything. Rough tiering:

  • Required — vision-level initiatives, new product lines, tier-1 launches, anything that requires > 1 quarter of build.
  • Optional — tier-2 launches; a brief usually suffices.
  • Skip — tier-3 (bug fixes, polish, minor features). The brief is enough.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • PM writing time. 8–20 hrs per PR/FAQ, plus revision cycles. Cheap relative to a misdirected quarter, but visible upfront.
  • Leadership read time. 15 min × N reviewers × M revision cycles. Real ask; protect the ritual.
  • Cultural lift. Teams used to slides need ~2 quarters to internalise prose-first norms. Resistance is normal; consistency from leadership is the fix.
  • Theatre risk. If the PM writes a PR/FAQ that leadership never seriously challenges (always approved on first read), the practice is theatre. Sometimes returning a document for revision is the proof the practice works.

Lifecycle

draft PR → draft FAQ (internal + external) → silent-read review → revise (up to 3 cycles) → build approval → mid-build re-read → pre-launch re-read

First draft is the longest (1–2 weeks). Review cycles are the value: each round surfaces a hard question the PM didn't anticipate, the document gets stronger. Build approval gates on PR/FAQ acceptance. Mid-build and pre-launch re-reads catch drift between document and build; both are short (30-min checkpoint each).

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
PR in internal language PM defaults to their own vocabulary. Give the PR to a new hire or customer; ask them to explain it back. If they can't, rewrite.
FAQ only easy questions PM writes questions they already know how to answer. Explicitly ask engineering, sales, design for the 3 hardest questions they have. Include every one — especially the uncomfortable ones.
PR/FAQ as bureaucratic checklist Teams go through the motions; leadership doesn't engage. Leadership must actively challenge. If the doc is never returned for revision, the process is theatrical. Aim for ≥ 2 revision cycles per major initiative.
Slides smuggled into review "Just a quick deck to walk through it." Prose only. Slides bypass the structure-of-thought test. If the PM needs slides to explain it, the document isn't done.
Customer quote sounds like marketing "Now I can do X 10× faster" — too generic. Real quote: specific situation, customer's voice, believable. If it could be on any product's landing page, rewrite.
Doc filed after approval, not re-read "We got approved, on to build." Mid-build + pre-launch re-reads. Drift between doc and build is the early warning of misalignment.

Worked example

PR · 1 page

Study App — Streak Freeze (PR dated 2026-09-01, written 2026-04)

# Study App lets learners pause their streak for a day —
# without losing it

FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE  ·  September 1, 2026  ·  Study App

Study App today launched Streak Freeze, a feature that lets learners
take a day off without losing the daily-learning streak they have
built. The change addresses the most-cited reason learners abandon
the app in their first month: the anxiety of breaking a streak they
care about.

Before Streak Freeze, a learner who built a 12-day streak had two
options on a bad day: study (even briefly) to keep the streak alive,
or break the streak and feel they had failed. In switch interviews
with 7 lapsed users, 4 said they had stopped using the app on days
they were tired because losing a streak felt worse than not learning.

Streak Freeze gives every learner one "pause day" per week. The
streak number holds; the learner returns the next day without
penalty. The freeze is automatic and ambient — no decision needed
in the moment.

"I used to dread my Tuesday commute home because if I didn't open
the app on the train I'd lose 12 days of progress. Now I can save my
freeze for a tired day without thinking about it. It feels like the
app finally has my back."
  — Maya, 27, marketing manager, learning Italian for 4 months

Streak Freeze is available to all learners starting today, with no
configuration required. The team will measure week-2 retention of
new signups exposed to the feature, with a floor of +5 percentage
points over the prior cohort.

# # #

Outcome. Writing this PR surfaced three things that hadn't been clear in the original idea: (1) the customer quote took 4 rewrites — the early versions sounded like marketing copy ("Streak Freeze gives users more flexibility"). The final quote came from a real interview transcript and was specific enough to be unfalsifiable. (2) The phrase "no decision needed in the moment" was a design constraint that hadn't existed before writing — the FAQ then had to answer "what if the user wants to choose when to freeze?" (answer: not in v1, automatic only). (3) The success metric (+5 pp week-2 retention floor) was forced into the PR; previously the team had said "we expect retention to improve" without quantifying. The PR/FAQ doubled as the working brief, with the brief itself just inheriting the AC and operational details.
