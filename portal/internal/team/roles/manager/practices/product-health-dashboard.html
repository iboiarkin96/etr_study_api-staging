Product health dashboard — always know if the product is working

A curated, always-on view of 8–12 metrics — designed so a 5-minute daily review answers "is the product healthy right now?"

TL;DR

A single-screen dashboard with 8–12 curated metrics: NSM at the top, 3–5 input metrics below, one activation metric, one retention metric, 1–2 business metrics, and explicit counter-metrics. Each metric has a target and a yellow/red alert threshold; red breach triggers a Slack alert in < 30 min, not at the next review.

What it is

A product health dashboard is a deliberately curated, always-visible display of 8–12 metrics that together answer "is the product healthy right now?". It's not a comprehensive analytics suite — it's an early warning system. A suite has hundreds of metrics and needs hours to interpret; a health dashboard has a dozen and needs five minutes.

Structured as a hierarchy: the NSM at the top, supported by 3–5 input metrics that predict it. Below, one activation metric, one retention metric, 1–2 business metrics, plus explicit counter-metrics (the things that must not regress). Each metric has a target value, a yellow watch threshold, and a red alert threshold; red breach triggers a Slack notification within 30 minutes — not "investigate at next review".

Why we need it

Without a health dashboard, product problems get discovered late — often at the quarterly business review when the data spans three months of undiscovered decline. The dashboard shortens detection from months to days. A 5-point retention drop in a single week is visible Monday morning; without the dashboard, it's visible in late October when the quarterly slide gets prepared.

The curating exercise is itself valuable. Picking the 12 metrics that matter forces a conversation about what success means. Teams that can't agree on 12 metrics don't agree on what product health is. The dashboard makes that disagreement visible and resolvable.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Duolingo — automated alerts

Duolingo's "streak health" dashboard centres on their NSM (DAU completing ≥ 1 lesson) with four alert levels: green / yellow (5% below 30-day avg) / orange (10%) / red (15% or unexpected weekend/holiday). Orange + red trigger automatic Slack alerts to product leadership within 30 minutes of detection.

What we take: Automated threshold alerts beat human monitoring every time. Build the alert into the dashboard; a dashboard without alerts is passive display.

Source: Duolingo Engineering

Figma — same-period prior-year comparison

Figma's "product pulse" has exactly 10 metrics, reviewed in a 15-minute Monday standup. Each metric is compared to the same week of the prior year, not just week-over-week — to surface seasonal patterns. A 10% drop after a US holiday is normal; a 10% drop in week 23 of the prior year is a real signal.

What we take: WoW comparison is noisy for products with seasonality. Prior-period comparison is more informative; year-over-year is required for any product older than a year.

Source: Figma Product Blog

Shopify — counter-metrics on the same screen

Shopify's merchant health dashboard pairs GMV (NSM) with explicit counter-metrics on the same screen: merchant CSAT, support backlog, fraud rate. Optimising GMV alone is impossible — every NSM tile sits next to its counter.

What we take: Counter-metrics live on the same screen, not in a separate report. Physical proximity creates the optical pressure: you can't celebrate the NSM tile without seeing the counter tile next to it.

Source: Shopify Engineering Blog

Amplitude — public dashboards

Amplitude's product team makes the health dashboard visible to the whole company on a Slack-pinned link + a TV in the office. Everyone sees the same numbers; PMs explain anomalies in the open. Public visibility eliminates "I didn't know we were down" conversations.

What we take: Visibility multiplies the value of the dashboard. A private PM dashboard catches problems for one person; a public dashboard catches problems for the whole company.

Source: Amplitude Product Analytics

Across teams: automated alerts, prior-period comparison, counter-metrics on-screen, public visibility, 5-min review cadence. Skipping any one of these degrades the dashboard from "early warning system" to "decorative chart wall".

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

North-star metric

Single top tile. Current value, vs 7-day ago, vs prior-year same-week, vs target. Alert threshold defined.

Input metrics (3–5)

Leading indicators that predict NSM. Each owned by a squad; if NSM drops, one of these explains why.

Activation tile

D1 or D7 activation rate — % of new users reaching first value moment.

Retention tile

W4 or M3 retention. Compared to same-week prior year, not just week-over-week.

Counter-metrics on same screen

CSAT, error rate, support load — anything that gaming the NSM could degrade.

Yellow + red alert thresholds

Pre-defined per metric. Red breach → Slack alert in < 30 min, named on-call investigates that day.

5-minute review cadence

Daily PM glance; Monday team review. Beyond 5 min, the dashboard is too complex.

Public visibility

Pinned in Slack, on a TV; everyone sees the same numbers. Private = one-person early warning.

How it works in this project

Study App's dashboard lives in Grafana (self-hosted), reading from the canonical event warehouse + PostHog. The board has 12 tiles: 1 NSM (weekly lessons / learner), 3 input metrics (activation, session-depth, weekly-return), 1 activation tile (D1), 1 retention tile (W4), 1 business tile (MRR), and 5 counters (CSAT, error rate, p95 lesson-render, tickets/100 users, fraud signals).

Yellow / red thresholds are defined per tile and stored as Grafana alerts. Red breaches Slack #pm-alerts within 30 minutes; the on-call PM (rotating) investigates that day. The dashboard URL is pinned in #pm-team and reviewed by the PM each Monday at 09:00. A short ADR locks the 12-tile cap, threshold-definition process, and the rule that adding a 13th tile requires removing an existing one.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Curation as discipline — the 12-tile cap

The hardest discipline is saying "no" to the 13th tile. Every stakeholder has a metric they want monitored; every PM has 30 metrics they'd like to track "for context". A dashboard with 30 tiles isn't a dashboard, it's a wall — nobody reads it, nobody acts on it, the 5-minute review becomes a 30-minute scroll. The 12-tile cap is the practice's load-bearing constraint. Tile 13 requires removing a tile; nobody removes their own tile easily; the cap creates productive friction.

Yellow / red thresholds — pre-commitment

Thresholds set after a metric drops are rationalisations. "Well, 38% is fine because last year we were at 30%" is a number negotiated under pressure. Pre-commit: define yellow (watch) and red (investigate today) thresholds when the dashboard is set up, before seeing any breach. Yellow is typically -5% from 30-day average; red is -10% or absolute floor. Document the thresholds; when a breach happens, the action is automatic.

Comparison choice matters

Three comparison anchors, ranked:

  1. Year-over-year same week (best for seasonal products) — controls for holidays, school terms, weather.
  2. 30-day rolling average (smooths weekday/weekend noise) — best for products without strong seasonality.
  3. Week-over-week (noisy, easy to misread) — useful only for stable mature products.

Pick the right anchor per metric. Engagement metrics → 30-day rolling. Revenue → year-over-year (most companies are seasonal). Error rate → week-over-week is fine (errors aren't seasonal).

Counter-metrics adjacent to primaries

If the NSM tile lives in the top-left and the counter tile lives in a second tab, the team optimises the NSM and ignores the counter. Physical proximity matters: place the counter next to its primary so they can't be read separately. NSM going up + counter going down isn't a celebration; it's a tradeoff that needs naming. The dashboard layout is what makes that tradeoff visible.

The "good news" rule for review meetings

Weekly review meetings should start with the metric that's most concerning, not the metric that looks best. PMs naturally start with wins (it feels good); the discipline is to start with red and yellow tiles first. If the meeting runs out of time, you've discussed the urgent items. Starting with green tiles always means red tiles get postponed.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Instrumentation tail. Every tile needs data wired from the warehouse. 12 tiles × cleanup + dashboard build = several engineer-weeks initially. Plan it as a project, not a side task.
  • Threshold maintenance. Thresholds drift over time as the product matures. Quarterly review: are the thresholds still right? Tightening or loosening one is a leadership-level decision, not a Slack message.
  • Alert fatigue. Loose thresholds → constant red breaches → team stops investigating. Tight thresholds → real signal missed. Calibrate during the first quarter; expect false positives until thresholds stabilise.
  • "I want my metric on it" politics. Stakeholders lobby for their metric on the dashboard. The PM is the curator; every addition must remove something. Without that rule the dashboard ages into a wall.

Lifecycle

draft 12 candidate tiles → define thresholds → instrument + dashboard build → wire automated alerts → public visibility → 5-min daily glance + weekly review → quarterly threshold review

The first build is a 3–4 week project: curation workshop (2 hrs), threshold definition (PM + analytics, 2 hrs), instrumentation (engineering, 1–3 sprints depending on warehouse maturity), alert wiring (1–2 days). Steady-state cadence: daily 5-min PM glance, Monday team review (15 min), quarterly threshold review (1 hr, leadership level).

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
50-tile dashboard Every stakeholder added their metric. Hard cap 12 tiles. Beyond that it's a wall, not a dashboard. Drill-downs go elsewhere.
No alert thresholds Team agrees metrics matter but can't commit to what "bad" looks like. Define yellow + red thresholds before launching the dashboard. If the team can't agree on thresholds, they don't agree on health.
Reviewed monthly, not daily It takes too long to interpret. Cut tiles until the 5-minute review is achievable. The cadence is the practice; monthly reviews surface problems too late.
WoW comparison only Easiest to compute. Year-over-year same-week for seasonal products; 30-day rolling for others. WoW is too noisy to act on.
Counters in a separate tab "They have their own dashboard". Counter sits next to primary. Same screen, same review. Optical pressure forces honest interpretation.
Private PM dashboard Fear of public scrutiny. Pin publicly. Public visibility multiplies value: the whole team catches problems, not just the PM.

DASHBOARD URL  (public)
  https://grafana.studyapp.internal/d/product-health

Outcome. The dashboard caught the error_rate spike (T09) within 30 minutes of breach — a deploy at 11:30 had introduced a bad query in the lesson-render pipeline; the on-call PM saw the alert, paged on-call eng, rollback at 12:15. Total user impact: ~45 minutes of degraded experience. Without the dashboard + alert, the same incident would have been noticed at Monday's review or when support tickets accumulated — likely 12+ hours of impact. The yellow on weekly_return (T04) drove the next sprint's discovery focus; investigating it revealed a notification-timing regression that wasn't visible on any other tile.
