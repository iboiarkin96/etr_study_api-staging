A/B testing — let users decide, not opinions

Controlled experiments that replace "I think" with "the data shows" — and stop changes that quietly harm the metrics that matter.

TL;DR

Pre-register a hypothesis, calculate the sample size, randomise users into A (control) and B (variant), and do not look at results until the sample is reached. If B beats A on the primary metric at p < 0.05 and no guardrail degrades, ship B. Otherwise kill it — independent of how much effort went in.

What it is

A/B testing (controlled online experimentation, split testing) measures the causal impact of a product change. Users are randomly assigned to a control arm (A, the current experience) or a treatment arm (B, the new variant). Both arms run simultaneously on real traffic; once the pre-computed sample size is reached, the primary metric is compared between arms with a statistical hypothesis test.

A valid test needs five pre-registered things: a hypothesis ("changing X moves Y by ≥Z%, because…"), a single primary metric, a set of guardrail metrics, a minimum detectable effect, and the sample size implied by them. Anything decided after the data lands — including the metric, the threshold, or the stopping rule — invalidates the test.

Why we need it

Human intuition about what will move a product metric is correct roughly one third of the time. This number is consistent across Google, Microsoft, Booking.com, and Airbnb's published experimentation programmes. Two thirds of confidently proposed improvements either do nothing or actively make the metric worse. Without A/B tests, those two thirds ship anyway and are invisible — the team feels productive and the product silently drifts.

Experimentation also flips the team's incentive. "We shipped it" stops being the win condition; "we learned whether it worked" replaces it. That single shift is the foundation of evidence-based product management — and the reason mature product teams ship slower and grow faster.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Booking.com

Runs >1 000 simultaneous A/B tests at any time on a single central platform (XP). Every significant change is tested; "no feature ships without a test" is enforced at the engineering level, not just by PMs. The platform owns randomisation, power analysis, guardrails, and result visualisation so every team uses the same math.

What we take: Experimentation at scale is an infrastructure decision before it is a culture one. Without a shared platform, A/B testing becomes a per-team craft skill with inconsistent methodology — and bad methodology is worse than no test.

Source: Booking.com AI & Research

Microsoft (ExP)

ExP runs tens of thousands of experiments per year across Bing, Azure, Office, and Teams. Their published finding: only 1-in-3 internal ideas pass the success metric. Guardrail metrics (revenue, retention, page-load p95) are enforced by the platform — a positive primary metric is not enough; any guardrail regression kills the ship.

What we take: Guardrails are non-negotiable, and they must be defined before the experiment runs. After results land, objectivity is gone.

Source: Microsoft ExP Platform

Netflix

Tests virtually every product decision — thumbnail art, recommender weights, UI layout, email subject lines. A central statistics team reviews experiment designs before they launch; a badly-designed test is killed before it produces misleading results, not after.

What we take: Design review (pre-launch) matters as much as result analysis (post-launch). Underpowered, confounded, or peeking-friendly designs produce numbers that fool the team. Add a design-review gate to the workflow.

Source: Netflix Tech Blog

Airbnb

Built its ERF (Experiment Reporting Framework) after a public post-mortem on a search-ranking test that "won" by 1% but was wrong — the analysis hadn't accounted for sample ratio mismatch. The platform now refuses results when SRM is detected and surfaces a chi-square check on every test.

What we take: Trust in the platform comes from the checks it runs against itself. The platform should refuse to declare a winner when its own assumptions are violated.

Source: Airbnb — Experimentation at scale

Across every team at scale the playbook converges: a single shared platform; pre-registered hypothesis + primary metric + guardrails + sample size; refusal-to-decide when assumptions break (SRM, underpower); and a kill rate that's tracked and celebrated, not hidden.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Hypothesis

"If we change X, then Y will move by at least Z%, because [mechanism]." Written and reviewed before exposure starts.

Primary metric

Exactly one. The decision to ship or kill is read off this metric and nothing else.

Guardrail metrics

Metrics that must not regress: revenue, retention, p95 page-load, error rate. Any guardrail regression kills the ship.

Sample-size calc

N derived from baseline rate, MDE, α = 0.05, power = 0.8. Computed before launch, not back-fit after.

No-peek rule

Results are not opened until N is reached. Early peeking inflates false-positive rate by 3-5×.

SRM check

Chi-square test on the actual A/B split versus the intended split. If it fails, the result is thrown away — not interpreted.

Decision record

One line per test: hypothesis, result, decision (ship / kill / iterate), and link to dashboard. Becomes the team's experiment log.

How it works in this project

Study App will adopt GrowthBook (self-hosted, open-source) as the experimentation platform. Variants are gated by a feature flag; assignment is computed deterministically from user_id so a user always sees the same arm. The metric pipeline reads from the warehouse event stream and joins to the assignment table.

A short ADR will lock the default minimum detectable effect (5% relative), the standard guardrail set (signup-completion, week-2 retention, lesson-render p95, JS error rate), and the design-review gate — every test description is reviewed for hypothesis quality and power before traffic is allocated. The launchpad page at internal/experiments/ will list every active test with arm, primary metric, current sample, and projected stop date.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Sample size — why "1 000 users" is not enough

The required N depends on four numbers: baseline conversion rate (p), minimum detectable effect (MDE), significance level (α, conventionally 0.05), and statistical power (1 − β, conventionally 0.8). A rough formula for a two-proportion test:

N per arm ≈ 16 · p · (1 − p) / (MDE · p)²

Intuition: detecting a small lift on a low baseline takes a lot of users. A 1% relative lift on a 5% baseline conversion needs ~125 000 users per arm. A 10% relative lift on the same baseline needs ~1 250. The error is symmetric, so if you only have 5 000 users to spend, the MDE you can detect is the one that's mathematically possible — not the one the PM wants.

Sample Ratio Mismatch — the silent killer

If you intended a 50/50 split but the actual exposure is 51.3/48.7, something is broken: a bot filter that hits one arm harder, a cache that serves stale assignments, a JS error that crashes B's instrumentation. SRM doesn't make B look better — it makes the comparison meaningless. Run a chi-square test on the assignment counts on every result. If the SRM check fails, throw the test out and find the bug; do not interpret the numbers.

Novelty and primacy effects

A new variant often gets a temporary bump because it's new — users click on it out of curiosity. The effect decays in 1-2 weeks. Tests shorter than one full business cycle (typically a week) overestimate the lift. Conversely, a confusing new design suffers a primacy penalty that fades as users learn. Minimum test duration: one week; for changes to long-lived journeys, two.

The multiple-comparison trap

If you test 20 metrics at α = 0.05, you'll get ~1 "significant" result by chance even when nothing changed. Pick the primary metric first; treat any other metric as descriptive, not decisive. If you genuinely need multiple primary metrics, apply Bonferroni (α / k) or Benjamini-Hochberg (FDR control). Both make it harder to declare a win — that's the point.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Slower shipping. A power-correct test takes 1–4 weeks. A PM who wants to ship Friday cannot also have a credible result on Monday. The escape valve is to ship a tiny ramp (5%) for safety while the proper test runs at 50/50 alongside.
  • Traffic is finite. Each test eats user-weeks. A small product can run 3–5 concurrent tests; a giant one can run 1 000. Concurrent-test ceiling is the binding constraint, so the team has to prioritise — RICE or a similar method usually decides what gets traffic.
  • Statistics literacy is required. A team that runs tests without understanding power, SRM, and multiple comparisons produces confident-looking but misleading numbers. Plan for training plus a stats reviewer on staff.

Lifecycle

hypothesis drafted → design review → power calc → ramp 5% safety → 50/50 to N → SRM + guardrail check → ship · kill · iterate

A test starts as a hypothesis PR in the experiment log. Design review confirms the hypothesis is testable, the primary metric is honest, and the guardrails are real. The power calc decides whether the test is feasible — many die here, which is the right outcome. A 5% safety ramp catches catastrophic bugs (errors, page-load regressions) without burning the sample. The full split then runs to the pre-computed N. On stop, the platform runs SRM and guardrail checks; only if both pass does the primary-metric comparison run. The decision is recorded — including kills.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Peeking and stopping early PM is impatient; sees a promising early trend and calls it a win. Lock the minimum N before launch. The platform refuses to render a result before N is reached. If genuinely sequential decisions are needed, use a sequential method (mSPRT, group-sequential bounds) — not standard p-values with manual stopping.
SRM not checked Team trusts the "50/50 split" the flag tool reports. Chi-square the actual assignment counts on every result. If the split is off (p < 0.001 on the SRM check), the test is invalid — find the bug, do not interpret the numbers.
Too many variants at once Team wants to test 4 ideas in one sprint. Each extra arm divides the per-arm traffic; an A/B/C/D needs 4× the users for the same power. Run sequential A/B tests unless traffic genuinely supports multivariate. The cost of a long queue is smaller than the cost of underpowered tests.
Shipping because effort was invested Sunk-cost fallacy — killing a finished feature feels wasteful. A killed test is not a failed sprint; it prevented a degrading change from reaching 100% of users. Track the kill rate as a healthy metric — healthy programmes kill 50–70%.
One-day tests Calendar pressure; "we'll get enough traffic by tomorrow." Run for at least one full business cycle (a week). One-day samples miss weekend-versus-weekday seasonality and exaggerate novelty effects; the result almost always shrinks when extended.

Worked example

Test record

EXP-042 — Lesson-CTA copy: "Continue" vs "Mark as complete"

id:          EXP-042
hypothesis:  If the lesson-end CTA reads "Mark as complete" (not "Continue"),
             then 7-day completion rate of started courses will rise by ≥4%,
             because the verb names the user's goal instead of the next system action.

primary:     7d course-completion rate (per started-course, per user)
guardrails:  - signup-completion (must not drop by >1 pp)
             - lesson-render p95 (must not exceed 600 ms)
             - JS error rate (must not rise by >10%)

baseline:    18.2% (last 28 days)
MDE:         4% relative (i.e. detect ≥18.93%)
alpha:       0.05    power: 0.80
N per arm:   ≈ 21 800   (≈ 12 days at current traffic)

ramp plan:   day 1-2: 5% safety ramp on B (guardrails only)
             day 3+:  50/50 to N
stop rule:   reach N or 21 days, whichever first

Outcome. On day 14 N is reached. SRM check passes (split 50.1/49.9, p = 0.34). Guardrails all green. Primary metric: B = 19.4% vs A = 18.1%, lift = +7.2% relative, p = 0.008. Decision: ship B at 100%; record kept in the experiment log. Without the test, the team would have shipped both A's wording and a back-up "Next lesson" variant that, in a follow-up test, regressed completion by 3% — the kind of change that's invisible without an experiment.
