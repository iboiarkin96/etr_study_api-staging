North-star metric — the single number that matters

One metric that captures the core value the product delivers to customers — and predicts long-term revenue better than revenue itself.

TL;DR

The North-Star Metric (NSM) is the single product metric that captures the core value the product delivers. It sits between vanity activity (DAU, page views) and lagging revenue — it moves when customers succeed and predicts revenue 1–3 quarters ahead. Every squad orients OKRs to it; every product brief declares its expected NSM impact; if a feature wouldn't move it, the feature needs a strong justification to ship.

What it is

The north-star metric, popularised by Sean Ellis (GrowthHackers) and Amplitude, is the single product metric that best captures the core value exchange between product and customer. It is not revenue (an outcome) and not a vanity metric (may not correlate with value). It is the metric that moves when customers genuinely succeed.

Classic examples: Spotify — time spent listening per active user per week (music discovery + enjoyment). Airbnb — nights booked (the transaction that delivers value to both sides). Slack — messages sent per active team (collaboration that drives retention).

The NSM is supported by 3–5 input metrics — leading indicators the squads directly influence. If the NSM rises, at least one input must explain why. If inputs rise but NSM doesn't, either the inputs are wrong or there's a counter-effect (anti-pattern: more sessions but shorter ones = inputs up, NSM flat).

Why we need it

Without a north-star, squads optimise local metrics that don't compound into business value. One squad lifts signups while another's retention drops — and there's no shared metric to surface the trade-off. The NSM is the single reference point every squad orients to; cross-squad trade-offs become visible and discussable.

It also disciplines product conversations with leadership. Instead of "we shipped 12 features", the PM says "NSM moved from 4.2 to 5.1 — these two features drove it, these two didn't, here's what we're cutting". Shifts the conversation from activity to impact.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Amplitude

Amplitude codified the NSM framework and uses it internally: their NSM is "weekly learning users" — users with ≥ 3 queries per week. Captures the core value of analytics discovery. Roadmap is evaluated against NSM impact, not against revenue (downstream).

What we take: The NSM test — would a feature that lifted revenue but didn't move the NSM be worth shipping? If yes, the NSM is wrong. The NSM is the metric you'd sacrifice short-term revenue to improve, because you know it predicts long-term retention.

Source: Amplitude — North Star Playbook

Facebook — "10 friends in 7 days"

Facebook's growth-era NSM was "monthly active users who returned ≥ 7 days in their first month". The famous "10 friends in 7 days" insight came from input-metric analysis: users who hit 10 friends in their first week were dramatically more likely to become long-term users. That number became the onboarding goal.

What we take: The NSM should help teams discover the specific behaviours that predict retention, not just measure retention itself. Input-metric correlations with NSM produce activation rules.

Source: Facebook Engineering Blog

HubSpot

HubSpot uses "weekly active teams" as the NSM — a team that performed at least one significant action (contact created, campaign run, deal updated). Decomposed into per-hub input metrics (marketing, sales). Quarterly product reviews are structured around NSM movement and which inputs contributed.

What we take: Multi-product companies still need one NSM. Product-line metrics are inputs, not separate north-stars. Having five north-stars is having none.

Source: HubSpot Product Blog

Shopify — GMV with counter-metrics

Shopify's NSM is GMV (gross merchandise volume processed through the platform). They pair it with explicit counter-metrics — merchant retention, support backlog, fraud rate — so optimising for GMV alone can't degrade merchant quality. GMV up + counter down = launch killed.

What we take: Even a great NSM needs counter-metrics. Without them, the NSM becomes a target to game; with them, it's a measurement honest about trade-offs.

Source: Shopify Engineering Blog

The pattern across teams: one NSM, 3–5 input metrics, explicit counter-metrics, weekly review, mandatory in every product brief. Skipping any of those four turns the NSM into a poster on the wall.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

The NSM itself

One metric, customer-value framed, leading (not lagging). Every squad can explain how their work influences it.

Input metrics (3–5)

Leading indicators decomposing the NSM. Each squad owns 1–2 inputs. If all inputs rise, NSM should rise.

Counter-metrics

Metrics that must not regress while NSM rises. Prevents gaming.

Weekly review cadence

NSM + inputs reviewed weekly at product leadership level. Anomalies investigated within 48 h.

Required in product briefs

Every brief declares expected NSM and input impact. Features without a hypothesised NSM impact need explicit override.

Quarterly review of the NSM itself

Is the NSM still the right one? Has the business model shifted? NSM-change is a quarterly leadership decision, not a sprint task.

How it works in this project

Study App's NSM is weekly lessons completed per active learner. It captures the core value of the product (learning that compounds), is leading (predicts week-4 retention and paid conversion 1–2 quarters ahead), and every squad can explain how their work influences it.

Input metrics (3): activation rate (first-lesson completion within 24 h), engagement depth (avg lessons/session for active users), retention (W4 cohort retention). Counter-metrics: per-lesson CSAT (no rushing — quality must hold), error rate on lesson playback, support ticket volume per 100 active users.

Reviewed weekly at Monday product sync; NSM and inputs charted on the public product-health dashboard. Every product brief in docs/briefs/ has a "NSM hypothesis" section. A short ADR locks the NSM, inputs, counter-metrics, and the rule that NSM changes are leadership decisions, not squad decisions.

Deep dive — the theory you need

The NSM test — would you sacrifice revenue for it?

The clearest test of whether you have the right NSM: a feature comes along that you're confident would lift revenue this quarter but you don't think it would move the NSM. Do you ship it? If yes, your NSM is wrong (because you've revealed that revenue matters more, so revenue is the actual north-star). If no, you trust the NSM as the leading indicator — and that trust is the entire point. Teams without an NSM ship the short-term revenue lift every time, even when it hurts long-term retention. The NSM is a precommitment device against that.

Decomposing the NSM into inputs — the L-formula

The Amplitude playbook uses an "L-formula" to decompose NSM into inputs:

NSM = breadth × depth × frequency × efficiency

For Study App's NSM (weekly lessons completed per learner):

  • Breadth — how many learners are active (activation × retention)
  • Depth — how many lessons per active session
  • Frequency — how many sessions per week
  • Efficiency — % of started lessons completed

The decomposition gives every squad a place to attribute their work. Onboarding squad → breadth. Content squad → depth. Engagement/notifications squad → frequency. Player squad → efficiency.

Vanity vs NSM — the three-question test

  1. Does it require customer success? A page view doesn't; a completed lesson does.
  2. Would it move even if marketing went silent? Signups would crater without marketing; engagement depth wouldn't.
  3. Would you celebrate it going up alone? DAU going up while NSM is flat is suspicious; NSM up alone is genuinely good news.

A metric that fails any one of these three is vanity, not NSM.

One NSM vs portfolio — the trade-off

Some thought leaders argue for a "portfolio" of 3 NSMs (acquisition + engagement + monetisation). The compromise reads as balanced but breaks the entire point: when two NSMs conflict, the team has no rule to decide. The discipline of one NSM forces honest priority. Multi-product companies handle it via product-line input metrics, not multi-NSM.

NSM vs OKR vs HEART

These three are layered, not competing. NSM = the single number for the company. OKRs = quarterly objectives that should ladder up to NSM movement; the Key Results are input-metric targets. HEART = the UX-quality framework that says "for each product focus, here's how to measure it"; the HEART metrics feed into NSM inputs. Healthy: NSM → OKRs → HEART. Unhealthy: each is run independently and they don't connect.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Long instrumentation tail. NSM + 3–5 inputs + counter-metrics + per-brief tracking = significant analytics work. Budget a dedicated analytics engineer if the product is past v1.
  • NSM stability vs adaptability. The NSM should be stable for years (Spotify's NSM hasn't changed); but business model pivots may demand it change. Quarterly review is the discipline; weekly fiddling is the failure mode.
  • Leadership alignment cost. Convincing leadership to track an NSM (not revenue) requires patient explanation. Without leadership buy-in, every squad ends up optimising for revenue under the table.
  • Counter-metric vigilance. Without explicit counters, NSM becomes a gameable target. Spend time defining counters; revisit them when shipping experiments.

Lifecycle

draft 3 candidate NSMs → test against 3 questions → pick one → decompose into 3–5 inputs → define counter-metrics → instrument + dashboard → weekly review · quarterly NSM review

First cycle takes 2–4 weeks: candidate workshop, leadership alignment, instrumentation, dashboard. The NSM is then stable for at least 4 quarters; inputs and counters can be refined more often. The quarterly NSM-review meeting asks one question: has the business model shifted enough that the NSM no longer captures core value? If yes, leadership decides on a new NSM and the cycle restarts.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Revenue as the NSM Leadership cares about revenue most. Revenue lags. Pick a leading metric that predicts revenue 1–3 quarters out — and that you'd sacrifice short-term revenue to improve.
Five NSMs for five squads Each squad wants ownership of "their" metric. Squad metrics are inputs; the NSM is company-level. One NSM, 3–5 inputs, each input owned by 1–2 squads.
NSM tracked but not decisive Reviewed weekly but features ship regardless. Make "expected NSM impact" a required field in every product brief. Features without an expected impact need leadership override to enter the sprint.
No counter-metrics "Our NSM is honest enough." Any metric without counters becomes a target to game. Name 2–3 counters explicitly; review them on the same dashboard.
NSM changed in-quarter Team gets excited about a new framing. NSM changes are leadership-level quarterly decisions. Mid-quarter changes break longitudinal comparisons and signal lack of discipline.
Inputs that don't actually predict NSM Selected by intuition, not regression. Validate input → NSM correlation with at least 8 weeks of historical data. Inputs that don't predict NSM are vanity dressed as discipline.

Worked example

NSM definition

Study App — "weekly lessons completed per active learner"

NORTH-STAR METRIC
  weekly_lessons_completed_per_active_learner
  = COUNT(lesson_completed events) / COUNT(DISTINCT active learners), per ISO week
  active = ≥ 1 lesson started in the week (denominator definition locked)

WHY THIS METRIC
  • requires customer success (a completed lesson, not a view)
  • leading: 4-quarter regression shows it predicts paid-conversion (R² = 0.71)
  • squads can explain influence: onboarding → activation, content → depth,
    notifications → frequency, player → completion rate

INPUT METRICS (each owned by a squad)
  I1  activation_rate     = % of new signups completing first lesson in 24h
                            owner: onboarding squad
  I2  session_depth       = avg lessons completed per active session
                            owner: content squad
  I3  weekly_return       = % of last-week-active returning this week
                            owner: engagement squad
  I4  completion_rate     = lessons completed / lessons started
                            owner: player squad

COUNTER-METRICS (must not regress)
  C1  per-lesson CSAT (1-tap, post-completion)        floor: ≥ 4.2 / 5
  C2  player error rate (technical errors per session) floor: ≤ 0.5%
  C3  support tickets per 100 active learners          floor: ≤ 2.5

REVIEW
  weekly  — Monday product sync; anomaly = investigation within 48h
  monthly — input → NSM regression refit (detects predictive drift)
  quarterly — leadership-level: is NSM still right?

USAGE IN BRIEFS
  Every product brief in docs/briefs/*.md MUST include:
    "NSM hypothesis": expected impact on the NSM and which input(s)
    "Counter-metric risk": which counter-metric this could regress

Outcome. Before adopting an NSM, the team optimised three different metrics (DAU, signup-completion, paid-conversion) with no shared priority — a feature could improve signup-completion while degrading completion rate (longer onboarding fatigue) and nobody noticed. After adoption, three things changed: (1) two candidate features in the next quarter were cut at brief review because their expected NSM impact was zero; (2) a redesign that lifted DAU 8% but dropped completion-rate 3 pp was killed at the counter-metric check; (3) the input → NSM regression revealed that activation (I1) was the highest-leverage input — that squad got prioritised hiring the next quarter. None of those decisions would have surfaced without the NSM.

Quality bar — what good looks like

A checklist of what a healthy implementation has. ✓ done ◐ partial ○ todo

  • ○

    Single NSM, customer-value framed, leading

    Passes the three-question test (customer-success, marketing-resilient, celebrate-alone).

  • ○

    3–5 input metrics, each squad-owned

    Decomposed via breadth × depth × frequency × efficiency or similar.

  • ○

    2–3 counter-metrics with explicit floors

    Quality / error / support — anything that gaming the NSM would degrade.

  • ○

    Input → NSM regression validated quarterly

    R² ≥ 0.5 ideally; inputs with low predictive power are replaced.

  • ○

    Required field in every product brief

    "Expected NSM impact" + "counter-metric risk" — no waiver without leadership.

  • ○

    Weekly review with anomaly investigation

    Movement > ±5% triggers 48-hour root-cause.

  • ○

    NSM stable for ≥ 4 quarters

    Changes are quarterly leadership decisions, never mid-quarter pivots.
