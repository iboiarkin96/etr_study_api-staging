Group A · 5 practices

System design & patterns

How architects design systems that scale, evolve, and stay understandable. Domain-driven design, event-driven patterns, fitness functions, and formal diagramming are the craft layer of the role. When this group is weak, systems accumulate accidental complexity, teams step on each other's domains, and the next refactor takes months. Strong system design is the foundation every other group relies on.

01

Domain-Driven Design — model the business first

3.5→9.0

What it is. DDD is a design approach by Eric Evans (2003) in which the software model mirrors the business domain. A shared ubiquitous language eliminates translation friction between domain experts and engineers. Bounded contexts define explicit model boundaries; context maps show how contexts interact.

Why it matters. Without domain boundaries, microservices share database tables, the same concept gets three different names, and every cross-team change triggers a coordination spiral. DDD gives teams autonomy with clear ownership.

How to apply. Run an Event Storming workshop to discover bounded contexts. Draw the context map. Assign one owning team per context. Write a ubiquitous language glossary. Enforce boundaries in code through anti-corruption layers.

02

Event-Driven Architecture — decouple through events

3.0→8.5

What it is. Services communicate by publishing and consuming immutable domain events rather than calling each other directly. CQRS separates the write model (commands) from the read model (projections). Event sourcing stores the full history of state changes as an ordered log of events.

Why it matters. Synchronous point-to-point calls create brittle coupling — one slow service cascades into a full outage. Events decouple availability, enable temporal decoupling, and make audit trails free.

How to apply. Define a domain event vocabulary (AsyncAPI). Pick a broker (Kafka / SQS). Implement at least one consumer group with at-least-once delivery. Add schema registry for backwards compatibility.

03

C4 Model — architecture diagrams as code

4.0→9.0

What it is. The C4 model by Simon Brown gives four levels: Context (the system in the world), Container (what the system is made of), Component (internals of a container), and Code (class-level, rarely needed). Diagrams are stored as PlantUML or Structurizr DSL in the repo and rendered in CI.

Why it matters. A new engineer reading a C4 Context diagram understands the system in five minutes instead of five days. Without it, architecture knowledge lives only in the heads of the three people who were in the room.

How to apply. Start with one Context and one Container diagram per service. Store as PlantUML. Render in CI. Add a diagram review to the PR checklist for any change that modifies a container boundary.

04

Fitness Functions — guide evolutionary architecture

2.0→8.0

What it is. Fitness functions (from Ford, Parsons & Kua's Building Evolutionary Architectures, 2017) are automated tests that verify architectural characteristics — latency budgets, coupling limits, security posture, and dependency rules — on every CI run. They make architectural drift visible immediately.

Why it matters. Manual architecture reviews catch violations weeks after they are introduced. Fitness functions catch them in the PR. The architecture stops being a slide deck and becomes a failing test.

How to apply. Start with three tests: a latency assertion (p99 < 200 ms), a cyclic dependency check, and an allowed-dependency allowlist. Run them in CI on every PR. Expand when violations are found.

05

Hexagonal Architecture — isolate the domain core

2.5→8.5

What it is. Hexagonal Architecture (Ports & Adapters), coined by Alistair Cockburn in 2005, separates the pure domain model from all external dependencies. The domain has no import of frameworks, databases, or HTTP clients. Adapters translate between the domain's ports and the outside world.

Why it matters. When domain logic is entangled with Spring annotations or SQL queries, migrating databases takes months. Hexagonal architecture makes the core independently testable and technology-agnostic.

How to apply. Define ports as interfaces. Implement adapters (REST, DB, queue) separately. Run domain unit tests with no framework dependencies. Measure: domain test suite should run in < 500 ms with no I/O.

Technology strategy

How architects make and communicate technology choices. Radar, build-vs-buy, roadmapping, PoC discipline, and API-first design are the decision-making layer of the role. When this group is weak, technology choices happen ad-hoc, the stack becomes a patchwork of experiments, and the roadmap is a wish list with no signal-to-noise ratio.

06

Technology Radar — portfolio of tech choices

3.0→8.5

What it is. A Technology Radar (pioneered by ThoughtWorks in 2010) is a visual assessment of the tech portfolio across four rings: Adopt, Trial, Assess, Hold. Reviewed quarterly by the architecture guild, it makes technology choices visible and reversible — the team can see which experiments to invest in and which to stop.

Why it matters. Without a radar, the stack quietly accumulates every library someone tried on a weekend project. The next on-call engineer finds six HTTP clients and no policy on which to use.

How to apply. Start with current libraries in tech-radar.json. Mark obvious HOLDs. Schedule a 30-minute quarterly review. Publish the radar as a static page via the Zalando renderer.

07

Build vs Buy vs OSS — structured decision framework

4.5→8.5

What it is. A structured framework for evaluating whether to build, buy, or adopt open-source for any significant system component. Key dimensions: differentiation (is this a competitive advantage?), TCO, vendor lock-in risk, integration complexity, and team capability. Documented as an ADR with a scored comparison table.

Why it matters. Startups fail by building infrastructure that commodities provide for free, and by buying SaaS products that create lock-in for core differentiators. The framework prevents both failure modes.

How to apply. Use a 5-column ADR: option, differentiation score, 3-year TCO estimate, lock-in risk, verdict. Require this for any component above $5k/year or 2 engineer-months to build.

08

Technical Roadmapping — align tech with business

3.5→9.0

What it is. A rolling 12-month technical roadmap that shows infrastructure investments, platform migrations, and capability builds alongside the product roadmap. Each item is tied to a business outcome (e.g. "enable 10× traffic") and has a cost-of-delay estimate to help prioritise.

Why it matters. Without a technical roadmap, engineering debt accumulates invisibly until a crisis forces a rewrite. A visible roadmap lets executives make informed trade-offs between features and platform work.

How to apply. Use a Now/Next/Later format. One row per initiative with: outcome, cost-of-delay, owner, and status. Review monthly with CTO. Share with product quarterly.

09

PoC & Spike — de-risk before committing

4.0→8.0

What it is. A Proof of Concept (PoC) answers "can we build it?" — a focused experiment with a clear success criterion and a defined throw-away policy. A Spike (from XP) answers "how long will it take?" — a time-boxed exploration (max 2 days) with a written output. Both are explicitly not production code.

Why it matters. Architecture mistakes committed to production cost 10–100× more to fix than if they were caught in a 2-day PoC. The discipline of "write it down and throw it away" prevents PoC code from silently becoming production code.

How to apply. Define the question before starting. Set a time box (1–3 days). Write a one-page output: what was learned, what the answer is, recommendation for/against proceeding. Never promote PoC code to prod without rewrite.

10

API-First Design — contract before code

4.5→9.5

What it is. API-First means the OpenAPI (or AsyncAPI) contract is written, reviewed, and agreed before any implementation begins. The API becomes the primary design artifact. Client SDKs, mock servers, and documentation are generated from the spec. Drift between spec and implementation is caught by contract tests in CI.

Why it matters. Startups that code first and document later accumulate API debt that makes every new integration expensive. An agreed contract lets mobile and backend teams work in parallel from day one.

How to apply. Use OpenAPI 3.1. Lint with Spectral. Generate a mock server (Prism). Add a contract test step to CI. Generate the public reference docs from the spec on every release.

11

Architecture Decision Records — numbered, durable

5.0→9.0

What it is. An ADR is a short numbered file with three sections: Context, Decision, Consequences. Michael Nygard introduced the format in 2011. Each ADR has a permanent number, a status (proposed / accepted / superseded), and is stored in the repo alongside the code it describes. It is never deleted — only superseded.

Why it matters. A year from now, the team will not remember why the project chose gRPC over REST for internal services. The ADR remembers. New hires read ADR 0042 and stop asking the question in chat.

How to apply. Use MADR 4.0 template. Store in docs/adr/. Require an ADR for any decision that affects more than one team or is hard to reverse. Generate an index page in CI.

12

RFC Process — collaborative design before code

3.5→8.5

What it is. An RFC (Request for Comments) is a written proposal for a significant technical decision, open for structured commentary before it is accepted or rejected. Used by Rust, React, Node.js, and most large open-source projects. The RFC separates "what should we do" (RFC) from "here is what we decided" (ADR).

Why it matters. Without an RFC process, significant changes get decided in hallway conversations. Critical concerns surface only after implementation begins. Written proposals force precision and create a forum for asynchronous, cross-team input.

How to apply. Define a minimum RFC template (problem, proposal, alternatives, risks). Assign a 1-week comment window. Accept/reject with a written rationale. Archive as a linked ADR on acceptance.

13

Architecture Review — structured change governance

3.0→8.5

What it is. A lightweight Architecture Review Board (ARB) or design review process that evaluates significant changes before they reach production. Not a heavyweight gate — a lean, async-first review with a defined scope (changes crossing service boundaries, new dependencies, data model changes), a checklist, and a 48-hour SLA.

Why it matters. Without a review process, teams make autonomous decisions that look locally correct but create global problems. Security gaps, cross-team coupling, and data model conflicts are caught after the fact, when they are expensive to fix.

How to apply. Define when a review is required (scope checklist). Use a shared async channel. Keep reviews to a 2-page design doc + a checklist. SLA: 48 hours for feedback, 5 days for decision.

14

Stakeholder Mapping — communicate by audience

4.0→9.0

What it is. A stakeholder map categorises every audience by influence and interest using a Power-Interest grid. For each quadrant, a communication strategy is defined: what to tell them, how often, and in what format. Architects who skip this end up writing 30-page design docs for audiences who need a 3-slide deck.

Why it matters. Architecture work that is not communicated at the right level of abstraction to the right audience stays invisible. Invisible work does not get funded. Stakeholder mapping is the difference between technical excellence and organisational influence.

How to apply. Draw the Power-Interest grid for your current initiative. For each quadrant, define format and cadence. For High-Power/Low-Interest (executives): one-pager monthly. For High-Power/High-Interest (engineers): full design doc.

15

Design Docs — discuss before you build

3.5→8.5

What it is. A Design Doc is a 2–10 page written proposal covering context, problem, 2–3 options with trade-offs, the chosen path, success metrics, risks, and open questions. Written before implementation begins. Made canonical at Google, where any engineer can write a design doc and any engineer can comment on it.

Why it matters. When designs are discussed in writing first, wrong paths are caught before a week of coding, not after. A reader who returns a year later can see the rejected alternatives and the reason for the choice.

How to apply. Add a design-docs/ folder. Use a template with required sections. Require a design doc for any change touching more than one service or introducing a new dependency. Link each design doc to its resulting ADR.

16

Non-Functional Requirements — make the ilities explicit

3.5→9.0

What it is. Non-Functional Requirements (NFRs) are the measurable quality attributes of a system: performance (p99 latency ≤ 200 ms), availability (99.9%), scalability (10k RPM), security (no stored plaintext passwords), and maintainability (MTTR ≤ 1 hour). Captured per service, validated in CI, and reviewed at architecture review.

Why it matters. Without explicit NFRs, "fast enough" and "secure enough" mean different things to every engineer on the team. NFRs turn implicit expectations into testable acceptance criteria.

How to apply. Define NFRs per service in a YAML file. Categories: performance, availability, security, scalability, maintainability. Add an NFR review to the architecture review checklist. Automate the measurable ones in CI.

17

SLO & SLA Design — reliability as a contract

3.0→9.0

What it is. SLOs (Service Level Objectives) are internal reliability targets the team commits to: "99.9% of API requests complete in under 200 ms". SLAs are contractual commitments to customers. Error budgets (100% − SLO) define how much unreliability is acceptable per month before stopping feature work. Introduced by Google SRE.

Why it matters. Without SLOs, on-call alerts are noise: everything fires, nothing is prioritised. With SLOs, the team has a shared definition of "the system is healthy" and an automatic trigger to slow down feature work when reliability is degrading.

How to apply. Define one SLO per critical user journey. Pick three SLIs: availability, latency, and error rate. Set error budgets. Alert on error budget burn rate, not raw error rate. Review monthly.

18

Chaos Engineering — break it before users do

2.0→8.0

What it is. Chaos Engineering is the practice of intentionally injecting failures into production or staging environments to verify that the system handles them gracefully. Pioneered by Netflix's Chaos Monkey (2011). A chaos experiment follows the scientific method: steady state, hypothesis, blast radius, analysis, and remediation.

Why it matters. Systems that are never tested under failure conditions accumulate hidden dependencies. Chaos Engineering surfaces cascading failures, timeout misconfigurations, and missing circuit breakers before a real outage does.

How to apply. Start with GameDays (planned, manual experiments). Define steady state for each service. Inject one failure type per experiment (kill a pod, throttle a dependency). Run first experiments in staging. Graduate to prod after 3 successful staging runs.

19

Threat Modeling — security by design

2.5→8.5

What it is. Threat modeling is a structured process for identifying security threats before building. STRIDE (Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information disclosure, Denial of service, Elevation of privilege), developed by Microsoft, is the canonical framework. Applied per service at design time, producing a threat model document with mitigations.

Why it matters. Security vulnerabilities found in production cost 100× more to fix than ones caught at design time. Threat modeling shifts security left and gives the team a shared threat vocabulary.

How to apply. Run a 2-hour STRIDE session per new service using the DFD (Data Flow Diagram) as input. Assign a risk score (CVSS or DREAD) to each threat. Record mitigations as security requirements in the design doc. Review annually.

20

Capacity Planning & FinOps — design for cost

3.0→8.5

What it is. Capacity planning estimates future resource needs (compute, storage, bandwidth) based on growth projections and measured utilisation. FinOps (Financial Operations for cloud) combines engineering and finance to optimise cloud spend. Together they prevent both over-provisioning (wasted money) and under-provisioning (outages at scale).

Why it matters. Cloud bills that surprise the CFO kill startup growth. Architects who cannot model capacity become a blocker when the board asks "can we handle 10× traffic for Black Friday?"

How to apply. Write a capacity plan per critical service: current utilisation, 6-month projection, headroom required. Tag all cloud resources with team and service. Review cloud cost weekly. Set a unit cost baseline (cost per request / cost per active user).

21

Tech Debt Management — make it visible and prioritised

4.0→8.5

What it is. Tech debt management is the practice of cataloguing, quantifying, and prioritising technical liabilities in a structured register. Ward Cunningham coined the term in 1992. Each debt item has an owner, an interest rate (how much it slows development per sprint), and a payoff cost (engineer-days to resolve). Reviewed monthly with engineering leadership.

Why it matters. Invisible tech debt slows every feature. When debt is visible and quantified, the team can make informed trade-offs: "this legacy module costs us 2 days per sprint — it pays back in 3 months if we replace it."

How to apply. Keep a debt register in a shared doc. Each entry: description, interest rate (days/sprint), payoff cost, owner, priority. Review at monthly engineering all-hands. Allocate 15–20% of sprint capacity to debt payoff.

22

Engineering Principles — shared design standards

4.5→9.0

What it is. Engineering principles are short, opinionated statements that define how the team makes design decisions. Examples: "prefer boring technology", "delete more code than you add", "optimise for developer experience". They are living documents — reviewed annually and updated when a principle consistently fails in practice.

Why it matters. Without shared principles, each engineer brings different defaults from their last job. Code reviews become battles of preference. Principles replace preference battles with documented intent.

How to apply. Start with 10 principles. Write each as an imperative sentence + a 2-sentence rationale. Cite the principle in code review when enforcing it. Review annually; retire any principle the team never cites.

23

Team Topologies — design the org for fast flow

3.0→9.0

What it is. Team Topologies (Skelton & Pais, 2019) defines four team types — Stream-aligned, Platform, Enabling, and Complicated Subsystem — and three interaction modes (Collaboration, X-as-a-Service, Facilitating). It makes Conway's Law explicit and intentional: the architecture is the team structure.

Why it matters. When team structure does not match the desired architecture, engineers work against the grain. Every cross-team dependency is a coordination tax. Team Topologies makes that tax visible and gives the architect a vocabulary to redesign it.

How to apply. Map current team types and interaction modes. Identify where X-as-a-Service should replace Collaboration (reduces cognitive load). Define Platform team scope. Review team boundaries when adding or splitting teams.

24

Architecture Mentoring — grow the next architects

3.0→8.5

What it is. Architecture mentoring is a deliberate practice of transferring architectural thinking to senior engineers who are growing into architect roles. It combines: pair-design sessions, architecture reading groups, shadowed design reviews, and regular 1:1s with explicit growth goals. Codified by Will Larson in Staff Engineer (2021).

Why it matters. Architects who do not mentor become bottlenecks. Every design decision funnels through one person, slowing the company. A team of 10 engineers who think architecturally moves faster than a solo architect with 10 implementors.

How to apply. Identify 2–3 engineers who want to grow into architecture. Invite them to design reviews as active participants (not observers). Assign them one design doc per quarter. Give structured feedback on the doc, not just the result.

25

Architecture Retrospective — learn from the system

2.5→8.0

What it is. An architecture retrospective is a quarterly structured review of the system's architectural health: which decisions aged well, which aged poorly, what new constraints emerged, and what the team would do differently. Distinct from a team retrospective — it focuses on the system, not the process. Output: an updated tech debt register and one ADR per decision reversed.

Why it matters. Architecture without feedback loops accumulates regret. Retrospectives make it safe to say "we were wrong in ADR 0017" and turn that admission into a learning artefact rather than a recurring frustration.

How to apply. Run quarterly (90 min). Format: 20 min what aged well, 30 min what aged poorly, 30 min what changed in constraints, 10 min output. Write one ADR per decision being reversed. Share the output with all engineers.
