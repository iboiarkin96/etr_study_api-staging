Chaos engineering — break it before users do

Verify resilience by intentionally injecting failures, surfacing the cascading dependencies a real outage would otherwise reveal at 3 am.

TL;DR

Chaos engineering injects failures into staging or production to verify the system handles them gracefully. Pioneered by Netflix's Chaos Monkey in 2011 and formalised in the 2017 Principles of Chaos Engineering, it follows the scientific method: steady state → hypothesis → bounded blast radius → inject → analyse → remediate. Systems never tested under failure accumulate hidden dependencies; chaos engineering surfaces them in a controlled experiment instead of at 3 am on a Sunday.

What it is

Chaos engineering is a structured discipline for testing system resilience. The Principles of Chaos Engineering (Rosenthal et al., 2017) define it as "the discipline of experimenting on a system in order to build confidence in the system's capability to withstand turbulent conditions in production." Five principles guide the practice:

  • Build a hypothesis around steady-state behavior — start with a quantitative definition of "normal" (orders per minute, p99 latency, login success ratio).
  • Vary real-world events — failures that actually happen: instance crashes, network partitions, dependency slowness, expired certs, region loss.
  • Run experiments in production — only production exhibits real traffic and real failure modes. Staging is a starting point, not the destination.
  • Automate experiments to run continuously — one experiment finds one issue; continuous experiments find issues as they appear.
  • Minimise blast radius — every experiment is bounded so a failed hypothesis cannot become an outage.

Game Days (planned, manual experiments with the team present) are the entry point. Continuous automated experiments (Chaos Monkey-style tools — Gremlin, AWS Fault Injection Service, LitmusChaos, Steadybit) are the mature state.

Why we need it

Systems that are never tested under failure conditions accumulate hidden dependencies. A team believes their service handles database failover; in reality the connection pool was misconfigured three months ago and the service hangs for 60 seconds on failover. Nobody knows until a real failover at 3 am on a Sunday.

Chaos engineering surfaces these issues in controlled conditions. The team runs an experiment, the service hangs, the team fixes the connection pool — in working hours, with the full team available, with no customer impact. Companies that adopt the discipline see a measurable reduction in production incident severity because the failure modes are discovered in experiments rather than in real outages.

How others do it

Netflix — the originator

Netflix created Chaos Monkey in 2011 after migrating from on-prem to AWS. The tool randomly terminates production instances during business hours to force engineers to build resilient services. Netflix later extended this to Chaos Kong (regional failures), Latency Monkey (network slowness), and the broader Simian Army. Their resilience to AWS regional outages (the 2012 Christmas Eve event being the most famous public test) is directly attributable to years of Chaos Monkey-induced engineering.

What we take: Start with instance termination as the first experiment type. It is the simplest, has the broadest applicability, and surfaces the most issues per hour invested. Graduate to network partitions and dependency slowness only after instance termination is fully solved.

Source: Netflix Chaos Monkey

Principles of Chaos — industry consortium

The Principles of Chaos Engineering, published at principlesofchaos.org in 2017, was authored by a working group from Netflix, Google, Microsoft, and other companies running chaos at scale. The principles became the canonical definition of the discipline. The companion book Chaos Engineering: System Resiliency in Practice (Rosenthal & Jones, O'Reilly, 2020) is the reference text. The community runs the annual ChaosConf and maintains the open-source LitmusChaos toolchain (now a CNCF graduated project).

What we take: Use the five principles as a checklist for every experiment design. The most common failure mode is skipping the "minimise blast radius" principle — running an experiment with no kill switch and no bounded impact.

Source: Principles of Chaos Engineering

AWS Fault Injection Service

AWS launched Fault Injection Service (FIS) as a managed chaos engineering service in 2021. It provides built-in actions for terminating EC2 instances, throttling network bandwidth, stopping RDS instances, and injecting latency. The service includes safety mechanisms — stop conditions on CloudWatch alarms that automatically halt experiments when the blast radius exceeds the configured threshold. AWS FIS lowered the operational bar for adopting chaos engineering: teams no longer need to build the experiment infrastructure or the kill-switch plumbing themselves.

What we take: Use a managed chaos tool (AWS FIS, Gremlin, Harness Chaos) rather than building your own. The managed safety mechanisms — auto-stop on alarms — are valuable enough on their own to justify the spend.

Source: AWS Fault Injection Service

Shopify — Game Days

Shopify runs quarterly company-wide Game Days where engineering teams simulate Black Friday-scale traffic plus a major failure (region loss, payment-provider outage, database failover). Their public posts describe the discipline: a written experiment plan reviewed in advance, an incident-commander role rotated each Game Day, a single Slack channel for the run, a same-day blameless retro. The Game Day cadence forces resilience to be an attribute of the system, not just an aspiration.

What we take: Adopt Game Days as the entry point. Quarterly company-wide; monthly per-service. The ritual matters as much as the tool — it is the place where the team learns to operate under failure together.

Source: Shopify Engineering blog

Anatomy of a chaos experiment

Steady-state metric

A quantitative baseline for "the system is healthy". Typically the same SLI used for the SLO (success ratio, p99 latency). Defined before the experiment; the experiment compares post-injection metrics to this baseline.

Hypothesis statement

A specific, falsifiable prediction. "When we terminate one EC2 instance in the order-service auto-scaling group, the success rate will remain above 99% within 60 seconds." Vague hypotheses produce vague experiments.

Blast-radius bound

Explicit limits on impact: one instance at a time, one AZ, business hours only, kill switch wired to CloudWatch alarms. The blast radius is documented in the plan, agreed by the team, and enforced by tooling.

Kill switch

An automated stop wired to the steady-state alarm. If the SLI degrades past the threshold during injection, the experiment halts immediately. The kill switch is tested in advance — an untested kill switch is no kill switch.

Game Day ritual

A scheduled, manual experiment with the team present. Typical cadence: monthly per service for the first 6 months, then quarterly. Incident-commander role rotated each Game Day; one Slack channel for the run; same-day retro.

Action-item tracking

Every experiment produces 1–3 action items: misconfigurations to fix, missing circuit breakers, alerting gaps. Tracked to completion before the next Game Day. An experiment that produces zero actions tested nothing interesting — the hypothesis was too easy.

How to run it

  1. Pick one critical service (Week 1). Highest-blast-radius outage candidate. Map its dependencies. Define a steady-state SLI tied to the SLO.
  2. Write the experiment plan (Week 2). One page: steady state, hypothesis, blast-radius bounds, kill-switch criteria, who runs, who watches, rollback procedure. Reviewed by SRE + service owner.
  3. Stage-run three times (Week 3–4). Execute in staging. Confirm the kill switch works. Confirm the steady-state alarm fires. Three clean runs before any production attempt.
  4. Run Game Day 1 in production (Week 5). Business hours, full team present, single Slack channel, incident-commander assigned. Inject the minimum-bounded fault. Watch SLIs. Halt at first signal of degradation past threshold.
  5. Same-day blameless retro (Week 5). What happened vs what the hypothesis predicted. Action items captured with owners and due dates. No-blame framing.
  6. Track actions to closure (Week 6+). Action items become tickets in the service backlog. Reviewed at the next Game Day. Closed-loop is what turns the experiment into improvement.
  7. Repeat monthly; expand scope (Month 2+). New hypothesis each month: dependency slowness, network partition, region failover. After 6 successful Game Days, evaluate continuous automated chaos via AWS FIS / Gremlin.

Deep dive — what makes the discipline work

The scientific method is the discipline. Chaos engineering is not "kill a server and see what happens". It is a hypothesis-driven experiment with a falsifiable claim, a bounded blast radius, and a kill switch. The discipline is what separates resilient learning from a self-inflicted outage. Skip the hypothesis and you cannot tell whether the experiment succeeded or failed — you just know something happened.

Bounded blast radius is non-negotiable. Every experiment must answer four questions before injection: (1) what is the smallest fault that tests the hypothesis? (2) what is the steady-state metric and its threshold? (3) what halts the experiment automatically? (4) what is the manual rollback if automation fails? An experiment without all four is not an experiment — it is gambling.

Staging proves the harness; production proves the system. Three clean staging runs prove the kill switch, the alarms, the rollback. Production runs prove the system. Teams that skip staging produce customer-visible incidents on the first try; teams that never graduate from staging never learn anything new because staging traffic is not real traffic.

Game Day ritual matters as much as the tool. The Game Day is when the team learns to operate under failure together. Incident-commander role rotated, single Slack channel, same-day blameless retro, action items with owners. The ritual builds the muscle memory that the next real incident will demand. The tool is interchangeable; the ritual is not.

Closed-loop or it does not count. An experiment that produces three action items that never get done is worse than no experiment — it wastes the team's time and trains them to ignore findings. Action items become tickets with owners and due dates; reviewed at the next Game Day; closed before the next experiment starts. The closed loop is what turns chaos engineering from theatre into engineering.

Continuous automated chaos is the mature state, not the entry point. Chaos Monkey-style random termination in production is what mature teams run. New teams that start there before they have run manual Game Days produce outages they cannot explain. Earn the right to automate by running 6+ successful Game Days first.

Lifecycle of an experiment

Designed

Plan written: steady state, hypothesis, blast radius, kill switch, rollback. Reviewed by SRE + service owner.

Staged

Three runs in staging. Kill switch verified. Steady-state alarm verified. Rollback rehearsed.

Game Day

Production run, business hours, full team present. Single Slack channel. Incident-commander assigned. Inject minimum bounded fault.

Retro

Same-day blameless review. What was predicted vs what happened. 1–3 action items with owners and due dates.

Closed

Actions delivered before the next Game Day. Experiment retired or scheduled for re-run after fixes. Findings published to the team.

Automated

After 6+ successful Game Days the experiment graduates to a continuous automated run (AWS FIS, Gremlin) with the same kill-switch wiring.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
Running experiments without a kill switch Team injects a failure and discovers the blast radius is larger than expected. There is no automated stop. The experiment becomes a real outage. Configure auto-stop on a CloudWatch alarm (or equivalent) before starting. The experiment halts when the steady-state metric degrades past threshold. Test the kill switch in advance — an untested kill switch is no kill switch.
Starting in production before staging is stable Team is eager to "run chaos in prod" and skips staging. The first production experiment causes a customer-visible incident. Run the experiment in staging at least three times successfully before promoting to production. Staging reveals the obvious failures; production reveals the subtle ones. Skip neither.
Experiments designed to confirm, not test Hypothesis is "the system handles this failure" and the experiment is designed to succeed. The team congratulates themselves; nothing is learned. Frame hypotheses to be falsifiable. The experiment must be capable of producing a negative result. Design around scenarios the team is genuinely uncertain about, not scenarios they are confident in.
Action items not tracked to closure The experiment produces three findings; they get a Slack mention and never become tickets. Next Game Day finds the same issue. Every finding becomes a ticket with an owner and a due date. Reviewed at the next Game Day. Closed loop is what turns chaos engineering from theatre into engineering.
Jumping straight to continuous automated chaos Team copies Netflix Chaos Monkey without first running manual Game Days. Production failures with no team context become incidents nobody can explain. Earn the right to automate. Run 6+ successful Game Days first. Graduate single experiments to continuous runs one at a time, with the same kill-switch wiring as the manual versions.
Blameful retros after a failed experiment Experiment surfaces an embarrassing misconfiguration. Retro turns into a finger-pointing session. Team avoids chaos engineering after that. Blameless framing is mandatory. Findings are properties of the system, not the engineer. The experiment did its job — it surfaced a real issue. Frame retros around "what does the system need" not "who messed up".

Worked example — lesson-player DB failover

A Game Day plan for the lesson-player service. Hypothesis: when the primary RDS Postgres instance fails over to the replica, the player API maintains ≥ 99% success ratio within 60 seconds.

# GD-007 · Lesson-player RDS failover
date:        2026-05-26 · 10:00–11:30 EU
incident-commander: @anna (rotating)
service:     lesson-player
owner:       @oleg (player team)

## Steady state
SLI: 5-min rolling success ratio on /lesson/{id}/state
Baseline: ≥ 99.5%   Alarm threshold: < 99.0% sustained 60 s

## Hypothesis
When the primary RDS Postgres instance is failed over to the replica
(simulated via AWS FIS aws:rds:failover-db-cluster), the player API
maintains success ratio ≥ 99% within 60 seconds.

## Blast-radius bounds
- staging cluster only for runs 1–3; production for run 4
- single AZ; failover to in-region replica only
- business hours (10:00–11:30 EU)
- KILL SWITCH: CloudWatch alarm on success ratio < 99% / 60 s
              → AWS FIS stop-experiment + restore primary

## Procedure
1. T-15 min: announce in #chaos-gameday; verify alarm; verify kill switch
2. T-5 min:  confirm steady state via Grafana
3. T0:       trigger failover via AWS FIS
4. T0–T120s: watch success ratio; if < 99%, kill switch fires
5. T+10 min: confirm recovery; capture logs
6. T+30 min: blameless retro; capture action items

## Predicted outcome
Success ratio dips to ~95% for ~20 s during DNS swap, recovers
to ≥ 99% within 60 s. If recovery exceeds 60 s, the connection pool
is misconfigured — investigate idle-timeout vs DNS TTL.

## Actual outcome (post-run)
Run 1 (staging):  recovery in 38 s. ✓
Run 2 (staging):  recovery in 42 s. ✓
Run 3 (staging):  recovery in 35 s. ✓
Run 4 (prod):     recovery in 78 s. ✗ — kill switch fired at 62 s.

## Action items
A1  pool idle-timeout (3600 s) > DNS TTL (30 s) — reduce to 60 s
    owner: @oleg · due: 2026-06-02
A2  add DNS-cache-flush hook on RDS failover event
    owner: @anna · due: 2026-06-09
A3  add success-ratio panel to player-api Grafana home
    owner: @oleg · due: 2026-05-30

## Findings
Closed loop: re-run GD-007 after A1+A2 ship.

The plan is deliberately one page. The discipline is in the structure — steady state, hypothesis, bounds, kill switch, procedure, prediction, actuals, actions — not in the prose. The same template applies to every Game Day.

Quality bar

  • Every experiment has a written plan. Steady state, hypothesis, blast-radius bounds, kill switch, rollback, owners. Reviewed by SRE + service owner before injection.
  • Kill switch is automated and tested. Auto-stop on a CloudWatch alarm; verified during the staging runs; never untested in production.
  • Three clean staging runs before production. No production experiment runs without three successful staging runs of the same experiment.
  • Hypothesis is falsifiable. The experiment is capable of producing a negative result; the team is genuinely uncertain about the outcome going in.
  • Monthly Game Day cadence per critical service. Each Tier-1 service runs one Game Day per month for the first 6 months, then quarterly.
  • Action items tracked to closure. Every Game Day produces 1–3 action items as tickets with owners and due dates; closed before the next Game Day.
  • Blameless retros. Findings framed as system properties, not engineer mistakes. The retro happens same-day while context is fresh.
