SLO & SLA design — reliability as a contract

A shared, measurable definition of "the system is healthy" that turns reliability into an engineering decision rather than a feeling.

TL;DR

SLOs (Service Level Objectives) are internal reliability targets backed by SLIs (Service Level Indicators) — measurable signals like availability or latency. SLAs are contractual commitments to customers, set deliberately looser than SLOs. The error budget (100% − SLO) defines how much unreliability is acceptable per month; once spent, feature work pauses for reliability work. Codified by Google SRE in the early 2000s, the SLO/error-budget model turns reliability from a feeling into an engineering decision and ends alert-fatigue by firing only on budget burn.

What it is

Four related concepts define modern reliability engineering:

  • SLI (Service Level Indicator) — a measured signal, typically a ratio: "good events / total events". Example: 2xx responses / total requests.
  • SLO (Service Level Objective) — a target for the SLI over a rolling window. Example: "99.9% of requests return 2xx over a rolling 30-day window".
  • Error budget — 100% minus the SLO. The allowed amount of failure. A 99.9% SLO over 30 days has a 0.1% error budget ≈ 43 minutes of downtime per month.
  • SLA (Service Level Agreement) — a contractual commitment to a customer, usually with financial penalties. SLAs are deliberately set looser than SLOs (e.g. SLO 99.9%, SLA 99.5%) to leave engineering headroom.

The error budget is the operational mechanism. When the team is well within budget, fast feature delivery is encouraged. When the budget is consumed, the team prioritises reliability work — bug fixes, reliability improvements — until budget is restored. The model creates an automatic feedback loop between feature velocity and reliability.

Why we need it

Without SLOs, on-call alerts are noise. Every error fires; the team becomes desensitised; real outages are missed in the chatter. There is no shared definition of "healthy" — one engineer thinks 99% is fine, another insists on 99.99%. Stakeholder conversations about reliability ("are we reliable enough?") have no anchor.

SLOs anchor the conversation. "We're at 99.92% against a 99.9% SLO" is a precise answer. The error budget gives the team a tool to negotiate trade-offs: when budget is plentiful, ship features; when budget is exhausted, slow down. This automatic regulation prevents both extremes — chronic reliability problems and chronic feature stagnation in pursuit of perfect uptime.

How others do it

Google SRE — originator

Google formalised the SLI/SLO/error-budget model in the early 2000s and published it externally in the Site Reliability Engineering book (2016) and the SRE Workbook (2018). Google operates thousands of SLOs across its services; the SRE team treats SLO design as a core engineering activity. Their guidance: pick three SLIs maximum per service (availability, latency, throughput or quality). More than that produces a dashboard nobody reads. Error budget enforced operationally — when budget is exhausted, feature work pauses until budget is restored.

What we take: Start with three SLIs maximum per service: availability, latency, and either correctness or throughput. Adding a fourth SLI before the first three are stable is premature.

Source: Google SRE Book — SLOs chapter

SLO Development Lifecycle (slodlc.com)

SLODLC is a community-driven framework from a working group at Google, Nobl9, and Mercari. Six stages: Discovery, Design, Implementation, Promotion, Observation, Iteration. Each has specific deliverables and decision criteria. The framework addresses the most common SLO failure: SLOs that are technically correct but never adopted because the lifecycle stopped at "implementation" without Promotion and Iteration phases.

What we take: SLO adoption requires explicit Promotion and Iteration phases. Designing an SLO is 20% of the work; making it the team's daily reference is the other 80%. Schedule monthly SLO reviews from the start.

Source: SLO Development Lifecycle

Stripe — five-nine SLA on payments

Stripe is publicly committed to a 99.999% SLA on payment processing — five nines, an extremely aggressive target. The engineering culture is built around defending this SLA: every team that touches the payment path operates SLOs an order of magnitude stricter than the SLA. Stripe also publishes an extensive status page with historical SLA reporting. Their public SLA commitment is a competitive differentiator and a major engineering constraint.

What we take: A public SLA is a marketing asset and a constraint. Set internal SLOs significantly stricter than the SLA — typically 0.1–0.5 percentage points stricter — to leave margin for the unexpected.

Source: Stripe status page

Multi-window burn-rate alerts (Google SRE 2018)

Google's SRE Workbook (2018) formalised multi-window, multi-burn-rate alerts: alert when 2% of the budget is consumed in 1 hour AND 5% in 6 hours. The combination catches fast burns (incidents) without firing on slow steady degradation that the team can address in working hours. The pattern has been adopted across the industry (Datadog, Grafana, Sloth, Pyrra) and effectively replaced static error-rate alerts as the modern reliability signal.

What we take: Replace static error-rate alerts with multi-window burn-rate alerts as soon as SLOs are in place. The new alerts are both quieter (no transient-error noise) and louder on real problems.

Source: SRE Workbook — Alerting on SLOs

Anatomy of an SLO/SLA set

SLI (measured signal)

A ratio: good events / total events. Typical choices: availability (2xx / total), latency (requests under threshold / total), correctness (validated outputs / total). Implemented as Prometheus queries or equivalent. Measured continuously.

SLO (target + window)

A target value for the SLI over a rolling time window. "99.9% over rolling 30 days" is canonical. Window matters: 30-day windows are stable enough to be actionable; 7-day windows are too noisy; 90-day windows are too slow to react to.

Error-budget policy

A written policy describing what happens when error budget is exhausted: feature work pauses, reliability work prioritises, post-mortem required, product leadership notified. The policy is the most important artefact — without it the SLO is informational, not operational.

Multi-window burn-rate alert

Alerts when the budget is consumed too fast. Google SRE pattern: 2% in 1 hour = page on-call; 5% in 6 hours = page on-call; 10% in 3 days = ticket. Calibrated to catch real problems without firing on transient errors. Replaces raw error-rate alerts.

SLA contract (external)

The customer-facing commitment with penalties for breach. Always looser than the internal SLO — typically 0.1–0.5 percentage points. Owned jointly by product, legal, and engineering. Reviewed annually at contract renewal.

Monthly SLO review

A 30-minute monthly review with engineering and product leadership. Reports SLO compliance, error-budget consumption, top causes of burn, any planned SLO adjustments. Without this ritual the SLO drifts from operational reality.

How to run it

  1. Pick the user journey (Week 1). Critical user-facing flow per service. SLOs measure what matters to the user, not what is easy to instrument.
  2. Define 1–3 SLIs (Week 1). Availability + latency at minimum. Optionally correctness or throughput. Each SLI is a good/total ratio backed by Prometheus or equivalent.
  3. Pick the SLO target with user research (Week 2). Derive from user tolerance, not current system capability. "Users abandon checkout after 3 s" → latency budget 3 s. 99.9% over rolling 30 days as the default window.
  4. Write the error-budget policy (Week 2). What happens when budget hits 0%? Feature freeze, reliability sprint, post-mortem, product leadership notified. Get product to sign off in writing.
  5. Wire multi-window burn-rate alerts (Week 3). Replace static error-rate alerts. 2%/1 h page; 5%/6 h page; 10%/3 d ticket. Validate against historical incidents.
  6. Set the SLA looser than the SLO (Week 3). 0.1–0.5 percentage points looser. Owned jointly with product and legal.
  7. Monthly SLO review (Month 1+). 30-minute meeting. Compliance, budget burn, top causes, adjustments. Without this the SLO drifts.
  8. Iterate quarterly. SLOs are not set-and-forget. Re-derive targets from new user data and system changes; retire SLIs that no longer reflect user-felt experience.

Deep dive — what makes an SLO operational

Three SLIs maximum per service. Google's guidance after decades of practice: availability, latency, and one of correctness or throughput. More SLIs than three produces a dashboard nobody reads and alerts nobody trusts. Resist the urge to instrument everything as an SLI — pick the user-felt ones and let the rest be diagnostic metrics.

Targets from user tolerance, not current capability. The most common mistake is setting the SLO at what the current system can comfortably hit. The customer's tolerance is what matters. "Users abandon after 3 s" is a latency budget; setting the SLO at the user's tolerance (3 s) regardless of what the system does today gives the team a real target. Easy SLOs feel safe and provide no signal.

The error-budget policy is the SLO. An SLO without a written policy describing what happens when the budget is spent is theatre. The policy must be signed by product leadership: feature freeze, reliability sprint, post-mortem required, named approver to lift the freeze. The first time the policy is enforced sets the precedent for the rest of the year — protect it.

Multi-window burn-rate alerts replace error-rate alerts. Static error-rate alerts ("> 1% errors for 5 min") produce alert fatigue: they fire on transient blips and miss slow burns. Multi-window burn-rate combines fast-burn detection (2%/1 h) with slow-burn detection (5%/6 h). Fast burns get paged; slow burns get tickets. The on-call experience transforms.

SLA looser than SLO, deliberately. SLA is what you promise the customer; SLO is what you promise yourself. The gap is your margin for change. A team that sets SLA = SLO has no headroom for refactors, migrations, or unexpected incidents — the next bad week becomes a contractual breach. 0.1–0.5 percentage points of margin is standard; more for fast-moving teams.

Match SLO to cost of failure. Internal admin tools: 99% is fine (87 hours/year). Customer-facing transactional: 99.9% (8.7 h/year). Payment processing: 99.99% (52 min/year). Each additional nine costs roughly 10× more — set the target at the cost of failure, not the cost of glory. Five-nine SLOs on internal tools waste budget that should fund the user-felt ones.

Promotion and Iteration are the missing 80%. SLODLC names what most teams skip: after Implementation, the SLO needs Promotion (onboarding, runbooks, dashboard linked from every channel) and Iteration (monthly review, quarterly target refresh). Designing the SLO is 20% of the work; making it the team's daily reference is the other 80%.

Lifecycle of an SLO

Discovered

User journey identified; candidate SLI proposed. User-tolerance research informs the target. Owner named.

Designed

SLI query + SLO target + window written down. Error-budget policy drafted. Reviewed with product leadership.

Implemented

Prometheus query landed. Burn-rate alerts wired. Dashboard live. SLO file in-repo.

Promoted

Onboarded in runbooks. Linked from on-call channel and engineering channel. Team trained on the alert types.

Active

Monthly review running. Burn alerts firing only on real budget burn. Policy enforced when budget hits 0%.

Iterated / Retired

Targets refreshed quarterly from new user data. SLIs no longer reflecting user experience are retired with a written note; superseded SLOs link to their successors.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
SLOs unrealistically high — 99.999% for everything Team aspires to "best in class" reliability and sets SLOs at five-nines across the board. Each additional 9 costs ~10× the previous. Team burns out chasing impossible targets. Set SLOs based on actual user need. Internal admin tools: 99% (3.6 d/year). Customer-facing transactional: 99.9% (8.7 h/year). Payment: 99.99% (52 min/year). Match target to cost of failure.
SLOs measured but not enforced — theatre Team publishes a dashboard but does not change behaviour when budget is consumed. Features keep shipping; dashboard becomes wallpaper. Reliability problems persist. Write the error-budget policy. Product leadership signs off on feature pause when budget hits 0%. First enforcement sets the precedent for the rest of the year.
SLOs derived from system capability, not user expectation Team picks SLO threshold from what the current system achieves. Comfortable target; does not represent customer tolerance. Customer leaves before SLO fires. Derive SLOs from user research or customer commitments. "Users abandon after 3 s" is a latency budget. Threshold inside user's tolerance, not at the edge of current capability.
Static error-rate alerts kept after SLOs land Team adds SLOs but keeps the old "alert on > 1% errors for 5 min" rules. Alert fatigue stays high; SLOs become decorative. Replace static alerts with multi-window burn-rate alerts (2%/1 h page; 5%/6 h page; 10%/3 d ticket). Validate against historical incidents before turning off the old alerts.
SLA set equal to SLO — no headroom Team promises customers the same number it tracks internally. First bad month becomes a contractual breach. No margin for migrations or refactors. SLA looser than SLO by 0.1–0.5 percentage points. Internal SLO is the engineering target; external SLA leaves room for the unexpected.
Too many SLIs — dashboard ignored Team picks 8 SLIs per service trying to cover everything. Nobody reads the dashboard. Alerts conflict. Reliability conversations drown in detail. Three SLIs maximum per service: availability + latency + one of correctness/throughput. Diagnostic metrics live elsewhere; SLIs are the user-felt headlines.

Worked example — lesson-player SLO set

Three SLOs covering the lesson-player critical user journey. Lives at services/lesson-player/slo.yaml; rendered into Grafana + the on-call runbook.

service: lesson-player
owner:   @oleg
reviewed: 2026-05-19
journey: "user opens a lesson, answers questions, sees mastery update"

slos:
  - id: SLO-01-availability
    description: "lesson-state endpoint succeeds"
    sli: |
      sum(rate(http_requests_total{
        service="lesson-player",
        endpoint="/lesson/{id}/state",
        status_code=~"2.."
      }[5m]))
      /
      sum(rate(http_requests_total{
        service="lesson-player",
        endpoint="/lesson/{id}/state"
      }[5m]))
    target:  "99.9% over rolling 30 days"
    budget:  "0.1% = ~43 min/month"
    sla:     "99.5% (looser by 0.4 pp)"

  - id: SLO-02-latency
    description: "p99 latency on lesson-state endpoint"
    sli: |
      histogram_quantile(0.99, sum(rate(
        http_request_duration_seconds_bucket{
          service="lesson-player",
          endpoint="/lesson/{id}/state"
        }[5m]
      )) by (le)) <= 0.2
    target:  "99% of 5-min windows have p99 ≤ 200 ms over rolling 30 d"
    budget:  "1% = ~7.2 h/month of slow 5-min windows"

  - id: SLO-03-correctness
    description: "attempt score persisted exactly once"
    sli: |
      sum(rate(attempt_score_persisted_total{result="success"}[5m]))
      /
      sum(rate(attempt_score_attempted_total[5m]))
    target:  "99.95% over rolling 30 days"
    budget:  "0.05% = ~22 min/month"

alerts:
  fast_burn:
    - "2% of monthly budget burned in 1 h  → page on-call"
    - "5% of monthly budget burned in 6 h  → page on-call"
  slow_burn:
    - "10% of monthly budget burned in 3 d → ticket on player team"

error_budget_policy:
  signed_by: "@product-lead 2026-05-12"
  rules:
    - "budget > 50% remaining: ship features normally"
    - "budget 10–50% remaining: prioritise reliability fixes in standup"
    - "budget < 10% remaining: feature freeze; reliability sprint;
       post-mortem required for each remaining incident"
    - "budget < 0%:  full freeze; product lead + architect approve
       any non-reliability merge"

monthly_review: "first Monday of month, 30 min, eng + product"

Three SLOs, signed error-budget policy, multi-window burn alerts, monthly review on the calendar. The file lives in-repo and is rendered into Grafana plus the on-call runbook — the SLO is operational, not decorative.

Quality bar

  • 1–3 SLIs per service. Availability + latency at minimum; one of correctness/throughput if it matters. More than three = redesign, not addition.
  • SLO target derived from user tolerance. Not from current system capability. User-research evidence cited.
  • Rolling 30-day window. 7-day too noisy; 90-day too slow. 30-day is the standard.
  • Written error-budget policy signed by product. What happens at 50% / 10% / 0% spelled out. Enforcement happens the first time.
  • Multi-window burn-rate alerts replace static error-rate alerts. 2%/1 h + 5%/6 h page; 10%/3 d ticket.
  • SLA looser than SLO by 0.1–0.5 pp. Internal tighter than external; engineering keeps headroom.
  • Monthly SLO review. 30-minute meeting. Compliance, burn, top causes, adjustments. Calendared.
  • Promotion + Iteration done. SLOs in runbook, linked from on-call channel; targets refreshed quarterly.
