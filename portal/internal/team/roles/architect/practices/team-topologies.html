Team Topologies — design the org for fast flow

Make Conway's Law explicit and intentional: the architecture is the team structure.

TL;DR

Team Topologies (Skelton & Pais, 2019) identifies four fundamental team types — Stream-aligned, Platform, Enabling, Complicated Subsystem — and three interaction modes (Collaboration, X-as-a-Service, Facilitating). The model makes Conway's Law explicit: the system architecture inevitably mirrors team structure. When team boundaries do not match the desired architecture, engineers work against the grain and every cross-team dependency is a coordination tax. Team Topologies gives the architect a vocabulary to redesign the structure deliberately.

What it is

Team Topologies is a model for designing software-team structure based on the realisation (Mel Conway, 1968) that "organisations design systems whose structure mirrors their communication structure". The four team types address different roles in the system:

  • Stream-aligned team — owns a value stream end-to-end. Aligned to a single, valuable flow of work (a product, feature set, or domain). The default; most teams should be stream-aligned.
  • Platform team — provides internal services (CI, observability, deployment) consumed by stream-aligned teams. Treats its outputs as a product with users, not as infrastructure to maintain.
  • Enabling team — grows capability in stream-aligned teams (security, performance, accessibility expertise). Time-limited engagements; the goal is to make the stream team self-sufficient.
  • Complicated Subsystem team — owns a component requiring deep specialism (ML systems, codecs, mathematical models). Justified only when the cognitive cost is too high for a stream-aligned team.

Three interaction modes describe how teams work together: Collaboration (high-bandwidth, short-term), X-as-a-Service (one team consumes another's product with minimal coordination), Facilitating (one team helps another build capability). The strategic goal is to maximise X-as-a-Service and minimise long-lived Collaboration — Collaboration is expensive coordination tax.

Why we need it

When team structure does not match the desired architecture, engineers work against the grain. A team responsible for "the checkout flow" but whose product depends on five other teams' services has five times the coordination cost of a team that owns the entire flow. Every architectural improvement requires negotiation across team boundaries. Conway's Law is not a complaint — it is a constraint the architecture has to work with.

Team Topologies gives the architect a vocabulary to redesign the structure deliberately. "We need to convert this Collaboration interaction to X-as-a-Service" is an actionable change that reduces coordination cost. Without the vocabulary, the same conversation devolves into vague complaints about meetings and process. Architects who can shape team structure unlock architectural improvements that would be impossible if the org were treated as fixed.

How others do it

Skelton & Pais — Team Topologies (2019)

Skelton and Pais published Team Topologies in 2019 (IT Revolution Press). The book synthesises DevOps practice, Conway's Law, and cognitive-load research into a concrete model. Their site provides workshops, certifications, and case studies. The model has been adopted at companies from 50 to 100 000 engineers — the patterns scale because they are based on cognitive-load constraints rather than process.

What we take: The cognitive-load argument is the most important contribution. Teams have fixed cognitive bandwidth; structure work so no team exceeds it. When a team is consistently overloaded, the answer is usually a different topology, not more headcount.

Source: Team Topologies — official site

Conway's Law — Mel Conway (1968)

Mel Conway's 1968 paper How Do Committees Invent? introduced what is now called Conway's Law: "Any organisation that designs a system will produce a design whose structure is a copy of the organisation's communication structure". Originally rejected by Harvard Business Review for "lack of scientific evidence", the law has since been empirically validated by studies from MIT and others. Team Topologies operationalises Conway's Law as a design tool — the "Inverse Conway Maneuver": design the team structure to produce the desired architecture.

What we take: Use the Inverse Conway Maneuver deliberately. Before changing the architecture, change team boundaries to match the target architecture. The architecture will follow within 6–12 months automatically.

Source: Fowler — Conway's Law

Spotify — Squad model

Spotify's Squad-Tribe-Chapter-Guild structure (popularised in 2014, before Team Topologies was published) anticipated many of the patterns. Squads are stream-aligned teams owning a value stream. Tribes group related Squads. Chapters are functional disciplines that share knowledge. The model has been widely copied (often poorly — see "Spotify Model Considered Harmful" critiques) but the underlying principle of stream-aligned teams with X-as-a-Service consumption of platform services is sound.

What we take: Copy the underlying principles, not the surface labels. "Squad" is a name; the meaningful question is whether the team is stream-aligned and has end-to-end ownership.

Source: Spotify Engineering Culture

Internal Developer Platform (IDP) movement

The Internal Developer Platform community (CNCF, Humanitec, Backstage from Spotify) operationalises the Platform-as-a-Product idea. IDPs expose a self-service catalogue of templates, environments, and pipelines that stream-aligned teams consume without raising tickets. Platform teams measure success in adoption + internal NPS rather than infra uptime alone. Backstage (Spotify open-source) is the de facto IDP framework; CNCF's Platform Engineering whitepaper formalises the model.

What we take: Adopt the Platform-as-a-Product mindset. Self-service catalogue (Backstage or equivalent) plus adoption/NPS metrics. Stream teams choose the platform because it's easier than working around it.

Source: Internal Developer Platform

Anatomy of a topology

Team-type mapping

Each team has an explicit type: Stream-aligned / Platform / Enabling / Complicated Subsystem. Default is Stream-aligned; the other types must justify their existence. Stored in a team registry next to membership and on-call rotation.

Interaction-mode map

A diagram showing each pair of interacting teams and the mode (Collaboration / X-as-a-Service / Facilitating). Collaboration interactions are explicitly bounded in time — "collaborate for 8 weeks, then transition to X-as-a-Service".

Cognitive-load assessment

Quarterly check on each team's cognitive load on a 1–5 scale. In scope: services owned, technologies used, on-call burden, cross-team dependencies. Teams scoring 4+ are overloaded and need scope reduction or topology change.

Platform-as-product metrics

Platform teams track adoption rate, internal NPS, docs quality, onboarding time. Platforms with low adoption are not "doing platform wrong" — they are products without product-market fit.

Team-type ratio

Published target ratio: ~80% stream-aligned, ~10–15% platform, ~5% enabling, <5% complicated subsystem. Variations are fine; a 30% platform-team ratio is a warning sign.

Annual topology review

Once-yearly review with engineering leadership. Inputs: cognitive-load data, dependency-map, cross-team retrospectives. Output: changes to team types, interaction modes, or boundaries for the next year. Without it, topology drifts.

How to run it

  1. Map the current topology (Week 1). List every team. Tag each as Stream-aligned / Platform / Enabling / Complicated Subsystem. Draw current interactions (Collaboration / XaaS / Facilitating) between them.
  2. Assess cognitive load (Week 1–2). Each team rates its own load 1–5 against services owned, technologies, on-call, dependencies. Teams scoring 4+ are flagged.
  3. Identify the most painful Collaboration interaction. The one that produces the most coordination meetings or the most cross-team escalations. That is your first conversion target.
  4. Design the target topology. Use the Inverse Conway Maneuver: draw the architecture you want, then map teams to its bounded contexts. Keep stream-aligned dominant (~80%).
  5. Convert the worst Collaboration into XaaS. One platform interface, documented, self-service. Stream teams consume; platform team owns. Set adoption + internal-NPS metrics.
  6. Time-bound Enabling engagements. Enabling teams set explicit start + end dates per engagement. End state: stream team self-sufficient on that capability.
  7. Re-balance ratios annually. The annual topology review checks ratios, cognitive load, dependency density. Adjust team types and boundaries for the next year.

Deep dive — what makes the topology hold

Cognitive load is the constraint. The most important Team Topologies contribution is naming cognitive load as the limiting resource. Teams have fixed bandwidth — services owned, languages, runtimes, on-call rotations, dependencies. When a team is overloaded, the answer is rarely more headcount; it is usually a different topology that reduces the load (XaaS platform absorbs the runtime; complicated subsystem absorbs the codec; stream-aligned scope is narrowed). Headcount alone does not fix overload because the load itself doesn't split.

Stream-aligned by default — everything else justifies itself. The 80% target for stream-aligned is non-negotiable. Every platform, enabling, or complicated-subsystem team must justify why a stream-aligned team can't own that work. The justification is real (genuine deep specialism, leverage across many stream teams) or it isn't — when ratios drift to 30% non-stream, the org has accidentally rebuilt the central-IT pattern Team Topologies was designed to replace.

X-as-a-Service is the desired steady state. Collaboration is appropriate during transitions — "let's work together for 8 weeks to figure this out". After that, the interaction should converge to X-as-a-Service: a platform interface, documented, self-service, with minimal cross-team meetings. Long-lived Collaboration is a sign that the work hasn't crystallised into a product. Name the transition date when Collaboration begins.

Platform-as-a-Product is the cultural shift. Platform teams that don't see stream teams as customers ship internal tools nobody uses. The shift requires three changes: adoption + NPS as success metrics (not lines shipped), a product manager or product-minded lead, and optional adoption — make the platform attractive enough that stream teams choose it. A platform that needs to be mandated has already failed product-market fit.

Enabling teams are time-bound by design. An enabling team that exists indefinitely has become a permanent dependency, which contradicts the model. Each engagement has a start, an end, and a definition-of-done: "stream team X can now run security reviews itself". When done, the enabling team moves on. Permanent enabling teams are usually mis-classified — they are platforms.

Inverse Conway is the architect's lever. Trying to change architecture without changing team boundaries fights Conway's Law and loses. Inverse Conway: change boundaries first, watch the architecture follow within 6–12 months. The lever is slow but reliable; trying to change architecture against the grain of team structure is the failure mode that produces "we redesigned but the system looks the same".

Cognitive-load surveys are the data. Quarterly self-reported load (1–5) is the cheapest, most actionable signal. Subjective but consistent over time. Teams scoring ≥ 4 for two quarters running need a topology change, not a pep talk. The data is what makes the conversation about restructure go from feelings to facts.

Lifecycle of a team in the topology

Formed

Team chartered with an explicit type (default Stream-aligned). Scope, services owned, interaction modes with others recorded in the team registry.

Active

Team operating in its type. Cognitive-load survey every quarter. Adoption / NPS tracked for platforms; capability uplift tracked for enabling engagements.

Overloaded

Cognitive-load ≥ 4 for two consecutive quarters. Topology change triggered — scope narrowed, platform takes over a runtime, or split into two teams.

Re-typed

Type changes (e.g. Stream-aligned promoted to Platform when its product gains many consumer teams). New interaction modes published; ratio check at the annual review.

Disbanded

Enabling engagement ends; stream team is now self-sufficient. Or product retired; stream team scope absorbed by another team.

Merged / split

Two related teams merge when scope is too small; one team splits when scope is too large. Both moves are normal lifecycle, not failures.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
Renaming teams without changing how they work Org adopts Team Topologies labels without changing interaction modes, dependencies, or scope. Nothing actually changes. The exercise is theatre. Change the work before changing the labels. Identify the most painful Collaboration interaction and convert it to X-as-a-Service. The label change comes after, documenting what already happened.
Too many Platform / Enabling / Complicated teams Org perceives non-stream teams as more senior or important. Engineers gravitate to them. Stream-aligned proportion shrinks. Direct customer-value delivery slows. Maintain a published ratio: ~80% stream-aligned, ~10–15% platform, ~5% enabling, <5% complicated subsystem. A 30% platform-team ratio is a warning sign.
Platform teams not treating outputs as products Platform team builds tools and forces stream teams to use them. No adoption metric, no internal NPS, no documentation effort. Stream teams resent the platform and bypass it. Success metric is stream-team adoption + satisfaction, not lines of platform code shipped. Quarterly internal NPS. Optional adoption — make the platform attractive enough that stream teams choose it.
Long-lived Collaboration mode Two teams collaborate indefinitely. The relationship never converges to a stable interface. Coordination meetings multiply. Time-bound every Collaboration: "8 weeks together, then transition to X-as-a-Service". Set the transition date at start. Long-lived Collaboration = work hasn't crystallised into a product yet.
Enabling team that becomes permanent An enabling team is created, succeeds, and… stays. Becomes a permanent dependency that contradicts the model. Every enabling engagement has start + end + definition-of-done. When done, team disbands or moves to the next engagement. Permanent enabling teams are usually mis-classified platforms.
Architecture changed without changing team boundaries Architects redesign the system on paper but team boundaries stay the same. Conway's Law re-imposes the old shape within 6 months. Use the Inverse Conway Maneuver. Change team boundaries first; the architecture follows. Slow but reliable; the only lever that beats Conway's Law.

Worked example — Study App topology snapshot

The Study App team registry as of 2026-05. 6 teams, target ratios met, two Collaboration windows time-bounded, one overloaded team flagged for re-scoping at the next quarterly review.

# Study App · team topology (2026-05)
reviewed:    2026-05-19  ·  next annual review: 2027-04

teams:
  - name:        Lessons squad
    type:        stream-aligned
    scope:       lesson-player, lesson-authoring, mastery
    cog-load:    3 / 5  (healthy)
    interacts:
      - target: Platform · CI       mode: x-as-a-service
      - target: Platform · Data     mode: x-as-a-service
      - target: Enabling · Security mode: facilitating  (8-wk uplift, ends 2026-07-15)

  - name:        Identity squad
    type:        stream-aligned
    scope:       auth, sessions, RBAC
    cog-load:    3 / 5
    interacts:
      - target: Platform · CI       mode: x-as-a-service

  - name:        Notifications squad
    type:        stream-aligned
    scope:       email, push, in-app
    cog-load:    4 / 5  (FLAG: overloaded 2 quarters; re-scope next review)
    interacts:
      - target: Lessons squad       mode: collaboration  (4 wks, ends 2026-06-10)

  - name:        Platform · CI
    type:        platform
    scope:       build, test runners, container registry
    cog-load:    3 / 5
    metrics:     adoption 100% · internal NPS +42 · onboarding median 1 d
    interacts:
      - mode:    x-as-a-service to ALL stream teams

  - name:        Platform · Data
    type:        platform
    scope:       event stream (MSK), warehouse, schema registry
    cog-load:    4 / 5  (newer, growing — monitor)
    metrics:     adoption 75% · NPS +18 · onboarding median 3 d

  - name:        Enabling · Security
    type:        enabling
    engagement:  Lessons squad uplift (8 weeks)
    started:     2026-05-20
    ends:        2026-07-15
    DoD:         Lessons squad self-runs threat models + dep-CVE triage

ratios:
  stream-aligned:        3 / 6  = 50%   (BELOW 80% target — too few)
  platform:              2 / 6  = 33%   (ABOVE 15% target — too many)

  complicated subsystem: 0 / 6  = 0%

    sub-area back into a stream-aligned ownership.

Real topology is rarely tidy — Study App is platform-heavy and the ratio check flags it. The honest snapshot is more useful than a polished one: it gives the annual review actual signal to act on.

Quality bar

  • Every team has an explicit type. Stream-aligned / Platform / Enabling / Complicated Subsystem in the team registry.
  • Target ratios published and tracked. ~80% stream-aligned; ~10–15% platform; ~5% enabling; <5% complicated subsystem. Deviations explained.
  • Interaction modes documented. Each cross-team interaction tagged Collaboration / XaaS / Facilitating, with Collaboration windows time-bounded.
  • Quarterly cognitive-load survey. 1–5 scale. Teams ≥ 4 for two quarters trigger topology change.
  • Platform teams track adoption + NPS. Not lines of code shipped. Mandated platforms with low adoption flagged for redesign.
  • Enabling engagements time-bounded. Start + end + definition-of-done. Permanent enabling teams reclassified.
  • Annual topology review. Engineering leadership. Inputs: load, dependency map, retros. Output: type / boundary / mode changes for the next year.
