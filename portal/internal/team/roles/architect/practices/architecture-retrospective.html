Architecture retrospective — learn from the system

A quarterly review of architectural health — makes it safe to revise yesterday's decisions before they become tomorrow's incidents.

TL;DR

Quarterly 90-min review of the system (not the process): which decisions aged well, which aged poorly, what new constraints emerged, what to do. Open with Kerth's Prime Directive for psychological safety. Output: updated tech-debt register + one ADR per decision reversed. Architecture without feedback loops accumulates regret; teams that don't retrospect repeat the same mistakes every 18 months.

What it is

An architecture retrospective is a quarterly structured review that examines the system, not the team's process. 90-min agenda:

  • Aged well (20 min) — which decisions from prior quarters are still serving us? Celebrate them and identify patterns to repeat.
  • Aged poorly (30 min) — which decisions are causing friction? Which assumptions have been invalidated? The meat of the session.
  • New constraints (30 min) — what changed in the world: new regulation, new scale, new product direction, new tech that changes the trade-off space.
  • Actions (10 min) — convert findings into concrete next steps: new ADRs, debt-register items, design docs to schedule.

Participants: architect + 2–4 senior engineers, optionally a leadership rep. Documented; actions tracked and reviewed at the next retrospective.

Why we need it

Architecture without feedback loops accumulates regret. Year-1 decisions fit year-1 reality; year-2 brings 10× traffic, larger team, product pivot, new tech. Without a retrospective ritual, mismatches become chronic friction that nobody names.

The retrospective creates safety for revising decisions. "We were wrong in ADR 0017" feels like failure; the retrospective frame transforms it into a learning artefact: "given what we knew then, that was reasonable; given what we know now, here's the updated decision." The trail (old ADR → retrospective notes → superseding ADR) preserves the learning.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Will Larson — engineering strategy retrospectives

Larson distinguishes engineering strategy retrospectives from team retrospectives. Quarterly cadence, hold the strategy to the standards of an engineering decision, update the strategy document explicitly. Strategies never reviewed quietly become dogma. His essay "Good engineering strategy is boring" argues that the clearly-articulated, reviewed strategies beat the brilliant unreviewed ones.

What we take: Document the strategy and review it on schedule. The discipline of write-down-then-revisit produces better strategy than brilliance does.

Source: lethain.com — Good engineering strategy is boring

Martin Fowler — evolutionary architecture

Fowler's Building Evolutionary Architectures articulates the explicit acknowledgement that architectures must change and the team needs feedback loops to know when/how. Fitness functions provide continuous fine-grained feedback; retrospectives provide periodic strategic feedback. Together: the loop that prevents ossification.

What we take: Fitness functions + retrospectives are complementary. Functions catch drift continuously and fine; retrospectives catch drift periodically and strategically.

Source: ThoughtWorks — Evolutionary architecture

Norm Kerth — Prime Directive

Kerth's Project Retrospectives (2001) coined the Prime Directive: "Regardless of what we discover, we understand and truly believe that everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time, their skills, and the resources available." The foundational psychological-safety statement for any retrospective.

What we take: Open every architecture retrospective with the Prime Directive. Establishes safety for honest analysis of past decisions; without it, "aged poorly" becomes blame.

Source: Retrospective Wiki — Prime Directive

Spotify — quarterly tech-radar review

Spotify pairs architecture retrospectives with a quarterly tech-radar update. The retrospective surfaces which technologies / patterns are working or not; the tech radar formalises the conclusion (adopt / trial / assess / hold) so other squads can act on it.

What we take: Pair the retrospective output with the tech radar update. The retrospective is the input; the radar is the durable artefact that travels across teams.

Source: Spotify Engineering Blog

Across teams: quarterly cadence, psychological-safety opening, pre-read, actions with owners, durable artefacts (debt register + tech radar + ADRs). Without these, retrospectives are venting; with them, they're a feedback loop.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Quarterly cadence

Once per quarter, 90 min. More frequent = noise; less frequent = misses context shifts. Scheduled a year in advance.

Pre-read document

1-page pre-read 48 h before: ADRs from the quarter, major incidents, scope changes, new constraints. Session starts at analysis, not recap.

Prime Directive opening

Architect reads Kerth's directive. Psychological safety for honest review of past decisions.

Silent-write start

Each section opens with 5 min silent writing (sticky notes or shared doc) before group discussion. Surfaces ideas the architect wouldn't have raised.

Updated debt register

Output artefact 1: tech debt register with new items + reprioritised existing ones.

Superseding ADRs

Output artefact 2: one ADR per architectural decision being reversed or amended. Links back to retrospective notes as context.

Public distribution

Notes shared with all engineers. Other teams know what's being reconsidered. Creates accountability for actions.

How it works in this project

Study App runs the architecture retrospective on the first Friday of each new quarter (90 min, calendar-set for the year). Pre-read at docs/architecture/retrospective-YYYY-QN.md; notes appended after the session. Participants: architect + 2 senior engineers + eng lead.

Actions go into docs/architecture/actions.md with named DRI + deadline; reviewed at each monthly architecture review. Superseding ADRs are authored within 2 weeks of the retrospective. A short ADR locks the quarterly cadence, the Prime-Directive opening, and the silent-write format.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Why "system not process"

Team retrospectives (Scrum-style) focus on how the team worked: meetings, ceremonies, collaboration. Architecture retrospectives focus on the system: which technical decisions held up, which didn't, what the system itself is teaching the team. The distinction matters because the two retrospectives produce different artefacts (process changes vs design changes) and reward different behaviours (psychological safety in both, but the architecture retro requires technical rigour the team retro doesn't). Running them combined dilutes both.

Aged-well section — not just morale

The "aged well" section is easy to skip ("we don't have time for celebration"). It's actually the most strategic section: it identifies patterns to repeat. If ADR 0017's choice of PostgreSQL was good, what made it good? Was it the team's existing knowledge? The boring-tech principle? The match to actual data shape? Naming the pattern lets the team apply it to the next decision. Without the aged-well section, retrospectives become a list of regrets and miss the duplication opportunity.

The Prime Directive — why it works mechanically

Kerth's Prime Directive isn't just morale-boosting language. It works mechanically: by stating up front that past actors did the best they could, the conversation can examine the decision (what was wrong about it, given what we know now) without examining the decider (who's at fault). The decoupling is what makes honest analysis possible. Teams that skip the Prime Directive default to implicit blame; the implicit blame makes honest "aged poorly" discussion impossible.

Silent-write start — beat the loudest voice

If the architect opens "what aged poorly?" by speaking first, the architect's views dominate. Even with the best intentions, others nod along. 5 min of silent writing first (each participant writes 3 items per section on sticky notes / shared doc) surfaces ideas the architect wouldn't have raised. Then the group discusses. The architect facilitates more than they speak.

What to do when you find a "we were wrong" moment

The retrospective surfaces a decision that aged poorly. The temptation is to act on it immediately. Better: write a superseding ADR. The ADR captures the context (including a link back to the retrospective notes), the new decision, and consequences. The supersession trail is the institutional memory; the immediate fix without an ADR loses the learning.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • 90 min per quarter + ~2 hr pre-read prep. Architect time. Cheap relative to recurring incidents from un-revised decisions.
  • Discomfort of "we were wrong". The first 1–2 retrospectives are painful as the team learns to discuss past decisions critically without defensiveness. Cultural fit matters.
  • Action overhang. Each retrospective adds 3–10 actions. If they accumulate without closure, the practice loses credibility. Close-rate tracked at each subsequent retro.
  • Documentation tax. Pre-read + notes + ADRs = real writing. Without this, the retro is a verbal exchange with no durable output.

Lifecycle

schedule (year-ahead) → pre-read T-48 h → Prime Directive opening → silent-write per section → group discussion → actions + ADR drafts → distribution + tracking

Calendar-set yearly; pre-read 48 h before; 90-min session opens with Prime Directive; each section begins with silent writing; concrete actions captured. Within 2 weeks: superseding ADRs authored, debt register updated, notes distributed. Actions tracked at monthly review until next retrospective.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Becomes a complaint session "Aged poorly" dominates; no actions agreed. Time-box discussion. Reserve final 10 min for actions with named owners + deadlines. No ownerless actions.
Skipped under deadline pressure Feels like overhead; postponed and forgotten. Calendar-set a year in advance. Skipping requires explicit justification, not silent deferral.
Only architect speaks Architect dominates; engineers nod. Silent-write at the start of each section. Architect facilitates more than they speak.
No Prime Directive "It's obvious we did our best". Read it aloud. The opening creates the mechanical safety that makes critical analysis possible.
Skipped aged-well section "We don't have time for celebration". It's not celebration — it's pattern-identification. Spend 20 min naming what to repeat.
Verbal-only output, no ADR Decisions made; no superseding ADR follows. 2-week deadline post-retro: every reversed decision gets a superseding ADR with the retro notes as context.

Worked example

Retro notes · 1 page

Study App — 2026-Q1 architecture retrospective notes

# 2026-Q1 architecture retrospective
date:        2026-04-05
attendees:   @ivan (architect) · @oleg (eng lead) · @maria (sr eng) · @anna (data)
facilitator: @ivan
prime directive: read aloud at start

## Aged well (20 min)

- ADR 0017 — PostgreSQL primary store
    held up at 50k MAU; the team's pre-existing PG knowledge made
    incident response 3× faster than DynamoDB would have. Pattern
    to repeat: boring-tech with team familiarity over novel-tech.

- ADR 0024 — event-streaming via Kafka self-hosted (in EU)
    EU residency was correct call; saved 4 weeks of GDPR work in
    Q3. Pattern: front-load regulatory constraints.

- ADR 0031 — OpenAPI as primary contract
    Mobile team integrated against the mock for 6 weeks in parallel
    with backend. Saved estimated 3 sprints.

## Aged poorly (30 min)

- ADR 0019 — synchronous email-verification at signup
    became the #1 activation funnel drop (38% drop at this step).
    Decision was sound in year 1 when fraud was the bigger risk;
    by year 2, growth is the bigger risk and async verification is
    feasible. → write ADR 0099 superseding.

- ADR 0023 — single PostgreSQL database (not microservices)
    starting to hurt at 50k MAU. Lesson tables and analytics tables
    compete for connections. → not yet wrong, but trending; add
    "logical split" to tech-debt register; revisit Q3.

## New constraints (30 min)

- iOS 18 background-fetch behavior changed; current sync model
  now wastes battery. Mobile team needs new event-stream pattern.
- Compliance: EU AI Act drafts include "training data lineage"
  requirements. If we add recommendation ML, we need lineage
  tooling from day 1.
- Hiring: 2 new sr engineers joining Q2; on-call coverage increases;
  consider whether on-call burden assumptions in ADR 0034 still hold.

## Actions (10 min)

A1  Write ADR 0099 superseding 0019: async email verification
    DRI: @oleg                    by: 2026-04-19
A2  Add "logical DB split for analytics" to tech-debt register
    DRI: @ivan                    by: 2026-04-12 (this week)
A3  Spike new mobile sync model accommodating iOS 18 background-fetch
    DRI: @maria                   by: 2026-04-26
A4  Investigate ML training-data lineage tools (Marquez, OpenLineage)
    DRI: @anna                    by: 2026-05-10
A5  Distribute these notes to #engineering by EOD Monday
    DRI: @ivan                    by: 2026-04-07

next retrospective: 2026-07-05 (Friday, first of Q3)

Outcome. Two superseding ADRs followed within 2 weeks (0099 for email verification; an architecture-spike ADR for the mobile sync change). Action A4 surfaced a real finding: an emerging "EU AI Act lineage" constraint was not on anyone's radar; the team scheduled a dedicated design doc for ML-track planning in Q2. The aged-well section's pattern ("boring-tech with team familiarity beats novel-tech") was explicitly cited in the next quarter's vendor-evaluation discussions, preventing two adoption efforts that would have drained months without payback. Without the retrospective, the 0019 supersession would have happened in response to a growth crisis instead of a calm Friday afternoon.
