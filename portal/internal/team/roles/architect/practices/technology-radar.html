Technology radar — portfolio of tech choices

A living map of the technology portfolio reviewed quarterly so the team can see which tools to invest in and which to stop using.

TL;DR

A technology radar is a visual assessment of the tech portfolio across four rings — Adopt (use in production), Trial (use in a project to learn), Assess (explore with a spike), Hold (don't start new projects with this) — and four quadrants (Tools / Platforms / Languages & Frameworks / Techniques). Pioneered by ThoughtWorks in 2010 and open-sourced by Zalando as a renderer. Reviewed quarterly by the architecture guild, published as a static page. Makes choices transparent, prevents stack drift, gives new hires a fast orientation.

What it is

A technology radar is a structured way to communicate technology choices across an engineering organisation. It was popularised by ThoughtWorks, who have published a public radar every 6 months since 2010. The radar covers four quadrants — Tools, Platforms, Languages & Frameworks, Techniques — and four rings:

  • Adopt — recommended for broad use. Used in production, well understood, low risk. The "Golden Path".
  • Trial — worth pursuing in a low-risk project. Team has experience. Ready at scale with some caution.
  • Assess — worth exploring with a PoC or spike to understand the implications. Promising but not yet proven.
  • Hold — should not be started in new projects. Abandoned libraries, security risks, evaluated-and-rejected options.

The internal radar is maintained as a JSON file (Zalando format) and rendered as a static HTML page. Reviewed quarterly at the architecture-guild session. Each entry has a 2–3 sentence rationale that explains why it sits in its ring.

Why we need it

Without a radar, the stack quietly accumulates every library someone tried on a weekend project. A new engineer asking "which HTTP client should I use?" gets five different answers from five engineers. Libraries in Hold status — abandoned, insecure, replaced — continue to appear in new services because nobody communicated the decision to stop using them.

The radar makes choices explicit and visible. A new hire reads the radar on day one and understands the team's technology philosophy in 20 minutes. Hold decisions are documented with rationale, not just institutional memory. The quarterly review creates a forcing function for the team to reflect on emerging technologies and deprecate ones that are not working.

How others do it

ThoughtWorks — the originator

ThoughtWorks has published the Technology Radar every 6 months since 2010. Each edition covers ~80–100 items across four quadrants. The editorial process involves 50+ senior ThoughtWorks technologists reviewing and debating each item. The ThoughtWorks radar placed items like Docker (2014, Assess), Kubernetes (2017, Trial), and Infrastructure as Code (2011, Adopt) on the radar years before mainstream adoption — a reliable leading indicator for the industry.

What we take: The editorial process matters as much as the output. A radar produced by one architect is less valuable than one produced by a 5-engineer guild session. Schedule the quarterly session; the debate is the product.

Source: ThoughtWorks Technology Radar

Zalando — open-source renderer

Zalando open-sourced the Tech Radar renderer (github.com/zalando/tech-radar) as a static HTML/JS page that reads a JSON config. Adopted by hundreds of companies to host their own internal radars. Zalando uses the radar to coordinate technology choices across 2 000+ engineers and 150+ teams. Their internal radar is reviewed by a central architecture board quarterly and includes a "why" for every Hold item.

What we take: The Zalando renderer is the fastest way to publish an internal radar. Start with the JSON format; it takes one afternoon to populate with current technologies.

Source: github.com/zalando/tech-radar

Spotify — Golden Paths

Spotify's architecture chapter maintains an internal "Golden Path" — a curated stack of Adopt-level technologies supported by the platform team with templates, documentation, and on-call. Technologies outside the Golden Path can be used but are unsupported. The Golden Path concept is an operationalised version of the Adopt ring: not just "this is good" but "this comes with platform support, a template, and documentation".

What we take: Elevate Adopt items to "Golden Path" status with operational support. An Adopt item without a template or documentation is only half as useful as one with both.

Source: Spotify Engineering — Golden Paths

AOE / build-your-own-radar tooling

AOE's open-source build-your-own-radar renderer (used by ThoughtWorks for their public radar) reads a Google Sheet or CSV and produces an interactive SVG radar. The format has become a de facto standard alongside Zalando's renderer. Many companies maintain their radar as a spreadsheet (low-friction edits) and let the renderer produce the visualisation — engineers edit data, not HTML.

What we take: Maintain the radar as data (JSON or sheet) and let a renderer produce the visualisation. Lower edit-friction = more frequent updates = a radar that stays current rather than stale.

Source: build-your-own-radar (ThoughtWorks)

Anatomy of a radar

JSON source file

A tech-radar.json in the repo root, Zalando format. Each entry: name, ring (adopt/trial/assess/hold), quadrant (tools/platforms/languages/techniques), description (2–3 sentence rationale). Versioned in git; changes reviewed in PRs.

Four quadrants

Tools (CI, monitoring, editors), Platforms (cloud services, databases, brokers), Languages & Frameworks (programming languages, web frameworks, ORMs), Techniques (patterns, practices, methodologies). Each quadrant should carry 5–15 items at any time.

Rationale per item

Every entry includes 2–3 sentences of why it sits in this ring. Hold items in particular carry decision-record-quality rationale: "We moved to X because Y" or "abandoned upstream since [date]". Without rationale, entries get questioned and ignored.

Quarterly review session

30-minute architecture-guild session. Agenda: (1) review new proposals (max 5 per session), (2) review items that changed ring, (3) retire items no longer relevant. Output: updated JSON committed. Next session date scheduled at the end.

Assess cap + spike owner

Assess is capped at ~5 items at any time. Promoting to Assess requires a named spike owner and a due date. Items in Assess > 6 months without a spike are moved to Hold or retired. Keeps Assess from becoming a wish list.

Renderer + canonical URL

Static page rendered from the JSON (Zalando or AOE renderer). One canonical URL referenced from onboarding, design-doc template, ADR template. The renderer is interchangeable; the data is the asset.

How to run it

  1. Inventory the current stack (Week 1). List every library, framework, database, broker, language, and major technique currently in use. 60–120 items is normal at the start.
  2. Slot each into a ring + quadrant (Week 1). Group session with 3–5 senior engineers. Default to Adopt for things that work; Hold for things being phased out; Assess for promising unknowns; Trial for current pilots.
  3. Write a 2–3 sentence rationale per item. Hold items get the most rigour — they are decision records. "Abandoned upstream since [date]" or "Superseded by [X] for [reason]".
  4. Stand up the renderer (Week 2). Zalando or AOE renderer pointed at tech-radar.json in the repo. One canonical URL.
  5. Link from onboarding + design-doc template (Week 2). New hires read it on day one. Design-doc template asks "is this on the radar?" so authors check before adopting new tech.
  6. Schedule the quarterly review. Architecture guild + tech leads. 30 min. Calendared recurring; next date appears on the radar page itself.
  7. Promote + regress items each quarter. Trial that worked → Adopt. Assess with no spike → Hold. Adopt with critical bugs → Trial or Hold. Items in Hold for > 2 quarters with no remaining usage → retired.

Deep dive — what makes the radar a decision tool

The rings encode a verdict, not a vibe. Each ring is a specific action: Adopt = "use by default"; Trial = "pilot, then graduate"; Assess = "learn, don't commit"; Hold = "stop starting". Items that don't fit one of those verdicts don't belong on the radar. Vague placements ("we kinda like Rust") dilute the signal until nobody trusts it.

Hold rationale is the most valuable field. Adopt items don't need much explanation; Hold items need the most. Why is this Hold? When did it move there? What replaced it? Without that rationale, engineers question Hold entries and slip back to the old tool. Treat Hold rationale with the same rigour as an ADR — it's a decision record.

Cap Assess to prevent wish-list radar. Engineers propose every interesting technology they read about; the Assess quadrant fills with 40 items; nobody spikes any of them. Cap at ~5 items. Promotion to Assess requires a named spike owner and a due date. Items stuck in Assess > 6 months get moved to Hold or retired — silence is a signal too.

The editorial debate is the product. The radar's value comes from the quarterly review session, not the published artefact. A radar produced by one architect alone reflects one person's preferences; one produced by a 5-engineer guild debate reflects collective experience. Schedule the session; protect it; the disagreements in the room are where the radar earns its trust.

Golden Path turns Adopt into operational support. An Adopt item without a template, documentation, and on-call support is half as useful. Spotify's Golden Path concept elevates Adopt items to "we operate this for you" status. New services start from a template; problems get on-call response. The Adopt ring becomes a contract between the platform team and stream teams, not just a recommendation.

Items move; the radar is a snapshot. Movement between rings is the radar's vital sign. A radar where nothing has moved in 4 quarters is fictional — the world changed; the radar didn't. Track movement explicitly: "this quarter: 3 promotions, 2 regressions, 5 new items, 4 retired". A healthy radar shows visible churn at the edges.

Quadrants prevent monoculture in one slice. Four quadrants — Tools / Platforms / Languages & Frameworks / Techniques — force the team to think about all four. A radar with 30 Tools and 2 Techniques is unbalanced; the Techniques quadrant is where the biggest leverage usually hides (event-driven, contract-first APIs, trunk-based development). Force entries in every quadrant.

Lifecycle of a radar item

Proposed

Engineer proposes a candidate via PR to tech-radar.json. Rationale drafted. Architect reviews before the next quarterly session.

Assess

Accepted as worth a spike. Named owner + due date. Cap of 5 items. PoC report links back to the radar entry.

Trial

Promoted from Assess after a successful spike. Used in one or two real projects. Team building scale experience.

Adopt

Promoted from Trial after multiple successful uses. Template + docs + on-call support — the Golden Path. Default for new services.

Hold

Regressed from any inner ring (or directly evaluated and rejected). Written rationale required. Existing usage migrates out; no new projects start with it.

Retired

Item no longer in active stack (fully migrated out, or never adopted). Removed from the radar after 2 quarters in Hold with zero usage. Historical entry kept in git history.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
Radar never updated after initial population Created with enthusiasm, published, never reviewed. After 12 months it's stale and nobody trusts it. Quarterly review wasn't scheduled. Schedule the quarterly review as a recurring calendar event on the day the radar is first published. The next review date appears on the radar page itself.
Too many items in Assess (wish-list radar) Engineers propose every interesting technology they read about. Assess fills with 40 items; nobody has time to spike them all. The radar becomes aspirational. Cap Assess at ~5 items. Promoting to Assess requires a named spike owner and a due date. Items in Assess > 6 months without a spike are moved to Hold or retired.
Radar owned by one architect, not the team CTO or lead architect populates the radar alone. Engineers feel no ownership. Reflects one person's preferences rather than the team's collective experience. Run the quarterly review as a group session. Any engineer can propose an item. Final ring placement is by guild consensus with the architect as tiebreaker — not unilateral.
Hold items without rationale Items marked Hold with no explanation. Engineers question them and slip back to the old tool. The Hold ring loses credibility. Every Hold entry carries a 2–3 sentence rationale at the same rigour as an ADR. "Abandoned upstream since [date]" or "superseded by [X] for [reason]". No bare "hold".
No movement between rings Items never get promoted or regressed. The radar is identical 4 quarters running. The world changed; the radar didn't. Track movement at every review: promotions, regressions, new entries, retirements. A healthy radar shows visible churn at the edges. No movement = the review isn't doing its job.
Adopt items without operational support Adopt ring lists technologies but no template, docs, or on-call. Stream teams adopt them and then bounce because there is no support. Promote Adopt to Golden Path: template + docs + on-call. An Adopt item without operational support is half-baked; either build the support or drop the item back to Trial.

Quality bar

  • Single canonical radar. One JSON file in-repo; one rendered URL linked from onboarding, design-doc template, ADR template.
  • Every entry has a 2–3 sentence rationale. Hold entries carry decision-record-quality reasoning.
  • Four quadrants populated. Tools / Platforms / Languages & Frameworks / Techniques — none left empty.
  • Assess capped at ~5 items. Each with a named spike owner and due date. Stale Assess items moved out.
  • Quarterly review session. Architecture guild, 30 min, calendared recurring. Next review date visible on the page.
  • Visible movement. Each review reports promotions, regressions, new entries, retirements. No movement = the review isn't doing its job.
  • Adopt items have operational support. Golden Path: template + docs + on-call. Adopt without support drops back to Trial.
  • Edits via PR with review. Treated as code — JSON edited in git, changes reviewed.
