Technical roadmapping — align tech with business

A rolling 12-month view of platform investments and capability builds, aligned to business outcomes so executives can make informed trade-offs.

TL;DR

A technical roadmap is a rolling 12-month plan of infrastructure investments, platform migrations, and capability builds — each tied to a business outcome and a cost-of-delay estimate. Unlike a feature roadmap, it visualises the "below the waterline" work that enables product velocity: observability, database migrations, auth rewrites, debt paydowns. Now/Next/Later (Janna Bastow, Intercom) is the preferred format because it communicates confidence appropriately — Later is intent, not commitment. Without a visible technical roadmap, engineering debt accumulates invisibly until a crisis forces an expensive rewrite at the worst possible time.

What it is

A technical roadmap is a structured, time-horizoned plan of platform investments and capability builds. It is distinct from a product roadmap (which tracks features) and a sprint backlog (which tracks tasks). The technical roadmap tracks the "below the waterline" work: observability, infrastructure migrations, security hardening, tech-debt paydown, new platform capabilities.

Each item has:

  • Business outcome — what business goal does this enable? "Enables 10× traffic scale", "Reduces P1 incident rate by 50%". Items without a business outcome are tasks, not roadmap items.
  • Cost of delay — what does it cost per month not to do this? Forces prioritisation by business value rather than technical preference. Even a rough estimate beats none.
  • Owner — engineer or team accountable for delivery. No "TBD" on Now-horizon items.
  • Status / horizon — Now (this quarter, committed), Next (1–2 quarters, directional), Later (6–18 months, intent).

The Now/Next/Later format (popularised by Janna Bastow and Intercom) is preferred over a Gantt chart for technical roadmaps because it communicates confidence appropriately — Later items are intent, not commitment.

Why we need it

Without a visible technical roadmap, engineering debt accumulates invisibly. Product managers and executives see only feature delivery. The platform work that enables product velocity — observability, database health, auth reliability — is invisible until it fails in production at the worst possible moment. The quarterly "we cannot ship this feature because auth needs to be rewritten first" conversation becomes a crisis instead of a planned trade-off.

A visible roadmap with cost-of-delay estimates changes the conversation. "This auth rewrite costs $30k/month in delayed features if we don't do it in Q2" is a business conversation, not a technical complaint. Executives who can see the trade-off make informed decisions. Architects who cannot frame technical work in business terms lose the resource-allocation argument every time.

How others do it

Janna Bastow / ProdPad — Now/Next/Later

Janna Bastow popularised the Now/Next/Later roadmap format (around 2014, at ProdPad) as an alternative to Gantt-style date-based roadmaps. The argument: Gantt charts communicate false precision and become commitments accidentally. Now/Next/Later communicates confidence appropriately — Now is committed, Next is directional, Later is intent. The format has been widely adopted across product and engineering, including by Intercom, Atlassian, and Basecamp.

What we take: Use Now/Next/Later for technical roadmaps too. Confidence fades across horizons. Communicate the framing explicitly — first slide, every review — and repeat it so stakeholders don't interpret Later as a commitment.

Source: ProdPad — Now / Next / Later

Intercom — "Saying no" to platform work

Intercom popularised the "Product Roadmap is Not a Feature Factory" philosophy and uses Now/Next/Later for both product and technical roadmaps. Their technical roadmap is maintained by the VP of Engineering and shared with product leadership quarterly. Items on the roadmap are explicitly linked to product strategy outcomes — "enable real-time features" links to a WebSockets platform item. Without that link, platform work competes with features in a zero-sum game.

What we take: Technical roadmap items must be linked to product outcomes to compete for resources. "We need to migrate the database" loses to "we need to ship checkout v2" unless you can show what checkout v2 is blocked by.

Source: Intercom — Product strategy means saying no

Netflix — 8-year cloud migration narrative

Netflix's cloud migration (from physical data centres to AWS, 2008–2016) is the most documented long-running technical roadmap execution: 8 years, communicated consistently as a business-enablement story. The technical roadmap was decentralised — each product team owned a 12-month roadmap for their domain — but an architecture council reviewed cross-cutting concerns quarterly. The migration narrative stayed consistent: "this enables the business to do X" rather than "we're paying down technical debt".

What we take: Long-running migrations require a multi-year roadmap communicated quarterly. The narrative must stay consistent and outcome-focused: "this enables the business to do X" — not "we are paying down technical debt".

Source: Netflix Tech Blog — cloud migration rationale

Will Larson — engineering-strategy stack

Will Larson's An Elegant Puzzle (2019) and Staff Engineer (2021) formalise the technical roadmap as a core staff-engineer deliverable. Larson's "engineering strategy" model defines three layers: technical vision (multi-year), technical roadmap (12-month), technical work (quarterly). The roadmap's primary output is a prioritised list of bets with a written rationale per bet — documented enough to survive the architect who wrote it changing roles.

What we take: Write the roadmap so it survives your absence. The rationale per item must be explicit enough that a new architect can continue the work without re-deriving the reasoning. Vision sits above the roadmap; quarterly work sits below.

Source: Will Larson — good engineering strategy is boring

Anatomy of a technical roadmap

Now / Next / Later horizon

Now = this quarter (committed, owned, estimated). Next = next 1–2 quarters (directional, may shift). Later = 6–18 months (intent, likely to change). Never more than 5 items in Now — commitment is the bottleneck, not ideas.

Business-outcome link

Every item states the business outcome it enables in one sentence: "[Initiative] enables [outcome] by [mechanism]". Example: "SLO rollout enables a 99.9% uptime SLA for enterprise customers by giving the team a shared definition of healthy". Items without it are not roadmap items — they are tasks.

Cost-of-delay estimate

Business cost per month of not doing this. Forces prioritisation by value rather than elegance. Even a rough estimate ($10 k/month in delayed feature capacity) beats none. Use CD3 (cost of delay ÷ duration) to compare items competing for the same team.

Confidence labels

Now = commitment + estimate. Next = ~ 50% directional. Later = no commitment. Labels stated on every roadmap surface so stakeholders don't interpret Later as a delivery promise.

Owner per Now item

Named owner (engineer or team) for every Now item. No "TBD" at the Now horizon. Next items can be unassigned; Later items often unassigned by design.

Monthly review + quarterly product sync

Monthly with engineering leadership: status of Now, movement Next→Now, new items entering. Quarterly with product leadership: enough cadence to align feature sequencing around technical dependencies.

How to run it

  1. Draft the technical vision (Week 0). 1–2 page multi-year vision: where the platform is heading. Sits above the roadmap; rarely changes. Reviewed annually.
  2. Inventory candidate initiatives (Week 1). Mine the tech-debt register, recent incidents, product-roadmap dependencies, security audit findings. 20–40 candidates is normal at the start.
  3. Write outcomes + cost of delay per candidate (Week 1–2). "[Initiative] enables [outcome] by [mechanism]" + monthly cost-of-delay estimate. Drop any candidate that can't get a real outcome sentence.
  4. Sort by CD3 (Week 2). Cost of delay ÷ duration. Highest CD3 wins. Picks the items that pay back fastest, not the ones that look biggest.
  5. Slot into Now/Next/Later (Week 2). Now ≤ 5 items, all with owners and estimates. Next 1–2 quarters, directional. Later 6–18 months, intent.
  6. Publish + present. One-page roadmap with confidence framing on the first surface. Walk product leadership through the outcome links and CD3 reasoning at quarterly sync.
  7. Monthly engineering review (30 min). Status of Now, movement, new items. Notes shared with the team.
  8. Quarterly product sync. Re-align feature sequencing with technical dependencies. Move items between horizons as priorities shift.

Deep dive — what makes the roadmap influence resourcing

Outcome link is the entry ticket. Items written in technical language — "migrate MongoDB to PostgreSQL", "rewrite the auth service" — lose every resourcing argument to features. The translation "[Initiative] enables [outcome] by [mechanism]" forces the architect to surface the business value, and items that fail the translation aren't really roadmap items — they're tasks. Refuse to add an item without the sentence; the refusal is where the architect adds the most value.

Cost of delay turns priority into math. "We should do this" is a feeling. "$30 k/month of delayed feature capacity if we don't do this in Q2" is a number. Even rough estimates ($, eng-days, contract risk) beat no estimate. CD3 — cost of delay ÷ duration — picks the items that pay back fastest, which is rarely the items that feel biggest. Trust the math; track the wins and the math earns trust.

Confidence fades by design. Now/Next/Later is not three buckets of equal certainty. Now is committed (owner, estimate, decision). Next is directional (~50% confidence — may shift). Later is intent (no commitment, no owner, may never happen). Communicate this framing on every surface; otherwise executives interpret Later as a date-based promise and the architect gets held to commitments that were never made.

Five-item cap on Now. Capacity is the bottleneck, not ambition. A Now horizon with 12 items signals lack of focus; nothing gets finished; everything slips. Cap at 5 and force the prioritisation conversation. Items waiting at the front of Next are healthier than a packed Now.

The roadmap must survive the architect. Will Larson's test: can a new architect continue the work without re-deriving the reasoning? Each item carries enough written rationale — outcome, cost of delay, mechanism, alternatives considered — that the answer is yes. Roadmaps tied to one architect's tacit knowledge collapse the moment that architect changes role.

Quarterly cadence with product matters more than annual planning. Annual roadmaps are stale by month 3. Monthly engineering reviews catch internal drift; quarterly product syncs align feature sequencing around technical dependencies. The product team plans Q2 features knowing the auth rewrite is in Now, not after asking why the auth changes broke their plan.

Vision sits above the roadmap, work sits below. Three layers: vision (multi-year, rare changes), roadmap (12-month, quarterly refresh), work (sprint backlog, weekly). Confusing the layers — putting vision items on the roadmap, or sprint tasks on the roadmap — destroys the format. Each layer answers a different question for a different audience.

Lifecycle of a roadmap item

Candidate

Identified from incidents, debt register, product dependency, audit finding. Outcome + cost of delay drafted; not yet on the roadmap.

Later

On the roadmap as intent. No owner, no commitment. Strategic direction signal; revisited quarterly. May be replaced by better items.

Next

Directional for 1–2 quarters. ~50% confidence. Owner may be tentative. Stakeholders aligned on the dependency it removes.

Now

Committed this quarter. Named owner. Estimate. Outcome and cost of delay re-validated. Cap of 5 items in Now at once.

Delivered

Outcome achieved. Reported at the next monthly review. Item retired with a short note on actual cost vs estimate, lessons.

Dropped / Re-scoped

Item no longer relevant: business priority shifted, alternative emerged, cost of delay re-estimated low. Dropped with a written note so the decision is durable.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
Items without business-outcome links Roadmap written by engineers for engineers. Items described in technical terms ("migrate from MongoDB to PostgreSQL") with no business rationale. Product leadership sees engineering vanity projects and deprioritises them. For every technical item, write: "This enables [outcome] by [mechanism]". Refuse to add items without this sentence. It is harder than it looks — which surfaces items that lack a business case.
Roadmap treated as commitment, not direction Executives interpret Now/Next/Later as date-based commitment. When Next items slip, the architect is held accountable for promises that were never intended. Label confidence explicitly on every surface. Now = commitment. Next = directional (~50%). Later = intent (no commitment). Repeat the framing every review.
Roadmap never updated — stale within a quarter Created at the start of the year, then forgotten. By month 3, the context has changed, three Now items have been de-prioritised, new urgent items have emerged. Roadmap is fiction. Add roadmap review to the monthly engineering leadership calendar. 30 minutes. The roadmap must reflect current reality, not the year's original plan.
Too many items in Now Architect tries to satisfy everyone. Now ends up with 12 items. Nothing finishes; everything slips; trust in the roadmap collapses. Cap at 5 items in Now. Sixth item must displace an existing one. Items waiting at the front of Next are healthier than a packed Now.
No cost-of-delay estimate "We should do this" is the strongest argument the architect has. Loses every prioritisation battle to features with quantified revenue impact. Estimate cost of delay for every item. Rough is fine. Use CD3 to compare. Numbers beat feelings in the resourcing conversation, every time.

Quality bar

  • One technical roadmap per team or product line. Lives in the repo / docs portal; linked from the team README.
  • Now / Next / Later format. Confidence framing on every surface; Later is intent, not promise.
  • Max 5 items in Now. Sixth item displaces an existing one. Cap enforced at review time.
  • Every item has a business-outcome sentence. "[Initiative] enables [outcome] by [mechanism]". No outcome → not on the roadmap.
  • Every Now item has cost of delay + owner + estimate. Estimates are time-bound; owners named.
  • Items sorted by CD3. Cost of delay ÷ duration. Trust the ratio over taste.
  • Monthly engineering review. 30 min. Status, movement, new items. Calendared.
  • Quarterly product sync. Align feature sequencing around technical dependencies.
  • Roadmap survives the architect. Written rationale per item; a successor can continue without re-deriving.
