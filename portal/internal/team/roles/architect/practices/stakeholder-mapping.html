Stakeholder mapping — communicate by audience

Tailor each communication's format and depth to the audience's power and interest so architecture work becomes visible and funded.

TL;DR

Stakeholder mapping categorises every audience by influence and interest using a Power × Interest grid (Mendelow, 1991). Each quadrant gets a tailored communication strategy: what to share, how often, and in what format. Architects who skip this end up writing 30-page design docs for audiences that needed a 3-slide deck. Stakeholder mapping is the difference between technical excellence and organisational influence — architecture work that is not communicated at the right level stays invisible, and invisible work does not get funded.

What it is

Stakeholder mapping is a structured process for identifying every audience affected by an architectural initiative and defining how to communicate with each. The canonical tool is the Power × Interest grid, attributed to Aubrey Mendelow (1991), which places stakeholders on two axes:

  • Power — how much influence does this stakeholder have over the initiative's success? Can they approve or block resources? Does their endorsement determine adoption?
  • Interest — how much does this stakeholder care about the outcome? Are they actively affected day-to-day?

The grid produces four quadrants, each with a recommended communication strategy:

  • High power, high interest — Manage closely. Detailed design docs, weekly syncs, decision involvement.
  • High power, low interest — Keep satisfied. Monthly 1-pager, quarterly executive review, no surprises.
  • Low power, high interest — Keep informed. Tech talks, public RFCs, public ADRs. They become advocates.
  • Low power, low interest — Monitor. Quarterly newsletter or all-hands mention. Minimal investment.

Why we need it

Architects who do not map stakeholders default to one of two failure modes. Either they over-communicate to everyone (long design docs for executives who needed three slides) or under-communicate to everyone (a Slack message announcing a major change to people who needed a meeting). Both fail.

The Power × Interest grid forces deliberate communication. Architecture work that is not communicated to high-power stakeholders does not get funded — even if it is technically correct. Architecture work that is not communicated to high-interest engineers is not adopted — even if it is mandated. The grid prevents communication from being a side effect of "whoever happens to ask" and turns it into a deliberate strategy.

How others do it

Will Larson — Staff Engineer model

Will Larson's Staff Engineer (2021) explicitly identifies "communicating to executives" as a core staff-engineer skill. The book defines four staff-engineer archetypes (Tech Lead, Architect, Solver, Right Hand) and notes that all four require executive-stakeholder communication. Larson's guidance: write the executive summary as a standalone artefact readable in 3 minutes, with technical detail in linked appendices. The summary answers "what business outcome does this enable?" before any technical content.

What we take: Write the executive summary as a standalone deliverable. If the executive only reads the first paragraph, they should still know what to fund and what to expect. Technical detail is a linked appendix.

Source: StaffEng — Will Larson

Camille Fournier — translator role

Camille Fournier (former CTO of Rent The Runway, author of The Manager's Path) writes about the "manager-as-translator" role and applies the same logic to architects. Her guidance: an architect is a translator between engineering and business. The translation requires understanding the business stakeholder's actual decision context — what they will and won't approve, what their constraints are, what language they use. Stakeholder mapping is the input to that translation work.

What we take: For each high-power stakeholder, document their decision context: KPIs, constraints, preferred format. Use this profile when writing for them. Faster to tailor one 1-pager than to discover after a rejection that the executive needed a different frame.

Source: Camille Fournier — elidedbranches.com

Nielsen Norman Group — UX-derived method

NN/g's stakeholder-analysis methodology, derived from UX research, formalises stakeholder mapping for product and engineering teams. NN/g recommends the two-axis grid plus a stakeholder-interview round to validate each stakeholder's actual interest — assumptions about interest often differ from reality. Stakeholders who appear high-interest on the org chart may be low-interest in practice, and vice versa.

What we take: Validate the map with a brief 15-minute interview per high-power stakeholder. Ask: "What level of involvement do you actually want?" The answer is often less than the architect assumed.

Source: NN/g — Stakeholder analysis

Lara Hogan — sponsor & ally network

Lara Hogan's Resilient Management and her widely-cited posts on building a "manager Voltron" extend stakeholder mapping into sponsor + ally + connector + safety-net roles. For any initiative, identify who sponsors funding, who provides cover when things go wrong, who connects you to other teams, and who tells you the truth. The lens is interpersonal rather than purely formal — power-interest grids miss the relational work.

What we take: Augment the Mendelow grid with named sponsors and allies. For every high-power stakeholder, name the person who will advocate for the initiative when you are not in the room. Without an advocate, even good initiatives stall.

Source: Lara Hogan — building your manager Voltron

Anatomy of a stakeholder map

Power × Interest grid

A 2×2 grid plotting each stakeholder by power (low/high) and interest (low/high). Drawn for each significant architectural initiative. Stored in the design doc. Max 12 stakeholders per grid — beyond that the map becomes unreadable.

Strategy per quadrant

For each quadrant define: (1) what content to share, (2) format, (3) cadence. High-Power/High-Interest gets the full design doc with weekly updates. Low-Power/Low-Interest gets a quarterly newsletter mention. Strategy lives next to the grid.

Stakeholder profile

For each high-power stakeholder: name, role, KPIs/OKRs, typical objection patterns, preferred format (deck / 1-pager / verbal briefing / Slack thread). Compiled from past interactions. Living document.

Sponsor & ally annotation

Augment the grid with named sponsors and allies per high-power stakeholder. Who advocates when you are not in the room? Who provides cover if things go wrong? Without named sponsors, even technically correct work stalls.

Asymmetric investment

Time investment per stakeholder is proportional to their Power × Interest score, not equal across all stakeholders. A CTO who can approve $500 k in headcount deserves 5× the communication investment of a peer team that is mildly affected. The asymmetry is the point.

Quarterly refresh

Map revisited at every architecture retrospective. New stakeholders added; departed stakeholders removed; power/interest re-validated. Maps that are not refreshed are fiction within 6 months.

How to run it

  1. List everyone (Day 1). Brainstorm every audience the initiative touches: leadership, peer architects, engineering teams, product, security, finance, customers, vendors. Aim for breadth; prune later.
  2. Score Power and Interest (Day 1). Each on 1–5. Power = can they fund / approve / block? Interest = are they affected day-to-day? Average per dimension.
  3. Validate by interview (Week 1). 15 min per high-power stakeholder. Ask: "How involved do you want to be? What format do you prefer? What would make you say no?" Recalibrate the score.
  4. Place on the 2×2. Cap at 12 stakeholders per grid. Cluster similar roles. Result: clear quadrants.
  5. Write the strategy per quadrant. For each: content, format, cadence. Differences between quadrants are deliberate — not "send everyone the same doc".
  6. Name sponsors and allies. Per high-power stakeholder, identify who advocates when you are not in the room. Build the relationship before you need it.
  7. Build profiles for high-power stakeholders. Their KPIs, language, preferred format, objection patterns. Use the profile when writing communications.
  8. Refresh quarterly. At the architecture retrospective. People leave, OKRs shift, interest changes. Maps that don't refresh become fiction.

Deep dive — what makes the map produce influence

Asymmetry is the point. Time spent on stakeholder X should be proportional to their Power × Interest score, not equal across the board. Spending 30 minutes a week on a CTO who can approve $500 k of headcount beats spending the same time on a peer team that is mildly affected. The default — equal time everywhere — is the failure mode the map is designed to fix.

Validate interest by interview, never assume. Org-chart power is observable; actual interest is not. The CTO may have 12 competing concerns and want a monthly 1-pager, not a weekly meeting. A 15-minute interview per high-power stakeholder ("how involved do you want to be? what format works?") is the cheapest way to avoid over- or under-investing for the next six months.

Write the audience-specific summary first. The executive 1-pager is not "the first page of the design doc" — it is a different artefact with a different goal. It answers "what business outcome does this enable, what does it cost, what risks?" in 3 minutes. The technical design doc is linked, not embedded. Trying to make one doc serve both audiences satisfies neither.

Sponsors and allies are part of the map. Mendelow's grid is necessary and not sufficient. Lara Hogan's lens — who sponsors funding, who provides cover, who connects you across teams, who tells you the truth — extends the formal grid into the relational work that gets things done. Name the sponsors before you need them; the relationship takes months to build and weeks to use.

Stakeholder profiles compound over time. For each high-power stakeholder, keep a living profile: their KPIs/OKRs, the language they use, the objections they've raised before, the formats they prefer. The next communication writes itself when the profile is current. Without profiles, every interaction starts from zero.

Maps decay; refresh them. Org changes, role changes, OKR shifts — a map drawn in January is fiction by July if nobody refreshes it. The architecture retrospective is the natural forcing function: every quarter, revisit each map and update power/interest scores. Maps that aren't refreshed produce communications targeted at people who no longer matter.

The grid is for an initiative, not a person. The same stakeholder can be high-interest on one initiative and low-interest on another. Draw a separate grid per major initiative; do not maintain one global grid for "all stakeholders". Initiative-scoped maps stay accurate; global maps become bureaucracy.

Lifecycle of a stakeholder map

Drafted

Brainstormed list of all audiences. Initial Power × Interest scores by the architect. Stored in the initiative's design doc as a section.

Validated

15-min interviews with high-power stakeholders. Scores recalibrated. Sponsors and allies named. Strategy per quadrant written.

Active

Communications executed against the strategy. Profiles updated after each significant interaction. Asymmetric investment maintained.

Refreshed

Quarterly review at the architecture retrospective. New stakeholders added; departed ones removed; scores re-validated against current reality.

Re-scoped

Initiative scope expands or shifts. Map redrawn for the new scope. Old map archived with a "superseded by" link.

Archived

Initiative shipped or cancelled. Map archived with the design doc. Stakeholder profiles preserved for future initiatives that touch the same people.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
One-size-fits-all communication Architect writes a 20-page design doc and shares the link with everyone. Executives skim the first page and bounce; engineers read everything. Mis-pitched for both audiences. Write the audience-specific summary first (1 page for executives) and the full design doc separately (10–20 pages for engineers). The summary links to the doc, not the other way around.
Assuming interest level instead of asking Engineers assume the CTO is high-interest when in reality the CTO has 12 competing concerns and is medium-interest. Architect over-invests in CTO communication and under-invests in the actual decision-maker (a director). Validate interest with a 15-minute stakeholder interview. Ask: "How much detail do you want, and how often?" The answer recalibrates the strategy. Re-validate annually.
Map drawn once and never updated Map created at initiative start and never revisited. Six months in, the CTO has left, two new VPs joined, and the map is fiction. Communication continues on auto-pilot to people who no longer matter. Revisit at every architecture retrospective (quarterly). Add new stakeholders; demote those whose interest or power changed. Living artefact, not a one-time deliverable.
No named sponsor for the initiative Map identifies high-power stakeholders but no one is named as the advocate when the architect isn't in the room. First budget review without the architect kills the initiative. Per high-power stakeholder, identify the person who will advocate. Build that relationship intentionally — it takes months and rescues the initiative in weeks.
One global stakeholder map for everything Architect maintains a single org-wide map. Every initiative gets the same audience and the same cadence. Strategy fits no initiative well. Draw a separate map per significant initiative. Stakeholders' power/interest varies by initiative; only initiative-scoped maps stay accurate.
Communications written without a profile Each new doc to a high-power stakeholder starts from scratch. Same objections discovered repeatedly. Architect's writing time multiplied. Maintain stakeholder profiles: KPIs, language, objection patterns, preferred format. Reuse the profile for every new communication. Updating the profile takes minutes; writing without one wastes hours.

One initiative, one grid, three high-power profiles, named sponsors. Updates land in the same file as the RFC, so the map travels with the initiative. Refreshed at the next architecture retrospective.

Quality bar

  • One map per significant initiative. Stored in the design doc / RFC. No global "all stakeholders" map.
  • Power and interest validated by interview. 15 min per high-power stakeholder before the strategy is set.
  • Strategy per quadrant documented. Content, format, cadence — explicitly different between quadrants.
  • Sponsors and allies named. For every high-power stakeholder, a named advocate is identified.
  • Profiles maintained for high-power stakeholders. KPIs, language, objection patterns, preferred format. Updated after each significant interaction.
  • Asymmetric investment. Communication time proportional to Power × Interest, not equal across stakeholders.
  • Quarterly refresh. Architecture retrospective updates each active map. Departed stakeholders removed; new ones added.
