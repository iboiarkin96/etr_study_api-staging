Capacity planning & FinOps — design for cost

Make cloud spend a deliberate engineering decision rather than a CFO surprise.

TL;DR

Capacity planning estimates future resource needs (compute, storage, bandwidth) from growth projections and measured utilisation. FinOps — formalised by the FinOps Foundation in 2019 — pairs engineering and finance to optimise cloud spend. Together they prevent over-provisioning (wasted money) and under-provisioning (outages at scale). Architects who cannot answer "can we handle 10× traffic for Black Friday and at what cost?" become blockers when leadership asks.

What it is

Capacity planning and FinOps are two complementary disciplines that together govern cloud resource usage:

  • Capacity planning — estimating future resource needs from current utilisation and projected growth. Output: a per-service plan that answers "if we grow 5× in twelve months, what will we need and when?".
  • FinOps — the financial-operations practice of running cloud at scale. The FinOps Foundation (founded 2019, now under the Linux Foundation) defines a three-phase model: Inform (visibility into spend), Optimise (action on findings), Operate (continuous governance).

Together they cover the full lifecycle: design-time decisions (instance types, region choices), operational tagging and reporting, ongoing optimisation (reserved instances, rightsizing, savings plans), and budget alerts that catch unexpected cost growth before it shocks the next finance review.

Why we need it

Cloud bills that surprise the CFO kill startup growth. A 3× cost increase quarter-over-quarter, discovered at month-end, derails the next funding conversation and forces emergency cost-cutting that prioritises optics over architecture. Equally, under-provisioning creates outages at peak load — the architect who cannot model capacity becomes a blocker when the board asks "can we handle 10× traffic on launch day?".

FinOps and capacity planning prevent both failure modes. Tagging gives finance visibility into which products and teams generate cost. Capacity plans turn growth into a deliberate decision. Unit costs (cost per request, cost per active user) become a KPI that engineering and finance share. An architect who can frame technical choices in unit-economics terms becomes a strategic asset to leadership.

How others do it

FinOps Foundation

The FinOps Foundation, established in 2019 and now under the Linux Foundation, defines the canonical framework. Their three-phase model (Inform / Optimise / Operate) and twelve principles are the industry standard. The Foundation runs a free certification programme (FinOps Practitioner) and publishes the annual State of FinOps report based on hundreds of practitioner surveys. The framework is vendor-neutral but most strongly used in AWS, GCP, and Azure deployments.

What we take: Start in the Inform phase. Tag everything before trying to optimise anything. The first quarter of FinOps work is almost entirely visibility — without tagging, optimisation has no target.

Source: FinOps Foundation framework

Google SRE — capacity planning

The Google SRE Book's chapter on Non-Abstract Large System Design includes a detailed capacity-planning methodology: load testing as the canonical input, headroom factors (typical 2× over peak), and the role of resource forecasting in incident postmortems. Google has documented how the same techniques apply at startup scale with simpler inputs — the discipline is what scales, not the spreadsheet.

What we take: Build a 2× headroom factor into capacity plans by default. Real-world peaks consistently exceed forecasts. Headroom is the difference between a stretching system and an outage.

Source: Google SRE — Non-Abstract Design

Spotify

Spotify's FinOps practice is publicly documented on their engineering blog and at conference talks. Spotify achieved 25%+ year-over-year cost efficiency improvements by implementing aggressive tagging, team-level cost ownership, and unit-cost KPIs (cost per monthly active user). Each Squad sees its own cost dashboard and is responsible for its spend. The Squad model from Team Topologies aligns directly with FinOps team-level ownership — each Squad is also a cost centre.

What we take: Push cost ownership down to the team. A team that owns its bill makes architectural choices that consider cost; a team that doesn't see its bill optimises only for performance and convenience.

Source: Spotify Engineering blog

Atlassian — unit economics

Atlassian shares "cost per active user" as a board-level KPI and runs an internal "Cloud Cost Council" that reviews unit economics monthly. Engineering teams compete to drive their own unit cost down; product launches must include a unit-cost forecast in the same document as performance targets. Their FinOps at Atlassian talks describe how unit economics moved cost conversations out of finance and into product strategy.

What we take: Treat unit cost as a product metric, not a finance metric. The launch checklist should include a unit-cost forecast alongside latency and reliability targets.

Source: Atlassian Engineering — Cloud

Anatomy of capacity planning & FinOps

Resource tagging

Every cloud resource tagged with team, service, environment, cost-centre. Tags enforced at provisioning (Terraform module, AWS Config rule). Untagged resources are flagged for removal weekly. Without tagging, FinOps is impossible — you cannot optimise what you cannot attribute.

Capacity plan per service

A short document per critical service: current utilisation (peak and average), 6- and 12-month projection, headroom factor (2× default), trigger points for scaling. Plain Markdown or spreadsheet — sophistication of format matters less than discipline of regular updates.

Unit-cost KPI

Cost per request, per active user, per transaction. Tracked monthly. Trend matters more than absolute value: unit cost going up means the system is becoming less efficient as it grows; unit cost going down means architectural decisions are paying off.

Weekly cost review

A 15-minute weekly check by the architect or designated FinOps lead. Reviews spend trend, top-5 cost growers, unexplained anomalies, forecast variance against budget. Catches surprises within days, not at month-end.

Budget alert

AWS Budgets / GCP Budgets configured per team with alerts at 80% and 100% of monthly budget. Alerts go to the team owner and the architect. Without alerts, cost surprises arrive at month-end when the bill is already final.

Optimisation backlog

A ranked list of candidate optimisations (rightsizing targets, reservation candidates, idle resources, storage-class moves) with estimated saving and effort. Reviewed quarterly; top items become engineering tickets. Without a backlog, optimisation is opportunistic and forgotten.

How to run it

  1. Tag everything (Week 1–4). Mandate four tags — team, service, environment, cost-centre — and enforce them in Terraform / AWS Config / GCP Org Policy. Block deployments that don't tag.
  2. Stand up dashboards (Week 2–4). Per-team cost dashboards in AWS Cost Explorer / GCP Billing / Azure Cost Management. Each team sees its own number; the architect sees the rollup.
  3. Pick the unit-cost metric (Week 4). One number that matters: cost per MAU, per request, per transaction. Publish it monthly. Make it visible to the leadership team.
  4. Draft capacity plans (Week 4–8). One page per critical service. Current utilisation, 6/12-month projection, scaling trigger. Owner is the service tech lead; the architect reviews.
  5. Configure budgets and alerts (Week 6). Per-team budgets with 80% and 100% alerts. Wire alerts to Slack channels the team actually reads.
  6. Run the weekly cost review (Week 8+). 15 minutes every Monday. Spend trend, anomalies, forecast variance, backlog. Keep it short and ritualistic.
  7. Quarterly optimisation sprint (Week 12+). One sprint per quarter dedicated to working the optimisation backlog: rightsizing, reservation purchases, idle-resource cleanup, storage-class moves.

Deep dive — making cost a first-class concern

Why unit cost beats total cost. Total cloud spend always grows with the business; that growth tells you almost nothing about efficiency. Unit cost — cost per request, per active user, per transaction — separates "we grew" from "we got less efficient as we grew". A startup whose total bill triples while MAU quintuples is winning; one whose bill doubles while MAU is flat is losing. Frame every conversation in unit-cost terms and the FinOps function stops feeling like a budget cop.

The order of operations matters. Rightsize before you reserve. A 3-year reserved instance on the wrong size locks in a 30% mistake. Right-sizing typically frees 20–40% before any reservation discount; only then is a reservation purchase rational. After that, savings plans tend to beat instance-specific reservations in flexibility for a similar discount.

Capacity-plan headroom is non-negotiable. Forecasts are wrong; peaks consistently exceed mean by more than humans intuit. A 2× headroom factor over forecast peak is the SRE-canonical default. For latency-sensitive workloads (lesson player, checkout) push it to 3×. For batch jobs (analytics, billing) you can drop to 1.5× because failure is recoverable.

The tagging policy is the prerequisite to everything. Optimisation, reporting, charge-back, unit-cost calculation — none of it works without tags. Make tags mandatory at provisioning. Block untagged resources in CI/CD. Audit weekly. The team that gets tagging right in the first quarter unblocks every later FinOps move; the team that delays tagging spends the rest of the year fighting attribution arguments.

Push ownership down. A central FinOps team that owns decisions becomes a bottleneck and is perceived as adversarial. A central FinOps team that owns tooling and benchmarks — and pushes decisions to the engineering teams that made the architectural choices — scales and stays welcome.

Lifecycle of a capacity plan

Draft

Service tech lead writes the first plan from current utilisation. Architect reviews. Headroom factor and scaling triggers agreed.

Active

Plan in use. Quarterly review compares projection to reality; re-forecast based on actual growth. Plan is a living document, not a launch artefact.

Trigger hit

Utilisation crosses scaling trigger (e.g. 70% of plan ceiling). Engineering team executes pre-agreed scaling action (add instances, shard, archive).

Reforecast

Growth diverges materially from projection (faster or slower). Plan is re-baselined; new 12-month projection issued; budget revisited with finance.

Retired

Service decommissioned or merged. Plan archived with a final cost line item. Tags removed; budget reallocated.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
Reserved instances / savings plans bought before rightsizing The team buys 3-year reservations to capture the discount, but the underlying workloads are over-provisioned. They lock in the wrong size for 3 years. Rightsize first (match instance types to actual utilisation), then buy reservations on the right-sized footprint. Rightsizing typically frees 20–40% before reservations enter the picture.
Capacity plans written once at launch and never updated The team writes a plan at launch with a 12-month projection. Six months in, real growth diverges from projection. Nobody updates the plan. The original plan is fiction. Schedule a quarterly capacity review per critical service. Compare projection to reality. Re-forecast based on actual growth. The plan is a living document, updated quarterly.
Cost ownership centralised in a FinOps team A central FinOps team owns all cost decisions. Engineering teams have no visibility into their own bill. The FinOps team becomes a bottleneck and is perceived as adversarial. Push cost ownership to engineering teams. Each team sees its own dashboard. FinOps function provides tooling, training, and benchmarks — not decisions. Decisions live with the teams that make the architectural choices.
Tagging policy declared but not enforced A policy says "all resources must be tagged" but nothing blocks untagged provisioning. Untagged resources accumulate; attribution arguments derail every cost review. Enforce tags at provisioning: Terraform module rejects missing tags, AWS Config rule flags violations, CI/CD blocks the deploy. Audit weekly; treat untagged resources as candidates for deletion.
Headroom factor dropped under cost pressure Finance pushes back on the 2× headroom as "wasted capacity". The team drops to 1.2×. The next traffic spike causes a P1 outage. Frame headroom as insurance with a price tag: a 2× headroom on a $50k/month service costs $50k/month and prevents outages whose cost is far higher. Hold the line at 2× for latency-sensitive workloads; allow 1.5× only for batch.

Worked example — Study App cost dashboard

A weekly cost-review snapshot for the lesson-player service, three quarters into FinOps adoption. The architect runs through this in 15 minutes every Monday.

  → +8.7% MoM (forecast +6%) — investigate

## Unit cost
  cost per MAU: $0.018 (target ≤ $0.020) ✓
  cost per lesson played: $0.0021 (target ≤ $0.0025) ✓

## Top 5 cost growers
  1. RDS (db.r6g.2xlarge × 2)         $1 480  (+$120)

  4. S3 — raw lesson uploads             $310  (+$15)
  5. ElastiCache — session              $180  (+$5)

## Capacity headroom (against plan)
  RDS:        62% of ceiling — OK
  player-api: 71% of ceiling — TRIGGER hit, scale +1 task

## Actions this week

The dashboard is plain text on purpose: it is read in a 15-minute meeting and pasted into the engineering channel. The discipline is the weekly review, not the sophistication of the format.

Quality bar

  • Tagged coverage ≥ 95%. At least 95% of monthly spend is attributable to a team, service, environment, and cost-centre. Untagged spend < 5% and trending down.
  • Unit cost published monthly. One headline unit-cost metric (cost per MAU or per request) reported every month, with a 12-month trend chart visible to leadership.
  • Capacity plan per critical service. Every Tier-1 service has an active plan reviewed at least quarterly; trigger points are pre-agreed and runbooks linked.
  • Budgets and alerts in place. Every team has a monthly budget with 80% and 100% alerts wired to a Slack channel the team actually reads.
  • Weekly cost review held. The 15-minute weekly review happens on schedule; minutes posted in a shared channel; anomaly investigations tracked to closure.
  • Quarterly optimisation sprint. One sprint per quarter dedicated to working the optimisation backlog; saved $/year reported back to the leadership team.
