Build vs buy vs OSS — structured decision framework

Evaluate every significant component systematically — to avoid building what commodities provide and buying what differentiates you.

TL;DR

A structured framework for evaluating whether to build, buy, or adopt OSS for any significant component. Five dimensions: differentiation, 3-year TCO, lock-in risk, integration complexity, team capability. Output: scored comparison table documented as an ADR. Trigger: above $5k/year SaaS, 2 eng-months to build, or strategic. Startups that skip the framework either build commodity infra or buy strategic constraints.

What it is

A decision framework applied to any significant new component or capability. "Significant" = above $5k/year SaaS cost, or 2+ engineer-months to build, or strategically important. Five dimensions:

  • Differentiation — does building this provide competitive advantage? If competitors can buy the same SaaS, building it is not differentiating.
  • 3-year TCO — true total cost including build + run + maintain + opportunity cost. SaaS sticker price often beats build-cost on year 1 but loses on year 3.
  • Vendor lock-in risk — what does exit look like? Can you swap providers in 1 quarter or is it a 1-year migration?
  • Integration complexity — how does this fit your data flow, auth, observability?
  • Team capability — do you have the skill to operate the chosen path? Knowledge gap is itself a cost.

Output: scored comparison table (build / buy / OSS) documented as an ADR. Required for every triggering decision.

Why we need it

Without a framework, decisions are made by available framing: the engineer who likes building defaults to build; the founder who likes vendors defaults to buy. Both default modes are wrong about half the time. Building commodity infrastructure (auth, email delivery, log shipping) is the most common founder mistake — it ties up engineers for months on capabilities the market provides cheaply. Buying core product capability (the recommendation engine, the unique algorithm) creates strategic dependencies that block future moves.

The framework forces an explicit trade-off conversation. Documenting the trade-offs as an ADR makes the reasoning durable; future architects can revisit if conditions change (vendor goes bankrupt, scale changes economics, new OSS emerges). Without documentation, the same decision gets re-litigated every 18 months.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Joel Spolsky — "build what differentiates"

Joel's 2001 essay "Strategy Letter II" articulated the rule: build what differentiates your product; commoditise everything else. The corollary: never build commodity infrastructure when SaaS exists. Joel's argument is grounded in 25 years of observation that the time spent building commodity ate the time available to build differentiation.

What we take: The differentiation test is the load-bearing one. "Would building this win us customers?" If no, buy it.

Source: Joel on Software — Strategy Letter

Basecamp — "It just doesn't matter"

Basecamp / 37signals' default is to pick the boring option and ship; they explicitly bias toward not-building. DHH's posts about preferring OSS over SaaS for infrastructure ("we own our own servers") are matched by his preference for SaaS over build for product (Stripe for payments).

What we take: Default to not building. Building is the expensive option; you should need a reason to choose it, not a reason to avoid it.

Source: Basecamp / DHH posts

Stripe — buy at scale, build at fit

Stripe famously bought payment processors and built on top, then built their own card-network connectivity once volume justified it. The pattern: buy until the volume + differentiation flips the math. The TCO recalculation is built into their planning cycle.

What we take: The build-vs-buy answer is not static. Re-evaluate when scale, differentiation, or vendor terms change. Mark the trigger in the ADR ("re-evaluate when volume > X").

Source: Stripe engineering posts

Adobe — Kubernetes / OSS contribution

Adobe contributes heavily to Kubernetes / OSS rather than maintaining forks. The argument: their needs converge with the upstream community 95%; the remaining 5% are best contributed back. Forks become maintenance burdens; upstream contribution shares the burden.

What we take: When using OSS, contribute upstream for needed changes. Forks are short-term wins and long-term costs.

Source: Adobe Engineering Blog

Across teams: differentiation test first, default to not-build, re-evaluate periodically (the answer changes with scale), contribute upstream when OSS. Quick build-everything cultures slow product velocity; quick buy-everything cultures accumulate vendor lock-in.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Trigger threshold

$5k/year SaaS, 2 eng-months build, or strategic. Below threshold: no formal eval needed.

Differentiation test

"Would building this win us customers?" The load-bearing question. Honest answer kills most build proposals.

3-year TCO model

Build cost + ongoing maintenance + opportunity cost vs SaaS cost growth + lock-in cost. Spreadsheet attached to the ADR.

Lock-in exit plan

For Buy: what does swap look like? For OSS: who maintains if community withers? Plan documented even if not executed.

Scored comparison table

Build vs Buy vs OSS rows × dimensions columns × 1–5 score per cell. Highest total wins; ties broken by lock-in.

Re-evaluation trigger

"Revisit when volume > X" or "when annual cost > $Y". Marks future re-decision moments.

ADR-documented decision

The decision lives in an ADR with the scored table + considered options. Survives team changes.

How it works in this project

Study App applies the framework to any new component crossing the $5k/year or 2 eng-month threshold. The evaluation lives in docs/decisions/build-vs-buy/<component>.md as a scored table; the resulting decision is captured as a full ADR in docs/adr/. Re-evaluation triggers are tracked in docs/decisions/revisit-triggers.md; the quarterly architecture retrospective reviews any that crossed.

Default biases: for commodity infrastructure (auth, email, log shipping, payments) — buy or OSS. For learning-specific algorithms (recommendation, adaptive difficulty, content matching) — build because that's the moat. A short ADR locks the framework dimensions and the trigger thresholds.

Deep dive — the theory you need

The differentiation test — honest answer

"Would building this win us customers?" An honest answer kills most build proposals. Engineers want to build (it's interesting); the differentiation test asks the strategic question. If three competitors all use Stripe for payments, building your own payment-processor doesn't differentiate — it just delays your other work. If your recommendation algorithm is the reason customers choose you, that's the build case.

3-year TCO — what gets counted

True TCO is more than initial price:

  • Build TCO: initial dev cost + ongoing maintenance (typically 30-50% of initial / year) + opportunity cost of engineers not working on differentiation + on-call burden.
  • Buy TCO: annual SaaS cost (often grows 30-50%/year as you scale) + integration cost + lock-in cost (what does exit cost?) + vendor risk premium.
  • OSS TCO: integration cost + operational cost (your team runs it) + maintenance cost (you keep up with versions, security patches) + community-divergence cost if you customise.

Build sticker is usually lowest at year 1; SaaS is highest at year 1 but flattens; OSS is medium with operational risk. Reality at year 3 is often inverted: build is highest because of maintenance and opportunity cost; SaaS is lowest if scale is reasonable; OSS in the middle.

Vendor lock-in — the often-ignored dimension

Lock-in is easy to underweight at decision time and devastating at exit time. Three lock-in axes:

  • Data lock-in: can you export your data in a usable format? How long does it take?
  • Schema lock-in: how much of your code references vendor-specific concepts (DynamoDB partition keys, Salesforce object model)?
  • Operational lock-in: vendor SLAs, support routes, billing terms — what changes if vendor changes terms?

For each Buy candidate, write the exit plan as a 1-page doc. If the exit plan is "rewrite everything", the lock-in cost is high — adjust the TCO accordingly.

The re-evaluation trigger

The right answer to build-vs-buy changes with scale. Stripe-built-then-replaced their payment processor when volume justified the change. The decision should encode the trigger: "revisit when volume > 100k tx/month" or "when annual cost > $50k". Mark it in the ADR; track it in a revisit-trigger doc that the quarterly retro reviews. Without explicit triggers, the org keeps an obviously-wrong decision because nobody is responsible for re-deciding.

OSS — adopt vs fork vs extend

OSS has three flavours of engagement:

  • Adopt — use as-is. Lowest cost. Most common.
  • Extend — write plugins, hooks, or sidecars. Medium cost. Upstream-friendly.
  • Fork — diverge from upstream. Highest long-term cost. Avoid unless absolutely necessary.

Default to adopt; extend only when necessary; fork only when the upstream community is dead or rejects your changes. Forks become maintenance burdens that compound over years.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Decision time. A real build-vs-buy eval is 4–10 hrs. Cheap relative to a wrong decision.
  • Cultural friction. Engineers who default to build resent the formal eval. Show wins via averted build mistakes.
  • Vendor evaluation skill. Comparing 3 SaaS vendors requires due diligence the team may not have. Pair with someone who does (CFO for cost, security lead for compliance).
  • Re-evaluation discipline. Markers in the ADR are easy to write and easy to ignore. The retrospective is the check.

Lifecycle

trigger crossed → differentiation test → scored 3-way comparison → 3-yr TCO + lock-in → ADR with revisit trigger → implementation → quarterly retro checks triggers

Trigger crosses threshold → differentiation test (most build proposals fail here) → scored 3-way comparison if pass → TCO + lock-in modelled → ADR with revisit trigger written → implementation. Quarterly retro inspects any revisit triggers that crossed.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Default-to-build culture Engineers like building; the differentiation question never gets asked. Default to not-build. Need a reason to choose build, not a reason to avoid it.
TCO model is initial cost only Sticker price comparison favours build. 3-year TCO including maintenance, opportunity cost, and lock-in. Build sticker is cheapest; build TCO usually isn't.
Lock-in ignored at decision time Vendor selection focuses on features. Write the exit plan as a 1-page doc per buy candidate. High exit cost → adjust TCO; if can't exit, find another vendor.
No revisit trigger Decision lives forever. Encode "revisit when X" in the ADR. Quarterly retro tracks. Wrong decisions correct at the threshold, not 3 years later.
Fork OSS at the first divergence Easier to fork than to negotiate with maintainers. Adopt → extend → fork (last resort). Forks compound maintenance over years; upstream contribution shares the burden.
Vendor due-diligence skipped Sales process is the eval. Independent due diligence: customer references, SLA history, financial health, exit terms. Sales pitches are not evidence.

Worked example

Scored decision

Email delivery — Buy vs Build vs OSS

# Decision: Email delivery for transactional email
trigger: $8k/year SaaS estimated · 1.5 eng-months to build
context: Study App needs to send 200k transactional emails / month
         (signup confirm, password reset, lesson reminders)

## Differentiation test
"Would building our own SMTP infrastructure win us customers?"
NO. Customers don't pick learning platforms on email delivery quality.
Commodity capability. Build is rejected at the test.

## Comparison: SES (buy) vs Postal (OSS)

dimension              SES (buy)              Postal (OSS, self-host)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3-yr TCO              $30k                   $40k (ops + maintenance)
integration time     2 days                  3 weeks
team capability      ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (we know AWS)    ⭐⭐ (no SMTP ops experience)
lock-in              low (DNS-based exit)    n/a (self-hosted)
deliverability       ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (AWS reputation)  ⭐⭐⭐ (we build reputation)
DKIM/SPF/DMARC       managed                 we configure
compliance           SOC 2, GDPR docs        we audit ourselves
score (1–5 / dim)    24                      14

## Decision: AWS SES

## Revisit triggers
- volume > 5M emails/month: SES cost crosses $30k/year, re-eval OSS
- deliverability complaints > 1% bounce: re-eval at vendor + alternatives
- AWS suspends or rate-limits us: emergency fallback to SendGrid or Postal

## ADR follow-up
ADR 0058 — "Use AWS SES for transactional email" to be authored
before implementation begins. Will document the comparison + revisit
triggers above as the permanent record.

Outcome. Decision took ~6 hours of evaluation across architect + SRE + finance. Build was rejected at the differentiation test in 15 min — saving an estimated 6 engineer-weeks. The SES vs Postal comparison showed SES was the right call despite higher sticker price, because team capability + deliverability dominated the score. The revisit triggers are tracked in docs/decisions/revisit-triggers.md; at the 2027-Q1 retro, the team reviewed and confirmed SES was still right (volume was 1.2M/month, well below the 5M threshold).
