Architecture mentoring — grow the next architects

Deliberately transfer architectural thinking so the team multiplies — and the architect stops being a bottleneck.

TL;DR

A deliberate practice of transferring architectural thinking to senior engineers growing into architect roles. Four activities: pair-design, reading group, shadowed reviews, growth 1:1s. Codified by Will Larson in Staff Engineer (2021). Architects who don't mentor become bottlenecks; a mentored team of 10 architectural thinkers moves faster than a solo architect with 10 implementors.

What it is

Architecture mentoring is the deliberate practice of building architectural thinking in senior engineers. Not the same as project mentoring or career mentoring — it specifically targets system-design skill at the architect level. Four complementary activities cover the territory:

  • Pair-design sessions — architect + mentee co-author a real design doc. Mentee drives; architect coaches. Weekly, 1–2 hours.
  • Reading group — small group reads canonical texts together (DDIA, Building Evolutionary Architectures, Staff Engineer, Team Topologies). Monthly, 60 min, focused questions.
  • Shadow reviews — mentees attend architecture reviews as active participants, expected to give feedback. Architect debriefs afterward.
  • Growth 1:1s — monthly with explicit architectural growth goals. Not project status; not career generalities. "This quarter, lead one design doc end-to-end."

Goal: by the end of the 6–12 month engagement, the mentee authors and leads design reviews independently and is recognised as a credible architectural voice.

Why we need it

Architects who don't mentor become bottlenecks. Every significant design decision funnels through one person. Growth velocity caps at the architect's individual throughput. When the architect leaves, takes leave, or is reassigned, architectural decisions stall.

A mentored team of 10 senior engineers who think architecturally moves faster than a solo architect with 10 implementors. Throughput scales because decisions parallelise. Mentoring also creates succession: the next architect comes from inside, with full context, instead of months of external ramp-up. Larson's "the work of a Staff Engineer is to multiply the team" applies precisely here.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Will Larson — Staff Engineer (2021)

Defines four staff archetypes (Tech Lead, Architect, Solver, Right Hand) plus 14 interviews on how senior engineers grew and grow others. Core argument: the staff engineer's work is to multiply the team's output, and mentoring is the highest-leverage way to do that.

What we take: Use the archetype framework with mentees. The "Architect" archetype is specific — not "senior engineer with more years". Naming which archetype a mentee is growing into clarifies the path.

Source: StaffEng — Will Larson

Camille Fournier — stretch opportunities

In The Manager's Path Fournier articulates "stretch opportunities" — work slightly beyond current capability, with the senior as a safety net. Explicit growth goals, regular feedback, and stretch work are the engine of growth in her framework.

What we take: Create stretch opportunities deliberately. Mentee leads work slightly beyond current ability — not work they already can do (no growth) or completely beyond (failure). The architect catches them mid-fall.

Source: Camille Fournier — elidedbranches

Google — mentoring as measured work

Google's L6+ engineer's responsibility explicitly includes growing L5 engineers into L6 candidates. Promotion criteria emphasise "scope" and "impact" — both require mentoring as a multiplier, not just personal output. Mentor performance is a primary signal in the review.

What we take: Make mentoring a measured part of the architect role, not a side activity. Performance review includes mentee growth alongside the architect's own design output.

Source: Software Engineering at Google

Shopify — design doc co-authoring

Shopify pairs senior architects with growing engineers as explicit co-authors on internal design docs. The co-author byline shows up in the doc front-matter; the mentor's name is on the artefact alongside the mentee's. Cultural signal: mentoring is visible, valued, and shared work.

What we take: Co-author bylines make mentoring visible. Single-author docs hide who did the coaching; dual-author docs surface the multiplication effect.

Source: Shopify Engineering

Across teams: explicit growth goals, stretch opportunities with safety net, mentoring measured in reviews, visible co-authorship. Without these, "mentoring" becomes vague coffee chats and the architect remains a bottleneck.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Mentee selection

2–3 mentees at a time. Strong engineering judgement + desire to grow into architecture + current scope adjacent to architect's. Ask explicitly.

Pair-design cadence

Weekly 1–2 hr, co-author real design docs. Mentee drives keyboard + structure; architect coaches questions, alternatives, risks.

Quarterly growth goals

2–3 named goals per mentee. "Lead one design doc end-to-end", "facilitate one architecture review", "give one tech talk".

Structured feedback

3 things that worked + 2 to improve per doc. Specific, not "good job". Vague feedback doesn't transfer learning.

Reading group

3–5 books per year. Monthly 60-min discussion with prepared questions. Drives shared vocabulary.

Shadow + active participation

Mentees attend architecture reviews; expected to give feedback. Architect debriefs the mentee's contribution afterward.

Graduation criteria

Authors + leads design reviews independently, recognised by peers. Typically 6–12 months. Graduated mentee becomes mentor for next cohort.

How it works in this project

Study App's architect (current single architect) runs the mentoring programme with 2 mentees at any time. The list is at docs/team/architecture-mentees.md: name, start date, current growth goals, design docs co-authored. Pair-design sessions are scheduled Thursday afternoons (90 min). Reading group is monthly first-Friday: 60 min discussion of one chapter from the current book.

Growth goals are reviewed at monthly 1:1; results discussed at the quarterly architecture retrospective. Mentee graduation is celebrated publicly in the team channel — and the new architect-track engineer becomes a mentor for the next cohort.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Coaching the question, not the answer

When a mentee is stuck on a design choice, the impatient architect provides the answer. The mentee learns the answer but not the process. Coaching the question instead: "What alternatives have you considered? What's the failure mode you're worried about? Who is the worst-case customer for this?" The mentee learns to ask those questions independently, which is the actual skill of architecture. Set a time-box: "if you're stuck in 30 minutes, ask me a specific question". The specific-question discipline is itself the practice.

The four staff archetypes (Larson)

Not every senior engineer wants to become an architect. Will Larson's framework names four distinct senior roles:

  • Tech Lead — guides a single team's execution; deep on one stack.
  • Architect — system-level design across multiple teams; lighter on day-to-day code.
  • Solver — drops into hard cross-cutting problems; ad-hoc, no team.
  • Right Hand — extends a senior leader; primarily organisational.

Architecture mentoring is for engineers growing into the Architect or Solver archetypes. Tech Leads and Right Hands need different mentoring. Mismatching the archetype to the development path produces frustration on both sides.

Stretch opportunity calibration

The right level of stretch:

CalibrationSignalResult
Too easyMentee finishes quickly, no questions, no struggleNo growth
Right stretchMentee makes progress with hesitation, asks 2–4 specific questions, needs ~1 mid-course correctionGrowth
Too hardMentee paralysed, demoralised, can't find the question to askErosion of confidence

Calibration is the architect's job. Pick the next assignment based on the mentee's current capability + 20%. Wrong calibration is the most common mentoring failure.

Career conversation vs technical mentoring — separate slots

If the same 1:1 covers technical growth + career advice + project status, all three suffer. The technical mentoring conversation has a specific agenda: design doc in progress, recent review feedback, growth goal this quarter. Career advice is valuable but distinct — reserve a separate slot. Mixing them dilutes both.

Graduation matters — both for the mentee and the next cohort

An endless mentoring engagement signals that the architect doesn't trust the mentee — or doesn't want to relinquish the gate. Set explicit graduation criteria up front; celebrate them publicly when reached. The graduated mentee becomes a mentor for the next cohort, which is the only way the practice scales beyond the original architect's bandwidth.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Architect time. ~4–6 hrs/week with 2 mentees. Pair sessions + 1:1s + feedback on docs. Real ask; protect the slots.
  • Throughput dip. Letting the mentee drive makes any given design doc slower. Investment in throughput at the team level pays back at month 6–9.
  • Selection bias. Wrong mentees waste both parties' time. Be willing to end engagements that aren't producing growth.
  • Architect ego. Some architects enjoy being the bottleneck — the gate-keeping is the identity. Mentoring requires giving up that role. Cultural fit matters.

Lifecycle

identify mentee (explicit ask) → set growth goals (Q1) → weekly pair-design + feedback → shadow reviews · reading group → stretch lead (Q2–Q3) → graduation review → mentee becomes mentor

6–12 month arc. Q1: foundation (pair-design, reading, shadow). Q2–Q3: stretch — mentee leads design docs solo with architect-on-call. Q4 (or month 12): graduation review — mentee leads a real architecture review and authors a complete design doc with no coaching. Graduated mentee becomes a mentor.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Architect does the work "because it's faster" Mentee struggles; architect takes the keyboard. Let the struggle happen — it's the learning. Time-box: "stuck in 30 min, ask me a specific question". Coach the question, not the answer.
Mentoring becomes career advice 1:1 drifts into promotions, politics, vague aspirations. Reserve a separate slot for career. Mentoring 1:1 has a technical agenda: doc in progress, last review, this quarter's goal.
Wrong-archetype mentees Architect picks a strong engineer who doesn't want the role. Ask explicitly: "do you want to grow into architecture?". Respect the answer. Senior ICs are valid; not everyone wants the path.
Wrong-stretch calibration Assignment too easy (no growth) or too hard (paralysis). Pick capability + 20%. Calibrate at quarterly review.
No graduation Engagement drags forever; architect doesn't relinquish. Explicit criteria up front. Celebrate publicly. Graduated mentee mentors next cohort.
Vague feedback "Good job!" / "Looks fine". 3 things that worked + 2 to improve, specifically. "Alternatives section was thorough — consider adding cost comparison next time".

Worked example

Growth-goal record

Mentee: @oleg (eng lead → architect track), Q3 2026

# Architecture mentoring · @oleg · Q3 2026
mentor:    @ivan
archetype: Architect (Larson)
started:   2026-04-01  ·  target graduation: 2026-Q4

## Q3 growth goals (3)

G1  Lead end-to-end design doc for "events pipeline v2"
    success: doc authored, 1 review session led, ADR merged
    architect involvement: weekly pair-design (1.5 h), feedback after
                            each review
    status (mid-quarter): doc 60% complete, 1 review session led

G2  Facilitate one architecture review (not just attend)
    success: agenda set, reviews driven, action items captured
    architect involvement: shadow first time, full lead second time
    status (mid-quarter): shadowed 2026-W22, leads on 2026-W26

G3  Read "Building Evolutionary Architectures" + lead reading group
    success: chapter discussion led; one fitness function PR proposed
    status (mid-quarter): on chapter 4 of 8; discussion led 2026-W23

## Stretch level (calibration)
Goal G1 is 20% stretch — @oleg has authored simpler docs solo before,
but never one that spans 3 teams. Pair-design provides the safety net;
the team-coordination skill is the genuine growth area.

## Feedback log (per session)

2026-W21 pair-design (events pipeline v2):
  + Alternatives section explored 3 options with real trade-offs (good)
  + Open Questions section anticipated reviewer pushback (good)
  + Diagrams were clear; the C4 container view captured boundaries
  - Cost analysis was hand-waved — needs real estimates next iteration
  - Risk section listed risks but no mitigations — pair these next time

2026-W23 reading group on "Evolutionary Architectures" ch. 3:
  + Discussion question opened with concrete project example
  + Pulled @maria's experience into the conversation
  - Ran 15 min over because no time-box; set timer next time

## Graduation criteria (end of Q4)
☐ Authored 2+ design docs end-to-end (currently 1 in progress)
☐ Led 3+ architecture reviews (currently 0 led, 1 planned)
☐ Recognised as architectural voice in 2+ unprompted slack threads
☐ Self-identified next growth (succession from being mentee to mentor)

Outcome. Mid-quarter check showed @oleg on track for two of three goals; G2 (facilitating reviews) was lagging because the next scheduled review kept getting bumped. The architect intervened by ensuring @oleg facilitated a smaller / lower-stakes review first (a service deprecation discussion) to build confidence before the larger upcoming one. By Q4 end, all three goals were met; @oleg led the events-pipeline v2 review unaided and was publicly recognised as the second architect-track engineer. He took on his own first mentee (an SRE-lead with interest in distributed systems) in Q1 of the next year — the multiplication effect Larson describes.
