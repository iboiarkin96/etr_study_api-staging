Threat modeling — security by design

Identify and mitigate security threats at design time, when they cost minutes instead of months to fix.

TL;DR

Threat modeling is a structured process for identifying security threats before building. STRIDE (Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information disclosure, Denial of service, Elevation of privilege) — developed at Microsoft in the 1990s — is the canonical framework. Applied per service at design time, it produces a threat-model document with mitigations recorded as security requirements. Vulnerabilities found in production cost 30–100× more to fix than ones caught at design — threat modeling shifts security left and gives the team a shared threat vocabulary.

What it is

Threat modeling is a structured analysis performed at design time to identify security threats and the mitigations required to defend against them. The STRIDE framework, developed at Microsoft in the late 1990s by Loren Kohnfelder and Praerit Garg, categorises threats into six types:

  • Spoofing — impersonating another user or system. Mitigated by strong authentication (mTLS, MFA).
  • Tampering — modifying data in transit or at rest. Mitigated by integrity controls (signatures, hashes, IaC).
  • Repudiation — denying that an action occurred. Mitigated by audit logs and signed events.
  • Information disclosure — exposing sensitive data. Mitigated by encryption + least-privilege access.
  • Denial of service — making the system unavailable. Mitigated by rate limiting, quotas, WAF.
  • Elevation of privilege — gaining permissions beyond what is authorised. Mitigated by RBAC, zero-trust, least privilege.

The session input is a Data Flow Diagram (DFD) — boxes for processes, arrows for data flows, cylinders for data stores, dashed lines for trust boundaries. The team walks through each element and asks each STRIDE question. Identified threats are scored (CVSS) and assigned mitigations. The output is a threat-model document with required security controls listed as engineering requirements.

Why we need it

Security vulnerabilities found in production are catastrophically more expensive than ones caught in design. Industry data from IBM and others puts the cost ratio at 30–100× for design-time vs production-time security findings. Beyond direct cost, a single high-severity vulnerability can erode customer trust, trigger compliance penalties, or end a startup. The 2017 Equifax breach (147 M people affected) originated from a known vulnerability that better threat modeling and patching processes would have caught.

Threat modeling also creates a shared security vocabulary on the team. When the team has run a STRIDE session together, "what about authorisation here?" becomes a natural question in code review. Without threat modeling, security is the responsibility of whoever happens to think of it — unreliable at startup speed.

How others do it

Microsoft — STRIDE & SDL

STRIDE was developed at Microsoft in the late 1990s and adopted as the threat-modeling standard in the Microsoft Security Development Lifecycle (SDL) in the early 2000s. Microsoft maintains the free Threat Modeling Tool, which generates threat lists from DFDs automatically. The SDL was a response to the security crises of the late 1990s (Code Red, Nimda) and is the canonical example of shifting security left.

What we take: Use the Microsoft Threat Modeling Tool for the first 5–10 sessions as training wheels. Automatic threat enumeration teaches the team STRIDE before they internalise it. Once internalised, free-form diagram + STRIDE walkthrough is faster.

Source: Microsoft Threat Modeling Tool

OWASP — open-source guidance

OWASP maintains the canonical open-source guidance on threat modeling: the Threat Modeling Cheat Sheet, the Application Threat Modeling Guide, and OWASP Threat Dragon (open-source tool). Guidance covers both STRIDE and PASTA (Process for Attack Simulation and Threat Analysis), a heavier methodology used by financial institutions. OWASP also publishes the Top 10 Web Application Security Risks, updated every 3–4 years.

What we take: Use the OWASP Top 10 as a parallel checklist alongside STRIDE for web applications. STRIDE is comprehensive; Top 10 is prioritised. Combining them catches both systematic threats and high-frequency real-world attacks.

Source: OWASP Threat Modeling

Adam Shostack — Four Questions

Adam Shostack (former Microsoft) wrote the canonical textbook on threat modeling (2014, Wiley). His Four Questions Framework simplifies the practice to: (1) What are we working on? (2) What can go wrong? (3) What are we going to do about it? (4) Did we do a good job? Shostack argues STRIDE is the most learnable threat framework because its categories map directly to security properties developers already understand.

What we take: Use Shostack's Four Questions as the session structure. Simpler than the full SDL methodology and produces 80% of the value. Reserve full STRIDE deep-dives for high-risk services.

Source: Shostack — Threat Modeling book

CISA / Threat Modeling Manifesto

The Threat Modeling Manifesto (2020) — authored by Shostack, Avi Douglen, and others — defines the values + principles for threat modeling that align with modern engineering practice: a culture of finding and fixing design issues; a varied toolbox; not "one true methodology". US CISA published Secure-by-Design guidance in 2023 that effectively mandates threat modeling for critical-infrastructure software.

What we take: Pick the lightest method that produces real findings. Don't lock in to one methodology; STRIDE for most services, PASTA for high-stakes financial flows, OWASP Top 10 for web-facing endpoints. The manifesto authorises pragmatism.

Source: Threat Modeling Manifesto

Anatomy of a threat-modeling session

Data Flow Diagram (DFD)

Diagram showing processes (circles), data stores (cylinders), external entities (boxes), data flows (arrows), trust boundaries (dashed lines). The DFD is the input to threat enumeration. Without a DFD the session devolves into ad-hoc brainstorming.

STRIDE walkthrough session

2-hour session per service. Participants: service owner, architect, security engineer (if available), one other senior engineer. Walk through each DFD element; ask each STRIDE question. Record threats with description, affected element, severity, status (open/mitigated/accepted).

Risk scoring (CVSS 3.1)

Each threat gets a CVSS 3.1 severity (open standard) — preferred over DREAD (subjective). Threats above a configurable threshold (typically CVSS ≥ 7.0) require mitigation before launch. Lower severity can be accepted with documented rationale.

Mitigation requirements + tickets

Each unmitigated threat above threshold translates to a security requirement on the implementation team — tracked as a ticket with owner and due date. Verified before launch and re-verified annually. Without explicit tracking, mitigations are aspirational.

Accepted-risk register

Threats below threshold or with cost-prohibitive mitigations can be accepted, but the acceptance is documented: severity, rationale, expiry date (re-review at most annually). Acceptance without a written record is just an oversight.

Annual re-review

Threat models go stale as services evolve. New features add attack surface; new dependencies introduce new threats. Annual re-review (or after major architecture changes) catches drift. Without re-review, threat models become historical documents.

How to run it

  1. Draw the DFD (Day 1 morning). Service owner + architect sketch the data flow: processes, stores, external entities, flows, trust boundaries. Cap at one A4 page; sub-diagrams for detail.
  2. Schedule the 2-hour session. Service owner, architect, security rep (if available), one other senior engineer. Calendar-block, not "we'll find time".
  3. Walk through STRIDE × every element (90 min). Each DFD element × each STRIDE letter. Record threats with description, affected element, attack scenario, current controls.
  4. Score each threat (CVSS 3.1, 15 min). Threshold typically CVSS ≥ 7.0 = mitigate before launch. 4.0–7.0 = scheduled mitigation. < 4.0 = accept with rationale.
  5. Convert to tickets (last 15 min). Every threat above threshold becomes a ticket with named owner and due date before launch. End the session with tickets created, not with a list-of-findings document.
  6. Document accepted risks. Below-threshold threats accepted with written rationale and expiry date. Lives in the threat-model doc next to the mitigations.
  7. Block launch on the tickets. Launch gate checks: all CVSS ≥ 7.0 tickets closed, lower ones tracked, accepted-risk register reviewed. No tickets, no launch.
  8. Re-review annually + on major changes. New flow, new external integration, new data class → re-run STRIDE for the affected slice. Architecture review checklist includes "threat model updated?".

Deep dive — what makes the practice land

The DFD is the work. Most "threat modeling" failures are actually missing-DFD failures. Without a diagram, the team brainstorms threats ad-hoc, repeats themselves, misses entire classes. The DFD is the canvas STRIDE paints on. Spend 30 minutes on the DFD before the threats discussion; the rest of the session pays it back tenfold.

STRIDE is exhaustive on purpose. The discipline is asking every letter at every element. Skipping "this isn't a spoofing risk, obviously" is exactly where threats hide. Many real-world breaches sit in the categories engineers thought obviously didn't apply. Walk the matrix; trust the framework over intuition.

CVSS over DREAD. DREAD's subjective 1–10 scores produce scores that vary wildly between assessors. CVSS 3.1 is an open standard with documented sub-scores; two assessors arrive at similar numbers; auditors and customers recognise it. Use CVSS for severity and stop debating DREAD numbers; the time saved is the win.

Tickets, not documents. A threat-model document with 30 findings that never become tickets is theatre. End every session with tickets created in the engineering tracker, named owner, due date before launch. Block the launch gate on the tickets. The conversion to tickets is the load-bearing step; without it the document is decorative.

Acceptance must be written. "We're accepting that risk" said in a meeting is not acceptance — it's silent omission. Written acceptance carries severity, rationale, named accepter, expiry date (≤ 1 year). The discipline of writing it down forces honest conversations and gives the next assessment a real starting point.

Engineers run the sessions, not the security team. A central security team running all threat models is a bottleneck and produces models the engineers don't own. The security team's job is to teach. Facilitate the first 2–3 sessions per service team, then step back; engineers run their own from there. Self-sufficient teams scale; central security does not.

Re-review is mandatory. Threat models age. New features add attack surface; new dependencies introduce new threats; trust boundaries shift as services split. Annual re-review (and on major architecture change) catches the drift. Architecture-review checklist includes "threat model updated?" so the question is asked at every relevant moment.

Lifecycle of a threat model

Drafted

DFD sketched. Service owner + architect aligned on scope and trust boundaries. Session scheduled.

Modelled

2-hour STRIDE walkthrough complete. Threats recorded with CVSS scores. Mitigations + accepted risks captured in writing.

Mitigations in flight

Above-threshold threats converted to tickets with named owners and pre-launch due dates. Engineering team executing.

Cleared for launch

All CVSS ≥ 7.0 tickets closed and verified. Lower tickets tracked. Accepted-risk register reviewed and signed off. Launch gate passes.

Active

Service in production. Re-review triggers: major architecture change, new external integration, new data class, annual cadence.

Re-modelled

DFD refreshed, STRIDE rerun on the affected slice. Delta tickets created. Accepted-risk expirations renewed or replaced.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
Threat model produced once at launch, never updated Team runs a session before launch, ships, never revisits. Two years later the service has tripled in scope; original model covers 30% of the current system. Schedule annual re-reviews. Architecture-review checklist item: "Has the threat model been updated for this change?" Treat threat models as living documents.
Threats identified but never converted to tickets Session produces 30 threats; nobody owns the conversion to mitigation tickets. The document sits in a folder; engineering work proceeds without mitigations. End every session with ticket creation. Each unmitigated above-threshold threat becomes a ticket with named owner + pre-launch due date. Block launch on the tickets.
Threat modeling treated as security-team-only Security team runs models in isolation, presents findings to engineers. Engineers passively receive; don't learn the methodology. Security team becomes a bottleneck. Train engineers to run their own. Security team facilitates first 2–3 sessions per service team, then steps back. Self-sufficient teams scale.
No DFD — ad-hoc brainstorming Team skips the DFD and dives straight into "what could go wrong?". Same threats repeated; entire categories missed; session feels unproductive. 30 minutes on the DFD before the STRIDE walkthrough. The diagram is the canvas; threats are placements on it. No DFD, no session.
Silent risk acceptance "We're accepting that risk" said in a meeting; never written down. Months later nobody remembers who accepted what, on what basis, with what expiry. Written acceptance only: severity, rationale, named accepter, expiry ≤ 1 year. Lives in the threat-model doc next to mitigations. Re-reviewed on expiry.
DREAD scoring → endless debate Team uses DREAD; subjective 1–10 scores produce arguments instead of decisions. Time spent on numbers; less time on mitigations. Use CVSS 3.1 — open standard with documented sub-scores. Faster, more consistent, recognised by auditors and customers. Stop debating; start fixing.

Quality bar

  • Threat model per Tier-1 service. Lives in-repo next to the service; DFD source committed (PlantUML).
  • DFD before threats. 30 minutes on the diagram before STRIDE walkthrough; no diagram, no session.
  • STRIDE × every element. Every letter asked at every DFD element. No "obviously doesn't apply".
  • CVSS 3.1 scoring. Open standard; DREAD retired. Sub-scores documented for high-severity findings.
  • Every above-threshold threat → ticket. Named owner, pre-launch due date. Launch gate blocks on ticket closure.
  • Written accepted-risk register. Severity, rationale, accepter, expiry ≤ 1 year. Re-reviewed on expiry.
  • Engineers run sessions. Security team facilitates the first 2–3 per service team, then steps back. Self-sufficient teams.
  • Annual re-review + on major change. Architecture-review checklist asks "threat model updated?". Stale models flagged.
