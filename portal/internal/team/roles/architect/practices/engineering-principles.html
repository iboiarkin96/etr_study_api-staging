Engineering principles — shared design standards

Short opinionated statements that replace preference battles in code review with documented intent.

TL;DR

Engineering principles are short, opinionated statements that define how the team makes design decisions. Examples: "prefer boring technology", "delete more code than you add", "optimise for developer experience". A typical set is 8–12 principles, each an imperative sentence + 2-line rationale + example. They are living: reviewed annually, cited in code review, retired when they stop adding signal. Stripe, Spotify, Basecamp all publish theirs. Without shared principles each engineer defaults to their last team's habits and code reviews become preference battles.

What it is

Engineering principles are short, declarative statements that codify how the team makes design decisions. A principle is not a rule (rules are mechanical and enforced by linters); it is a heuristic that engineers apply judgement to. A good principle has three properties:

  • Opinionated — it takes a clear position. "Use the right tool for the job" is not a principle, it is a tautology. "Prefer boring technology" is a principle because it expresses a preference.
  • Falsifiable — it could be wrong, and a reasonable engineer could disagree. Principles nobody would disagree with provide no signal.
  • Citable — short enough to reference in code review. "Per principle 4, can we simplify this?" is a normal review comment.

A typical set is 8–12 principles. Each is an imperative sentence followed by a 2-line rationale and a concrete example. The set is published (publicly or internally) and reviewed annually. Principles the team never cites are retired; new principles emerge from recurring code-review conversations.

Why we need it

Without shared principles, each engineer brings the defaults from their previous team. The ex-Google engineer favours one approach; the ex-Amazon engineer favours another. Code reviews become preference battles — "this is better because I prefer it" — that consume time and erode collegiality. The same debate recurs every time a new engineer joins.

Principles replace these recurring debates with a documented baseline. New engineers read the principles in onboarding and understand which trade-offs the team has already decided. Code-review feedback grounded in a principle ("we prefer boring technology, and Redis here is unnecessary because Postgres can do this with a table") is harder to argue with than personal preference. The principles also act as a forcing function during principle review — when the team writes them down, hidden disagreements surface and get resolved.

How others do it

Stripe — public engineering principles

Stripe publishes its engineering principles publicly. Examples: "Be a builder", "Always raise the bar", "Move with urgency". Principles are short, action-oriented, and cited in onboarding and design reviews. Stripe also has more specific technical principles ("Use commodity tools for commodity problems", "Build what you need to be the best in the world at") that guide build-vs-buy decisions.

What we take: Publish principles publicly when possible. Public principles are a recruiting signal, a customer-facing trust signal, and accountability for the team to actually live by them.

Source: Stripe engineering principles

Dan McKinley — Choose Boring Technology

Dan McKinley (former Etsy principal engineer) published Choose Boring Technology in 2015. It became one of the most cited engineering principles in the industry. The post introduces "innovation tokens": each new technology costs a token; you have a limited budget; spend the budget on technologies that differentiate the business, not on commodity infrastructure. Foundational reference for principles at hundreds of companies.

What we take: Steal proven principles from established essays rather than inventing new ones. "Choose Boring Technology" has internalised a decade of industry learning; reinventing the equivalent from scratch is wasted work.

Source: McKinley — Choose Boring Technology

Basecamp / 37signals — Shape Up + handbook

Basecamp's Shape Up handbook and public employee handbook codify principles like "Sleep on it", "Better to half-do a thing than half-ass it", and "It just doesn't matter". The principles guide both product and engineering decisions. Basecamp's commitment to small teams (rule of three) and 6-week cycles are expressed as principles, not as process diagrams.

What we take: Principles can be opinionated about not doing things. "It just doesn't matter" is a principle that gives engineers permission to ignore micro-optimisations. Principles set the floor and the ceiling.

Source: Basecamp employee handbook

Spotify — principles that span tech and org

Spotify's engineering culture (documented in their "Engineering Culture" videos and blog) is built around principles like "Autonomy enabled by alignment", "Squads own their part end-to-end", and "Fail fast, learn fast". Reinforced through onboarding, internal talks, and the structural design of the Squad-Tribe-Chapter-Guild model. Spotify treats principles as the connecting tissue between technical architecture and organisational structure.

What we take: Principles work best when they apply to both technical and organisational decisions. "Prefer reversible decisions" applies equally to architecture and to team structure.

Source: Spotify Engineering blog

Anatomy of a principle

Imperative statement

A 5–10 word imperative sentence. "Prefer boring technology." "Delete more code than you add." "Optimise for developer experience." The brevity is the feature — it makes the principle citable in code review.

Rationale

2–3 sentences explaining why this principle exists. The rationale lets the team judge edge cases. A principle without rationale becomes a rule applied dogmatically; a principle with rationale becomes a heuristic applied judgementally.

Example or counter-example

A specific instance the team agreed on. For "Prefer boring tech": "Don't pick CockroachDB when Postgres is sufficient. Pick CockroachDB only when you need cross-region writes — which you probably don't yet." Examples anchor the principle in concrete decisions.

Citation surface

A stable short name or number so reviewers cite it in PRs. "Per P-03, why Kafka here instead of SQS?" Citing externalises the disagreement — it becomes the team's documented standard vs the proposal, not reviewer's opinion vs author's.

Annual review

Once a year the team reviews every principle. Three outcomes: keep (still useful), revise (rationale has shifted), retire (no longer cited in practice). The review prevents dogma — principles evolve with the team.

Cap on count

8–12 principles maximum. New principles require retiring an existing one. The cap forces prioritisation — only principles that genuinely matter survive. A set of 30 principles is a set of zero principles.

How to run it

  1. Mine recurring debates (Week 1). Look at the last 6 months of code reviews and design discussions. Patterns of disagreement (boring vs novel, build vs buy, micro-services vs modular monolith) are the seeds of principles.
  2. Draft 8–12 candidate principles (Week 2). One imperative sentence + 2-line rationale + example each. Steal from Stripe, McKinley, Basecamp where the principle already fits.
  3. Pressure-test each candidate. For every principle ask: "could a reasonable engineer disagree?" If no, sharpen it. "Write good code" fails; "Delete more code than you add" passes.
  4. Review with the team (Week 3). 90-minute working session. Each principle gets discussed; explicit thumbs up/down. Disagreements get resolved before publication.
  5. Publish in the repo. docs/engineering-principles.md with stable IDs (P-01 … P-12). Linked from onboarding, design-doc template, code-review guide.
  6. Cite in code review. Reviewers reference principles by ID. The architect models the behaviour in their own reviews for the first month so it becomes normal.
  7. Annual review (Year 1 anniversary). Keep / revise / retire each principle. Celebrate retirements — they mean the team has learned. Updated principles are signs of a living culture, not a failed one.

Deep dive — what makes principles bite

Falsifiability is the test. A principle that nobody could disagree with does no work. "Write good code" is aspirational without being opinionated; "Prefer boring technology" is opinionated because a reasonable engineer could prefer the opposite. The discomfort of being opinionated is what gives a principle its signal. If you cannot imagine an engineer pushing back, the principle is too vague.

Citation matters more than authorship. Principles that get cited in code review change behaviour; principles that sit unread in a doc do not. Stable short IDs (P-03, P-07) make citation easy; long names ("Our principle of preferring established technology over novel choices") make citation effortful. The architect models the behaviour in their own reviews until it becomes the team norm.

Rationale lets the team handle edge cases. A principle is a heuristic, not a rule. The rationale explains why the principle exists; the why is what lets the team judge when the principle does not apply. "Prefer boring tech because each new technology costs an innovation token, and the budget is finite" tells the team when boring is wrong — when the specific business problem genuinely requires the novel choice.

Examples are the load-bearing artefact. Abstract principles are read once and forgotten; principles with concrete examples are remembered because the example becomes the mental anchor. "Choose Postgres before Cassandra, until you can show a specific need" is more memorable than "Prefer proven over novel". Update examples annually as the team's reference incidents change.

The cap is the hard part. Teams want to add principles ("we should also have one about testing", "and about security", "and about documentation"). After two years the set is 30 principles and nobody remembers any of them. The cap of 8–12 forces a trade: a new principle pushes an old one out. The negotiation is where the team learns what it actually values.

Retire publicly. Retiring a principle should be a celebrated act, not a quiet edit. The retirement explains what the team learned. "We retired 'Always prefer microservices' because in practice we now prefer a modular monolith for new domains" is more useful than the original principle ever was. Make retirements visible in the annual review and in changelog entries.

Lifecycle of a principle

Candidate

Emerged from a recurring code-review debate or a recent essay. Drafted as imperative + rationale + example. Not yet endorsed by the team.

Adopted

Endorsed in the working session. Assigned a stable ID. Added to the published set; referenced in onboarding and design-doc template.

Active

Cited in code review and design discussions. Examples updated as new reference incidents land. The architect models citation until it becomes team-wide.

Revised

Rationale or example updated to reflect what the team has learned. Statement may be sharpened but the ID stays stable so old citations remain valid.

Retired

Team no longer cites it; the world has shifted. Retirement note explains what changed. Old PRs that cite it remain valid for context but no new citations are expected.

Superseded

Replaced by a sharper or more current principle. Supersession trail kept so future readers can trace the evolution; both IDs remain referenceable in history.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
Principles that are tautologies — nobody could disagree Team writes "Write good code" and "Be a good engineer". Nobody disagrees; nobody changes behaviour. Principles are aspirational without being opinionated. Pressure-test: "could a reasonable engineer disagree with this?" If no, the principle is too vague. Sharpen it until disagreement is visible — that is where it adds value.
Too many principles (30+) — none get cited Team adds every good idea they hear. After a year the set is 30. Nobody remembers them. Reviews revert to personal preference. Cap at 12. New principles require retiring an existing one. The cap forces prioritisation — only principles that matter most survive.
Principles never updated — become dogma Written in year 1 and never revisited. By year 3 the team has grown and some principles no longer fit. Team still cites them because retiring feels like admitting failure. Annual review with explicit retirement option. Make retirement celebrated — it means the team has learned. Updated principles are signs of a living culture.
No example — principle stays abstract Author writes a clean statement and a tidy rationale but no concrete example. Reviewers cannot anchor the principle to a real decision; the principle stays theoretical. Every adopted principle has at least one concrete example. Update examples annually as new reference incidents land. Examples are the load-bearing artefact.
Principle authored top-down by architect alone Architect writes the principles in isolation. Team has no buy-in; nobody cites them; principles feel like rules imposed from above. Run the working session with the team. Each principle gets explicit endorsement. Disagreements resolved in the room. Buy-in is non-negotiable.
Principles too long to cite Statement is two sentences, names are five words long. Reviewers cannot quote it in a comment without losing the thread. 5–10 word imperative. Stable short ID (P-03). Long-form lives in the document; the citation surface stays terse.

Worked example — Study App principles set

The 10-principle starter set for Study App. Each line is the citable form; the published document at docs/engineering-principles.md has rationale and examples for each.

# Study App · engineering principles (v1, 2026-05)

P-01  Prefer boring technology.
      Each new tech costs an innovation token. We have ~3 to spend.
      e.g. Postgres before Cassandra until cross-region writes prove necessary.

P-02  Modular monolith first; microservices on top of DDD only when justified.
      Operational cost of microservices outpaces team-autonomy benefit until
      the org genuinely demands it.

P-03  Delete more code than you add.
      Removing a feature flag or dead module is celebrated like shipping.
      Small codebases are kind to humans.

P-04  Optimise for developer experience.
      < 30 s feedback loop. < 5 min onboarding. Painless deploys.
      DX compounds; friction compounds faster.

P-05  Prefer reversible decisions.
      Two-way doors first. One-way doors get an ADR and a review.
      e.g. cloud vendor lock-in is a one-way door; ORM choice is two-way.

P-06  Write the design doc before writing the code.
      Cheapest moment to discover a wrong path is on paper.

P-07  Ship behind feature flags by default.
      Decouple deploy from release. Flag every new behaviour for > 0 users.

P-08  Domain language in code; vendor language behind ACL.
      Stripe and Auth0 vocabulary never reaches the core domain.

P-09  Observability is a feature, not a phase.
      Logs, metrics, traces shipped with the feature, not after launch.

P-10  Blameless retros after every incident.
      Findings are properties of the system, not the engineer.

Set capped at 12. New principle requires retiring an existing one.
Annual review: 2027-05.

The set is short, opinionated, and citable. Reviewers reference "P-02" in PRs the way they reference Jira tickets. The annual review is on the calendar; retirements get an explicit changelog entry. The team owns the set, not the architect alone.

Quality bar

  • Set capped at 8–12 principles. New principle requires retiring an existing one. The cap forces prioritisation.
  • Every principle is falsifiable. A reasonable engineer could disagree. Tautologies retired immediately.
  • Stable short ID per principle. P-01 … P-12. Citation surface stays terse so reviewers can quote it inline.
  • Imperative + rationale + example. All three present. Examples updated annually as reference incidents land.
  • Published in-repo, linked from onboarding. docs/engineering-principles.md referenced from onboarding, design-doc template, code-review guide.
  • Cited in code review. Reviewers reference principles by ID. Architect models the behaviour for the first month.
  • Annual review with explicit keep / revise / retire. Calendared. Retirements celebrated and recorded with the reason.
