Tech-debt management — visible and prioritised

Catalogue, quantify, and prioritise technical liabilities so the team can make informed trade-offs instead of paying interest invisibly.

TL;DR

Tech-debt management catalogues, quantifies, and prioritises technical liabilities in a structured register. Ward Cunningham coined the metaphor in 1992 to explain quick-and-dirty code to non-engineers — shipping fast incurs debt that must later be paid with interest. Each item has an owner, an interest rate (days/sprint of slowdown), and a payoff cost. Priority = interest ÷ payoff. Reviewed monthly with engineering leadership; 15–20% of sprint capacity protected for payoff. Invisible debt slows every feature; visible debt becomes a deliberate decision.

What it is

Tech-debt management is the discipline of treating technical liabilities as a structured backlog rather than as a vague complaint. The register is a shared list of debt items, each captured with five attributes:

  • Description — what is the debt? Specific enough that someone new to the codebase can understand the problem.
  • Interest rate — how much does this debt cost per sprint in slowdown? Engineer-days of delay caused by working around it. Estimate from team observation, not exact measurement.
  • Payoff cost — engineer-days to fully resolve, including implementation, testing, and rollout. Add a 30% buffer.
  • Owner — team or engineer accountable for tracking and (eventually) paying it off. Without an owner, debt items go stale.
  • Priority — derived from interest ÷ payoff. High-interest, low-payoff items are the quickest wins.

Each item is also tagged with its flavour using Fowler's quadrant — deliberate vs inadvertent × prudent vs reckless. The register is reviewed monthly with engineering leadership. A target of 15–20% of sprint capacity is protected for debt payoff — enough to make steady progress without blocking feature delivery.

Why we need it

Invisible tech debt slows every feature. A team that spends 30% of every feature's time working around a broken auth abstraction is paying interest constantly — but nobody knows because the interest is amortised across all work. When the architect says "we should refactor auth", it sounds like discretionary cleanup. When the architect says "the broken auth abstraction costs us 3 engineer-days per sprint across the team — 36 days/year, paying back the 20-day fix in two months", it becomes a business case.

The register also creates accountability. Without it, every senior engineer has a mental list and complains about a different subset. With it, the worst debts are visible to product leadership and prioritised against features in a transparent forum. Debt that gets ignored is debt nobody bothered to add to the register — fix that first.

How others do it

Ward Cunningham — the original metaphor (1992)

Cunningham coined "technical debt" in a 1992 OOPSLA experience report to explain quick-and-dirty code to non-engineers using a financial analogy: shipping fast incurs debt that must later be repaid with interest. Cunningham later clarified that the metaphor applies specifically to deliberate debt — code shipped knowing it would need rework — not accidental design mistakes. The clarification matters because deliberate and inadvertent debt have different owners and different urgency.

What we take: Tag every register entry with its flavour. Deliberate debt is paid back on plan; inadvertent debt is fixed; reckless debt triggers a process change so it stops happening. Different flavours, different responses.

Source: Ward explains the debt metaphor

Martin Fowler — debt quadrant

Martin Fowler extended Cunningham's metaphor with the technical debt quadrant (2009): deliberate vs inadvertent × prudent vs reckless. The four flavours: prudent deliberate ("we know we should ship the SLA work later"), reckless deliberate ("no time for design"), prudent inadvertent ("now I know how I should have done that"), reckless inadvertent ("what's layering?"). The categorisation is diagnostic — different flavours need different organisational responses, not just different fixes.

What we take: Use the quadrant as a triage tool. Reckless inadvertent debt indicates a skills gap (training, mentoring); reckless deliberate indicates a planning failure (process change). Prudent debt of either kind is normal engineering.

Source: Fowler — Technical Debt Quadrant

ThoughtWorks — Managing technical debt

ThoughtWorks has published extensively on tech-debt management through the Tech Radar and consulting practice. Their recommended approach: single shared debt register, monthly reviews with leadership, 15–20% sprint capacity allocated to payoff. They distinguish "tech debt" (manageable, known) from "tech wasteland" (so much accumulated debt the system is unmaintainable) — the threshold is roughly when the team spends more time working around debt than building features.

What we take: 15–20% sprint allocation is the industry standard. Less and debt grows faster than it is paid; more and feature velocity suffers. Calibrate based on the current debt load.

Source: ThoughtWorks — Managing technical debt

Stripe — investment ratio

Stripe explicitly invests in paying down tech debt as a strategic asset, not a cleanup activity. Their engineering principles include "leave the campsite cleaner than you found it" and their internal practice is to allocate dedicated debt-payoff capacity periodically rather than only opportunistic refactoring. Stripe publishes engineering "investment ratios" (% features / % platform / % debt) as part of their planning rhythm — making the trade-off explicit at the leadership level.

What we take: Publish an explicit investment ratio (e.g. 60% features / 25% platform / 15% debt) at the team level. Makes the trade-off visible and gives the architect a tool to justify debt work in the same conversation as feature work.

Source: Stripe engineering principles

Anatomy of a debt register

Debt register

Shared document — spreadsheet, GitHub issues with a 'tech-debt' label, or a Linear project. One row per item. Required columns: description, flavour, interest, payoff, owner, priority, status. Stored in a known location; linked from the team's README.

Interest-rate estimate

Engineer-days per sprint of slowdown caused by this debt. Estimated by the team — exact measurement is impractical. The estimate forces the question "how much does this actually cost?". An item with no measurable interest is preference, not debt.

Payoff-cost estimate

Engineer-days to fully resolve. Includes implementation, migration, rollout. Underestimates cause repeated debt-payoff failures — be generous. Add 30% buffer for unforeseen complications.

Fowler quadrant tag

Each entry tagged: deliberate or inadvertent × prudent or reckless. Drives the response: reckless inadvertent → training; reckless deliberate → process change; prudent debt → scheduled payoff.

Allocation discipline

Each sprint reserves 15–20% capacity for debt payoff. Protected: features that take longer than expected do not cannibalise debt time. Without protection, debt time is the first thing sacrificed under feature pressure.

Monthly review

30-minute review with engineering leadership. Reports: top 5 items by interest, items paid off this month, items added, total debt cost trend. Without this ritual the register is invisible to leadership.

How to run it

  1. Stand up the register (Week 1). Pick the storage (Linear project, GitHub issues with label, or shared doc). Define columns: description, flavour, interest, payoff, owner, priority, status. Link from the team README.
  2. Seed it from existing pain (Week 1–2). Senior engineers contribute the 5–10 items each that cost them most. Architect facilitates a 60-min session to consolidate, deduplicate, and tag with Fowler quadrant.
  3. Estimate interest + payoff (Week 2). Per item, team estimates engineer-days/sprint cost and engineer-days to fix. Approximate is fine — the discipline of estimating is more valuable than precision.
  4. Compute priority (Week 2). P1: ratio ≥ 0.3 (pays back in < 4 months). P2: 0.1–0.3. P3: < 0.1. Rank within each tier.
  5. Protect 15–20% sprint capacity (Week 3+). Reserve in every sprint. Features that overrun cut scope, not debt time. The protection is what makes the register operational.
  6. Run monthly leadership review. 30 min. Top 5 by interest, items closed, items added, ratio trend, cost trend. Calendared. Notes shared with the team.
  7. Quarterly cleanup. Close items no longer relevant (system removed, refactored elsewhere). Aim for 30–50 items per team — manageable and meaningful. A register of 200 items is signal noise.
  8. Annual ratio review. Engineering leadership: is the 15–20% allocation right? Adjust based on debt load, business cycle, hiring rate.

Deep dive — making debt visible

Interest is the load-bearing field. Every entry must carry a measurable interest cost (engineer-days/sprint of slowdown). Items without one are preferences, not debt — reject them at intake. The interest field is what converts "we should clean this up" into "this costs us 3 engineer-days a sprint, paying back the 20-day fix in two months". Leadership can argue with preferences; they cannot argue with payback periods.

Priority by ratio, not by feeling. Priority = interest ÷ payoff. High-interest, low-payoff items win first because they pay back fastest. The ratio is brutal — a beloved refactor with 0.5 days/sprint interest and a 60-day payoff loses to a small ugly fix that nobody likes but that costs 2 days/sprint and 4 days to do. Trust the ratio over taste; track the wins and the ratio earns trust.

Fowler quadrant drives the response, not just the priority. Reckless inadvertent debt ("what's layering?") signals a skills gap → mentor or train. Reckless deliberate ("no time for design") signals a planning failure → process change. Prudent inadvertent ("now I know how I should have done it") is normal learning. Prudent deliberate ("we'll come back for this") is the well-behaved kind that the register tracks best. Use the quadrant for diagnosis, not just labelling.

Protect the 15–20% from the inside. The biggest failure mode is debt time cannibalised by overrunning features. Protect by structure: continuous small payoff in every sprint, not dedicated payoff sprints. Dedicated sprints are easy to cancel; continuous allocation is invisible to cancel. When a feature runs over, scope-cut the feature, not the debt time.

Register that grows without closing is noise. Items added, items never removed, register hits 200 items in a year, nobody trusts the priority signal. Quarterly cleanup is the antidote: close items no longer relevant, merge duplicates, retire items where the system has been replaced. Aim for 30–50 items per team — large enough to capture real debt, small enough to read.

Publish the investment ratio. Make the trade-off explicit at the leadership level: % features / % platform / % debt. Stripe publishes their ratio at planning rhythm; doing the same gives the architect a tool to defend debt time when feature pressure spikes. "We agreed 15% to debt; here is what 15% buys us this quarter" is harder to override than "trust me, we need to clean this up".

Tech wasteland is a different conversation. When the team spends more time working around debt than shipping features, the register is no longer enough — the system has crossed into "tech wasteland". The answer is a strategic rewrite or rearchitecture project, with its own funding and timeline, not just a higher debt allocation. Recognise the threshold and escalate honestly.

Lifecycle of a debt item

Proposed

Engineer adds the item with description + flavour. Interest and payoff drafted. Owner suggested.

Triaged

Architect or tech lead validates: measurable interest? Right flavour? Real debt or preference? Accepted, merged with an existing item, or rejected.

Prioritised

Interest ÷ payoff computed. Slotted P1 / P2 / P3. Owner confirmed. Ranked within the tier.

In progress

Pulled into a sprint within the protected 15–20% allocation. Owner driving. Status updated in the register.

Paid off

Fix landed in prod; interest no longer accruing. Item closed with a short note on what changed. Featured in next monthly review.

Retired

Item closed without payoff because the underlying system was removed or replaced. Closed with a note explaining why; quarterly cleanup catches stragglers.

"Tech debt" used for anything an engineer dislikes Engineers add personal preferences (different framework, different style) to the register. The list grows; the priority signal weakens; leadership stops trusting it. Require an interest-rate estimate for every entry. Items without measurable interest (days/sprint slowdown) are rejected at intake. Preferences without cost are preferences, not debt.

Quality bar

  • Register exists in a single shared location. Linked from the team README. One row per item; required columns documented.
  • Every entry has measurable interest. Items without it rejected at intake. Preferences without cost are not debt.
  • Every entry has an owner. Named, not "TBD". Owner-less items go stale within a quarter.
  • Every entry has a Fowler quadrant tag. Drives the response — training, process change, or scheduled payoff.
  • Priority computed by ratio. Interest ÷ payoff. Wins by ratio, not by personal preference.
  • 15–20% sprint capacity protected. Continuous small payoff every sprint; feature overruns cut scope, not debt time.
  • Monthly leadership review. 30 min. Top 5 by interest, closed items, added items, cost trend. Calendared.
  • Quarterly cleanup. Close irrelevant items. Register stays at 30–50 items per team.
