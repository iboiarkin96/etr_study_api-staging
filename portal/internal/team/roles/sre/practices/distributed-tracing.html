Distributed tracing — follow one request across every service it touches

A trace_id propagated across every service in a request path, with spans that record latency and context per hop, forms the Gantt chart that answers "where is the time going?" for any individual request — without redeploying or adding ad-hoc logging.

TL;DR

Distributed tracing propagates a trace_id across every service in a request path. Each service emits spans — records of work with start time, duration, status, and attributes — and the spans assemble into a Gantt-style tree showing exactly where time is spent across the call graph. Instrumented via OpenTelemetry; spans shipped to a backend (Tempo, Jaeger, or a managed equivalent) for storage and query. Sampling is the lever that makes the cost tractable: 1–10% head-based for normal traffic, 100% tail-based for errors and latency outliers. Traces enable the pivot from "the P99 is slow" to "this specific DB query in this specific service at this specific percentile is the bottleneck".

What it is

Distributed tracing is the practice of attaching a shared identifier to a request and recording, at every service that handles it, a structured record of the work done. Each record is a span — start time, duration, status, parent pointer, and a bag of attributes. The spans share a trace_id and form a tree through their parent_span_id links. The tree is the trace; the trace is the Gantt chart that answers "where is the time going" for a single request, end to end, without bolting custom logging onto each service in turn.

A complete tracing implementation has five moving parts: an SDK in every service that creates spans and propagates context; propagation headers (W3C traceparent, or B3) carried on every outbound call; a collector that batches and samples the spans before they reach storage; a backend (Tempo, Jaeger, a managed equivalent) that indexes the spans by trace and supports query by service, operation, attribute, and duration; and a correlation layer that emits the active trace_id into every log line so the pivot from trace to log is one click rather than a copy-paste. Lose any one of the five and the value collapses — a sampler that drops errors is worse than no sampler, a collector without backpressure takes the application down, an SDK without correlation gives you traces nobody can cross-reference.

Why we need it

Metrics tell you the aggregate is slow. Logs tell you something went wrong on a specific line of code. Traces tell you which of fifteen services in a request path owns the latency for this request at this percentile right now. Without tracing, every performance investigation in a microservice system reduces to the same costly loop: guess where the time is going, add logging, redeploy, wait for the next incident, repeat. With tracing, the same investigation takes one query and the next thirty seconds of attention.

Traces also surface the failure modes that are invisible to any other signal. N+1 database queries that look fast individually but add up to two seconds in aggregate. Retry storms that are smeared across per-service error metrics and look like normal traffic until you look at a single trace and see the same downstream call attempted four times. Cold-start latency in a serverless function that looks like a random P99 spike on the histogram and like a 1.8-second span in the trace. Cache-miss tail behaviour. Cross-AZ hops that nobody intended to add. None of these are visible on a metrics dashboard; all of them jump out of a single trace.

How others do it

Short tour of teams whose tracing practice is published and copied. Each card is one organisation, what they do, and what we take from it.

Google (Dapper)

Dapper is the original distributed-tracing paper that every modern system descends from. Google's published rationale frames the problem precisely: at production scale, sampling is not an optimisation but the only way the system stays affordable. Dapper used uniform low-rate sampling with a separate path for tagged "interesting" traces. The 2010 paper still reads as the design document for OpenTelemetry's collector.

What we take: Sampling is a first-class design concern, not an afterthought. Decide the sampling policy before instrumenting; the alternative is a storage bill that arrives faster than the value does.

Source: Dapper, a Large-Scale Distributed Systems Tracing Infrastructure (Google, 2010)

Uber (Jaeger)

Uber built Jaeger to handle thousands of services and millions of traces per second. Two design decisions stand out: adaptive sampling that increases the rate for rare endpoints so they are never invisible, and tail-based sampling for anomalies — collect every span, decide post-hoc which traces to keep based on error status or latency. The tail sampler catches the traces that matter most while the head sampler keeps the bill flat.

What we take: Sample errors at 100% even when sampling normal traffic at 1%. Errors are the rarest and the most valuable; dropping them in the sampler defeats the entire reason for tracing.

Source: Jaeger Architecture

Honeycomb

Honeycomb's published best-practices treat traces as the primary observability signal — metrics are derived from spans, logs are derived from span events. Their guidance: instrument deeply with attributes (user_id, tenant, feature flag), keep spans cheap (no expensive serialisation in the hot path), and pivot from a slow trace directly to "all traces that look like this" by attribute. Tracing as the lookup index for everything else.

What we take: Attributes are not decoration. Every span carries the high-cardinality fields the investigation will need (tenant, route, user-tier); without them the trace is a Gantt chart with no way to filter the population.

Source: Honeycomb tracing best practices

Shopify

Shopify's observability team makes trace-to-log correlation a first-class workflow. Every log line in the aggregator carries the active trace_id; every Grafana panel that shows a span has a "Logs for this trace" button that opens the log query pre-filtered. Engineers never copy-paste a trace_id — the pivot is one click. The correlation is automatic at the logging-library layer, not asked of the engineer.

What we take: The pivot has to be built into the dashboard, not left to discipline. Manual copy-paste is a pivot that engineers skip; one click is a pivot they use.

Source: Shopify Engineering Blog

Across every team whose tracing practice is public, the same five decisions appear: instrument via a vendor-neutral SDK, propagate W3C trace context, sample aggressively for normal traffic and keep 100% of errors, carry rich attributes for post-hoc filtering, and make trace→log correlation a one-click operation.

Anatomy

The parts a complete tracing practice has, with one sentence on each. None are optional.

OpenTelemetry SDK

Vendor-neutral SDK in every service. Auto-instrumentation covers HTTP, gRPC, database drivers, and message queues without code changes; manual spans are added only for custom operations the auto layer doesn't reach.

Context propagation

W3C traceparent header on every outbound HTTP and gRPC call. Internal RPC frameworks add it once, in the shared client; service teams never propagate context by hand.

Sampling policy

1% head-based for normal traffic. 100% tail-based for any request that returns 5xx or breaches the SLO latency threshold. The error sample is the most valuable — it is never dropped.

Collector

OpenTelemetry Collector deployed as a DaemonSet (per-node) plus a small gateway tier. Batching, sampling, retry, and backpressure live in the collector — never in the application.

Backend

Tempo for storage; Grafana for query. Indexed by service, operation, and attribute; supports duration-range queries so latency outliers are findable in seconds.

Trace→log correlation

Active trace_id injected into every log line via the logging-library middleware. Grafana panels link from spans to the matching log query. One click, not copy-paste.

Definition-of-Done gate

A new service does not reach production without emitting traces. A service that generates no spans is invisible in the call graph and silently undermines every latency investigation that touches it.

How it works in this project

Study App uses the OpenTelemetry SDK for Go in every backend service. The two shared modules — otelhttp for HTTP handlers and clients, and otelgorm for database calls — handle 95% of instrumentation automatically; service teams add manual spans only for cache lookups, S3 reads, and background jobs that the auto layer doesn't touch. Spans are exported to a per-node OpenTelemetry Collector via OTLP gRPC, batched there, and forwarded to Tempo for storage. Grafana queries Tempo by trace ID, by service + operation, and by duration range.

The sampling baseline is 1% head-based on normal traffic — a deliberate choice over the more common 5–10% because Study App's request volume is high enough that 1% is statistically sufficient for percentile work, and the cost difference compounds month over month. On top of that, a tail-based sampler in the collector keeps 100% of traces that contain a span with status ERROR or whose root duration exceeds the service's SLO latency budget. The two policies together give cheap coverage for healthy traffic and complete coverage for the traces investigations actually need. Every log line written by the shared logger package carries the active trace_id in a trace_id correlation field — see structured logging for the format — and every Grafana panel that shows a span has a "Logs for this trace" link that opens Loki pre-filtered.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Spans, context, and trace assembly

A span is a single unit of work — a function call, a database query, a downstream RPC. It carries an opaque span_id, a parent_span_id pointing at the span that caused it, a shared trace_id identifying the whole request, a start timestamp, a duration, a status, and an attribute bag. Spans are emitted independently by each service; the backend assembles them into a tree by following the parent pointers. The trace exists nowhere as a single object until the backend builds it — there is no central coordinator and no synchronous handshake. That is what makes the design scale: each service writes spans into its local buffer and the topology emerges from the pointers.

Context propagation is the mechanism that makes those pointers possible. When service A calls service B, A's SDK writes the current trace_id and the current span_id into the outbound request headers. B's SDK reads them on the way in and starts a new span whose parent_span_id is the value it just received. If A forgets to propagate — or if a proxy strips the header, or a custom HTTP client bypasses the instrumentation — the trace breaks at A→B and B's spans appear as a new root, orphaned from A. That is the most common failure mode in real deployments, and the reason auto-instrumentation in the shared HTTP client matters more than any individual manual span.

Head-based vs tail-based sampling

Head-based sampling decides at the root span whether to keep the trace. The decision is cheap (one random number at the entry point) and propagates through the context so every downstream service inherits it — either all spans of a trace are kept or none are. The cost is informational: the sampler has no way of knowing the trace will turn out to be interesting, so it drops some errors and some slow requests purely by bad luck.

Tail-based sampling defers the decision to the collector, after all spans of a trace have arrived. The collector buffers a few seconds' worth of spans, looks at the assembled trace, and applies rules: keep if any span has status ERROR; keep if root duration exceeds a threshold; keep if a specific attribute is present; otherwise drop. The decision is precise but expensive — the collector must buffer every span temporarily, and a single trace whose spans arrive seconds apart can blow the buffer. The standard production answer is to run both: cheap head sampling for the baseline plus tail sampling for the slice that matters.

W3C Trace Context vs B3 propagation

Two propagation formats compete in the wild. W3C Trace Context (traceparent, tracestate) is the modern standard, endorsed by every major vendor and required by OpenTelemetry. B3 (X-B3-TraceId, X-B3-SpanId) is the older Zipkin format, still common in legacy services and in some service-mesh sidecars. Mixing them silently breaks traces: a service that only reads W3C and gets a B3 header creates a new root. The right answer in a brownfield environment is to enable both readers in every SDK (OpenTelemetry supports this via composite propagators) and to standardise on writing W3C going forward; the wrong answer is to assume the network is homogeneous.

Cost economics — 1% sampling vs 100% with tail rules

At Study App's projected steady-state RPS, 100% trace retention would cost on the order of low-thousands of euros per month in object storage alone, before query cost. 1% head sampling cuts that to low-tens of euros. The tail-sampling overlay — keep errors and latency outliers — adds back roughly 0.5–1% of trace volume, because errors and slow requests are a small fraction of total traffic. The combined policy stores ~1.5–2% of all traces and yet, on the investigations that matter, the recall is effectively 100%: every error trace and every slow trace is present. The dropped 98% is healthy traffic whose value is statistical; the percentile shapes computed from 1% of N=10⁹ requests are indistinguishable from the shapes computed at 100%.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Instrumentation surface. Auto-instrumentation reaches HTTP, gRPC, and the major database drivers; everything outside that surface — custom protocols, background jobs, batch processors — needs manual spans. The cost is real but bounded: a service typically adds five to ten manual spans on top of the auto layer.
  • Collector operational burden. The collector is a stateful, mid-tier service with backpressure, batching, retry, and sampling logic. It needs the same SLO discipline as any production service; a collector that loses spans loses traces, silently.
  • Attribute hygiene. Spans need rich attributes to be useful, but every attribute is a storage and indexing cost. The discipline is to add the high-cardinality fields the investigation will need (tenant, route, user-tier) and drop the rest; the failure mode is a span with 80 attributes nobody ever filters by.
  • Cardinality vs cost. Tracing accommodates high-cardinality attributes that metrics cannot — but only at the trace storage cost. The trade-off is explicit: put the high-cardinality data in spans, not in metric labels, and let the trace store carry that cost in exchange for the metric store staying cheap.

Lifecycle

SDK installed → context propagated → spans emitted → batched in collector → sampled (head + tail) → exported to backend → indexed & queried

A trace begins life when the SDK is added to a service and the shared HTTP/gRPC middleware is enabled. From that point, every incoming request creates a root span, and the SDK propagates the trace context to every outbound call. Each downstream service attaches its own spans as children. Spans buffer locally in the SDK, batch into OTLP frames, and stream to the per-node collector, where the sampler decides which traces to keep — head sampling drops most healthy traffic up front, tail sampling adds back the errors and the latency outliers. The kept spans export to Tempo, are indexed by trace ID, service, operation, and attribute, and become queryable in Grafana within a few seconds of emission. Traces age out per the retention policy (7 days for healthy, 30 days for traces with errors) so storage stays bounded.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Context propagation gaps break the trace One service uses a bare HTTP client that bypasses the instrumented one; a proxy or service-mesh sidecar strips the trace header; a background job doesn't carry context from the request that scheduled it. Standardise on the instrumented HTTP and gRPC clients in the shared library — make the bare client harder to use than the instrumented one. Audit the mesh config for header-allow lists. For background jobs, persist the parent trace_id with the queued payload and reconstruct the context on the worker side.
100% sampling in production "We want to see everything" — and nobody costed the storage bill before turning it on. 1% head-based for healthy traffic, 100% tail-based for errors and latency outliers. The combined policy keeps every interesting trace at a fraction of the cost; percentile shapes are stable at 1% of high-volume traffic.
Sampler drops errors The team applied a uniform sample rate to all traffic, including the rarest and most valuable traces — errors. Always overlay a tail sampler that keeps 100% of traces containing a span with status ERROR or whose root duration exceeds the SLO latency budget. Errors and slow requests are the entire reason tracing exists; never drop them by accident.
Spans without attributes Auto-instrumentation produces a span but adds only generic fields (HTTP method, status); the team never adds the high-cardinality attributes the investigation will need. Enrich every span with the dimensions that will drive filtering: tenant, route template, user tier, feature flag, request size class. Without those, the trace is a Gantt chart with no way to slice the population it came from.
No trace→log correlation The logging library was never wrapped to inject the active trace_id; engineers paste IDs by hand. Wrap the logger once, centrally, so every log line carries the current trace_id. Add a "Logs for this trace" link to every Grafana span view. Manual pivots are skipped pivots.
Mixed W3C and B3 propagation Some legacy services or sidecars only speak B3; new services only read W3C. Traces silently break at the boundary. Enable the composite propagator (W3C + B3) in every SDK so reads succeed regardless of the inbound format. Standardise on writing W3C; let the composite reader hide the historical inconsistency until the legacy is gone.

Worked example

Trace tree

HTTP GET /lessons/42 — assembled trace as shown in Tempo / Grafana

trace_id: 4bf92f3577b34da6a3ce929d0e0e4736
duration: 184 ms
status:   OK

▼ HTTP GET /lessons/{id}            [api-gateway]        0 ms ─────────────── 184 ms   OK
  attrs: http.route=/lessons/{id}  user.tier=free  tenant=eu-1

  ▼ rpc lessons.Get                 [lessons-svc]        4 ms ───────────── 178 ms   OK
    attrs: rpc.system=grpc  lessons.id=42

    ▼ db.Query SELECT lessons       [lessons-svc]        7 ms ──── 41 ms                  OK
      attrs: db.system=postgres  db.statement="SELECT id,title,body FROM lessons WHERE id=$1"

    ▼ cache.Get  lessons:42:body    [lessons-svc]       42 ms ─── 45 ms                   MISS
      attrs: cache.backend=redis  cache.hit=false

    ▼ s3.GetObject  lessons/42.md   [lessons-svc]       46 ms ────────── 171 ms          OK   ← slow
      attrs: s3.bucket=study-lessons  s3.key=lessons/42.md  s3.region=eu-central-1

    ▼ cache.Set  lessons:42:body    [lessons-svc]      172 ms ─ 177 ms                    OK
      attrs: cache.backend=redis  ttl=300s

What this trace tells us. The request took 184 ms end to end. The Postgres query is fast (34 ms). The Redis cache misses and the service falls back to S3, which dominates the budget at 125 ms — the single largest contribution to total latency. None of that is visible on the per-service P99 dashboard: lessons-svc's 95th percentile latency might be 60 ms because most requests hit the cache. The trace shows the cold-read tail behaviour for one specific request, with the offending span identified, the S3 key in the attributes, and the region — enough to file the fix without any redeployment, log search, or cross-team coordination. Pre-tracing, this investigation would have taken a day of guesswork; with tracing it takes one query and thirty seconds of attention.
