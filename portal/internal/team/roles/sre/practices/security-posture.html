Security Posture Review — a quarterly forcing function for security debt

A structured quarterly review that aggregates security signals across all services — vulnerability backlog age, secret hygiene score, compliance posture, attack surface changes — and produces a prioritised remediation plan reviewed by engineering leadership.

TL;DR

A security posture review is a quarterly structured assessment that aggregates the outputs of all continuous security practices (vulnerability management, secret hygiene, compliance posture, penetration test findings) into a unified view of the organisation's security risk. It produces three outputs: a security posture score (a weighted composite of open CVE count by age and severity, compliance drift rate, unrotated secret count, and attack surface exposure); a top-10 risk list with named owners and remediation deadlines; and a trend line (improving, stable, or degrading) that is reported to engineering leadership. Unlike an annual audit, the quarterly posture review is a management tool for tracking remediation velocity, not a compliance certification. It closes the loop between the continuous signals produced by automated tooling and the strategic decisions made by engineering leadership about where to invest in security improvement.

What it is

A security posture review is a quarterly structured assessment that synthesises the outputs of all continuous security practices into a unified view of organisational security risk. It answers the question: "Are we getting more or less secure over time?" — with data, not intuition.

Five inputs are aggregated into the posture assessment:

  • Vulnerability backlog age: The number of open CVEs per severity level and their age relative to SLA. The metric is not "do we have open CVEs?" (all organisations do) but "are we closing CVEs faster than new ones arrive, and are we within SLA?" A stable or shrinking backlog at or under SLA is the target.
  • Secret hygiene score: Per-service score combining: no unrotated secrets past policy age, no secrets with overly broad access scopes, no secrets accessed from unexpected services. Sourced from the secret management platform's audit logs.
  • Compliance drift rate: Number of new compliance findings per week from CSPM, divided by the number of findings closed. Drift rate > 1.0 means the backlog is growing. Drift rate < 1.0 means the backlog is shrinking. Target: below 0.5 (closing twice as fast as finding).
  • Penetration test findings: Outstanding findings from the most recent internal or external penetration test, by severity. Critical and High pen test findings have the same SLA as vulnerability management: 24 hours and 7 days respectively.
  • Attack surface changes: New public-facing endpoints, new third-party integrations, new data classifications. Any significant attack surface expansion in the quarter is noted and its security review status is confirmed.

Why we need it

Continuous security tooling produces signals — CVE findings, compliance drift alerts, secret hygiene warnings — but without a synthesis layer, these signals don't aggregate into a decision-making view for leadership. Engineering leaders who have to make investment decisions ("do we hire a security engineer? do we buy a DAST tool?") need a trend signal, not a ticket count. The posture review converts the raw signal volume into a directional trend that supports strategic decisions.

The quarterly cadence is also a forcing function. Security debt, like technical debt, accumulates gradually and is never the most urgent thing. A quarterly review where the posture score is presented to engineering leadership creates the accountability that makes security debt visible and comparable to feature work. Teams whose posture score degrades quarter-over-quarter have to explain the trend — which is a stronger forcing function than any automated alert.

How others do it

Riot Games

Riot Games developed a "Security Champions" programme where each engineering team has a designated security champion — an engineer who participates in quarterly security posture reviews and is responsible for the team's security backlog. The champion is not a security expert; they are a developer who receives security training and acts as the liaison between the security team and the engineering team. The posture review is conducted team-by-team, not organisation-wide — each team owns its own posture score and review cadence. This distributed model scales security review to hundreds of engineers without requiring a large security team.

What we take: Assign per-team security champions who own the team's posture score. The security team sets the framework and runs the tooling; the champion owns the remediation velocity. Security is not a centralised function — it's a distributed responsibility with centralised coordination.

Source: Riot Games Technology Blog

Salesforce

Salesforce's Trust organisation (their SRE/security function) publishes a public "Trust Score" for their platform at trust.salesforce.com — an aggregated metric combining uptime, security posture, and compliance status. Internally, each product team has a corresponding internal posture score that is reviewed in the quarterly business review alongside feature delivery metrics. Security posture is a first-class business metric, not a separate security function metric. Teams whose posture score is below threshold are not permitted to add new publicly-facing features until it improves — security debt gates feature delivery.

What we take: Security posture as a gate for feature delivery is the correct model for organisations where security debt competes with feature debt for attention. When a degrading posture score blocks a product release, it becomes visible to product management — which creates accountability that internal security reporting cannot.

Source: Salesforce Trust Portal

Anatomy

Posture score calculation

Weighted composite (suggested weights): CVE SLA compliance 35%, secret hygiene 25%, compliance posture 25%, pen test finding age 15%. Each component is scored 0–100 based on its metric (e.g., CVE SLA compliance = % of open CVEs within SLA). The composite score is calculated per service and aggregated to an org-wide score. Weights are reviewed annually — they reflect current priority.

Penetration testing cadence

Annual external penetration test for the full platform. Quarterly internal red team exercise for Tier 0 services. Penetration test findings are tracked in the same vulnerability management system as CVEs — same SLAs, same SLA tracking. The penetration test report is not a compliance artefact; it's an operational risk signal that feeds the posture review.

Attack surface inventory

Maintained as a versioned document in the engineering wiki: all public-facing endpoints (URLs, IP ranges, API gateways), all third-party integrations with data access, and data classification (PII, financial, sensitive internal). Reviewed quarterly in the posture review. Any change to the attack surface added during the quarter requires confirmation that a security review was completed before the change went live.

Leadership review format

30-minute quarterly presentation to engineering leadership: posture score trend (4 quarters), top-5 risks with owner and deadline, one slide on "what improved this quarter" and one on "what degraded." The format is fixed — no long presentations. The goal is a decision about investment priorities, not a comprehensive security briefing. Leadership output: approved investment items (headcount, tooling, training budget) and accepted risk items.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Posture review treated as a compliance exercise"We pass the audit — posture must be fine."Compliance and security posture are different. Compliance is binary (in/out of a defined requirement); posture is a direction (improving/degrading). A fully compliant organisation can have a degrading security posture if new attack surfaces are being added faster than controls are being applied. Measure both separately.
Top-10 risk list with no owners"Security is everyone's problem."Every item on the risk list has one named owner — the engineering lead of the team responsible for the service or practice. "Everyone's problem" is no one's problem. The owner is accountable for the remediation deadline, not for fixing it personally — they assign it to the right engineer within their team.
Security debt accepted without re-evaluation dates"We know about this risk — it's been in the report for two years."Any risk accepted in the posture review has an explicit re-evaluation date, no more than 6 months out. At the next review, the accepted risk is reassessed: has the context changed? Has the risk grown? Risks that stay in "accepted" status for more than 12 months require VP-level sign-off to remain accepted.
