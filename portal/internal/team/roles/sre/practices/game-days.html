Game Days & DR — practise failure before failure practices you

Scheduled, controlled failure injection and disaster recovery exercises that validate runbooks, on-call procedures, and system assumptions — before a real incident reveals that the DR plan has never actually worked.

TL;DR

A game day is a scheduled exercise where the team intentionally injects a failure into a service (or simulates one via tabletop), executes the runbook, measures the response, and produces a written report of gaps found. Distinct from chaos engineering (which is automated and continuous), game days are human-run, scenario-based exercises that validate people and process as much as they validate systems. DR (disaster recovery) exercises specifically test the recovery procedure for catastrophic failures: regional outage, data corruption, key compromise. Target cadence: quarterly game days for Tier 0/1 services; annual DR test for all services. Every game day produces at least one runbook update or system improvement. A game day with no findings means the scenario was not challenging enough.

What it is

A game day is a scheduled, controlled exercise where the engineering team intentionally injects a failure scenario — or simulates one via tabletop — and executes the response procedure as if it were a real incident. Unlike automated chaos engineering (which runs continuously against production or staging), game days are human-run, scenario-based exercises designed to test the people and process as much as the system.

Three formats:

  • Tabletop exercise: A facilitator describes a scenario ("the primary database has failed over to the replica and the replica is degraded"). Team members narrate what they would do, what they would check, who they would call. The facilitator injects complications. No systems are touched — the exercise is purely about decision-making and process. Lowest blast radius, highest process coverage. Good for validating incident command and communication procedures.
  • Staged injection: A failure is injected into a staging environment (or a pre-production canary) and the team executes the full response procedure using real tools. The team doesn't know the injection scenario in advance — they respond as if it were production. Tests runbooks, tooling, and team coordination. Moderate blast radius, high technical coverage.
  • DR exercise: The team validates the disaster recovery procedure for a catastrophic failure: regional failover, backup restoration from a specific date, key rotation under incident conditions. These are production-impacting by definition — they are scheduled during low-traffic windows with customer communication. High blast radius; validates that the DR plan actually works.

Why we need it

DR plans that are never tested are fiction. Every untested assumption in a DR plan is a potential 2-hour extension of a real disaster. Common discoveries in first DR tests: the backup restore procedure takes 4 hours, not 45 minutes as documented; the credentials for the backup system expired 6 months ago; the failover procedure assumes a service that was deprecated; the on-call who wrote the runbook left the company. Game days find these gaps when the stakes are controlled, not during a real P0.

Game days also build muscle memory. Teams who have executed a rollback under game day conditions execute it faster and with less decision overhead during real incidents. The cognitive cost of "what am I supposed to do here?" is eliminated when the procedure has been practiced.

How others do it

Amazon Web Services

Amazon's internal game day practice, which seeded AWS's "operational excellence" principle, uses a "wheel of misfortune" for tabletop exercises — a random scenario generator that produces failure combinations the team has not explicitly planned for. The randomisation prevents the "we've already trained for this" bias that makes planned scenarios less valuable. Their game day rule: the facilitator is an SRE who does not belong to the team — external facilitation prevents the team from unconsciously steering toward scenarios they know they can handle.

What we take: External facilitation. A game day run by the team that designed the system is a demonstration, not a test. The facilitator should be someone who doesn't know the system well enough to steer toward safe scenarios.

Source: Amazon Builders' Library — Fire Drills

Slack

Slack runs "Failure Friday" — a weekly scheduled session where a random team runs a 30-minute tabletop game day, facilitated by an SRE. The scenarios are not random — they are drawn from a backlog of "what-if" questions submitted by engineers. The backlog drives the scenario selection. After each session, the findings are published in #game-days and the runbook updates are merged within 48 hours. The weekly cadence means every team participates 2–3 times per year without the overhead of scheduling a special event.

What we take: Make game days low-overhead and high-frequency rather than high-overhead and quarterly. A 30-minute tabletop run weekly beats a half-day exercise run once a year in terms of total coverage and team participation.

Anatomy

Scenario design

Scenarios are drawn from three sources: past incidents (replay and test the improved procedure), near-misses (events that didn't cause an outage but revealed a weakness), and hypothetical worst-cases based on the service's dependency graph. Each scenario has a stated hypothesis ("we believe our on-call can detect and remediate a database primary failure within 15 minutes") and a falsifying condition ("detection took more than 5 minutes" or "the runbook step 3 failed").

Blast radius control

Staged injection scenarios must have an explicit blast radius scope: which services are affected, which environments, and the abort procedure that stops the injection immediately if something unexpected happens. No game day injection should risk customer data or production SLO for more than 5 minutes. For Tier 0 services, injections require engineering lead sign-off and are never run during peak traffic hours.

Findings documentation

Every game day produces a written report within 48 hours: the scenario, the hypothesis, the findings (what worked, what didn't), and the resulting action items (runbook updates, system improvements, monitoring gaps). Published to the same channel as postmortems. The report is the artifact — a game day with no report doesn't count.

DR exercise schedule

Annual DR test for every service: restore from the most recent backup, validate the restore, run the failover procedure. Tier 0 services: bi-annual DR test. DR test results are submitted to the engineering lead as part of the annual reliability review. Services that have not run a DR test in 18 months are flagged for compliance review.

How it works in this project

Study App runs one game-day per quarter against the production-shaped staging environment. Scenarios escalate across the year: Q1 a single-AZ failure, Q2 a region failover, Q3 a dependency outage (auth provider, CDN, payment provider going dark), Q4 a data-corruption tabletop where the team must decide which backup generation to restore and which downstream consumers to notify. Each session has two named non-engineering roles: a facilitator from outside the owning team (drives scenario, holds the abort handle) and an observer who is silent for the live run and produces the written findings. Results feed two artifacts within 48 hours: a PR against the service's runbook in internal/sre/runbooks/ with the gaps closed, and a card on the SRE roadmap for any system change the scenario revealed. Game-day debriefs also drive the next quarter's incident-command training — whichever role struggled (comms, decision-making, coordination) becomes the focus of the next on-call shadowing rotation.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Scenario design — realistic, scoped, recoverable

A useful game-day scenario sits in a narrow band. Too easy (a known failure the runbook already covers) and it produces no findings — a "passing grade" that means the team learned nothing. Too hard (a multi-failure cascade with no working tooling) and the team disengages because nothing they do has any effect on the outcome. The sweet spot is a scenario that is realistic (drawn from the actual dependency graph or past near-misses), scoped (one root cause, predictable blast radius, an abort handle the facilitator can pull inside 60 seconds), and recoverable (a defined end-state the team can actually reach). The hypothesis statement — "we believe X can be detected and remediated in N minutes" — is what turns the exercise from theatre into an experiment.

The "no-warning" vs "scheduled" debate

Two schools exist. Scheduled game days announce scenario, time, and participants in advance — the team can prepare, brush up on the runbook, even read the dependency map the night before. No-warning game days drop the scenario on whoever is on-call at the time, no preparation, no special context. Scheduled exercises measure what the team's best response looks like; no-warning exercises measure what their real response looks like, which is the one that will happen at 3 a.m. The honest answer is to run both: scheduled quarterly to validate the procedure and onboard new engineers, no-warning twice a year to test that the procedure has actually been internalised. Skipping the no-warning variant is the most common reason game days fail to predict real-incident performance.

Observer notes vs metrics

A game day produces two streams of evidence: metrics (time-to-detect, time-to-mitigate, page count, runbook step durations) and observer notes (who said what, where the team paused, which decision got skipped, where confusion arose). Metrics are cheap to record and easy to chart over time; observer notes are expensive to capture but reveal the failure modes metrics cannot see — tribal knowledge, miscommunication, the on-call quietly fixing something out-of-band. Teams that lean only on metrics end up optimising the measurable and missing the meaningful. The discipline is: metrics for trend, notes for diagnosis. Both go in the written report.

Practising communication, not just engineering

The most expensive minutes of a real incident are rarely the technical ones — they are the minutes spent figuring out who is in charge, who is communicating with stakeholders, when to escalate, and what to tell customers. Game days that focus exclusively on the technical fix (which command to run, which dashboard to open) miss the larger failure mode: a team that knows the fix but cannot coordinate around executing it. A well-designed game day forces incident-command roles to be assigned explicitly, requires a written customer-comms draft before the exercise ends, and includes a stakeholder ping that has to be answered while the technical work is in progress. This is what makes the practice transfer to real incidents.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Engineering time. A quarterly game day costs roughly a half-day per participating engineer plus a day of facilitator and observer prep. For a team of six that is ~25 person-hours per quarter — real budget, not a free win.
  • Staging fidelity. Game days are only as useful as the environment they run in. A staging cluster that diverges from production by more than a few weeks of drift will produce findings that do not translate. Keeping staging production-shaped is a hidden cost of taking game days seriously.
  • Cultural risk. A team that treats game days as "the SRE team checks our work" disengages quickly. The practice only lands when game days are framed as the team's own experiment — the engineers own the hypothesis, the gaps, and the follow-up tickets, not the facilitator.

Lifecycle

scenario drafted → reviewed → pre-brief → live run → observation → debrief → action items

A game day starts two weeks before the run date as a draft scenario — one paragraph stating the failure, the hypothesis, and the falsifying condition. The owning team and the facilitator review it the following week: is the blast radius safe, is the abort handle real, is the scenario actually answerable within the time box? The pre-brief on the morning of the run names the roles (incident commander, on-call, comms lead, observer, facilitator) and walks through the abort procedure — what stops the exercise, who can call it, how the team gets back to a known-good state. The live run is time-boxed; the facilitator injects, the team responds, the observer takes notes silently. Observation continues through to recovery so the team's clean-up procedure gets practised too. The debrief follows immediately while the experience is fresh: what worked, what didn't, what surprised us. Action items land as runbook PRs and roadmap cards within 48 hours; an item without an owner and a date does not count.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Game day with no findings treated as success "The system handled the scenario perfectly." A game day with no findings means the scenario was not challenging enough. Increase scenario difficulty until findings appear. The goal is not to pass — it is to discover gaps before production does. A "passed" game day is a wasted exercise unless the team has evidence that they tested their actual failure modes.
DR plan validated by documentation review, not execution "We reviewed the DR plan last year and it looked correct." Execute the DR plan annually. Reading a plan and executing it are different activities. Restore from backup. Failover the primary. Measure the actual RTO. Every gap that appears during execution is a gap that would have appeared during a real disaster, at 3× the pressure.
Game days run only by senior engineers "The junior engineers won't know what to do." Junior engineers are the highest-value participants in a game day — they reveal the tribal knowledge that is not written down. Include them, observe where they get stuck, and update the runbooks to eliminate the need for prior knowledge. A runbook that requires a senior engineer to execute is not a runbook.

Worked example

Game-day plan

GD-2026-Q2 — Lessons API region failover, scheduled staged injection

id:              GD-2026-Q2
date:            2026-06-18  (Thu)  10:00–12:00 CEST
environment:     staging-prod-shaped  (NOT prod)
owning_team:     sre-platform
facilitator:     external — @marina (data-platform)
observer:        @ivan (silent during live run)

scenario:
  Primary region eu-west-1 becomes unavailable for the Lessons API.
  The replica region eu-central-1 must take traffic within RTO 15 min,
  with data loss bounded by RPO 5 min from the last replicated write.

hypothesis:
  We believe the on-call rotation can detect the region outage within
  3 minutes (page on SLO burn), execute the regional failover runbook
  within 12 minutes, and confirm green health checks in eu-central-1
  inside the 15-minute RTO budget.

falsifying conditions:
  - TTD > 5 min  (alerting is mis-tuned or routes wrongly)
  - TTR > 20 min (runbook is wrong, missing step, or stale credential)
  - any step requires unwritten tribal knowledge to complete

success criteria:
  - TTD ≤ 3 min  ·  TTR ≤ 15 min  ·  data loss ≤ RPO 5 min
  - customer-comms draft ready inside the time box
  - at least one runbook gap identified and ticketed

fault-injection commands:
  # block the eu-west-1 control plane from the lessons-api cluster
  kubectl --context staging-eu-west-1 \
    apply -f scenarios/gd-2026-q2/netpol-blackhole.yaml
  # simulate the regional load-balancer health-check failure
  aws --profile staging route53 change-resource-record-sets \
    --hosted-zone-id ZSTGEXAMPLE \
    --change-batch file://scenarios/gd-2026-q2/r53-fail-eu-west.json

abort procedure:
  facilitator runs:  scripts/gd-abort.sh GD-2026-Q2
  (removes the NetworkPolicy, restores Route53, posts in #game-days)

roles:
  IC:          on-call SRE  (live, takes coordination)
  on-call:     whoever is paged by Alertmanager
  comms lead:  rotating engineer, drafts status-page entry
  observer:    @ivan, silent, captures notes in /docs/gd/2026-q2.md
  facilitator: @marina, drives clock, holds abort handle

timeline:
  10:00  pre-brief, roles, abort walkthrough
  10:15  injection T0
  10:15–11:15  live run + recovery
  11:15  exercise close, environment restored
  11:20–12:00  debrief (hot wash) — what worked / didn't / surprised us

debrief agenda:
  1. metrics readout (TTD, TTR, data-loss bound, page count)
  2. observer notes — where did the team pause, which decisions skipped
  3. runbook gaps — concrete edits, owners, dates
  4. system gaps — tickets on the SRE roadmap, owners, dates
  5. comms review — would the customer-comms draft have shipped?
  6. next quarter — what scenario does this exercise suggest?

What this plan does. It frames the exercise as an experiment with a stated hypothesis, falsifying conditions, and measurable success criteria. The abort procedure is one command, owned by the facilitator, not the team — so the live run can be stopped fast if anything escapes the blast radius. The debrief agenda is fixed so that the conversation produces concrete runbook edits and roadmap tickets, not generalities. The plan itself becomes an artifact in scenarios/, re-runnable a year later to measure improvement.

Quality bar

A checklist of what a healthy implementation has. ✓ done ◐ partial ○ todo

  • ○

    Quarterly game day on the calendar

    One scheduled game day per quarter for Tier 0/1 services; dates published a quarter ahead, not improvised.

  • ○

    External facilitator

    Facilitator is not from the owning team. The team being tested cannot also drive the test.

  • ○

    Hypothesis + falsifying condition stated up front

    Every scenario has a written hypothesis and a clear condition under which the exercise is judged to have failed.

  • ○

    Abort procedure rehearsed

    One command, one named owner, walked through in the pre-brief. The facilitator can stop the exercise in under 60 seconds.

  • ○

    Written report inside 48 hours

    Scenario, hypothesis, findings, action items — published in the same channel as postmortems. No report, no game day.

  • ○

    At least one runbook update per exercise

    If no gap is found, the scenario was too easy. Difficulty ratchets up until findings appear.

  • ○

    Annual DR test executed, not reviewed

    Every service restores from backup and exercises its failover path at least once per year, measured against documented RTO/RPO.
