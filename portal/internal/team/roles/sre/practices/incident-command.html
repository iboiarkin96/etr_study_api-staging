Incident Command Structure — one person coordinates, one person diagnoses

A defined command structure for P0/P1 incidents — Incident Commander who coordinates and communicates, Tech Lead who diagnoses, Communications Lead who writes customer updates — so engineers aren't pulled in two directions at once.

TL;DR

The Incident Command System defines three roles for every P0/P1 incident: the Incident Commander (IC) who owns the incident timeline, coordinates responders, and makes escalation decisions; the Tech Lead who owns the technical investigation and remediation; and the Communications Lead who writes all external and internal status updates. Roles are assigned within 5 minutes of incident declaration. The IC and Tech Lead never swap tasks during an incident — their jobs are deliberately separated to prevent the most senior engineer from getting pulled into both diagnosis and communication simultaneously.

What it is

The Incident Command System (ICS) is a management framework developed for emergency services and adapted for software incident response. For P0/P1 incidents, three roles are assigned within 5 minutes of declaration:

  • Incident Commander (IC): Owns the incident timeline, coordinates all responders, makes escalation decisions, and drives the incident to resolution. The IC never touches the production system — they have no hands on keyboard. Their job is to ensure the right people are working on the right things and that everyone is informed.
  • Tech Lead: Owns the technical investigation. Diagnoses the root cause, coordinates engineers working on the remediation, and reports status updates to the IC. The Tech Lead is heads-down in the system and communicates outward only to the IC.
  • Communications Lead: Writes all external and internal status updates, manages the #incidents Slack channel, and shields the IC and Tech Lead from the flood of status questions that comes from stakeholders during a high-profile incident. Sends 30-minute status updates without being asked.

For P0 incidents, all three roles are required. For P1 incidents, the IC and Tech Lead are required; the Communications Lead may be optional if the incident is lower-visibility.

Why we need it

In the absence of a command structure, P0 incidents turn into chaotic Slack threads where 10 engineers are debugging simultaneously, nobody knows what's been tried, changes are made to production without coordination, and the most senior engineer ends up doing diagnosis, writing customer updates, answering manager questions, and coordinating remediation all at once. This is the single fastest path to a longer incident and a burnout event.

The command structure prevents this by giving everyone a single, clear job during the incident. The IC's job is coordination, not diagnosis. The Tech Lead's job is diagnosis, not communication. The Comms Lead's job is communication, not coordination. No one is pulled in two directions at once.

How others do it

Short tour of teams whose incident-command practice is published. Each card is one organisation, what they do, and what we take from it.

PagerDuty

PagerDuty's ICS implementation adds a fourth role for large incidents: the "Subject Matter Expert (SME)" — a domain specialist called in by the IC when the Tech Lead needs specific expertise (e.g., a database engineer for a DB incident, a security engineer for a potential breach). SMEs are not on-call — they're called specifically for the incident. The IC manages the SME engagement: when to bring them in, what question to answer, and when they can leave the bridge.

What we take: Train the IC role separately from the on-call rotation. IC skills (coordination, communication, escalation judgment) are different from diagnostic skills. Not every great engineer is a great IC. Run IC training as tabletop exercises — role-play an incident with rotating IC assignments.

Source: PagerDuty IC Training

Atlassian

Atlassian's incident command practice includes a "scribe" role (equivalent to the Comms Lead) whose specific job is to maintain a real-time incident timeline in the #incidents channel. The scribe records every action taken, every hypothesis tested, every decision made, with timestamps. This timeline becomes the primary input for the postmortem and eliminates the "what did we try and when?" confusion that typically delays postmortem completion.

What we take: The real-time timeline is more valuable than post-hoc reconstruction. A 30-second log entry during the incident — "14:32: hypothesis A ruled out, trying B" — produces a postmortem-ready timeline automatically. Post-hoc reconstruction from memory takes 2 hours and is always incomplete.

Source: Atlassian — Incident Commander

Anatomy

The parts a complete incident-command practice has, with one sentence on each. None are optional for P0/P1.

Role assignment within 5 minutes

The PagerDuty escalation policy automatically assembles the IC and Tech Lead for P0/P1. The IC then designates the Comms Lead from available engineers. If 5 minutes pass without role assignment, any on-call engineer can self-assign the IC role — never leave an incident without a coordinator.

IC tabletop training

Quarterly tabletop exercise: a facilitator presents a fake incident scenario, engineers rotate the IC role, and the group evaluates coordination decisions. 45 minutes, no production systems. The exercise builds IC judgment without the pressure of a real incident.

Incident bridge

A dedicated Zoom / conference bridge for P0/P1, opened by the IC within 2 minutes of incident declaration. All responders join. The bridge is moderated — the IC controls who speaks and when. Parallel Slack threads are allowed for debugging; the bridge is for coordination decisions.

IC coverage in postmortem

Every P0/P1 postmortem includes an evaluation of the incident command: was an IC assigned within 5 minutes? Did roles stay separated? Were escalation decisions made on time? This closes the learning loop on the process, not just the technical failure.

How it works in this project

Study App uses ICS-derived roles for every SEV0/SEV1: Incident Commander, comms lead, ops lead (the on-the-keyboard engineer, equivalent to the Tech Lead above), and scribe. The IC role is rotated through a list of trained engineers — the on-call rotation includes an explicit IC duty alongside the diagnostic on-call, so the engineer who fields the page is not also the engineer expected to coordinate it. Anyone in the trained pool can assume IC at T+0; the first responder declares the incident and either takes IC themselves or hands it to the next trained engineer on the bridge.

The communications side runs on templates rather than improvisation. Comms templates live in internal/sre/comms-templates/ — one per severity, with placeholders for impact, current hypothesis, ETA, and next-update time. The Comms Lead fills the template and posts on a 15-minute cadence for SEV0/SEV1 without waiting to be asked. The scribe maintains a timestamped log in the incident channel; that log becomes the primary timeline input for the postmortem, with no post-hoc reconstruction needed.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Why a system designed for wildfires runs software incidents

The Incident Command System was born in 1970s California firefighting. Multiple agencies — federal, state, county, city — would arrive at the same wildfire with their own radios, their own ranks, and their own ideas about who was in charge. The result was the FIRESCOPE programme's now-famous finding: more responders did not produce more capacity, because nobody knew who decided what. ICS was the answer: one Incident Commander, a fixed role hierarchy, a single channel of authority, and a vocabulary every agency learned the same way. FEMA's ICS-100 course is the modern descendant, taught to every emergency responder in the United States.

Software incidents inherit the same failure mode. Ten capable engineers on a Slack thread without a coordinator produce less remediation than three engineers with a clear IC. The mechanism is identical: capacity is wasted on coordination work that nobody owns — who is doing what, what has been tried, who decides to roll back. ICS imports the answer wholesale.

Why one decider matters more than ten experts

During an incident the bottleneck is rarely "we don't know what's wrong" — it is "we don't know who decides what to try next." Ten engineers each proposing a hypothesis produces ten parallel investigations and zero committed actions. A single IC asking "Tech Lead, what do you want to try next?" produces one committed action and nine engineers freed to either help with that action or wait. The IC's authority does not come from being the most senior — it comes from being the only person whose job is to decide. Senior engineers are most useful with their hands on the system, not refereeing the bridge.

Span of control — three to seven reports

ICS specifies that any one commander should have no more than three to seven direct reports — a finding from organisational psychology that long predates firefighting. Beyond seven, the commander cannot track state across reports and starts dropping context. In a software incident this means an IC with twelve engineers on the bridge must group them: an ops lead with the engineers actually touching the system, a comms lead with the people writing updates, a scribe maintaining the log. The IC then has three reports — ops, comms, scribe — not twelve. Without that grouping, the IC degrades into a high-latency Slack repeater and the incident slows down.

Communications cadence — 15 minutes for SEV0/SEV1

The cadence is not arbitrary. At 15 minutes, the Comms Lead has enough new information to be worth reading and not so much that stakeholders start poking the bridge directly to find out what is happening. Anything longer and managers start interrupting the IC for status; anything shorter and the Comms Lead is writing more than the ops lead is fixing. The template format matters as much as the frequency — impact statement, current hypothesis, ETA if known, time of next update, in that order — so that a reader scanning the channel for two seconds gets the headline before deciding whether to read further.

IC versus subject-matter expert — a structural separation

The most common failure of incident command is the IC who is also the smartest engineer in the room. They cannot resist diving into the diagnosis, they lose track of the timeline, and within twenty minutes the bridge has no coordinator. The structural fix is to designate the IC as someone who is explicitly not the deepest expert on the affected system. A less-senior IC asking "Tech Lead, what is your next step?" outperforms a senior engineer trying to do both jobs. SMEs are called by the IC, report to the IC, and leave the bridge when the IC releases them — never the other way around.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Training overhead. A trained IC pool requires quarterly tabletop exercises, written role guides, and a willingness to put junior engineers in the IC seat under simulated pressure. Skipping this produces an IC roster that exists on paper only.
  • Tolerance for less-senior coordination. Senior engineers must accept being directed by less-senior ICs during incidents. The cultural shift is real — without it, the senior engineer silently overrides every IC decision and the structure collapses.
  • Template discipline. The Comms Lead has to use the template even when the situation feels too urgent for templates. Without that discipline, every incident generates a freshly-improvised update format and stakeholders cannot scan-read across incidents.

Lifecycle

incident declared → IC named → roles filled → status updates running → mitigations applied → recovery confirmed → handoff to postmortem

The incident is declared when the page fires and the responder confirms a real impact, not before. The IC is named at T+0 — either the first responder takes the role or hands it to the next trained engineer on the bridge. By T+5 the remaining roles are filled: comms lead, ops lead, scribe. From there the status cadence runs every 15 minutes for SEV0/SEV1 — comms posts an update on the timer whether or not new information has arrived. The ops lead works through hypotheses and applies mitigations; the IC keeps the timeline moving and decides when to escalate or call SMEs. Recovery is confirmed against an explicit criterion (error rate back below SLO, customer-visible symptom cleared) and only then does the IC stand the incident down. The bridge does not close — it hands off to postmortem, where the scribe's timeline becomes the first input.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Most senior engineer takes both IC and Tech Lead They're the most capable person in the room and default to all roles. Separate the roles explicitly at incident declaration. If the most senior engineer is the Tech Lead, someone else must be the IC — even if they're less senior. A less-experienced IC is better than no IC.
IC mode starts only after 30 minutes of chaos "We thought it would resolve quickly." ICS activates at incident declaration, not after diagnosis fails. If it's a P0, the command structure engages immediately — it disengages when the incident is resolved, never before it starts.
No postmortem evaluation of the command process Postmortems focus on the technical failure only. Include an explicit section in every P0/P1 postmortem: "How did incident command work?" Was there an IC? Were roles separated? Was escalation timely? The process improves only if it's reviewed.
IC keeps the keyboard The IC sees a fix they could apply themselves and reaches for the terminal. The IC has no hands on keyboard, by rule. If the IC starts typing, the timeline goes untracked and the next decision arrives late. Hand the keyboard work to the ops lead and resume coordination.
Status updates drift past the 15-minute mark The Comms Lead is waiting for "real" news before posting. Post on the cadence regardless of new information. "Still investigating, hypothesis A ruled out, next update at HH:MM" is a valid update — stakeholders stop poking the bridge once the timer is reliable.
SMEs are pulled in without an IC briefing An engineer on the bridge directly @-mentions a domain expert. SMEs are called by the IC with a specific question and released by the IC when the question is answered. Drive-by SMEs join, sit confused, and either over-contribute or quietly leave.

Worked example

IC playbook

SEV1 — Lessons API 5xx spike — IC timeline through resolution

T+00:00  PagerDuty pages on-call SRE. Responder confirms 5xx spike on lessons-api.
         Declares SEV1 in #incidents. Says: "I am IC. Bridge open: zoom/sre-bridge."

T+02:00  IC opens the bridge. Posts pinned message:
           Incident: SEV1 · lessons-api 5xx
           IC: @ivan · Ops: tbd · Comms: tbd · Scribe: tbd
           Next update: T+15

T+05:00  IC assigns roles from engineers on bridge:
           Ops lead:   @alex (lessons-api owner, hands on keyboard)
           Comms lead: @sam  (uses sev1-update.md template)
           Scribe:     @lee  (timestamped log in #incidents)
         IC says to Ops: "What is your first hypothesis?"
         Ops: "Recent deploy at 14:02. Rolling back as primary action."

T+10:00  Ops begins rollback. IC pings #leadership with one-liner:
         "SEV1 on lessons-api, IC assigned, rollback in progress, ETA 10 min."

T+15:00  Comms posts first scheduled update (template-driven):
           Impact:     ~12% of lesson loads failing
           Hypothesis: regression from 14:02 deploy
           Action:     rollback in progress
           Next:       T+30

T+22:00  Rollback completes. Ops reports: "Error rate dropping. Watching for 5 min."
         IC does not declare resolution yet — waits for the SLO signal.

T+27:00  Error rate confirmed back under SLO threshold for 5 consecutive minutes.
         IC asks Ops: "Confident we are clear?" Ops: "Yes."
         IC declares recovery confirmed.

T+30:00  Comms posts resolution update. Scribe seals the timeline.
         IC stands incident down, schedules postmortem for next business day,
         hands the scribe log to the postmortem owner.

What this playbook shows. The IC never touches the system — at T+05 they ask the ops lead for the first hypothesis rather than proposing one, and at T+27 they ask the ops lead to confirm recovery rather than judging it themselves. The Comms Lead posts on the cadence even when "rollback in progress, no news" is the entire content, because a reliable cadence stops stakeholders from interrupting the bridge. The scribe's timeline is complete enough to drop straight into the postmortem template without reconstruction.
