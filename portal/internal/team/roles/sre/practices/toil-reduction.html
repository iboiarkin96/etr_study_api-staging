Toil reduction — automate the repetitive or pay for it in burnout

Toil is manual, repetitive operational work that scales with load and leaves nothing behind. Google's SRE model caps it at 50% of working time — the other half eliminates it through engineering. The cap is a forcing function, not a guideline.

TL;DR

Toil is work that is manual, repetitive, automatable, tactical, devoid of enduring value, and scales linearly with load. The 50% rule caps it at half of an SRE's time; the other half buys it down through engineering. Toil is tracked explicitly — every on-call ticket and recurring task is classified at close, the top sources are reviewed monthly, and each gets a named reduction project with a 90-day deadline. Unmeasured toil is the primary driver of SRE burnout and the reason on-call rotations collapse.

What it is

Toil reduction is the discipline of treating operational drudgery as a product backlog — measured, prioritised, and engineered down rather than absorbed quietly by whoever is on call this week. Google's SRE book defines toil by six properties: it is manual (a human does it by hand), repetitive (it has happened before and will happen again), automatable (a machine could do it given engineering investment), tactical (reactive, not a project the team chose), devoid of enduring value (closing the ticket leaves the system no better than it was), and it scales linearly with load (double the services and toil doubles too).

The discipline ships with a hard number. No SRE should spend more than 50% of their time on toil; the remaining 50% is engineering time spent buying it down. The cap is enforced by measurement — toil is logged at ticket-close time, totalled monthly, and the top sources are turned into named reduction projects with owners and deadlines. Classic toil examples: manually restarting a service after every deploy, rotating credentials on a calendar, clicking through dashboards to confirm a release is healthy, triaging the same recurring alert for the twentieth time.

Why we need it

Unmeasured toil is the most reliable destroyer of on-call rotations in the industry. Because toil scales with load, a team that does not actively engineer it down will spend an ever-growing fraction of its hours on the same predictable manual chores — and the hours are taken from the engineering work that would have eliminated them. The collapse is not dramatic; it is gradual, and by the time it is visible the team has already lost its best engineers to less repetitive jobs.

The cap is also a quality argument, not only a humane one. When engineers spend most of their week on routine manual tasks, they lose the situational awareness that makes them useful in real incidents — everything starts to feel like the same noise. Eliminating toil restores attention to the non-routine work that needs it. The 50% rule is the cheapest reliability lever available because it requires no new infrastructure: it only requires honest measurement and the courage to defend the engineering half of the week.

How others do it

Short tour of teams whose toil-reduction practice is public. Each card is one organisation, what they do, and what we take from it.

Google SRE

Google introduced the toil concept and the 50% rule in the original SRE book (2016). Implementation: every SRE classifies their work weekly between toil and engineering; managers review the ratio monthly. Teams above 50% must submit a written toil-reduction plan and stop accepting new services to operate until the ratio drops back below the ceiling. The measurement is enforced, not aspirational.

What we take: Toil tracking must produce a number, not a feeling. Classify every on-call ticket at close time and aggregate monthly — without the number the 50% rule is rhetoric.

Source: Google SRE Book — Eliminating Toil

Stripe

Stripe's infrastructure team runs quarterly "toil sprints" — one week per quarter spent exclusively on the top 3 toil sources from the previous quarter's data. During toil week no feature work is accepted. Engineers who deliver the largest reduction by hours-per-week saved are publicly named at the quarterly all-hands.

What we take: Dedicated capacity beats "we'll get to it" every time. Time is never found voluntarily — it must be protected on the calendar. A quarterly cadence also creates the measurement-to-improvement rhythm that compounds over years.

Source: Stripe Engineering Blog

GitLab

GitLab's public handbook treats toil reduction as an explicit OKR for the infrastructure organisation. Each toil source above one engineer-day per week gets a tracking issue with an owner, a target reduction, and a deadline. The OKR is reported in the company-wide quarterly review, which means stalled reduction work is visible to leadership rather than buried in a backlog.

What we take: Make toil reduction reportable. A reduction project that lives only in a private backlog will quietly be dropped; one that is on a public OKR keeps moving because its absence is noticed.

Source: GitLab Infrastructure Handbook

Charity Majors (Honeycomb)

Charity Majors' essays on operational maturity reframe toil as a tax on engineering velocity: every hour of toil this quarter is an hour not spent on the systems that would have removed it. Her recommendation is to make the tax visible — publish toil-as-percentage on the same dashboard as deploy frequency and incident rate so it is read together with the other engineering health metrics.

What we take: Toil is not a separate concern from velocity — it is the inverse of velocity. Show the two side by side and the trade-off becomes obvious to anyone reading the dashboard.

Source: charity.wtf — operational maturity essays

Across every public toil-reduction practice the playbook converges: explicit per-ticket classification, a hard percentage cap, protected reduction capacity on the calendar, and the toil number reported alongside the other engineering health metrics rather than tucked into a private wiki.

Anatomy

The parts a complete toil-reduction practice has, with one sentence on each. None are optional.

Classification at ticket close

Every on-call ticket and recurring chore is tagged toil or non-toil when it is resolved — one Jira field, 5 seconds per ticket. After 30 days you have a month of data; after 90 days you have a trend.

Weekly time tracker

A lightweight per-SRE log of hours spent on toil that week. Self-reported, but reconciled against the ticket data — if the log says 4 hours and the tickets total 12, the log is the one that gets corrected.

Top-3 monthly review

Top 3 toil sources by hours consumed. Each gets a named reduction project with a 90-day deadline and a single owner. Not a discussion — a decision list with names and dates.

25% elimination budget

Roughly half of the engineering-half of the week — about 25% of SRE time overall — is reserved for toil-reduction work. The budget is on the sprint plan, not added if hours appear.

Toil percentage metric

Total toil hours ÷ total on-call hours per month. Tracked on the engineering health dashboard alongside deploy frequency and MTTR; alerts when above 50% for two consecutive months.

Automation candidates feed the platform backlog

A toil source that survives one reduction project becomes a platform-team candidate — recurring drudgery shared across services is solved once at the platform layer, not service by service.

Post-automation tracking

Hours saved are measured for one month after a reduction project ships. Projects that did not actually reduce toil are reopened; "automated" is not the success criterion, "hours-per-week reduced" is.

How it works in this project

Study App will run its toil log inside Jira. Any ticket touching ops — incident response, recurring chore, on-call follow-up — is tagged toil at close time, with a free-text source description and a manual-hours estimate rounded to the nearest 0.25 hour. Alongside the ticket tag, each SRE keeps a weekly time tracker: a Friday-afternoon entry in a shared sheet recording hours spent on toil that week. The two data sources reconcile — the sheet catches recurring work that does not generate tickets (manual deploy babysitting, dashboard checks, the standing meeting that is really triage), and the Jira label catches what the sheet forgets.

The SRE budget allocates 25% of each engineer's week to toil-elimination work — that is, half of the engineering half of the 50/50 split. The remaining engineering hours go to roadmap projects. Once a month the team picks the top 3 toil sources by hours consumed; each becomes a named reduction project with a single owner and a 90-day deadline. Toil sources that survive one reduction cycle — meaning the same chore keeps appearing across multiple services — are promoted onto the platform-team backlog so they are solved once at the platform layer. The toil percentage and the top-3 sources are reported on the engineering health dashboard alongside deploy frequency and MTTR, reviewed in the SRE retro on the first Monday of each month.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Google's toil definition — why six properties, not one

The six-property definition exists because each property alone is too broad. "Manual" includes valuable human judgement work; "repetitive" includes well-designed regular meetings; "automatable" includes work that should not be automated for safety reasons. Toil is the intersection of all six — the work that is manual and repetitive and automatable and tactical and devoid of enduring value and scales linearly with load. Trim any property and the category leaks; keep all six and the boundary is sharp enough to classify a ticket in five seconds at close time.

The "scales linearly with load" property is the one most often missed. A one-time architectural migration is manual, repetitive within itself, and tactical — but it does not recur when the next service launches, so it is project work, not toil. A weekly credential rotation that gets longer every time a service is added is toil, because the cost grows with the size of the estate. The linear-scaling test is the one that separates legitimate ops work from compound interest on a debt.

Measuring toil — sampling vs full tracking

Two measurement modes work. Full tracking tags every ticket at close, which is cheap (5 seconds × N tickets) but misses ticketless work like manual deploy supervision and standing triage meetings. Sampling uses a structured one-day-per-quarter time-and-motion study where each engineer logs every task at 15-minute granularity — expensive but it catches the ticketless drag. Mature teams run both: continuous full tracking for trend lines, sampling quarterly to recalibrate the trend against ground truth. The number that goes on the dashboard is the reconciled one, not whichever was easier to collect.

The 50% cap — why that number

The 50% number is not arbitrary. It is the equilibrium at which incoming toil load — growing linearly with system size — is matched by outgoing engineering elimination. Below 50% the team is paying down debt faster than it accrues; above 50% the debt is growing and the team is on a path to collapse. The math is a simple flow equation: if engineering time per quarter eliminates X hours of recurring weekly toil, and growth adds Y hours per quarter from new services, the ratio holds steady when X ≥ Y. The 50% split is the empirical point at which X = Y for most teams. Allow 60% toil for a quarter and the next quarter starts behind.

Automation ROI — one-shot vs amortised

An automation project costs roughly the same as one cycle of the toil it eliminates — say 40 engineer-hours to build and harden a one-click runbook that replaces 2 hours/week of manual work. The break-even is 20 weeks; everything after that is pure margin. The mistake teams make is comparing the 40-hour cost against the next cycle (2 hours) and concluding it is not worth it. The correct comparison is against the integral of toil over the remaining lifetime of the system — often years. Sort the toil sources by hours per week × expected remaining lifetime and the top three are obvious; sort by "engineering interest" and you automate the most fun one instead of the most expensive one.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Up-front measurement overhead. Tagging tickets and filling in the weekly tracker feels like bureaucracy for the first month. After the second month the data exists, the top sources are visible, and the overhead is the lowest-cost piece of the system. Skipping the measurement is what makes the practice fail, not adopting it.
  • Capacity guarded against feature work. Reserving 25% of SRE time for elimination work means feature roadmaps move 25% slower than they would in a do-everything-now culture. The bet is that the saved hours compound and the roadmap moves faster by quarter four. Teams that refuse the bet hit the on-call collapse instead.
  • Cultural reversal. Engineers default to absorbing chores quietly because complaining feels unprofessional. The forcing function is the metric on the dashboard — once toil percentage is reported alongside MTTR and deploy frequency, absorbing chores quietly stops being a virtue and becomes a measurement gap.

Lifecycle

toil identified → categorised → ROI estimated → on the backlog → automation built → hours saved measured → retired or re-scoped

A toil item starts as a single tagged ticket — one engineer notices the same chore for the third time and applies the toil label. Once the monthly aggregate identifies it as a top-3 source, it is categorised (manual deploy chore, recurring alert triage, credential rotation, etc.) so similar items can be solved together. The ROI estimate is the simple ratio of hours-per-week × expected lifetime against the engineering cost to eliminate; only items with positive ROI inside a year reach the backlog. The reduction project is owned by one named engineer with a 90-day deadline. After the automation ships, hours saved are measured for one full month — if the saving is real, the item is retired; if the chore migrated rather than disappeared, the project is re-scoped and re-opened. Retired items are removed from the toil log, not merely closed.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Toil estimated from memory, not measured "We all know it is bad" feels true enough that nobody runs the numbers. Classify every on-call ticket at close — one Jira field, 5 seconds. After 30 days the data exists; after 90 days the trend exists. Without the number, the 50% rule is rhetoric and the conversation goes in circles every retro.
Reduction projects never started Feature work always wins in sprint planning; toil work feels optional. Reserve 25% of SRE time on the sprint plan for elimination work — protected capacity, not "if hours appear". Time is never found voluntarily; it has to be defended on the calendar before the sprint starts.
Automating the most interesting toil first Engineers gravitate to the technically novel problem, not the most frequent one. Sort by hours-per-week × expected lifetime and pick the top three. The most painful chore is almost never the most interesting one — the boring credential rotation usually beats the cool eventing pipeline on raw hours saved.
"Automated" treated as success The reduction project ships, the team celebrates, and nobody checks whether toil actually dropped. Measure hours saved for one full month after each automation lands. Projects that did not move the metric are reopened, not closed; "we shipped it" is not the success criterion, "hours-per-week reduced" is.
Toil counted only where there are tickets Manual deploy supervision, dashboard checks, and standing triage meetings do not generate tickets, so they are invisible to a ticket-only tracker. Run the weekly time tracker alongside the ticket label. Reconcile the two monthly — if the sheet says 4 hours and the tickets say 12, the sheet is the wrong number, not the ticket data. Run a quarterly time-and-motion sample to recalibrate.
Service-by-service automation of shared toil Each service team automates its own version of the same chore — six similar runbooks instead of one platform feature. Promote toil that recurs across services to the platform backlog. Solve it once at the platform layer; the per-service version is the wrong shape because the chore was never service-specific to begin with.

Worked example

Toil log entry

TOIL-0042 — Lessons API deploy verification — manual dashboard sweep

id:                 TOIL-0042
date_logged:        2026-05-12
source_ticket:      JIRA SRE-318 (closed, recurring at every deploy)
description:        |
  After each lessons-api deploy the on-call engineer clicks through six
  Grafana panels (5xx rate, p95 latency, db-pool saturation, queue depth,
  cache hit-ratio, login error) and confirms each is within band.
  Repeats per deploy. 12-18 deploys/week.
manual_hours_per_week:   2.5      # 8 minutes × 15 deploys (median)
expected_lifetime:       18 mo    # service is roadmap-stable
classification:          toil (all 6 properties)
proposed_automation:     post-deploy health-gate — Argo Rollouts analysis
                         step querying the six PromQL panels, abort + rollback
                         on out-of-band reading, post result to #deploys.
engineering_cost:        ~40 h    (1 sprint, 1 engineer)
roi_break_even:          16 weeks (40 / 2.5)
roi_at_18_mo:            195 h saved net of build cost
owner:                   @ivan
deadline:                2026-08-12  (90 days)
ship_date:               2026-07-29
measured_after_30d:      2.3 h/week saved (verified against Jira labels)
status:                  retired 2026-08-30

What this entry does. It turns a vague "we keep clicking through dashboards" complaint into a tracked engineering item with an owner, a deadline, and a measured outcome. Sorted into the monthly top-3 by hours-per-week, it beat two more interesting candidates because the math was unambiguous: 16-week payback, 195 hours saved across the service's roadmap lifetime, one engineer-sprint to build. The 30-day post-ship measurement confirmed the saving was real — 2.3 h/week against the 2.5 h estimate — so the item retired cleanly rather than reappearing in the next quarter's review.
