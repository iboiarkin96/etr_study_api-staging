Capacity planning — forecast load, validate with tests, hold headroom

Project demand six to twelve months ahead, prove the headroom claim with a real load test, and wire scaling rules that turn the projection into action. The wall stops being a surprise.

TL;DR

Capacity planning is a quarterly cycle: forecast demand from history plus the product calendar, load-test the forecast against a production-like environment, compute headroom against the SLO budget, and wire autoscaling or schedule a manual provisioning step. Five resource dimensions are tracked — compute, memory, DB connections, storage, third-party quota — because only one of them autoscales. The target is <20% probability of a budget breach over the next quarter at the modelled peak; below that, the plan ships. Above that, the architecture changes before the traffic arrives.

What it is

Capacity planning is the discipline of stating, with numbers and a date, how much load the system can carry before the SLO breaks — and then doing the architectural and provisioning work to push that point past the next planning horizon. It is not the same as autoscaling. Autoscaling reacts to load that has already arrived; capacity planning is what happens months before, so that the autoscaler has a pool to scale into, the database has enough connections to hand out, and the third-party API quota has been raised in time for the launch.

A complete capacity plan covers five resource dimensions — compute, memory, DB connections, storage, third-party quota — and produces three artefacts per quarter: a forecast (what demand we expect, expressed as peak RPS or daily active sessions), a load-test report (what the system actually does at that forecast), and a decision set (auto-scale where it works, schedule manual changes where it does not, raise the budget where the architecture needs a real lift). Anything less than that triple is a wish, not a plan.

Why we need it

"We'll add servers when we need them" is true for stateless CPU and false for almost everything else. DB connection pools have a hard ceiling that takes an architectural change to lift. Third-party quotas (Stripe, Twilio, Sendgrid) need a vendor negotiation with weeks of lead time. Reserved-capacity discounts have to be committed up front or the next quarter's bill arrives at on-demand pricing. The teams that hit these walls at 2 a.m. during a marketing campaign are the ones that modelled compute and forgot the other four dimensions.

The other half of the value is calibration. A team that has never load-tested its forecast does not know whether the forecast is right; it has a number on a slide that will be falsified by reality the first time peak traffic actually arrives. A 30-minute test against staging with a realistic open-model load generator is the cheapest possible falsification — far cheaper than the post-mortem of the outage that the un-validated forecast would have produced.

How others do it

Short tour of teams whose capacity practice is published and copied. Each card is one organisation, what they do, and what we take from it.

Google SRE

The SRE book dedicates a chapter to capacity planning. Every service carries a published demand forecast based on six months of historical traffic and a matching expansion plan; both are reviewed quarterly. A 20% overshoot of the forecast triggers an unplanned review rather than a quiet adjustment to the spreadsheet. Google also runs annual zone-failure simulations to validate that capacity commitments still hold from N−1 zones, not just from the full fleet.

What we take: Plan for the failure case, not only the growth case. "Handles 2× normal traffic" is a meaningful claim only if it survives the loss of an availability zone at the same time.

Source: Google SRE Book — Software Engineering in SRE (ch. 18)

Netflix

Netflix's Adaptive Concurrency Limits library treats every service's capacity as a runtime measurement, not a static config value. The library samples latency under load and lowers the in-flight concurrency limit when latency starts to climb — exposing the "knee" of the p99-vs-throughput curve as a live signal. The capacity number on the dashboard is the one the system just demonstrated, not the one an engineer typed in a year ago.

What we take: Capacity is a property the system reveals under load, not a constant. Anywhere a hand-tuned limit hides the real ceiling, replace it with a measurement.

Source: Netflix Tech Blog — Performance Under Load (Adaptive Concurrency)

AWS Well-Architected

The Reliability pillar prescribes a four-step capacity workflow: monitor saturation against an explicit headroom target, forecast using historical demand plus business-event multipliers, run scheduled load tests against a production-like environment, and use the results to drive both automatic scaling rules and reserved-capacity purchases. The framework refuses to treat any of those steps as optional.

What we take: Pair every forecast with a load test and every load test with a written scaling decision. Skipping either step turns the practice into theatre.

Source: AWS Well-Architected — Reliability Pillar

Brendan Gregg / Netflix performance

The USE method (Utilisation, Saturation, Errors) gives capacity planning a structured way to find the resource that will saturate first. For every resource the system uses — CPU, memory, NIC, disk, DB connection pool — check utilisation, queue length, and error rate. The resource with the steepest saturation curve under projected load is the next bottleneck, regardless of which resource the dashboard was previously watching.

What we take: The bottleneck moves. A capacity plan that only watches CPU misses the moment the DB connection pool became the new ceiling.

Source: Brendan Gregg — The USE Method

Across every published practice the pattern converges: forecast from history, validate with a load test, measure saturation across all resources (not just CPU), and turn the result into either an autoscaling rule or a scheduled architectural change.

Anatomy

The parts a complete capacity practice has, with one sentence on each. None are optional.

Five resource dimensions

Compute, memory, DB connections, storage, third-party quota. Each carries a current headroom number (consumption ÷ limit); anything under 40% triggers a review before the next quarter.

Demand forecast

Linear regression on at least six months of traffic, multiplied by the planned-launch calendar (new course pack, marketing burst, school-year start). Expressed as peak RPS and as daily active sessions, with a confidence band.

Load test

k6 or Locust open-model run against production-like staging, executing the forecast peak for at least 30 minutes. Any limit hit becomes an architectural ticket, not an alert tweak.

Headroom calculation

Compute the p99-latency-vs-saturation curve and find the "knee". Headroom is the gap between projected peak and the knee, expressed as a percentage of the SLO budget; the target is ≥30%.

Scaling plan

For each dimension: auto-scale (HPA rule, cluster-autoscaler bound, RDS read-replica policy) where elasticity exists, or a scheduled manual change with an owner and a date where it does not.

Quarterly review meeting

30-minute meeting with the infra lead, the product lead, and the on-call lead. Three outputs: reserved-capacity purchases, architectural change items for the next quarter, hard-limit updates. Action items leave the room with named owners.

Capacity matrix tied to SLO

A single table that maps each service's peak demand to the SLO it must hold under that peak. If the forecast says the SLO will breach, the plan is to change the architecture, not to lower the SLO.

How it works in this project

Study App will run capacity planning on a quarterly cadence anchored to the academic calendar — the load shape around term start and exam season dominates the year. Load tests live at infra/load/ with two complementary tools: k6 scripts for the open-model HTTP scenarios (lesson load, quiz submit, sign-in burst) and Locust scenarios for the longer stateful flows (course enrolment, payment retry). Both run against the production-like staging cluster and replay anonymised traffic shapes captured from the previous quarter's peak hours.

The capacity matrix is a single page under infra/capacity/matrix.yaml that maps each service to its peak demand, its current headroom across the five resource dimensions, and the SLO it must hold — see SLO & error budget for how the targets are defined. Compute scales through Kubernetes HPA backed by cluster-autoscaler for the node pool; the database scales horizontally via RDS read replicas with a connection-pool ceiling enforced in PgBouncer. Third-party quota (Stripe, Sendgrid) is tracked manually in the matrix because it does not autoscale — vendor lead time is recorded next to the limit. The quarterly review is the first Wednesday of each quarter; it produces a written decision set that lands in the engineering backlog with owners and dates.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Demand forecasting — the math behind the spreadsheet

The simplest useful forecast is a linear regression on the last six months of daily peak traffic, projected six to twelve months forward. It works because most product traffic, away from launch events, grows close to linearly on a quarterly timescale. The regression gives a point estimate and a confidence band; the band matters as much as the point because capacity planning is a tail problem, not a mean problem.

The linear baseline gets adjusted by two corrections. Seasonality is the first: Study App's traffic has a weekly cycle (Mondays high, Saturdays low) and an academic cycle (September spike, July trough). A seasonal-naïve decomposition — same-week-last-year multiplier on top of the linear trend — captures most of it without the machinery of a full SARIMA model. The second correction is the product calendar: each planned launch (new course pack, partner integration) gets an explicit multiplier from the product team, sized against the closest historical analogue.

Little's law — the bridge between RPS and concurrency

Little's law states that the number of in-flight requests equals the arrival rate times the average response time: L = λ × W. It is the single most useful equation in capacity planning because it converts the metric the product team understands (peak RPS) into the metric the system actually limits on (concurrency, connections, threads). A service serving 500 RPS at 200 ms p99 carries 100 in-flight requests; if the connection pool tops out at 80, the service is already over its limit even though CPU is fine.

Headroom math — the knee of the p99 curve

Latency under load is not linear. For most services, p99 latency stays flat until utilisation reaches roughly 70–80%, then climbs sharply — the "knee" of the curve. The headroom that matters is the distance between projected peak and the knee, not the distance to 100% utilisation. A service running at 60% CPU with a knee at 70% has only 10 points of headroom; a service at 60% with a knee at 90% has 30. The load test is what locates the knee — guessing it from CPU charts alone is reliably wrong by 10–20 percentage points.

Horizontal vs vertical scaling

Horizontal scaling (add more replicas) is the default because it composes with autoscaling, fault tolerance, and rolling deploys. Vertical scaling (bigger boxes) is the right answer when the bottleneck is per-replica state — a database that cannot be sharded without an application rewrite, a cache whose hit rate collapses below a working-set size, a JVM heap that needs the address space. The two are not interchangeable: a horizontally-scaled service can absorb a 10× spike in minutes; a vertically-scaled one needs a maintenance window. The capacity plan picks the axis per service and writes down why.

Load-test design — open vs closed model

A closed-model load test fixes the number of virtual users; each user waits for a response before sending the next request, so the test rate falls when latency rises. A closed model hides queueing — the system looks fine because the generator self-throttles. An open-model test injects requests at a fixed rate regardless of response time, the way real users do; queueing shows up immediately and the test exposes the knee. Production-realistic capacity tests are open-model; k6's constant-arrival-rate and Locust's constant_throughput primitives are the right choice. Closed-model tests have their uses (longevity, soak) but they are not capacity tests.

Trade-offs — over-provisioning vs surprise outage

  • Cost of over-provisioning. Every point of headroom costs money — replicas running at 30% utilisation, reserved capacity that goes unused, read replicas whose query load could be served by the primary. The bill is real and shows up monthly.
  • Cost of under-provisioning. A surprise outage during a marketing campaign costs revenue, trust, and a post-mortem week. The cost is non-linear: the second outage in a quarter is far more expensive than the first because the trust loss compounds.
  • Where the line sits. The pragmatic target is ≥30% headroom against the modelled peak and a load test no older than one quarter. Below 30%, the next traffic spike eats the budget; above 60%, the cloud bill is paying for nothing. The number is not a constant — it moves with the business cost of an outage.

Lifecycle

baseline measured → demand forecast drafted → load test executed → headroom validated → autoscale wired → manual changes scheduled → reviewed quarterly

The cycle starts with a measured baseline — six months of traffic and saturation data, not a guess. The product team then supplies the launch calendar, the SRE owner drafts the forecast, and the load test validates it against staging at the projected peak. If the headroom calculation comes in below target, the architectural change is logged before the autoscaling is touched — autoscaling a system that has hit its knee only buys minutes. Once the architecture is sound, the autoscaler is wired (HPA bounds, cluster-autoscaler quotas, RDS replica rules) and the manual changes (third-party quota raises, reserved-capacity purchases) are scheduled with named owners. The next quarterly review either confirms the plan held or triggers an unplanned cycle.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Planning only for compute Autoscaling makes compute feel like a solved problem; the other dimensions are quieter and easier to forget. Model all five dimensions — compute, memory, DB connections, storage, third-party quota. The dimensions that do not autoscale are exactly the ones that cause the outage.
No load test — the model is assumed correct Load tests take effort and the spreadsheet "looks right". Run at least one open-model load test per quarter against the service closest to its capacity limit. Models drift; load tests are the only honest falsification.
Closed-model load test hides queueing The default load-test tool template uses a fixed VU count; it self-throttles when the system slows down. Use k6 constant-arrival-rate or Locust constant_throughput. Inject requests at a fixed rate regardless of latency; queueing then shows up as real queueing, not as artificially smooth p99 charts.
Capacity review produces no decisions The review becomes a status report meeting instead of a decision meeting. Every quarterly review ends with at least one concrete output: a purchase order, an architectural ticket with an owner, or an explicit "fine until next quarter" sign-off. No artefact, no value.
Headroom measured against 100% utilisation, not the knee CPU dashboards top out at 100%; the knee of the latency curve is invisible without a load test. Measure headroom as the gap between projected peak and the knee of the p99-vs-saturation curve. The knee sits at 70–80% utilisation for most services — the last 20% is not usable capacity.
Third-party quotas treated as elastic The cloud bill autoscales, so the team assumes everything else does too. Track Stripe, Sendgrid, Twilio, and other vendor limits in the capacity matrix with their lead times. A quota raise that needs two weeks of vendor negotiation is the launch blocker; surface it at the quarterly review, not on launch day.

Worked example

Capacity worksheet

Lessons API — Q3 forecast, p99 ≤ 250 ms, 99.9% / 28 d SLO

service:            lessons-api
slo:                availability 99.9% / 28 d   latency p99 ≤ 250 ms
forecast_window:    2026-07-01 → 2026-09-30  (academic year start)

demand:
  baseline_peak_rps:        420
  trend_multiplier:         1.18        # linear regression, 6 mo
  seasonal_multiplier:      1.35        # September peak vs Aug baseline
  launch_multiplier:        1.10        # new course pack on 2026-09-05
  projected_peak_rps:       420 × 1.18 × 1.35 × 1.10 ≈ 736 RPS

littles_law:
  p99_latency_target_s:     0.250
  in_flight_at_peak:        736 × 0.250 ≈ 184 concurrent requests

capacity:
  current_replicas:         8           # 4 vCPU each
  measured_knee_per_pod:    ~120 RPS    # from k6 open-model run, 2026-05-20
  replicas_needed_at_peak:  ceil(736 / 120) = 7   → keep 8 for headroom
  headroom_pct:             (8×120 − 736) / (8×120) ≈ 23%   ← below 30% target

db_connections:
  pgbouncer_pool_size:      200
  in_flight_at_peak:        184       → 92% utilisation       ← raise pool to 300

third_party:
  stripe_rps_quota:         300       → forecast 280           ← request raise to 500 (10 d lead)
  sendgrid_send_rate:       1500/min  → forecast 1100/min      ← within limit

scaling_plan:
  hpa:                      min=8 max=14   cpu=60%   custom=p99_latency
  cluster_autoscaler:       node_pool_max  raised to 24 vCPU bound
  manual_changes:           pgbouncer pool 200 → 300 (PR-4821, owner @ivan, due 2026-06-15)
                            stripe quota raise (ticket OPS-118, owner @anna, due 2026-06-20)

review_by:                  2026-09-01     # one month before peak window

What this worksheet does. It turns a product calendar entry ("course pack ships in September") into a concrete plan: 736 RPS is the projected peak, 184 concurrent requests is what Little's law says will be in flight at that peak, and the capacity check shows compute is fine but the DB connection pool is the first hard wall to hit. The plan ships two artefacts — a PR raising the pool ceiling and a vendor ticket to raise the Stripe quota — both with owners and dates that land before the load actually arrives. The autoscaler rules are written down so the on-call knows what the system will do during the peak; nothing is left to "we'll figure it out at the time".
