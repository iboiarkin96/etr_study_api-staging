Incident Severity Framework — the response must be proportional to the impact

A shared classification system that maps customer and business impact to severity levels, driving proportional response: who is notified, how fast, through which channels, and what escalation path applies.

TL;DR

An incident severity framework maps 4 severity levels (SEV0–SEV3) to explicit qualifying criteria (customer impact, scope, revenue effect) and per-severity response SLAs: acknowledgement time, notification targets, communication frequency, and postmortem requirement. Without a shared framework, every engineer classifies incidents differently — SEV0-level impact sometimes gets a Slack message; minor internal degradation sometimes triggers 3 am pages. Shared criteria make the response proportional, predictable, and analysable across incidents over time.

What it is

An incident severity framework is a classification system that maps incidents to severity levels based on objective criteria. Each level specifies:

  • Qualifying criteria: The specific conditions that put an incident at this severity — customer scope (all users / 20% of users / internal only), business impact (complete service outage / major feature degraded / minor degradation), and revenue effect.
  • Response SLA: Maximum time to acknowledge the page at this severity level.
  • Notification targets: Who gets notified — on-call only, team lead, VP, CEO.
  • Communication frequency: How often status updates go out during the incident — every 15 minutes for SEV0, every 30 minutes for SEV1.
  • Postmortem requirement: SEV0 and SEV1 always require a postmortem. SEV2 and SEV3 are optional.

Why we need it

Without a shared framework, severity classification is personal. The engineer who classifies every problem as SEV0 generates executive panic about minor issues; the engineer who never classifies anything above SEV2 lets serious customer-facing issues go unescalated for hours. Both patterns are harmful — and both occur in organisations without explicit criteria.

Shared criteria also enable post-incident analysis. "We had 12 SEV1 incidents this quarter, 8 from the same root cause" is a meaningful pattern that drives engineering investment. Without consistent classification, the data is too noisy to aggregate.

How others do it

Short tour of teams whose severity practice is published and copied. Each card is one organisation, what they do, and what we take from it.

PagerDuty

PagerDuty's incident response guide defines severity levels with explicit customer impact language: SEV-1 = "critical issue affecting many customers"; SEV-2 = "major functionality broken for subset of customers"; SEV-3 = "minor issue with workaround"; SEV-4 = "cosmetic, no user impact." Their calibration exercise: quarterly meeting where engineers independently classify 10 historical incidents and compare results. Disagreements reveal ambiguities in the criteria that need clarification.

What we take: Run calibration exercises quarterly — not just write the criteria and assume everyone interprets them the same way. Calibration is how you discover that "significant revenue loss" means different things to different people, and why the SEV0 criterion needs a specific dollar threshold.

Source: PagerDuty Severity Levels

Atlassian

Atlassian uses a "customer impact" first approach to severity classification — the primary criterion is always "how many customers are affected and how severely?" Internal engineering impact (a degraded build pipeline, a slow internal tool) can only reach SEV2 at most, regardless of engineering discomfort. The rule prevents the "this is a SEV1 because it's embarrassing for us" classification that inflates severity for incidents with no customer impact.

What we take: Customer impact is the primary axis. A Tier 0 engineering system that's slightly slow but has no customer-visible effect is not a SEV0. Severity frameworks that allow internal engineering embarrassment to drive high severity classifications lose credibility with customers and executives quickly.

Source: Atlassian — Incident Severity Levels

Google SRE

Chapter 14 of the SRE book ("Managing Incidents") pairs severity with an incident command structure: high-severity incidents activate a named Incident Commander, an Ops lead, and a Comms lead from the moment severity is declared. Lower-severity incidents skip the formal roles. The point is not the label — it is that the label triggers a process that is otherwise too expensive to spin up on every page.

What we take: Severity is not a feeling, it is a switch. Declaring SEV0 or SEV1 activates incident command; declaring SEV2 does not. Tying process activation to the label removes the argument over "is this serious enough to call the IC?" — the severity already answered it.

Source: Google SRE Book — Managing Incidents

FireHydrant

FireHydrant's published guidance treats severity as a declared field, not a derived one: the on-call engineer states a severity within five minutes of opening the incident based on the information available, and the platform records that declaration time separately from the resolution data. A later, calmer "what was it really" assessment becomes a second field — the post-incident severity — but it never overwrites the original declaration.

What we take: Keep two severity fields, not one. The declared severity captures the decision the on-call made with partial information; the post-incident severity captures what we know in hindsight. Overwriting the first with the second destroys the data we need to evaluate escalation quality.

Source: FireHydrant — Incident Severity Levels Guide

Across teams whose severity practice is public, the same shape recurs: explicit qualifying criteria with examples, customer impact as the primary axis, quarterly calibration to keep interpretation aligned, severity as the trigger for process (incident command, comms cadence), and a clean separation between declared and post-incident severity so the historical record remains honest.

Anatomy

The parts a complete severity framework has, with one sentence on each. None are optional.

Customer impact criteria

Primary classification axis. Three dimensions: scope (% of users affected), depth (complete failure vs. degraded vs. minor), and duration (how long has it been occurring). All three must be assessable within 5 minutes of the incident start without additional investigation.

Response SLA per severity

SEV0: 5-minute ACK; SEV1: 30-minute ACK; SEV2: next business day; SEV3: sprint backlog. SLAs are tracked and reported monthly — an unacknowledged SEV0 that breaches the 5-minute SLA is itself an incident to investigate.

Escalation matrix per severity

SEV0: on-call + engineering lead + VP + CEO briefed within 15 minutes. SEV1: on-call + engineering lead. SEV2: on-call only. The escalation path is automatic based on severity — the on-call engineer doesn't decide whether to escalate to the VP; the severity level decides it.

Calibration exercises

Quarterly 30-minute meeting: present 5–10 historical incidents, have all engineers classify them independently, compare and discuss disagreements. Disagreements identify ambiguous criteria that need sharper language.

Declared vs post-incident severity

Two separate fields. The declared severity records the decision made with partial information at minute five; the post-incident severity records the hindsight assessment. Neither overwrites the other — both are kept for escalation-quality review.

Comms cadence by severity

SEV0: status update every 15 minutes in the exec channel and on the status page. SEV1: every 30 minutes. SEV2: an opening note and a resolution note. SEV3: ticket-only. Cadence is part of the severity contract, not a per-incident judgement call.

Severity distribution dashboard

Monthly chart of SEV0/1/2/3 counts and proportions. Drift in the proportions (e.g. SEV1 share rising from 15% to 40%) signals calibration drift before any one incident does.

How it works in this project

Study App uses a four-tier severity matrix defined in internal/sre/severity.html. The mapping is deliberately blunt so the on-call can declare a level inside five minutes with no further investigation: SEV0 = total outage (the product is unusable for substantially all users); SEV1 = partial degradation on a Tier 0 service (lessons-api, auth, payments) — a major flow is broken for a measurable share of users; SEV2 = single-service degradation on a non-T0 service or a workaround exists for the affected flow; SEV3 = cosmetic or internal-only impact with no customer-visible effect.

The declared severity drives two downstream contracts. The comms cadence — 15 minutes for SEV0, 30 minutes for SEV1, opening/closing notes only for SEV2, ticket-only for SEV3 — is enforced by the incident bot, not by memory. Incident command activates automatically on SEV0 and SEV1: a named Incident Commander, Ops lead, and Comms lead are paged from the on-call rotation. SEV2 stays with the responding engineer; SEV3 is a ticket. The post-incident severity is set during the postmortem in a separate field and never overwrites the declared value — that separation is what lets us measure escalation quality month over month rather than telling ourselves a tidy story in hindsight.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Severity is a routing key, not a judgement of seriousness

Every team that struggles with severity classification has confused two distinct questions. The first is "how bad does this feel?" — a question with no operational consequence. The second is "what response template should run?" — a question whose answer must be unambiguous within five minutes. The framework only addresses the second. Once the team accepts that, classification becomes mechanical: match impact to a row in the matrix, declare the level, run the template. The arguments about whether a particular outage was "really" a SEV1 or a SEV2 are arguments about feelings, not arguments about process.

Severity vs priority — the confusion that breaks the matrix

Severity describes impact on the user; priority describes the order in which we work on things. They correlate, but they are not the same axis. A SEV3 cosmetic issue affecting the homepage logo of a marketing campaign launching tomorrow might still be P0-priority for the marketing team. Conversely, a SEV1 incident that has already self-resolved is no longer P0-priority — it is a postmortem, not a fight. Teams that collapse the two axes end up with severity inflation (everything that's urgent becomes SEV1) and lose the response-template signal that severity is supposed to carry.

The impact dimensions — what the matrix actually measures

Four dimensions go into the impact assessment. Users affected: the share of active users encountering the failure, not the share of accounts that exist in the database. Revenue per minute: the per-minute opportunity cost while the incident continues, including refund liability and SLA credits. Data integrity: whether the failure can lose, corrupt, or expose data — this is the dimension that turns an otherwise SEV2 outage into a SEV0, because lost data does not come back when the service does. Regulatory: whether the incident triggers a notification obligation (GDPR, PCI, SOC2) — an obligation is operational, not theoretical, and it has its own clock.

Declaring early vs declaring accurately

The on-call has to choose between two failure modes. Declare too early on partial information and risk over-paging the org; declare too late and risk a SEV0 that ran as a SEV2 for forty minutes before someone realised. The published guidance from every mature team is the same: declare at the higher level when in doubt, downgrade later if the data supports it. The reason is asymmetric cost — a false SEV0 wastes thirty minutes of three executives' time; a missed SEV0 is the headline outage your customers tweet about. The asymmetry tilts every borderline call upward.

Downgrade and upgrade rules during the incident

Severity can change mid-incident, but only under explicit rules. Upgrade happens when new evidence shows the impact is broader or deeper than the initial assessment (a partial outage turning out to affect all paid tiers, not 20% of free users). Downgrade happens when the initial assessment proves wrong on the same evidence the on-call had at minute five — not when the incident is winding down. Downgrading just because mitigation is working confuses the historical record; that's what the post-incident severity field is for. Both moves are announced explicitly in the incident channel and logged with the time and reason.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Process overhead on the high tiers. Activating incident command for every SEV1 costs an Ops lead and a Comms lead from the on-call roster. Teams that resent the overhead start "informally" classifying borderline SEV1s as SEV2 to skip it — and lose the whole point of the framework.
  • Calibration is a permanent overhead. Quarterly calibration takes an hour of every engineer's time. Teams that skip it discover six months later that "20% of users affected" means something different to each person; recovering shared interpretation then costs more than the calibration would have.
  • Two-field bookkeeping. Maintaining declared and post-incident severity separately is more work than a single editable field. The reward is honest data on escalation quality; the cost is the platform integration to expose two fields and reports that distinguish them.

Lifecycle

detection → preliminary severity → declared → comms started → escalated / downgraded → resolved → postmortem severity locked

An incident starts with a detection event — a page, a ticket, or a user report. The on-call's first task is a preliminary severity, a working hypothesis based on whatever signal triggered the detection. Inside five minutes the severity is declared: the level is entered in the incident record and the matching response template fires. Comms start at the cadence the declared level requires; the incident bot tracks the clock. During the incident the level may be escalated on new evidence of broader impact, or downgraded if the initial assessment overstated the case — both moves are logged with reason and timestamp. After resolved, the postmortem opens a second severity field — the post-incident severity — capturing the hindsight assessment. That value is locked at postmortem sign-off; the declared severity is never edited. The two fields together feed the monthly escalation-quality report.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Severity inflation ("everything is a SEV1") Engineers prefer high severity to justify urgency. Track severity distribution over time. If 60% of incidents are SEV1, the criteria are wrong. A healthy distribution is roughly 5% SEV0, 15% SEV1, 50% SEV2, 30% SEV3. Deviation from this suggests calibration drift.
Severity downgraded retroactively "It turned out to be minor, so we'll call it SEV3." Severity is classified based on the information available at declaration time. Never reclassify the declared field after resolution — record the hindsight call in the separate post-incident severity field. Overwriting distorts the historical data and erodes engineer accountability for escalation decisions.
Framework exists, but engineers still free-classify The framework is in a wiki; it's not integrated into the incident workflow. Integrate severity classification into the incident declaration form (PagerDuty, Jira). The engineer must select a severity from a dropdown — the form shows the criteria for each level. Classification is not optional.
Severity confused with priority Both feel like "how important" axes; teams collapse them into one number. Keep severity (user impact, response template) separate from priority (work order). A SEV3 can be P0 for a launch; a self-resolved SEV1 can be P3 for the backlog. Each axis answers a different question.
Declaring late to "be sure" The on-call wants confirmation before paging executives. Declare at the higher level when in doubt; downgrade later if data supports it. A false SEV0 costs thirty minutes of exec time; a missed SEV0 is the public outage. The asymmetry tilts the call upward.
Incident command skipped on borderline SEV1 Activating IC + Ops + Comms feels expensive for "probably SEV1". The severity declaration is the trigger. If the level is SEV1, incident command activates — there is no "we'll see how it goes" branch. Teams that allow the branch lose the whole point of binding process to severity.

Worked example

Severity declaration record

INC-093 — lessons-api 5xx spike — declared SEV1 at minute 5

incident_id:        INC-093
detected_at:        2026-03-14T09:42:11Z   (PagerDuty page from SLO burn alert)
declared_at:        2026-03-14T09:47:03Z   (4m 52s after detection)

declared_severity:  SEV1
declared_by:        @ivan (on-call SRE)
declared_reason:    |
  lessons-api 5xx error rate sustained at ~60% for 15+ minutes
  across all upstream regions. Affects ~22% of active users in
  the lesson-playback flow. Tier 0 service. Workaround unavailable.
  Matches SEV1 criteria: partial degradation on T0 service.

response_template:  sev1
  - paged: on-call SRE, lessons-api owner, engineering lead
  - activated: Incident Commander (@petr), Comms lead (@maria)
  - channel:   #inc-093 (private) + #exec-incidents (digest)
  - cadence:   status update every 30 min until mitigated

escalations:
  - 2026-03-14T10:18:22Z  upgrade considered (revenue rate > threshold)
                          → held at SEV1 (mitigation in progress, scope stable)

resolved_at:        2026-03-14T10:43:51Z   (mitigation: roll back commit 8c4d2f1)

post_incident_severity:  SEV1
post_incident_locked_at: 2026-03-21T15:00:00Z   (postmortem sign-off)
postmortem_url:          ../../../../sre/postmortems/INC-093.html

What this record shows. Detection to declaration is under five minutes — that is the only timer the framework genuinely cares about at the start. The declared severity is the value the response template ran against; it is never edited. The mid-incident "upgrade considered" entry is the audit trail of an escalation decision that went the other way, with a one-line reason. The post-incident severity matches the declared severity in this case (SEV1), so calibration was clean — when the two fields diverge, the next monthly review asks why.
