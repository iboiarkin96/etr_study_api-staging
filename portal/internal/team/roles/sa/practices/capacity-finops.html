Capacity planning · FinOps

Back-of-envelope plans per service: QPS, storage growth, cost projection — refreshed every quarter.

TL;DR

Without capacity planning, infrastructure cost or scale surprises the team at the wrong moment. A back-of-envelope plan is enough to spot the curves going the wrong way long before they hit a wall — and to talk about money with finance in numbers, not feelings.

What it is

Capacity planning is a small document per service that answers: how much traffic do we expect over the next 6–12 months, and what does the infrastructure need to look like to serve it? It includes QPS, storage, memory, and the dominant cost line item.

FinOps is the wider habit of treating cloud cost as a first-class concern: budgets per service, monthly review, known optimisations, and a clear total cost of ownership figure.

Why we need it

Cloud spend creeps. A service that costs $400/month at launch can sit at $4 000/month two years later because no one was watching. The capacity plan makes the trend visible while it's still cheap to fix.

The plan also gives the team something to bring to finance and product. 'Sessions storage will reach 1 TB by Q4 unless we archive' is much easier to budget against than 'we might need more disk soon'.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Netflix

Long history of public capacity planning; built tools (Atlas, Mantis) for time-series capacity forecasting at the petabyte scale.

What we take: Capacity planning is a regular ritual, not a one-off project. Quarterly review is the minimum cadence at production scale.

Source: Netflix TechBlog — capacity

FinOps Foundation

Industry body that codified the FinOps practice: cross-functional cost management with engineering, finance, and product.

What we take: FinOps is a team sport. Engineering doesn't own cost alone; finance doesn't either. The practice is the collaboration.

Source: FinOps Foundation

AWS Well-Architected

Cost Optimization is one of the six pillars of the Well-Architected Framework; AWS publishes the questions every architect should ask.

What we take: Cost questions belong in architecture review, not just in finance review.

Source: AWS Well-Architected — Cost

Spotify

Public 'Cost-aware engineering' programme: engineers see the cost per service in real time; cost is a first-class metric next to latency and uptime.

What we take: Make cost visible to engineers and they will optimise. Hide it and they won't.

Source: Spotify Engineering — Cost-aware

The cross-cutting move: engineers see cost in the same dashboards as latency and uptime; the quarterly review pairs capacity with budget; small projections beat detailed ones nobody updates.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Service

One plan per service; lives next to the service docs.

Traffic projection

Daily QPS for the next 4 quarters; one chart per metric.

Storage projection

GB on disk + GB in cold storage; growth rate.

Cost projection

Dominant line item per quarter; total run rate.

Knobs

What the team can turn if numbers go off-plan (archive, scale, optimise).

Refresh cadence

Quarterly; ad-hoc on a >25% deviation.

How it works in this project

The plan will live at internal/catalog/<service>/capacity.html. The numbers come from the existing Grafana dashboards; the projection is a small Python script that pulls 90 days of history and extrapolates linearly (with a clearly stated 'this is naive' caveat).

The quarterly refresh updates the chart, the projection, and the cost figure. If the deviation from the previous quarter is more than 25%, an ad-hoc note is added explaining why.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Extrapolation methods — when each fits

A capacity plan needs a projection. Three common methods, with their failure modes:

MethodWhen it fitsWhen it lies
Linear Mature service with stable growth. Hides exponential growth in early product stages.
Exponential Early product or viral growth phase. Over-predicts long-term; misses the inevitable plateau.
Logistic (S-curve) Mature market with a ceiling (TAM-bound). Hardest to fit; needs a known ceiling.

The rule of honesty

State the model on the page. 'This projection is linear; if the growth turns out exponential, the 12-month number is off by ~2×.' A page that names its model preserves trust when the model is wrong; a page that hides the model loses trust permanently.

The 'knobs' section — three categories of lever

  • Reduce demand — archive old data, cache more aggressively, batch requests.
  • Increase efficiency — optimise the hot path, move to a cheaper instance type, switch storage tier.
  • Scale — add nodes, shard, move to a more powerful instance.

'Reduce demand' is almost always cheaper than 'scale'. The plan should propose them in this order.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Projection is wrong. Linear extrapolation hides exponential growth; exponential overpredicts maturity. Stating the model on the page is the only way to keep the trust when reality diverges.
  • Knobs section can hide hard decisions. 'We can scale' is comfortable; 'we must rewrite the storage layer' is not. The plan must name decisions the team would rather not face, or it becomes a comfort blanket.
  • FinOps is a meeting culture. Cost data without a forum to discuss it goes unread. Monthly engineering-plus-finance sync is the cheapest forum that actually changes behaviour.

Lifecycle

initial plan → quarterly refresh → deviation noted → mitigation in flight → retired

The plan starts as a draft for one service. Each quarter, the plan is refreshed against the latest 90 days. A deviation over 25% triggers a 'why is this off?' note. When a service is retired, its plan is moved to archive but kept for the history.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Plan is too detailed 30 pages with every cost line item; nobody updates it. One page per service. Detail beyond that goes in the dashboard, not the plan.
Linear extrapolation hides the curve Growth is actually exponential; the plan misses it. State the model. 'This is linear; if growth turns out exponential, the 12-month number is off by 2×.' Honesty signal.
No knobs section Plan shows the problem but no levers. Force a 'knobs' section: 'we can archive after 12 months', 'we can scale to N nodes', 'we can offload tags to cold storage'.
Refresh skipped The plan goes stale; the team trusts old numbers. 30-min quarterly slot blocks the service owner's calendar; 2 consecutive missed refreshes open a JIRA ticket against the service.

Worked example

Plan excerpt

Sessions service · capacity 2026 Q2

Today. 1.2 M sessions/day; peak QPS 220; 380 GB on disk; $1 400/month run rate.

Projection (linear).

  • Q3-2026: 1.5 M sessions/day; 490 GB disk; ~$1 700/month.
  • Q4-2026: 1.9 M sessions/day; 620 GB disk; ~$2 100/month.
  • Q1-2027: 2.4 M sessions/day; 790 GB disk; ~$2 600/month.

Knobs.

  • Archive sessions older than 12 months to cold storage (−45% disk, −20% monthly cost).
  • Move tags to a separate table (−15% latency on the hot query path).
  • Promote read replicas to two regions ($+250/month, halves P95 for EU readers).

Recommendation. Land the archive job in Q3; review storage line in Q4.

Outcome. The plan is reviewed at the start of each quarter alongside the risk-register session; any deviation > 25% from the projection updates risks.yaml and triggers a 30-min review with the service owner.
