Risk register — the risk log

A list of risks (technical, business, security, compliance) with severity, mitigation, owner, status, and review cadence.

TL;DR

Risks are easier to manage when they are written down. A register gives the team and the manager one place to scan for what could go wrong. Each risk has an owner, a mitigation plan, a severity, and a review date. Reviewed at the start of every quarter.

What it is

A risk register is a single table of risks the project knows about. Each row has a short title, a description, a severity (probability × impact), an owner, a mitigation plan, a current status, and a date for the next review.

The register covers four kinds of risk: technical (database growth, library churn, vendor lock-in), business (revenue concentration, key dependencies), security (known weaknesses, audit findings), and compliance (regulatory deadlines, contractual obligations).

Why we need it

Risks that live only in people's heads are easy to miss. A register makes them visible — the team can scan it in five minutes and see what they should think about this quarter. Forced ranking by severity stops the team from working on the loudest risk instead of the most damaging.

Written risks also age well. The register from a year ago tells the next team why the project picked the path it picked, and which risks are still live.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Atlassian

Public 'Risk Management' guide; treats risk registers as living tables refreshed every quarter, with named owners and concrete mitigation plans.

What we take: The register lives only if the quarterly review is on the calendar. Skipping it once kills the practice.

Source: Atlassian — Risk Management

NIST

Maintains the Risk Management Framework (SP 800-37); used as the basis for federal and many enterprise risk programmes.

What we take: The vocabulary (severity, likelihood, treatment) comes from NIST; adopt it instead of inventing your own.

Source: NIST RMF (SP 800-37)

Capital One

Public 'Tech Risk Engineering' role; treats risk as a discipline with engineering ownership, not a compliance-only function.

What we take: Engineering-owned risk is faster and more honest than compliance-owned risk. Lawyers should review, not own.

Source: Capital One Tech blog

The repeated rule: probability × impact for severity; one named owner per row; concrete mitigation with a date; quarterly review on the calendar. Without the quarterly review the register dies.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Title

Specific. 'Sessions DB growth at 12% MoM' beats 'database concerns'.

Severity

Probability × impact. A 1–5 scale on each axis; the product is the score.

Owner

One named person. Not 'the team', not 'TBD'.

Mitigation

Concrete action. 'Move tags to a separate table by Q3.' Not 'monitor closely.'

Status

open / mitigated / accepted / closed.

Next review

Specific date. 'Next quarter' is not a date.

How it works in this project

The register will live at internal/governance/risk-register/index.html. The source is YAML (risks.yaml); CI renders the HTML table. Reviewed at the start of every quarter, in the same session that walks postmortem action items.

A short ADR will lock the format and the rating scale so future owners don't have to re-invent them.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Probability × Impact — the 5×5 scoring

The dominant scoring scheme: 1–5 on probability, 1–5 on impact. The product is the severity score (1–25).

ScoreProbabilityImpact
1Very unlikely (< 5%)Negligible — no user impact
2Unlikely (5–20%)Low — minor degradation
3Possible (20–50%)Moderate — some users affected
4Likely (50–80%)High — many users affected
5Very likely (> 80%)Severe — outage or data loss

Severity bands

  • 1–6 Low: monitor; no active mitigation.
  • 7–12 Moderate: mitigation planned; owner assigned.
  • 13–19 High: mitigation in flight; owner reports monthly.
  • 20–25 Critical: escalate to leadership; mitigation is the team's top priority.

ALARP — As Low As Reasonably Practicable

Not every risk should be mitigated to zero. The ALARP principle (from safety engineering) says: mitigate down to the level where further mitigation costs more than the risk justifies. The register should record the residual risk after mitigation, not just the gross risk before.

Concrete: 'DB partition limit reached in Q4' is severity 16 today. After archiving sessions older than 12 months, the residual is severity 4. The team has decided 4 is acceptable; further mitigation (sharding) costs more than the residual risk justifies for the next year.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Severity scoring is subjective. 'Probability 3 / Impact 4' is a guess dressed as a number. Document the scoring rubric (what 'Probability 3' means in this team) or the score is meaningless.
  • Quarterly review is a meeting. Walking 20 risks takes an hour. If the team views it as overhead instead of insurance, the register drifts. Couple the review with the postmortem-AI review — same room, half the perceived cost.
  • The register can grow unbounded. Every concern becomes a row; the register becomes a wishlist. Cap at ~20 entries; anything below severity 6 lives in a 'watching' note, not a row.

Lifecycle

proposed → open → mitigation in flight → mitigated → accepted → closed

A risk starts as proposed in a PR. After review it becomes open with an owner and a mitigation plan. Mitigation in flight means work is happening; mitigated means the work is done and the risk score is now low; accepted means the team has decided to live with the risk as it is. Closed is for risks that no longer apply.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Register has 80 entriesEvery minor concern becomes a row; the register is unusable.Cap at ~20 entries. Anything below severity 6 is in a 'watching' note, not a row.
All risks owned by the tech leadThe tech lead can't own everything; nothing gets mitigated.Cap at 3 active risks per owner; if the tech lead has 5+, redistribute before the next quarterly review.
Mitigation is vague'Improve test coverage'.Concrete and time-boxed. 'Hit 80% coverage on the sessions service by 2026-07-01.'

Worked example

Register row

Sessions DB growth at 12% MoM

id: R-014
          title: Sessions DB growth at 12% MoM
          kind: technical
          description: |
          At current growth, the sessions table reaches the 1 TB partition limit by Q4-2026.
          Latency on aggregations already up 18% over the last 60 days.
          probability: 4   # 1–5; 5 = almost certain
          impact:      4   # 1–5; 5 = severe
          severity:    16  # 4 × 4
          owner: backend-leads
          mitigation: |
          Move tags to a separate table; archive sessions older than 12 months
          to cold storage. Owner: @backend-leads. Target: 2026-07-01.
          status: mitigation in flight
          next_review: 2026-07-01

The register renders the YAML into a sortable table; the owner is paged when the next-review date passes without movement.
