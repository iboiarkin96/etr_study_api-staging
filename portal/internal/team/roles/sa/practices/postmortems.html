Blameless postmortems

Every P1/P2 incident gets a postmortem with timeline, impact, root cause, action items, and a 'what we learned' section.

TL;DR

A blameless postmortem looks for the process problem, not the guilty engineer. The format is fixed: timeline, impact, root cause, contributing factors, action items with owner and date, lessons. Reviewed quarterly. Without postmortems, the same incident repeats every few months.

What it is

A postmortem is the document a team writes after an incident. Blameless means the team analyses the system that allowed the incident — never the person who pushed the button.

The page has six sections: a tight timeline, the customer-visible impact, the root cause, contributing factors (the system gaps that let the cause through), action items with owner and due date, and a short what we learned. A quarterly forum reviews the open action items.

Why we need it

The same incident — or one shaped just like it — happens again in six months when the postmortem is missing. Memory fades; the engineer who fixed it leaves; the next on-call relearns the lesson the hard way.

The blameless framing matters. If postmortems become a blame ritual, engineers hide near-misses, the team learns less, and the next incident is worse. Blameless creates the safety to write what really happened.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Google

Originated 'blameless postmortems' in the SRE Book. The framing is process-first, person-never; postmortems are a leading metric for engineering culture.

What we take: Blameless is a discipline, not a slogan. Every sentence that names a person must be rewritten to name the gap.

Source: Google SRE Book — Postmortem culture

Stripe

Publishes public postmortems on their engineering blog; the format is consistent — Impact, Timeline, Root Cause, Action Items.

What we take: Public postmortems build trust with customers. They cost nothing to write and pay back in credibility.

Source: Stripe blog — engineering

Cloudflare

Publishes detailed post-incident analyses ('RCA') for any incident that affected customers, often within 72 hours.

What we take: Speed matters. A postmortem published in 72 hours signals competence; one published in three months signals the opposite.

Source: Cloudflare blog — postmortem tag

Atlassian

Open-source 'Postmortem Templates' library and an Incident Handbook used by many teams.

What we take: Templates make it cheap to write a postmortem. The friction is the reason teams skip them.

Source: Atlassian Incident Management Handbook

Pattern: fixed template, written within days, named action items with dates, blameless framing throughout. Public postmortems are a trust multiplier — companies known for them attract better engineers.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Title

Specific. 'Sessions service unavailable for 18 min on 2026-05-08', not 'incident'.

Status

draft / under review / accepted. Accepted means action items are tracked.

Timeline

Plain bullets, UTC. 'T+0 alert fires; T+2 on-call ack; T+5 confirms with kubectl …'

Impact

Who saw what, for how long, in numbers.

Root cause

One sentence. Often a process gap, not a code bug.

Contributing factors

System decisions that allowed the cause to slip through (no alert, no review, no test).

Action items

Each with one named owner, a due date, and a link to the issue.

Lessons

Two or three paragraphs. What the team would tell their past selves.

How it works in this project

Postmortems live at internal/sre/postmortems/NNNN-YYYY-MM-DD-title.html. The template is in the UI Kit. The action-item tracker is whatever tool the team uses for engineering tasks; the postmortem links to the issues, never restates them.

Reviewed every quarter in a 60-minute session: walk the open action items, close the done ones, escalate the stuck ones. The session is recorded; minutes go on the index page.

Deep dive — the theory you need

'Blameless' — what it actually means

Blameless is not the absence of accountability. It is the choice to analyse the system that allowed the failure, not the person who pressed the button. The same incident, two framings:

BlamefulBlameless
Engineer X failed to verify the migration before merging.The migration runbook had no verification step. Engineer X followed it correctly.
On-call took 20 min to respond — too slow.The alert paged the secondary instead of the primary because of a stale schedule.
Reviewer approved a broken change.The CI test that would have caught it was silently skipped on this PR.

The Five Whys — when it works and when it doesn't

The classic Five Whys technique can dig too deep too fast. A safer variant:

  1. Why did the incident happen? Direct cause.
  2. Why did the direct cause go undetected? Monitoring gap.
  3. Why did the monitoring gap exist? Process gap.

Three Whys is usually enough. Beyond that you end up in 'why was the team understaffed' — a real concern, but not a postmortem action item.

Action item taxonomy

  • Prevent — fix the root cause so this exact thing can't happen again.
  • Detect — add monitoring so the same shape of incident pages earlier next time.
  • Mitigate — write or update the runbook so the next incident is shorter.

A good postmortem has at least one of each. A postmortem with only 'Prevent' items is over-confident; one with only 'Detect' is under-confident.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Writing takes a day. A full postmortem is 4–8 hours including the timeline reconstruction. After a SEV-2, that's a day where the on-call is also tired. Schedule the write within 48 h, not on the night of the incident.
  • Blameless requires culture. Writing 'engineer X' instead of 'the process gap' is a habit. New engineers slip; reviewers must catch it in every postmortem PR. Without that diligence, blameless turns into blame-by-proxy.
  • Action items pile up. 50 postmortems × 3 action items each = 150 open AIs. Track them in the same engineering tracker as feature work; quarterly review closes the dead ones honestly.

Lifecycle

incident closed → draft → reviewed → accepted → action items in progress → quarterly review

The draft starts within 48 hours of the incident closing. The author walks the timeline; the team contributes. Within 10 days, the postmortem moves to accepted and the action items are filed. The quarterly review walks every open AI in chronological order.

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead

Worked example

Postmortem fragment

Sessions service unavailable, 2026-04-12, 18 min

Status. accepted, 2026-04-22.

Impact. 12 minutes of full unavailability + 6 minutes of degraded latency on POST /api/v1/sessions. ~340 users affected. SLO budget consumed: 4 of 43 minutes/month.

Timeline (UTC).

  • 13:02 deploy of sessions@1.7.4 starts.
  • 13:04 pod readiness check times out; old pod terminated; new pod fails.
  • 13:05 5xx burst alert fires.
  • 13:07 on-call acks; opens the runbook.
  • 13:14 rollback executed.
  • 13:22 traffic stable; alert clears.

Root cause. The deploy's readiness check called an unreachable internal endpoint that had been renamed two days earlier.

Contributing factors. The rename PR didn't touch the readiness path. The integration test for readiness was skipped on PR #2814.

Action items.

  • Re-enable the readiness integration test in CI. Owner: @backend-leads. Due: 2026-04-18. Issue #4711.
  • Make readiness path a CODEOWNERS-required file. Owner: @platform. Due: 2026-04-25. Issue #4712.

Lessons. Renames that touch the deploy path are higher risk than they look; the integration test that would have caught this was already written and silently skipped. The right answer is to never skip the readiness test, not to add more tests.
