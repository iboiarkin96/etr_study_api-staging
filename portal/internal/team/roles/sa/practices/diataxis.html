Diátaxis — four quadrants, no mixing

Every page belongs to one mode — tutorial, how-to, reference, or explanation — and stays in that mode.

TL;DR

Diátaxis is a model from Daniele Procida that splits documentation into four modes. Tutorials teach a beginner. How-tos help someone with a specific task. References give precise data. Explanations build understanding. Mixing modes is the most common reason readers say 'this doc is unreadable'.

What it is

Diátaxis separates documentation into four kinds based on what the reader is doing. The four modes answer four different questions:

  • Tutorial — 'I'm new, teach me by doing.'
  • How-to — 'I know what I want, show me the steps.'
  • Reference — 'I need a fact, give it to me precisely.'
  • Explanation — 'I want to understand why.'

Each mode has different goals, different shape, and different rules. Mixing them — a tutorial with reference tables in the middle, or a how-to that drifts into explanation — confuses the reader because their expectations shift mid-page.

Why we need it

Readers come to docs with a question in mind. The first paragraph tells them whether they are in the right place. When a tutorial starts with a reference table, the beginner closes the tab. When a reference page has prose for half a page, the engineer who just wanted the field name closes the tab.

Picking one mode per page also disciplines the author. You can't smuggle 'this is also why we built it this way' into a how-to; that thought belongs in an explanation. The exile of the wrong content makes both pages better.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Django

Adopted Diátaxis in 2021; the docs were re-organised around the four modes (tutorials, how-tos, reference, explanation). Public migration log on the dev mailing list.

What we take: The migration takes a year and is worth it. Hits to support tickets and 'I can't find X' drop measurably.

Gatsby

Used Diátaxis from day one; their docs site is the most-cited 'good docs' example in static-site community circles.

What we take: Starting from Diátaxis is much cheaper than retrofitting it. The shape leaks into the URL structure.

Cloudflare

Restructured Developer Platform docs around the four Diátaxis modes in 2022; doc page templates explicitly carry the mode in the URL and the page header.

What we take: Showing the mode on the page (a chip, a colour) helps readers calibrate before they read a paragraph.

Divio (originator)

What we take: The originator's own site is the reference implementation. Worth reading on its own as a meta-example.

Across adopters: tag the mode visibly on the page, split mixed pages even when it hurts, and resist the temptation to invent a fifth mode. Four is enough.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Tutorial

Lesson-shaped. The reader follows the steps in order. Reproducible end result.

How-to

Recipe-shaped. The reader has the goal; you give the shortest path.

Reference

Lookup-shaped. Tables, field names, types, error codes. No narrative.

Explanation

Essay-shaped. Why we did it this way; alternatives we rejected; trade-offs.

Tag chip

Every page declares its mode at the top so the reader knows what to expect.

How it works in this project

The UI Kit ships one template per mode under services/portal/ui-kit/pages/templates/: doc-tutorial, doc-howto, doc-reference, doc-explanation, and a flexible doc-article for cross-cutting prose. Every internal page starts from one of these templates.

The page tags its mode in two places: visually (a coloured pill in the header) and in metadata (<body data-mode='howto'>). The Vale ruleset enforces small mode-specific rules — e.g. tutorials must use the imperative ('Open the file…'), references must not.

Deep dive — the theory you need

The 2×2 grid that produces the four modes

Diátaxis is not arbitrary. The four modes come from crossing two axes:

  • What is the reader doing? Acquiring a skill (gaining new ability) or applying a skill (using what they already know).
  • What kind of knowledge? Practical (steps, actions) or theoretical (understanding, mental model).

How to tell the modes apart in practice

  • Tutorial uses 'we' and the imperative ('Open the file…'). Has a guaranteed end-state.
  • How-to uses the imperative ('Open the file…'). No guarantee on what you start with.
  • Reference uses noun phrases ('The id field…'). No imperative, no narrative.
  • Explanation uses 'we' or third-person prose. Argues. Often contains the word 'because'.

If your draft contains all four registers, you have four pages, not one.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • More clicks for the reader. One Frankenstein page becomes four. The reader who wanted both 'how' and 'why' now navigates between two URLs. Mitigate with explicit cross-links at the bottom of each page.
  • Some content is cross-cutting. 'Why we use PlantUML' is half explanation, half reference. Forcing it into one mode loses information. The honest fix: a short explanation page that points at the reference; never the other way around.
  • Migration of legacy pages is slow. Splitting an existing 4 000-word doc into four mode-pure pages is a week of work. Most teams do it in batches over a quarter; that's a quarter of slower edits to those pages.

Lifecycle

mode chosen → template applied → drafted → reviewed → published

Authors pick the mode first, then the template. Reviewers check: is this really a tutorial, or is it three how-tos in a coat? If the doc straddles modes, split it. Once published, the mode is stable — changing the mode of a page is rare and intentional.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Tutorial drifts into referenceAuthor adds a 'full options table' in the middle.Split the page in two. Mark the tutorial with data-mode='tutorial'; a Vale rule warns on lookup tables inside tutorial pages.
How-to drifts into explanationAuthor justifies every step.If the how-to has > 2 paragraphs of 'why', extract them to a sibling explanation page; the how-to ends with a single 'see also' link.
Reference has a prose introThree paragraphs before the table.Cap the reference intro at one <p> of ≤ 30 words; everything else belongs in an explanation page.
Explanation has runnable stepsAuthor can't resist adding example commands.A Vale rule flags imperative verbs (Open, Run, Type) on pages tagged data-mode='explanation'; reviewers split or retag.

Worked example

One topic, four pages

Adding a new ADR

  • Tutorial. Write your first ADR (20 minutes). Step-by-step from cp template.html through to merge.
  • How-to. Supersede an existing ADR. Six numbered steps, no story.
  • Reference. ADR file structure. Required sections; status values; YAML front-matter fields.
  • Explanation. Why we use ADRs. The drift problem; why one numbered file; why never-edit-after-merge.

The same topic, four pages, four shapes. A reader picks the one that matches their question.

Outcome. Readers pick the page that matches their question on entry; average time-on-page rises (they finish what they came for); 'I can't find X' tickets drop measurably in the next quarter's support log.
