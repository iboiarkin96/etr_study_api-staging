Tech Radar — the project's technology portfolio

Quarterly chart of the libraries, languages, and tools we adopt, trial, assess, or hold. Every project drifts toward 'we have ten experimental libraries and no one is sure which is the canon'. A Tech Radar makes that drift visible — four rings (Adopt, Trial, Assess, Hold) group every technology; the source is a small JSON in the repo, CI renders the SVG, reviewed quarterly.

What it is

A Tech Radar is a chart with four concentric rings and four quadrants. The rings show the team's confidence in a technology — Adopt (use it now), Trial (use it in a non-critical place), Assess (worth a Friday afternoon), Hold (do not introduce). The quadrants split technologies by kind: Languages, Frameworks & Libraries, Platforms, and Tools.

The source is tech-radar.json; the rendered SVG is generated in CI. The radar is reviewed every quarter — items move inward as confidence grows, outward when problems appear.

Why we need it

Without a radar, the same library is evaluated three times in a year by three different engineers, and silently introduced into a fourth service by a fourth engineer who didn't know.

The radar gives the team one place to ask 'have we looked at this, and what did we conclude?' It also lets the project say 'no' in writing — putting a name in Hold is a public commitment.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

ThoughtWorks

Originated the format in 2010; publishes a public Technology Radar twice a year with hundreds of entries across languages, tools, platforms, and techniques.

What we take: The radar is most valuable as a snapshot in time. Compare two issues a year apart to see how the team's confidence shifted.

Source: ThoughtWorks Technology Radar

Zalando

Adopted the radar internally, then open-sourced the SVG renderer used by hundreds of teams worldwide.

What we take: The open-source renderer means you only maintain the JSON; the picture comes for free.

Source: Zalando tech-radar (GitHub)

AOE Inc.

Public quarterly radar with explicit Hold entries; documents why each rejected technology was rejected.

What we take: The Hold list is the most-read part of a public radar — it tells new engineers what not to spend Friday on.

Source: AOE Tech Radar (public)

The pattern across adopters: a small JSON file, an open-source renderer, and a calendar block every quarter. Skipping the quarterly review is what kills radars; the JSON file alone is not enough.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Adopt

Confident, in production, would pick again. Default choice.

Trial

Worth using in a real but non-critical project; some bumps still expected.

Assess

Interesting. Spend a Friday. Do not depend on it for a release.

Hold

Do not introduce. We have evidence (legal, performance, churn) it does not fit.

Quadrants

Languages • Frameworks & Libraries • Platforms • Tools.

Movement

Items move ring-by-ring per quarter; a fast demotion to Hold is allowed in emergency.

How it works in this project

The radar will live at services/portal/internal/analysis/tech-radar.json. CI runs the Zalando open-source renderer and writes tech-radar.svg next to it. The page analysis/tech-radar.html embeds the SVG and renders the legend.

Every entry has a one-paragraph note explaining why it sits where it sits. When an item moves, the note is updated with the date and the reason — the page is also a small log of the team's technology history.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Ring semantics — what 'Adopt' actually means

The four rings are not 'great / good / ok / bad'. They are statements about confidence:

RingStatementDecision rule
Adopt'We use this in production and would pick it again.'Default choice for new projects; no Design Doc needed.
Trial'We have used this in a non-critical project; some bumps still expected.'Allowed in production for non-critical paths; Design Doc names the risk.
Assess'We are interested; spend a Friday afternoon.'Allowed in prototypes; never in production.
Hold'We have evidence this does not fit our needs.'Forbidden in new code; existing usage is paid down.

Quadrant rationale

The four quadrants — Languages, Frameworks & Libraries, Platforms, Tools — are not arbitrary. They map roughly to the cost of change:

  • Languages — change rarely; a wrong call costs years.
  • Frameworks & Libraries — change occasionally; a wrong call costs months.
  • Platforms — vendor lock-in; a wrong call costs migrations.
  • Tools — change easily; a wrong call costs a sprint.

Items in the same quadrant compete for the same slot in the team's brain. Three database libraries in Adopt means the team is fragmented — pick one.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Quarterly review is real time. 30 minutes seems small until you miss two quarters and discover six unreviewed entries. Calendar block + reminder, or the practice quietly dies.
  • Political friction on Hold. Moving a popular library to Hold is a real conflict — an engineer's pet project is suddenly forbidden. The honest path: ADR explaining the decision, with the author of the affected code as co-author.
  • Small-team overhead. Below ~20 engineers, the radar can feel ceremonial. The signal-to-effort ratio improves with team size; very small teams may get more value from a one-page README of 'what we use and why'.

Lifecycle

proposed → assess → trial → adopt → hold → retired

A technology enters as proposed. After a short evaluation it lands in assess, trial, adopt, or hold. Items move ring-by-ring across quarters; fast demotion to hold is allowed in emergencies (CVE, licence change). Retired is for items no longer in use anywhere.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Vanity radarThe radar is impressive but no one updates it.30-min slot on the engineering calendar, last week of each quarter; a missed review fails the engineering-health audit.
Every library is 'Adopt'Authors don't want the friction of justifying a Trial.Force at least one demotion or new Hold per quarter; if a quarter has zero ring movements, the tech lead writes a one-paragraph note explaining why.
Hold list is emptySaying 'no' in writing feels political.Aim for ≥ 3 Hold entries; an empty Hold list is a signal the team is afraid to say 'no' in writing.
Radar drifts from realityJSON says Adopt; the service imports the Hold version.CI scans dependency manifests against the radar; mismatches fail the build.

Worked example

JSON entry

One row of the radar

{
"name": "PlantUML",
"quadrant": "Tools",
"ring": "Adopt",
"since": "2025-09",
"note": "Server-side rendering, predictable output, accessible. Adopted in ADR 0020.",
"owner": "ivan-boyarkin"
          }

Every entry carries a since date so the page also doubles as a small history. A move from Trial to Adopt updates the date; the previous date stays in a history[] array.

Outcome. tech-radar.svg renders with PlantUML in the Adopt ring; the page embeds the since date and the one-line note; quarterly review surfaces any ring movements with their rationale in the page history.
