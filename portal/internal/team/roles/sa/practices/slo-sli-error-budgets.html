SLO · SLI · error budgets

A measurable target for each user-facing service, with a budget that slows releases when reliability dips.

TL;DR

An SLO is a number the team agrees on — 'sessions endpoint responds with 2xx within 800 ms, 99.9% of the time'. SLIs are the metrics that measure that number. The error budget is the gap between the target and 100%; when the budget runs out, releases slow down until reliability recovers.

What it is

SLO (Service Level Objective) is a target — for example, 99.9% of requests to POST /sessions return a 2xx within 800 ms over a rolling 30-day window.

SLI (Service Level Indicator) is the metric that measures the SLO. For the example above, the SLI is the ratio of 'good' requests over 'total' requests, where 'good' is defined precisely.

Error budget is what's left of the target. 99.9% over 30 days = 43 minutes of allowed downtime per month. As long as the budget has time left, the team can ship. When the budget is spent, the team focuses on reliability until it recovers.

Why we need it

'Is the service good enough?' is a feeling without numbers. With SLOs it becomes a calculation. Product and ops share one frame: they argue about the SLO, not about how the service is doing this week.

The error budget is what makes the SLO operational. Without a budget, an SLO is decoration; with one, the team has a clear rule for when to ship features and when to fix reliability. The argument shifts from 'should we?' to 'do we have budget?' — much easier to settle.

Codified SLO/SLI/Error Budget in the SRE Book (2016) and the SRE Workbook (2018). Multi-burn-rate alerting was the most influential single chapter.

What we take: An SLO without an enforced budget is decoration. The policy ('what happens when the budget is gone') is what makes it real.

LinkedIn

Public talks describe how 200+ services use a common SLO platform; each service owns its SLO; reliability work is prioritised against the budget.

What we take: Centralised SLO tooling means engineers don't reinvent Prometheus rules per service.

Sloth (open-source)

Open-source CLI that generates Prometheus SLO rules from a small YAML spec. Used by hundreds of teams.

What we take: You don't have to write Prometheus rules by hand. The YAML format is the source; the rules are derived.

Source: Sloth — SLO generator

Nobl9

Commercial SLO platform built by ex-Google SREs; centralises SLO definitions, dashboards, budget burns, and policy.

What we take: When the team grows past ~50 services, a dedicated SLO tool pays back; per-team spreadsheets stop scaling.

Source: Nobl9

Across companies: pick a small set of SLIs per service; aim for the lowest defensible objective; alert on burn rate, not on raw failures; write the policy before you hit the budget.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

SLI definition

The exact query: which metric, which filter, which thresholds. Stored as YAML next to the service.

SLO target

99.9%, 99.95%, or 99.5% — pick one. Higher numbers cost more, not less.

Window

Usually 28 or 30 days, rolling. Quarterly is too slow; weekly is too noisy.

Budget

100% − target. Tracked as 'remaining' on a dashboard.

Burn rate

Speed at which the budget is being consumed. Alerts fire on burn rate, not on raw failures.

Policy

What the team does when the budget is at 50%, 25%, 0%. Written down before it happens.

How it works in this project

The project has none of this today. The rollout starts with one critical endpoint — most likely POST /sessions — and defines availability and latency SLOs for it. Prometheus rules express the SLIs; Grafana shows the budget burn.

Once one SLO is in place and watched in the weekly engineering sync for a month, the practice expands. Each new SLO requires an ADR that names the target, the window, and the policy.

Deep dive — the theory you need

Error budget math — concrete numbers

Error budget = (1 − SLO) × time window. For a 28-day window:

SLOAllowed downtime in 28 days
99%6 h 43 min
99.5%3 h 22 min
99.9%40 min
99.95%20 min
99.99%4 min

Read the table as cost: every extra 9 cuts the allowed downtime by ~10×. The cost of meeting it grows faster than that — adding a 9 typically doubles the infrastructure spend.

Multi-burn-rate alerts — the formula

The naïve approach — page when the error rate is high right now — gives too many false alerts. Multi-burn-rate alerts fire only when the rate is high and sustained:

Burn rate = (error rate in window) / (1 − SLO)

Page when:
burn_rate > 14.4 in a 1-hour window     # fast page: spending 2% of monthly budget in 1 h
AND burn_rate > 14.4 in a 5-minute window  # filter: not just one short spike

Or:
burn_rate > 6 in a 6-hour window        # slower page
        AND burn_rate > 6 in a 30-minute window

The 14.4× constant comes from 2% / (1 h × 1/720 h-per-month) — the exact rate at which a 1-hour outage would consume 2% of the monthly error budget at SLO 99.9%. The point is: the alert fires only when the trend is bad enough that the budget will run out unless you act.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Per-endpoint SLOs don't scale. 200 services × 5 SLOs each = 1 000 SLOs, none of them watched. Combine into user-journey SLOs (login → first session → first review) that span services; one journey covers a real user outcome.
  • Defining good SLIs is hard. 'Success' is rarely just 2xx; many endpoints have business-level failure modes (the request returned 200 with an empty result). The SLI definition is the bottleneck — budget a week per critical SLO.
  • The policy must be honored. An SLO with a 'freeze releases at 0% budget' policy that gets overridden by leadership the first time it fires is theatre. Pre-commit at the VP level before publishing the policy.

Lifecycle

defined → measured → alerting on burn → policy applied → reviewed quarterly

An SLO starts as a defined target. The SLI is wired; the dashboard shows the burn. Alerts fire on burn rate (multi-window, multi-burn-rate; not on every single failure). The policy kicks in at preset thresholds. Each quarter, the team reviews whether the target is still right.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
SLO is decorationNumber on a dashboard; nothing happens when it's missed.Write the policy first; the policy is what makes the SLO real.
SLI is fuzzy'Good response' is not defined exactly; numbers move when the definition shifts.Write the SLI as a Prometheus query. The query is the definition.
Targets are too highTeam picks 99.99% because it sounds right; can't ship anything.Pick the lowest target the team can defend. Raise later, never lower in panic.
Alerting on every failurePager fires on each 5xx; on-call burns out.Alert on burn rate over a window, not on count over a minute. Multi-burn-rate rules from the Google SRE workbook.

Worked example

SLO definition

POST /sessions latency SLO

service: sessions
slo: post_sessions_latency
objective: 0.999
window: 28d
sli:
good: |
sum(rate(http_request_duration_seconds_count{
service="sessions", method="POST", path="/api/v1/sessions",
status=~"2..", le="0.8"
}[5m]))
total: |
sum(rate(http_request_duration_seconds_count{
service="sessions", method="POST", path="/api/v1/sessions"
}[5m]))
policy:
at_50_percent_left: 'Discuss in weekly engineering sync.'
at_25_percent_left: 'Freeze non-critical changes to the sessions service.'
          at_0_percent_left:  'All hands on reliability until budget recovers.'

The YAML is the source. From it, CI generates Prometheus rules and a Grafana panel; the policy is rendered on the service page in the catalog.
