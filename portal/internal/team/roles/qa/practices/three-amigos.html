Three Amigos — SWE × QA × PM session before every story

A 30-minute structured conversation where developer, QA, and PM align on acceptance criteria and edge cases — before implementation begins.

TL;DR

Three Amigos (George Dinwiddie, 2009) is a pre-implementation session — 30 minutes max — where developer, QA, and PM answer three questions: what does done look like? what edge cases must be handled? what could go wrong? PM provides business context; developer provides technical constraints; QA provides edge-case scenarios. Misalignments here cost 30 minutes; in testing, days; in production, weeks. Output: a Given/When/Then acceptance-criteria list serving as the test oracle. Gojko Adzic's Specification by Example, BDD/Cucumber, and the entire Lean-Agile tradition converge on the practice. For Study App every new feature runs through Three Amigos before code starts.

What it is

Three Amigos is a pre-implementation conversation between three roles — PM (or analyst), Developer, and QA — about a single story before code is written. George Dinwiddie introduced the term in 2009 to name a long-existing BDD/XP practice. The session answers three questions: what does done look like, what edge cases must be handled, what could go wrong.

Output: a Given/When/Then acceptance-criteria list, owned by the trio, that serves as both spec for the developer and test oracle for QA. The session is short (15–30 minutes max), focused on one story, and produces a tangible artefact.

Why we need it

Without Three Amigos, the team writes specs in PM-land, code in SWE-land, and tests in QA-land — each role makes assumptions the others don't share. The misalignments emerge during testing; fixing them costs days. With Three Amigos, the misalignments emerge during a 30-minute conversation before code starts; fixing them costs minutes. The economic argument is dispositive; the cultural argument is parallel — the conversation builds shared understanding that compounds over time.

How others do it

George Dinwiddie · the coinage

George Dinwiddie introduced "the Three Amigos" in 2009 to describe the pattern of business analyst, developer, and tester reviewing each story together before development. The framing emphasised the trio's complementary perspectives — business, technical, quality — and the conversation as the deliverable.

What we take: the trio's conversation is the deliverable; the acceptance criteria are the artefact, but the shared understanding is the real value.

Source: Dinwiddie — The Three Amigos

Gojko Adzic · Specification by Example

Gojko Adzic's Specification by Example (2011) formalises the practice: every story is specified with concrete examples (Given/When/Then), developed against those examples, tested with those examples. Adzic's "specification workshop" is the structured form of Three Amigos. Templates, facilitation techniques, and worked examples from real teams.

What we take: the conversation should produce concrete examples (specific inputs and outputs), not abstract criteria.

Source: Adzic — Specification by Example

Cucumber community · Example Mapping

Matt Wynne's "Example Mapping" (Cucumber community, 2015) is the modern operational technique for running Three Amigos efficiently. The session uses index cards: yellow for the story, blue for rules, green for examples, red for questions. In 25 minutes the trio maps out the story space, surfaces ambiguities, and produces a Given/When/Then-ready artefact.

What we take: Example Mapping is the practical facilitation technique — 25 minutes, four card colours, structured output.

Source: Cucumber — Example Mapping

Atlassian · Three Amigos play

Atlassian's Team Playbook publishes a "Three Amigos" play template: 30-minute meeting, fixed agenda (story walkthrough → acceptance criteria → edge cases → risks), output is a completed Jira ticket with linked test scenarios. Atlassian's data: teams adopting Three Amigos reduced their escape rate by ~30% and reduced rework by ~25% within 12 weeks.

What we take: a templated agenda with a tracker-integrated output makes Three Amigos institutional, not optional.

Source: Atlassian — Team Playbook

Anatomy of a Three Amigos session

Attendees

PM (or analyst), developer, QA. Designers and SRE join for relevant stories. ≤ 5 attendees.

Trigger

Every new story before implementation; mandatory for stories above a size/risk threshold.

Duration

15–30 minutes max per story; longer means the story is too big and should be split.

Three questions

What does done look like? What edge cases must be handled? What could go wrong?

Output

Given/When/Then acceptance-criteria list with concrete examples; linked to the story ticket.

Facilitation technique

Example Mapping (story / rules / examples / questions cards) keeps the session structured and time-boxed.

How to run Three Amigos

  1. Schedule per-story before implementation. 30-min slot when the story enters the backlog; PM + SWE + QA accept.
  2. Use Example Mapping cards. Yellow = story, blue = rules, green = examples, red = questions.
  3. Walk through the three questions. Done? Edge cases? Risks? Each role contributes from their perspective.
  4. Surface concrete examples. "Given X, when Y, then Z" with specific values — not abstract criteria.
  5. Capture questions. Red cards for things the trio can't answer in the room; assign owners; resolve before implementation starts.
  6. Update the story ticket. Acceptance criteria as Given/When/Then; concrete examples linked; risks named.
  7. Track adoption. % of stories with a Three Amigos session is a process metric; aim for ≥ 90% on user-facing features.

Deep dive

The conceptual claim is that shared understanding can only come from shared conversation. A PM writing a spec alone produces a spec that reflects PM thinking; a developer reading that spec implements what they think it means; QA tests what they think the implementation should do — the three interpretations diverge at every step. The conversation forces alignment in real-time: when QA asks "what if the user is offline?", the PM and developer have to converge on an answer before code is written. The artefact is downstream; the conversation is upstream.

The three questions are deliberately broad and ordered. "What does done look like?" surfaces happy-path criteria. "What edge cases must be handled?" surfaces boundary, error, and validation paths. "What could go wrong?" surfaces risks, disruptions, race conditions. The order matters: starting with done establishes shared context; edge cases extend it; risks expand it. Reversing the order tends to derail the session into doom-spiralling.

The Example Mapping technique (Matt Wynne) is the operational form. Four card colours, 25-minute time-box, structured discovery. Yellow = the story. Blue = a rule. Green = an example of the rule in action. Red = a question the trio can't answer. The session ends when the questions are exhausted or new examples stop generating new rules.

Concrete examples beat abstract criteria. "The password must be strong" is abstract; "Given a user enters 'abc123', when they submit, then they see error code WEAK_PASSWORD with message 'Password must be 8+ characters with mixed case'" is concrete. Concrete examples become Given/When/Then scenarios that are both readable by the PM and executable by Cucumber/SpecFlow/Behave.

The trio is not literal. Designers join for UI stories; SRE for production-impact stories; security for auth/identity stories; customer support for stories addressing common complaints. The discipline is perspective-diversity, not headcount. Cap at ~5 attendees — beyond that, the conversation becomes a meeting and loses focus. Below 3, perspective-diversity collapses.

The 30-minute cap is operational. A 30-minute session is fast enough to schedule per story without becoming overhead; long enough to surface non-obvious questions. Sessions over 30 minutes usually signal the story is too big and should be split. Sessions under 15 minutes usually signal the story is too obvious — in that case, skipping for that story is fine.

Adoption metrics are the practice's accountability mechanism. % of user-facing stories with a Three Amigos session, tracked weekly, exposes whether the practice is happening or aspirational. Atlassian's data: teams above 90% adoption see ~30% lower escape rate; teams below 50% see negligible impact. The metric makes the discipline visible.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Session run by PM alone with input solicited later "I'll send the criteria and you can review" — defeats the conversation; misalignments stay hidden. Synchronous session with all three roles in the room.
Abstract acceptance criteria "The password must be secure" — no concrete examples; QA and SWE interpret differently. Demand a concrete example for every rule; use Given/When/Then with specific values.
Sessions running too long 1-hour Three Amigos becomes a meeting; the story is too big. 30-minute cap; if you can't finish, split the story.
Skipped under deadline "No time for Three Amigos this sprint" — the missed misalignment costs days. Adoption metric tracked; ≥ 90% on user-facing stories.
QA invited but treated as audience Session happens but QA isn't asked for input; practice is performative. Facilitator solicits QA's "what could go wrong?" deliberately; QA's questions get red cards.

Worked example — Study App quiz-autosave Three Amigos

Story: "As a learner, I want my quiz answers auto-saved so I don't lose progress if my connection drops."

Session (Week 19, 28 minutes): Attendees: PM (Sergey), Backend SWE (Marina), Frontend SWE (Anton), QA (Ivan). Designer joined for 10 minutes on the UI question.

Example Mapping cards:

  • Yellow (story): Auto-save quiz answers.
  • Blue (rule 1): Each answer is saved within 500ms of input. Green: Given a typed answer, when 500ms pass, then it's persisted server-side.
  • Blue (rule 2): If the network drops mid-save, the answer is retried on reconnect. Green: Given network drops after submit, when network returns 5 seconds later, then the answer is saved exactly once (no duplicate).
  • Blue (rule 3): The UI shows save status. Green: "Saving…" while in flight, "Saved" after persist, "Retrying…" on transient error.
  • Red (questions): What if the user submits the final quiz while a save is in flight? (Marina) — assigned to PM, resolved next day: hold submission until all in-flight saves complete or 10s timeout, then warn user. What about offline mode? (Anton) — out of scope, separate story.

Output: 3 acceptance criteria with Given/When/Then examples, linked to QA-417. The "answer is saved exactly once" rule (idempotency) was Ivan's contribution — without Three Amigos, the spec would have been "save the answer" and the duplicate-submission bug discovered in production.

Outcome: Feature implemented in Sprint 14, zero P0/P1 escapes on autosave, 1 P2 cosmetic issue caught in QA. Without Three Amigos, the idempotency requirement would likely have been missed (estimated cost-if-missed: 5 engineer-days for the production race-condition fix + 3 days of user-support cost).

Quality bar

Senior bar

Three Amigos runs for ≥ 90% of user-facing stories. Example Mapping cards used; output is Given/When/Then with concrete examples; questions tracked as red cards with owners. Adoption metric on QA dashboard. Specialists join for relevant stories. Session capped at 30 min.

Mid bar

Three Amigos runs for major user-facing stories; acceptance criteria documented in Given/When/Then; risks named.

Junior bar

Can run a 30-minute Three Amigos session given a story; ask the three questions; produce concrete examples; capture open questions.
