Exploratory Test Charters — structured unscripted testing

Gives unscripted testing a written goal and scope so tester creativity is directed toward the highest-risk areas — not scattered randomly.

TL;DR

An exploratory test charter (James Bach & the Session-Based Test Management movement, late 1990s) is a one-line mission for an unscripted testing session: "Explore [target] with [resources/tools], looking for [risk or info], in [time-boxed duration]." The tester designs and executes tests simultaneously, guided by the charter's scope, and produces a structured session report at the end (areas covered, bugs found, questions raised, time spent). Charters direct tester creativity toward the highest-risk areas without scripting it away. They are the standard companion to automated tests — automation covers what you know; exploration covers what you didn't think to script. Microsoft, Etsy, Spotify, and the entire Context-Driven Testing community all run chartered exploratory sessions; for Study App, every sprint includes at least one 60-minute charter per critical flow.

What it is

An exploratory test charter is a one-line written mission for an unscripted testing session. The canonical template (Elisabeth Hendrickson, James Bach) is: "Explore [target] with [resources/tools], looking for [risk or information], in [time-boxed duration]." The charter does not prescribe test cases; the tester designs and executes tests simultaneously, guided by the charter's scope. Each session is time-boxed (usually 60 or 90 minutes) and produces a structured session report.

Session-Based Test Management (SBTM, James & Jonathan Bach) is the formal management overlay: charters are tracked like work items, session reports use a fixed format (Charter / Setup / Coverage / Bugs / Issues / Notes / Time-breakdown), and metrics are computed from reports (bugs-per-hour, charter coverage). The Bachs' SBTM format is the de-facto standard for treating exploratory testing as a measurable, manageable practice — not a free-form activity.

Why we need it

Automated tests cover what you knew to test for; exploratory tests cover what you didn't. Every product ships with defects the test plan didn't anticipate — usability cliffs, race conditions, surprising user paths, accessibility gaps. Without exploratory testing, those defects are found by users; with exploratory testing, they are found in the team's hands. Charters make exploration structured (so the time is well spent) and reportable (so leadership can see the value) — without charters, exploration is hard to defend and is the first thing cut under deadline pressure.

How others do it

James & Jonathan Bach · SBTM

James Bach and his brother Jonathan formalised Session-Based Test Management in the late 1990s. The SBTM framework defines: the charter format (target + scope + duration), the session report format (Charter / Setup / Coverage / Bugs / Issues / Notes / Time-breakdown with T/B/S split — Test design and execution / Bug investigation / Setup), and the management practices (debrief, metrics, charter library). SBTM is documented in Lessons Learned in Software Testing (Kaner/Bach/Pettichord, 2001) and in countless conference papers; it is the canonical reference for managed exploratory testing.

What we take: the SBTM session-report format is mandatory — without a structured report, exploration is informal and unattributable; with it, exploration becomes a tracked artefact.

Source: Satisfice — Exploratory Testing

Elisabeth Hendrickson · Explore It!

Elisabeth Hendrickson's Explore It! (2013) is the modern practitioner's guide to chartered exploratory testing. Hendrickson contributes the charter template (the "Explore … with … to discover …" phrasing) and the personas/heuristics catalogue — concrete starting points like "Test as the angry user," "Test boundary conditions," "Test interrupted flows," "Test as the curious user." Hendrickson's framing makes the practice teachable: junior testers can run productive charters using the heuristic library before they have the experience to invent their own approaches.

What we take: publish a heuristic library next to the charter template — junior testers run higher-leverage sessions when they have specific lenses to apply.

Source: Hendrickson — Explore It!

Microsoft · Test Tours

Microsoft's "test tour" tradition (formalised by James Whittaker in Exploratory Software Testing, 2009 and the Microsoft How Microsoft Builds Software series) frames each exploratory session as a "tour" of the application: the FedEx Tour (follow data through the system), the Money Tour (touch every revenue-generating path), the Landmark Tour (visit every important feature). Tours are charters with a metaphor; testers find more bugs when given a vivid scenario than when given a generic "explore the feature" instruction.

What we take: use named tours/personas as charter starting points (Curious User, Angry User, Forgetful User, Power User); the metaphor primes the tester for a specific defect class.

Source: Microsoft — Perform Exploratory Tests

Etsy & Spotify · bug bashes & whole-team exploration

Etsy's release process and Spotify's "Squad Quality" practice include scheduled whole-team bug bashes — a structured exploratory testing session with engineers, designers, PMs, and customer-support staff all running charters in parallel for 60–90 minutes. Etsy reports that bug-bash sessions before major releases catch 30–40% of escapes that automated tests + scripted manual tests miss. The format is SBTM-style charters, distributed across the team, with a real-time slack channel for triage.

What we take: bug bashes scale chartered exploration across the team and surface defects automation cannot — schedule one before any high-stakes release.

Source: Etsy Code as Craft Blog

Anatomy of a charter

Target

The feature, page, flow, or behaviour to explore. Specific (the quiz-attempt flow), not vague (the whole app).

Resources / tools

What the tester uses — browser DevTools, Charles Proxy, an accessibility checker, a mobile device, a specific test account. Listed so the session is reproducible.

Risk / information

What we're looking for: usability issues, edge-case data, race conditions, accessibility gaps, security holes. One per charter.

Duration

Time-boxed: 30 min (short), 60 min (standard), 90 min (deep). Beyond 90 min focus degrades; split into multiple charters instead.

Session report

SBTM-style: Charter / Setup / Coverage / Bugs / Issues / Notes / Time-breakdown (T/B/S split). Filed in the tracker after each session.

Heuristic / persona

Optional but recommended: "Test as the angry user," "Test boundary conditions," "Test as the curious power user." Primes the tester for a specific defect class.

How to run a charter

  1. Write the charter. One line in the template: "Explore [target] with [tools], looking for [risk], in [duration]." Specific target, specific risk, specific time-box.
  2. Pick a heuristic. Choose a persona or testing tour (Curious User, Angry User, FedEx Tour, Landmark Tour) — the lens shapes what defects surface.
  3. Set up (≤ 5 min). Test data, test account, tools open, clean environment. Setup time eats the session; pre-prepare where possible.
  4. Explore (≥ 45 min). Design and execute tests simultaneously; follow your curiosity within the charter's scope. Take quick notes (one-line per observation); don't stop to triage bugs mid-session.
  5. Capture (≤ 7 min). Fill the session report — coverage notes, bugs found, questions raised, time breakdown (T/B/S percentages). The report is the artefact; without it the session is invisible.
  6. Debrief (≤ 3 min). Share findings with the team in a brief debrief; file bugs in the tracker with reproduction steps; tag follow-up charters if the session uncovered new risk.
  7. Track outcomes. Bugs-per-hour and charter coverage feed the QA dashboard; recurring themes inform next sprint's charter list.

Deep dive

The conceptual case for chartered exploratory testing rests on a specific claim about defects: that some defects are specifiable (you can write a test case for them) and others are discoverable (you can only find them by interacting with the product). Automated regression suites catch specifiable defects efficiently; exploratory sessions catch discoverable defects efficiently. The classes overlap but neither is empty — even the most rigorous test plan misses defects that emerge from unexpected interactions, surprising user paths, or environmental quirks the team didn't anticipate. Chartered exploration is the institutional answer to "what about the things we didn't think of?"

Scripted manual testing and exploratory testing are different practices, not endpoints on a spectrum. Scripted manual testing executes a predetermined sequence of steps and asserts predetermined outcomes — it is deterministic, repeatable, and most easily automated away. Exploratory testing designs tests in flight, follows curiosity, and produces findings that the test plan didn't predict — it is non-deterministic, harder to repeat, and irreducibly human. The mistake is treating exploratory testing as "manual testing without a script" — it is a distinct cognitive activity that requires training, technique, and structure (charters).

The SBTM session-report format is the operational heart of the practice. The T/B/S time breakdown — Test design and execution / Bug investigation / Setup — captures how the tester spent the session; ratios reveal practice quality. T-heavy sessions (70%+) mean test design dominated, which is healthy. B-heavy sessions (40%+) mean the session was derailed by one big bug; consider deferring investigation to a follow-up. S-heavy sessions (30%+) reveal infrastructure friction; invest in test data fixtures or env setup. The metrics make practice quality visible.

Personas and tours are the leverage device for novice exploratory testers. A junior tester told "explore the quiz flow" produces a shallow session; the same tester told "explore the quiz flow as a user with a slow 3G connection who keeps losing focus to their kids" produces a deep one. The persona primes attention; the tour names a path through the system. Whittaker's tour catalogue and Hendrickson's persona library are the standard reference; teams should curate a local library of personas tuned to their product (Study App's locale-switcher user, Study App's offline-mode user).

Time-boxing protects focus. A 60-minute session is the goldilocks duration: long enough to find non-trivial defects, short enough to maintain concentration. Sessions over 90 minutes degrade quickly (defect-finding rate drops sharply). Multiple sessions are better than one long session; the standard practice is two 60-minute sessions per day per dedicated exploratory tester, not one 8-hour exploration. The time-box also forces structure: setup must fit in 5 min, capture in 7 min — the budget is real.

Charter libraries grow over time and become institutional memory. A sprint's charter list typically contains 10–20 charters covering high-risk areas (new features, regressions, complex flows); over a year the library accumulates hundreds. Re-running historical charters against new releases is a powerful regression technique. Versioning charters (this charter ran in week 5, found these defects, was repeated in week 14 with these new findings) shows trend. The library lives in a wiki or a dedicated charter tracker; without it the institutional memory is lost.

The relationship between automation and exploration is "automation amplifies exploration." Automated tests handle the boring, repetitive checks (regressions, contract conformance, accessibility lint); exploration is freed to focus on the discoverable defects. Without automation, exploration time is consumed by re-running the same regression checks manually. Without exploration, automation creates a false sense of completeness. The pair is the right model — neither alone is sufficient, and over-investing in one starves the other. Etsy and Spotify both publish ratio targets: ~70% automated test investment, ~30% exploratory testing investment for products at their stage.

Lifecycle

1

Charter authoring

QA lead drafts charters based on sprint risk areas; each has target, tools, risk, duration; one heuristic suggested.

2

Assignment

Charters distributed to testers (and rotating engineers/PMs in bug-bash mode); time-boxed slots reserved on the calendar.

3

Session execution

60-minute session: setup → explore → capture → debrief, following the SBTM format.

4

Report & triage

Session report filed; bugs filed individually with reproduction steps; questions for product/engineering raised in the daily triage.

5

Library update

Charter and report added to the library; recurring high-leverage charters tagged for repeat runs.

6

Retrospective

Sprint retro reviews bugs-per-hour, T/B/S ratios, charter coverage; investment decisions for next sprint informed by the data.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Charter is too broad "Explore the app for 60 minutes" — no specific target, no specific risk; the session wanders. Pin a specific target (the quiz-attempt flow), a specific risk (data loss on connection drop), and a duration; vagueness is the enemy of productive exploration.
No session report Tester explores for 60 minutes, files three bugs, but no record of what was covered or what was missed. SBTM session report is mandatory; without it the session is invisible to leadership and the team has no institutional memory.
Sessions running long "Just 20 more minutes" — 90-minute session becomes a 2.5-hour exhaustion that finds little after the first 90. Hard time-box; if there's more to explore, write a follow-up charter — don't extend the current session.
Bug investigation derails exploration Tester finds a bug at minute 15, spends the next 45 minutes investigating it; coverage of the charter scope collapses. Bug-investigation time (B in T/B/S) is capped; if a bug deserves > 15 min, capture reproduction steps and defer investigation to a separate session.
Exploring without a heuristic Tester clicks around the feature with no lens; coverage is shallow and biased toward the happy path. Pick a persona or testing tour for every charter — Whittaker's tour catalogue is the cheapest leverage available.
Exploration cut under deadline "We don't have time for exploration this sprint" — the unscripted defects ship. Make exploratory time a release-gate criterion: every release has ≥ 2 chartered sessions covering critical flows; cuts are explicit risk-acceptance decisions.

Worked example — Study App quiz-attempt charter

Charter (Sprint 14, Week 14): "Explore the quiz-attempt flow on a mobile device (iOS Safari 17) with browser DevTools and Charles Proxy, looking for data-loss and visual-glitch bugs on poor network conditions, in 60 minutes. Heuristic: Angry User who keeps losing connection while submitting answers."

Setup (4 min): Provision a test learner account; enable Charles Proxy throttle preset "Edge / 240 kbps / 800 ms RTT"; open iOS Safari simulator; create a 20-question quiz; ensure the question bank is mixed (text, image, multi-select).

Explore (47 min): Started the quiz, answered 3 questions, dropped to "offline" via airplane mode mid-question. Found: Bug 1 — answer to question 3 was lost on reconnect; the autosave indicator showed "saved" but the server had no record. Bug 2 — the connection-lost banner overlapped the answer-submit button on iOS Safari, making the button unclickable. Question to product: should the autosave indicator be optimistic or wait for server confirmation? Continued by answering questions 4–15 with intermittent connection drops; Bug 3 — image-question images failed to load on reconnection but no retry was offered, leaving the question unanswerable. Bug 4 — the "submit final" action fired twice on a double-tap and produced duplicate attempts in the backend.

Capture (6 min): Session report filed; bugs 1–4 logged with steps, severity (Bug 1 = Critical, data loss; Bug 4 = Critical, duplicate side-effect; Bugs 2 & 3 = Major, UX issues); T/B/S = 60% / 30% / 10% (more bug investigation than ideal but justified by the severity).

Debrief (3 min): Shared in the team's #qa channel; Bug 1 and Bug 4 escalated to same-day hotfix triage. Follow-up charter authored: "Explore the autosave + duplicate-submit handling on 10 quizzes with progressively worse network profiles, in 90 minutes" — assigned to backend QA for the next sprint.

Outcome: 4 bugs found, 2 Critical (1 data-loss, 1 duplicate-attempt); both would have escaped if not for this chartered session. The bug-bash before the v2.1 release (week 15) ran 8 charters across 5 testers in parallel; 19 bugs found in total, of which 4 were Critical. Estimated cost of running the bug bash: 6 person-hours; estimated cost-if-missed of the 4 Critical bugs: ~16 engineer-days.

Quality bar

Senior bar

Every sprint includes ≥ 2 chartered exploratory sessions per high-risk flow. SBTM session reports filed for every session; bugs-per-hour and T/B/S ratios tracked on the QA dashboard. Charter library accumulates and is repeated against new releases. Pre-release bug-bashes scheduled before every major release; whole-team participation. Heuristics library curated and grows over time. Exploration time is a release-gate criterion, not the first thing cut under deadline.

Mid bar

Charters authored per sprint with target/tools/risk/duration. Session reports filed in a consistent format. At least one bug bash per major release. Charter library exists and is used.

Junior bar

Can run a 60-minute chartered session given a charter, follow the SBTM session-report format, and produce a structured report with bugs, questions, and time breakdown.
