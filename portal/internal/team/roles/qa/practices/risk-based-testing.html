01 Group A · Test Strategy & Risk

Risk-based Testing — allocate effort by risk

Prioritises test effort by likelihood × impact of failure — the highest-risk areas get the deepest coverage; everything else gets proportionate attention.

TL;DR

Risk-based Testing (RBT) replaces undifferentiated test selection with a deliberate risk matrix: risk = likelihood × impact. Each feature, change, or code area is scored; high-risk items get deep coverage (full EP+BVA+contract+exploratory), medium-risk items get standard coverage (smoke + targeted), low-risk items get smoke only. Formalised by Hans Schaefer (PRISMA, 1990s) and codified by Rex Black in Pragmatic Software Testing, RBT is the canonical method for allocating finite test budget against infinite test possibilities. ISTQB Advanced Test Manager treats it as the foundational strategy practice; ISO/IEC/IEEE 29119-2 names it explicitly. For Study App, the risk register is reviewed each sprint; risk scores drive test-suite tier assignments, exploratory charter priorities, and release-gate criteria.

What it is

Risk-based Testing (RBT) is a test-strategy practice that allocates test effort proportional to risk. Risk is computed as likelihood × impact — how often the failure could occur multiplied by how bad it would be if it did. Each test target (feature, code area, change, scenario) is scored against the matrix; the score maps to a test-effort prescription. The practice was formalised in the late 1990s — Hans Schaefer's PRISMA (Product Risk Management) method, Rex Black's Pragmatic Software Testing, and James Bach's heuristic risk-based approach all converged on the same matrix-driven model.

RBT is the strategic complement to test-design techniques (EP, BVA, decision tables). The techniques say "how to test"; RBT says "what to test first." It is the canonical answer to the universal QA question: "we can't test everything — what should we test?" ISO/IEC/IEEE 29119-2 (Software Testing) names risk-based testing as the foundational test-strategy method; ISTQB Advanced Test Manager dedicates a chapter to it.

Why we need it

Without RBT, test selection defaults to "test everything equally" (impossible at scale) or "test what's familiar" (which misses what's risky). Neither is honest. RBT makes the trade-off explicit: scarce test budget against infinite test possibilities. The risk matrix is the rationing mechanism. High-risk areas get deep coverage even when they aren't fun to test; low-risk areas get smoke coverage even when they are. The discipline is uncomfortable but necessary; it is the difference between strategic testing and reactive testing.

How others do it

Hans Schaefer · PRISMA

Hans Schaefer's PRISMA (Product Risk Management) method, published in the late 1990s and refined through hundreds of consulting engagements, is the canonical structured RBT process. PRISMA defines a workshop format: stakeholders rate each feature on a 1–5 likelihood scale and a 1–5 impact scale; the product (1–25) is the risk score; scores cluster into smoke/standard/deep tiers. PRISMA's contribution is the workshop technique — getting product, engineering, and QA to agree on scores in a 90-minute session — which is the operational hard part of RBT. The PRISMA brochure remains a free reference.

What we take: the risk workshop is the structural mechanism; without a cross-functional meeting that produces scores, RBT becomes QA's opinion alone.

Source: Schaefer — PRISMA Method

Rex Black · Pragmatic Software Testing

Rex Black's Pragmatic Software Testing (2007) and Managing the Testing Process (3rd ed., 2009) are the canonical English-language references on RBT. Black contributes the matrix framing (likelihood × impact = risk), the test-effort mapping (smoke / standard / deep), and the practice of risk-coverage reporting (tested-risks ÷ identified-risks per release). His ISTQB Advanced Test Manager curriculum is the de-facto industry training for risk-based testing.

What we take: report risk-coverage as a release-gate metric — risks identified vs. risks tested — same way coverage is reported.

Source: RBCS — Rex Black

James Bach · heuristic risk testing

James Bach's "heuristic risk-based testing" (Satisfice, late 1990s onward) is the Context-Driven version: instead of formal scores, the team uses heuristics like Bach's SFDPOT (Structure, Function, Data, Platform, Operations, Time) and quality criteria heuristic (Capability, Reliability, Usability, Performance, Installability, Compatibility, Supportability, Testability, Maintainability, Portability, Localizability) to surface risks during exploratory sessions. The heuristic approach trades formality for speed; it fits well with exploratory-charter practices and small teams.

What we take: Bach's heuristic checklists are useful prompts even when running a formal PRISMA workshop — they surface risks the workshop attendees would otherwise miss.

Source: Satisfice — Heuristic Test Strategy

Microsoft · Failure Mode & Effects Analysis (FMEA)

Microsoft's safety-critical and security-critical product teams (Azure, Defender, Windows kernel) use FMEA — a structured analysis adapted from automotive and aerospace safety engineering — to systematically enumerate failure modes per component, score each on Severity × Occurrence × Detection (RPN = S × O × D), and prioritise mitigations by RPN. FMEA is heavier than PRISMA but more rigorous; it's the right tool when the cost of a missed risk is catastrophic (security, payments, identity).

What we take: for high-stakes components (auth, billing, data integrity), supplement risk-matrix RBT with FMEA — the rigour pays off where the cost of escape is highest.

Source: Microsoft — Security Development Lifecycle

Anatomy of an RBT cycle

Risk register

A versioned list of identified risks: ID, description, likelihood, impact, score, mitigation, owner; lives in the QA repo or wiki.

Scoring rubric

Likelihood (1–5) and impact (1–5) definitions with concrete examples; without a rubric the scoring is incoherent across reviewers.

Risk workshop

90-min cross-functional meeting (QA + engineering + product) per sprint or release; produces scored risk items.

Test-effort mapping

Risk score → tier (smoke / standard / deep / deep+exploratory + chaos); explicit, not improvised.

Mitigation per risk

Test mitigation (specific test types), and where applicable, design mitigation (rate limit, idempotency key). Linked to the risk register entry.

Risk-coverage report

Tested risks ÷ identified risks, per release; gaps tied to risk-acceptance decisions with named owners.

How to run RBT

  1. Publish the scoring rubric. 5-point likelihood and impact scales with concrete examples; rubric is reviewed quarterly.
  2. Identify risks per sprint or release. Use SFDPOT or PRISMA-style brainstorm; cover features added, refactored, or touched, plus dependencies and infrastructure changes.
  3. Score in a workshop. 90-min cross-functional meeting; each attendee scores independently first, then reconcile; produces the risk register.
  4. Map score → tier. Predefined map (e.g. 1–6: smoke; 7–12: standard; 13–25: deep+exploratory) ensures consistency. No improvisation.
  5. Plan mitigations. For each risk, the test mitigation (which test layers, which charters); for high-score risks, design mitigation as well (idempotency, rate-limit).
  6. Execute and report coverage. Per-release risk-coverage report; gaps are explicit risk-acceptances with named owners.
  7. Retrospect on escapes. Every escape's RCA names the risk score it would have had if identified; under-scored risks tune the next rubric review.

Deep dive

The conceptual move of RBT is making the rationing visible. Every team rations test effort; the only question is whether the rationing is deliberate (matrix-driven) or accidental (whatever the tester thought of last). Visible rationing produces better outcomes for two reasons: it forces conversation about what matters (a useful exercise), and it produces an auditable trail when something escapes ("we scored this Likely × Moderate and ran standard tests; the actual likelihood was higher than we thought" — a learnable insight). Accidental rationing produces neither.

The scoring rubric is the operational backbone. "Likelihood 3" must mean the same thing to the developer and the QA. Without examples ("Likelihood 5 = the failure mode has occurred in the last quarter"), scores diverge by reviewer; with examples, scores converge. Schaefer's PRISMA workshop builds the rubric collaboratively in the first session; subsequent sessions just apply it. The rubric review (quarterly) tunes the scale based on escape-RCA data: under-scored risks lower the threshold for that risk class; over-scored risks raise it.

Likelihood vs. impact independence is the rubric's structural assumption. Likelihood is about the system: how often does this failure mode actually occur given the code, deploy frequency, traffic? Impact is about the consequence: what does the user, business, brand suffer if it fails? Mixing the two is the most common rubric error — "the payment system is high-risk because it's important" — but importance is impact, not likelihood. The payment system might be Critical impact with Rare likelihood (if heavily tested) or with Frequent likelihood (if new and complex). Separating the dimensions enables matrix decisions.

The cross-functional workshop is the highest-leverage activity. QA alone produces "QA's risk view"; engineering alone produces "what's hard to build"; product alone produces "what users care about." All three must score together. PRISMA's workshop technique — silent scoring first, then reconciliation discussion — surfaces disagreements (which are the most informative parts of the workshop) and reaches consensus. The 90-minute time-box is enforced; pre-circulated risk lists keep the meeting on schedule.

Test-effort mapping must be deterministic, not improvised. A score-to-tier map written in advance ("13–25 → deep + exploratory") removes case-by-case argumentation and produces consistent allocations. Improvisation lets recency bias dominate (whatever was hot last week gets deep tests). The map can be refined over time — adding chaos engineering for the top quadrant, adding mutation testing for the deep tier — but the map exists before the scoring session.

FMEA is the heavy variant for security-critical and safety-critical components. Where PRISMA uses Likelihood × Impact (S × O), FMEA adds a Detection dimension (D): how likely is the failure to be caught before reaching the user? The product S × O × D is the Risk Priority Number (RPN). FMEA's cost is high (it requires walking every component's failure modes individually) but its precision pays back for components like authentication, payments, encryption, and data integrity. The decision criterion: use PRISMA for general features, FMEA for components whose failure can cost the business significantly.

Risk-coverage reporting closes the loop with leadership. The metric — tested risks ÷ identified risks per release — gives a release-gate signal that is more honest than test count or coverage percentage. A release with 100% test pass rate but 60% risk coverage has explicit gaps; the gaps are documented risk-acceptance decisions. Leadership can choose to accept the residual risk or fund the remediation. Without risk-coverage reporting, the trade-off is invisible and the team is blamed for escapes that should have been planned for.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
QA scores alone The workshop never happens; QA writes the scores; engineering and product never agree to them. Cross-functional workshop is mandatory; without it the scores represent QA's opinion alone and lose authority.
No rubric "Likelihood 3" means different things to different scorers; scores diverge by reviewer. Publish the rubric with concrete examples; review quarterly based on escape-RCA data.
Mixing likelihood and impact "The payments system is high-risk because it's important" — importance is impact, not likelihood; the matrix collapses. Keep dimensions independent; impact is about consequence, likelihood is about probability.
Improvised test-effort mapping Each release the team improvises which risks get deep tests; recency bias dominates. Predefined score-to-tier map; same map every release until reviewed quarterly.
No coverage report Risks identified but coverage against them is never measured; gaps invisible. Risk-coverage report per release; gaps are explicit risk-acceptance decisions with owners.

Worked example — Study App release 2.1 risk register

Release 2.1 (week 22) included: new badge-eligibility engine, locale routing refactor, new lesson-rendering pipeline, accessibility-settings page, and a quiz-attempt autosave feature. Risk workshop ran 90 min on Monday with QA lead, 3 engineers, and the product owner.

Risk register (top items):

  • R1 — badge engine incorrect awards: Likelihood 4 (new code, complex rules) × Impact 4 (visible user trust) = 16 → deep + exploratory + property-based testing.
  • R2 — quiz autosave loses answers under poor network: Likelihood 4 (new feature, complex retry) × Impact 5 (data loss) = 20 → deep + exploratory + chaos network-throttle testing.
  • R3 — locale routing serves wrong locale: Likelihood 3 (refactor of stable code) × Impact 4 (poor UX for international users) = 12 → standard + 2 exploratory charters.
  • R4 — lesson-rendering pipeline regression: Likelihood 3 × Impact 4 = 12 → standard + visual regression on top 20 lessons.
  • R5 — accessibility-settings page WCAG violations: Likelihood 2 × Impact 3 = 6 → smoke (axe-core scan, no manual).

Total identified risks: 17 across the release. Risk-coverage report: 17 / 17 tested = 100%. Escape RCA after release: 1 escape (R2-related — a corner-case retry failure on iPad-specific Safari version). The escape's RCA tuned the chaos-test matrix for the next release.

Cost: 90 min workshop + ~2 days of additional deep/exploratory testing (concentrated on R1 and R2). Without RBT, the team would have spread effort evenly across all 5 features; R2's data-loss bug would likely have escaped at higher severity.

Quality bar

Senior bar

Risk register versioned alongside the spec; cross-functional workshop per sprint or release; published rubric with concrete examples; predefined score-to-tier map. Risk-coverage reported per release as a release-gate metric. FMEA applied to security and financial-critical components. Escape RCAs tune the rubric quarterly. The matrix is institutional — every release goes through it.

Mid bar

Risk register exists; risk workshop runs per release; scores map to test-tier; risk coverage reported per release.

Junior bar

Can identify risks for a given feature using SFDPOT prompts, score on a 1–5 likelihood × impact scale, and explain why high-risk areas get deep coverage.
