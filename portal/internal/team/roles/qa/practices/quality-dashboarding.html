Quality Dashboarding — make quality metrics visible to the whole team

A shared, always-current view of a small set of quality metrics — making quality the team's concern rather than QA's afterthought.

TL;DR

A quality dashboard publishes a small, curated set of quality metrics (typically 5–7 tiles) to the whole team, refreshed automatically each release. The canonical set: escape rate (with severity slice), open-defect count by severity, code+contract coverage, mean time to triage, CI pass-rate, flaky-test count, customer-reported-defect rate. Each tile shows current value, target, and 12-week trend. Below-target tiles for two consecutive periods become retro action items. The Toyota andon tradition, Spotify's Squad Health Check, Atlassian's Tech Health Score, and Microsoft's engineering scorecards all instantiate the same idea — quality visibility creates quality behaviour. For Study App, the dashboard lives on the internal portal, owned by the QA lead, and is the spine of the sprint review and the QA retrospective.

What it is

A quality dashboard is a single page that displays a small, curated set of quality metrics for a team or service, refreshed automatically each release. Typical content: 5–7 metric tiles, each showing current value, target, and trend (12-week rolling). The dashboard is public to the whole team — engineering, product, design, support — not only QA. It is the operational instrument of the "metric visibility" principle that runs through modern QA practice (Modern Testing Principles, Crispin & Gregory, Spotify Squad Health).

The standard tile set: escape rate (with severity slice), open-defect count by severity, code+contract coverage, mean time to triage, CI pass-rate, flaky-test count, customer-reported-defect rate. Other candidates depending on team focus: time-in-staging, deploy-frequency, MTTR, accessibility-violation count. The discipline is to keep the set small (Atlassian's research: dashboards with 5–7 tiles get read; dashboards with 15+ tiles get ignored).

Why we need it

Quality metrics that only QA sees produce quality decisions that only QA owns. Once metrics are visible to the team, developers spot rising defect counts on their own features, PMs see the cost of feature velocity, and leadership sees the trend that funding decisions affect. Visibility creates accountability; the dashboard is the structural mechanism. Without it, the QA team carries the cognitive load of quality alone; with it, quality becomes a shared concern with concrete data behind it.

How others do it

Toyota · andon & visual management

The Toyota Production System's andon board makes quality status visible to the whole factory floor — any worker can stop the line and the andon cord registers the issue on shared displays. Toyota's underlying claim: visibility creates immediate response; hidden problems persist. The principle migrated to software via Lean Software Development (Mary & Tom Poppendieck) and Kanban (David Anderson) — every modern quality dashboard is a software-translated andon board.

What we take: the dashboard's purpose is shared visibility, not data archaeology — design for the room glance, not the deep dive.

Source: Lean Enterprise Institute — Andon

Spotify · Squad Health Check

Spotify's Squad Health Check Model (Henrik Kniberg, 2014) is a per-squad dashboard covering 10 health dimensions (one of which is "quality of code/product"). Each dimension is rated red/amber/green by the squad itself and the rating is the input to engineering investment decisions. The dashboard is reviewed monthly. Spotify publishes the model openly; many companies have adopted variants. The model demonstrates that a small, narrative-driven dashboard can drive significant cultural change.

What we take: include a self-rated tile (the team's own subjective quality score) alongside automated metrics — the gap between subjective and objective tells a story automation can't.

Source: Spotify — Squad Health Check Model

Atlassian · Tech Health Score

Atlassian's internal Tech Health Score (described in Building Atlassian and engineering blog posts) combines test coverage, escape rate, deploy frequency, MTTR, and security-vulnerability count into a per-service composite score. The score is computed nightly, visible in the engineering Backstage portal, and ties to platform investment decisions: services below threshold get prioritised. Atlassian published data showing services that improved their Tech Health Score also reduced their incident rate by ~40% — the dashboard moved both perception and reality.

What we take: a per-service score (not just team-level) makes investment decisions concrete; the dashboard becomes the engineering-investment input.

Source: Atlassian Engineering Blog

DORA & Accelerate

The DORA (DevOps Research and Assessment) team, led by Nicole Forsgren, Jez Humble, and Gene Kim, identified four key metrics that distinguish elite from low-performing software organisations: deploy frequency, lead time, MTTR, and change-failure rate. Accelerate (2018) documents the empirical research. The DORA four-key metrics are now the de-facto standard tiles on engineering health dashboards across the industry — and "change failure rate" is essentially the escape-rate metric reframed for the deploy pipeline.

What we take: the DORA four-key metrics complement the QA tile set — together they capture engineering throughput and quality, which the team should track together.

Source: DORA — State of DevOps Research

Anatomy of the dashboard

Tile set (≤ 7)

Escape rate, open defects (severity slice), coverage, mean triage time, CI pass-rate, flaky-test count, customer-reported rate. Stop adding tiles at 7.

Per-tile content

Current value (large), target (small), 12-week sparkline, trend arrow (↑↓→), drill-down link. The room-glance is the design constraint.

Refresh cadence

Per release (weekly or sprint); automated pipeline; no manual updates (manual = stale by Tuesday).

Owner

QA lead owns the dashboard; per-tile owners for the data source; visible on the page header.

Below-target rule

Two consecutive periods below target → action item at next retro; one bad period is noise.

Drill-down paths

Each tile links to the underlying data (Jira filter, CI run history, coverage report) so engineers can investigate.

How to build the dashboard

  1. Pick the metric set deliberately. Start with the canonical QA five (escape, defects, coverage, triage, CI); add at most two team-specific tiles (e.g., accessibility violations).
  2. Define each metric precisely. Formula, source, refresh cadence, target, owner. Published next to the dashboard so the numbers are interpretable.
  3. Automate the pipeline. Metabase / Looker / Backstage / Grafana — whichever the org uses. Manual dashboards drift and are abandoned within a quarter.
  4. Set targets with the team. Targets are negotiated, not imposed. Coverage 80% is a typical starting point; escape rate ≤ 10%; CI pass-rate ≥ 90%. Adjust quarterly.
  5. Publish on the internal portal. The dashboard is on the team page, linked from the sprint review agenda, opened during the QA retro.
  6. Wire trend arrows + below-target rule. 12-week sparklines, trend arrows; two consecutive below-target periods triggers a retro action; visible on the tile.
  7. Review quarterly. Are the tiles driving the right behaviour? Are any tiles ignored? Adjust the set; treat as evolution, not stasis.

Deep dive

The conceptual foundation of quality dashboarding is Toyota's genchi genbutsu (go and see) and andon (visual management): make problems visible immediately so they can be addressed immediately. The translation to software is the same — when quality data lives only in tracker queries and CI logs, only QA sees it; when it lives on a shared dashboard, the team sees it. Visibility creates response. The hardest part is not the technology (every modern dashboard tool is adequate) but the discipline of curating the set and keeping it small.

Tile-set minimalism is the operational rule. Atlassian's empirical research (cited in Tech Health Score docs): dashboards with 5–7 tiles are read regularly; 8–10 tiles are read sometimes; 15+ tiles are read by no one. The instinct is to add tiles (capture more data); the discipline is to remove them (capture more attention). Every additional tile dilutes the others. The canonical QA set (5 tiles) is the floor; the team-specific set (≤ 7) is the ceiling.

The trend arrow is the most-overlooked design element. A current value answers "where are we now?"; a target answers "where should we be?"; a trend answers "are we getting better or worse?" The trend is the most actionable of the three. A team with 12% escape rate trending down is healthier than a team with 8% trending up; the dashboard must surface the trend, not just the snapshot. 12-week rolling sparklines are the standard format — long enough to smooth noise, short enough to react to recent changes.

Targets must be negotiated with the team, not imposed. An imposed target produces gaming (developers write tests-for-coverage rather than tests-for-correctness). A negotiated target — "what coverage level represents acceptable risk for this code?" — produces engagement. The negotiation is the cultural artefact; the target is the by-product. Spotify's Squad Health Check explicitly delegates target-setting to the squad; Atlassian's Tech Health Score uses platform-set thresholds with per-service exemptions. Both work; the constant is the conversation.

Drill-down paths convert the dashboard from "score" to "investigation tool." Every tile links to its source data — clicking the escape-rate tile opens the Jira filter for escapes this quarter; clicking the flaky-test tile opens the CI flaky-test report. Without drill-down, the dashboard is a report; with drill-down, it is the entry point to root-cause analysis. The standard tools (Backstage, Grafana, Looker) all support drill-down links natively.

The two-consecutive-periods rule is the trigger mechanism. A single bad sprint is noise (someone took PTO, a flaky test landed, a major incident consumed everyone). Two consecutive sprints below target is signal — something is structurally off and worth investigating. The rule prevents both over-reaction (one bad week = panic) and under-reaction (six months of drift = denial). The standard practice: below-target for two consecutive periods → retro action item; below-target for four consecutive periods → escalate to engineering leadership.

Composite scores (Atlassian's Tech Health, Spotify's Health Check overall) collapse multiple tiles into a single number for executive consumption. The benefit: leadership can see "service X is at 72/100, service Y is at 41/100" and prioritise. The cost: composite scores hide which dimension is failing, so engineers must look at the underlying tiles to act. The pragmatic recipe: publish both — the composite for executive summary, the tiles for engineering action. Pure composites without underlying tiles are reductive; pure tiles without composites overwhelm leadership.

Lifecycle

1

Select metrics

QA lead + tech lead + product owner agree on the tile set; ≤ 7; targets negotiated with the team.

2

Wire the pipeline

Automated data extraction; refresh per release; metric definitions published next to the dashboard.

3

Publish

Internal portal page; linked from sprint review agenda; opened during QA retro; visible to the whole team.

4

Operate

Trend monitored each release; two-consecutive-periods rule triggers retro action; drill-down used for investigation.

5

Tune

Quarterly: review which tiles drive action, which are ignored; adjust set and targets; treat as evolution.

6

Retire / refactor

Tiles that no longer surface anything actionable are retired; new failure modes get new tiles. Dashboard evolves with the team.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Too many tiles "Let's add a tile for X" — every quarter the dashboard grows; at 15+ tiles no one reads it. Cap at 7 tiles; new tile in = old tile out; quarterly tile audit retires ignored tiles.
Manually-updated dashboard QA lead updates the dashboard every Friday — until they're on PTO or the priority slips; by Tuesday it's stale and no one trusts it. Fully automated pipeline; manual = stale; if a metric can't be automated, don't put it on the dashboard.
Vanity metrics "Total tests run" or "lines of test code" — easy to count, doesn't predict quality outcomes. Pick metrics that predict outcomes (escape rate, coverage, CI pass-rate); drop counting-for-counting's-sake.
No targets, no trends Tiles show numbers but no context — "coverage is 78%" — is that good? Every tile has a target and a 12-week trend; the three together (current/target/trend) are the minimum viable tile.
Targets imposed, not negotiated Leadership sets coverage = 90%; team gamesthe metric by writing trivial tests; coverage rises but defect rate doesn't change. Targets negotiated with the team; revisited quarterly; gaming is a signal the target is wrong, not a moral failure.
Dashboard read only by QA Published on a QA-only wiki page; engineers and PMs never see it; visibility does not exist. Dashboard on the team page, linked from sprint review, opened at retros; treat visibility as a structural requirement.

Worked example — Study App quality dashboard, week 24

The Study App quality dashboard lives at /internal/team/roles/qa/dashboard.html and is linked from the team home page. It contains 7 tiles, refreshed weekly from the Jira + GitHub Actions + Codecov pipelines.

  • Escape rate: current 9%, target ≤ 10%, trend ↓ (was 22% at week 4); 12-week sparkline shows steady decline. Status: green.
  • Open defects: current 14 (P0: 0, P1: 2, P2: 5, P3: 7), target P0+P1 ≤ 5, trend →. Status: green.
  • Coverage: current 87%, target ≥ 80%, trend ↑ (was 65% at week 4); patch coverage on PRs ≥ 90%. Status: green.
  • Mean triage time: current 0.7 days, target ≤ 1 day, trend ↓. Status: green.
  • CI pass-rate: current 94%, target ≥ 90%, trend ↑. Status: green.
  • Flaky tests: current 4, target ≤ 5, trend ↓ (was 11 at week 8). Status: green.
  • Customer-reported defects: current 3/wk, target ≤ 5/wk, trend →. Status: green.

The dashboard's owner is the QA lead; per-tile data sources are documented in dashboard-readme.md. The two-consecutive-periods rule has triggered 3 retro action items in 24 weeks: stabilise test_locale_redirect flake (completed week 12), reduce mean triage time below 1 day (completed week 14), add scheduled-job idempotency test layer (completed week 16). Each action was tracked back to the dashboard tile that surfaced it.

Cultural impact: the dashboard is opened at every sprint review (5-min slot) and every QA retro (20-min escape walk-through anchored in the dashboard). PRs that drop coverage now get flagged in the PR review (developers reference the dashboard target). Engineering manager cites the dashboard in quarterly reviews. Subjective signal: developers ask "where's the dashboard?" when the link is missing from a meeting — the dashboard has become institutional.

Quality bar

Senior bar

Dashboard with 5–7 tiles, fully automated pipeline, refreshed per release. Each tile has current/target/trend + drill-down. Two-consecutive-periods rule triggers retro action items; targets negotiated with the team and revisited quarterly. Dashboard visible to the whole team — sprint reviews, retros, team page. Composite score for executive summary (optional). Cultural sign-off: the team asks about the dashboard when missing.

Mid bar

Dashboard with 5–7 tiles refreshed automatically; targets and 12-week trends; visible to the team; reviewed in QA retros.

Junior bar

Can build a 3-tile dashboard with current value and target from existing data sources (Jira, Codecov, GitHub Actions) and document the metric definitions.
