Quality Advocacy — make quality the team's culture, not only QA's job

Actively builds the team's quality culture by teaching test techniques, sharing metrics, and making the cost of defects visible.

TL;DR

Quality advocacy is the practice of making quality everyone's responsibility — not a gate at the end of the sprint. Concrete actions: QA shares the quality dashboard in sprint reviews so metrics are visible to all; QA teaches developers one test technique per quarter (BVA, EP, exploratory charters, contract testing) so they write better unit tests; QA makes the cost of escaped defects visible at retros ("this escape cost 3 engineer-days plus 2 hours of user support"); QA pairs with developers on test design for high-risk features. When quality is seen only as a QA-lane activity, developers write tests to meet a coverage number. Advocacy changes that dynamic. The Google "Tester is a coach" model, ThoughtWorks' Build Quality In, Microsoft's quality-engineering rebrand, and the Modern Testing Principles (Bach & Bolton; Crispin & Gregory) all converge on advocacy as the senior QA role.

What it is

Quality advocacy is the practice of QA acting as a coach for the team's quality capability rather than as the team's last-line quality inspector. The advocate's tools are conversation, teaching, demonstration, and metric visibility — not test scripts. The output is a team where developers write better tests because they understand the techniques; product managers prioritise quality work because they see the cost; designers consider edge cases because three-amigos sessions surface them.

The practice has roots in the Lean "quality at the source" movement (Toyota Production System) and the Agile/XP "whole-team responsibility for quality" principle. Modern formalisations include the Modern Testing Principles (Alan Page & Brent Jensen, 2015 — "Our priority is improving the business" not "we test"), Crispin & Gregory's Agile Testing series (the "Agile Testing Quadrants" and "Whole Team Approach"), and Google's "Tester is a coach" framing in Software Engineering at Google. The unifying claim is that QA's leverage is highest as a force multiplier, not as a substitute for engineering quality work.

Why we need it

When QA is the only group responsible for quality, two failure modes appear. First, developers offload quality concerns: "QA will catch it." This produces shallow unit tests and a high escape rate at integration boundaries. Second, QA becomes a bottleneck: every release waits for the QA queue. Quality advocacy breaks both failure modes by making quality a distributed capability. Developers who understand BVA, EP, and contract testing write tests that catch their own bugs; product managers who see escape-rate trends prioritise tech-debt work; designers who join three-amigos sessions design fewer edge cases.

How others do it

Google · "Tester is a coach"

Software Engineering at Google (Winters, Manshreck, Wright, 2020) documents Google's testing culture: dedicated test engineers are rare and act as coaches, not gatekeepers. Every Software Engineer (SWE) is expected to write tests for their own code; the Test Engineer (TE) role is force-multiplication — building test frameworks, training, and reviewing test strategies for high-risk areas. Test certification (a curriculum that teaches BVA, EP, contract testing) is required for new SWEs. The culture's underlying claim: "quality is a shared engineering responsibility," and TE leverage comes from spreading capability, not from independent verification.

What we take: the highest-leverage QA activity is teaching the engineering team — one trained developer prevents 10 future defects more efficiently than one QA finds 10 escaped defects.

Source: Software Engineering at Google — Testing Overview

Modern Testing Principles (Page & Jensen, 2015)

Alan Page (long-time Microsoft test engineer, co-author of How We Test Software at Microsoft) and Brent Jensen formulated the seven Modern Testing Principles. Principle 1: "Our priority is improving the business." Principle 4: "We are passionate about helping product teams deliver products faster, with higher quality." The principles reframe QA from "we test" to "we improve the team's testing capability." Page's AB Testing podcast and the principles themselves are the most-cited articulation of the modern QA-as-coach mindset.

What we take: the QA mission statement matters; "we improve the business" produces different work than "we test the product."

Source: Modern Testing Principles

Crispin & Gregory · Agile Testing

Lisa Crispin and Janet Gregory's Agile Testing (2009), More Agile Testing (2014), and Agile Testing Condensed (2019) are the foundational texts on the whole-team approach to quality. They contribute the Agile Testing Quadrants (a map of test types by purpose and audience) and the "whole-team responsibility for quality" principle. Their consulting practice has shaped the industry's understanding of QA as a coaching/facilitating role rather than a gatekeeping one. The texts are required reading for anyone moving from traditional QA to an Agile/DevOps environment.

What we take: the Agile Testing Quadrants give the team a shared vocabulary for "what kind of testing" — making test investment decisions explicit and team-wide.

Source: Crispin & Gregory — Books

ThoughtWorks · Build Quality In

ThoughtWorks' consulting practice (Steve Smith's Build Quality In, 2017) documents quality-coaching engagements at Fortune 500 clients: a small QA presence is leveraged across many engineering teams via test-strategy reviews, technique workshops, and metric visibility. The data: clients that adopted the coaching model halved their escape rate within 12 months while reducing QA headcount as a fraction of engineering — quality went up while the gatekeeper layer shrank. The model is now the recommended structure in ThoughtWorks' enterprise-transformation playbook.

What we take: the QA-as-coach model scales to many teams with few QA; the leverage is in the teaching and metric visibility, not in the gates.

Source: ThoughtWorks Insights

Anatomy of quality advocacy

Mission statement

"We improve the team's quality capability" — visible on the QA charter, repeated at on-boarding and retros.

Metric visibility

Escape rate, coverage, flakiness, and severity-distribution are visible to the whole team — in sprint reviews, on shared dashboards, in retrospectives.

Teaching cadence

One test-technique workshop per quarter (BVA, EP, exploratory charters, contract testing); 60–90 minutes; engineering attendance is the bar.

Pair-on-tests

QA pairs with the developer on the test design for high-risk PRs (auth, billing, identity); the SWE writes the test, QA reviews the design.

Cost-of-defect surfacing

For every escape, the retro names the cost in engineer-days, customer-support hours, and where applicable, dollar impact; cost becomes a shared concern, not a QA-only worry.

Three-amigos sessions

PM + developer + QA at design time, before code is written; QA contributes edge-case scenarios; the conversation prevents defects rather than catches them.

How to run quality advocacy

  1. Publish the QA mission. "We improve the team's quality capability." One sentence, visible on the QA charter, repeated at every retro and onboarding.
  2. Make metrics visible. Quality dashboard accessible to all; escape rate, coverage, flakiness reviewed in sprint reviews — not buried in QA-only meetings.
  3. Schedule one test-technique workshop per quarter. Pick a high-leverage technique (BVA, EP, contract testing, exploratory charters); 60–90 min; hands-on with the team's actual code.
  4. Pair on tests for high-risk PRs. When a PR touches auth, billing, identity, or data integrity, QA joins the developer for test-design review; the SWE writes the tests, QA reviews the strategy.
  5. Surface cost-of-defect at every retro. For each escape: engineer-days lost, support hours consumed, customer impact. The cost becomes a shared metric, not a QA worry.
  6. Run three-amigos for new features. PM + developer + QA at the feature kickoff; QA contributes edge cases; the conversation upgrades the spec.
  7. Measure cultural shift. Track: % of unit tests covering BVA boundaries on changed files (rising = developers internalised it); % of design docs that include a "testability" section; engineering attendance at QA workshops. Lagging indicators of culture change.

Deep dive

The conceptual claim of quality advocacy is a leverage argument: one trained developer prevents many future defects; one QA-found defect prevents only itself. If QA can teach a developer to write a BVA test, that SWE writes BVA tests on every feature for the rest of their career — a compounding return. If QA finds an off-by-one bug in this sprint, the developer might or might not internalise the lesson. Compounding teaching outperforms one-shot finding over any time horizon longer than a few months. The argument is not "stop testing"; it is "the highest-leverage testing is teaching."

The gatekeeper model is a structural antipattern. When QA is the gate, the developer's incentive is to ship the minimum that gets through QA — not to ship quality code. Quality becomes adversarial: developer vs. QA. When QA is a coach, the developer's incentive is to ship code their pair-reviewer (also-QA) would be proud of — collaborative, not adversarial. The structural change is small (move QA from "after dev" to "with dev") but the cultural shift is large. Google, ThoughtWorks, and the entire Lean-Agile tradition document the same finding: shifting QA from gate to coach reduces escape rate and improves developer satisfaction simultaneously.

Metric visibility is the operational backbone. Without shared dashboards, only QA sees the quality picture; the rest of the team operates on intuition. With shared dashboards, every team member sees the escape-rate trend, coverage drift, and flakiness rate — and they make decisions accordingly. Spotify's Squad Health Check, Atlassian's Tech Health Score, and ThoughtWorks' Build Health all instrument this principle. The hard part is selecting metrics that drive the right behaviour: coverage-only drives test-padding; escape-rate-only drives recency bias; the right set is the composite that Crispin & Gregory's quadrants make visible.

Teaching is the advocacy lever most often skipped because it doesn't feel like "real testing work." A 90-minute BVA workshop where 8 developers each write a BVA test for their next feature produces ~40 BVA tests over the next month — far more than QA could write alone. The investment is one QA-day; the return is dozens of developer-days of better tests. Workshops should be hands-on (work on the team's actual code, not toy examples), specific (one technique per workshop, not "testing in general"), and recurring (one per quarter is the rhythm).

Cost-of-defect surfacing is the bridge between technical quality and business priority. Engineers reflexively think of defects as "bugs to fix"; surfacing the cost reframes them as "incidents that cost N days and Y customer trust." The retro is the standard surface; the calculation is simple — engineer-days × engineering loaded cost + support-hours × support cost + customer-loss-estimate where applicable. Once leadership sees that a typical escape costs 5 engineer-days, they fund prevention; without the number, prevention loses to features in every prioritisation discussion.

Three-amigos sessions (PM, developer, QA at design time) are the highest-leverage prevention activity. The session asks one question: "what could go wrong with this feature?" QA contributes edge cases (locale, network conditions, malformed input, race conditions); the developer integrates them into the design; the PM updates the acceptance criteria. The defect is prevented in the spec, not caught later in code. Gojko Adzic's Specification by Example and Lyssa Adkins' agile-coaching literature both document three-amigos as a 3–5x return on time invested. For Study App, three-amigos is the default for new features and the optional-but-encouraged practice for refactors.

Pair-on-tests for high-risk PRs is the operational form of "QA reviews the test design." For auth, billing, identity, and data-integrity PRs, QA joins the developer at the design stage and helps design the test strategy: which boundaries, which contracts, which exploratory charters. The SWE writes the tests; QA reviews the strategy. The arrangement is the opposite of the gate model — QA helps make the developer's tests good, rather than re-testing after. The outcome is a stronger test suite and a developer who learns from each pairing.

4

5

Test-quality leading indicators: BVA coverage in unit tests, "testability" sections in design docs, engineering attendance at workshops.

6

Iterate quarterly

Quarterly retro reviews advocacy outcomes; adjust workshop topics, pairing scope, metric visibility based on what moved.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
QA stays a gatekeeper while claiming to be a coach Charter says "we improve capability" but every PR still routes through QA approval; the structure doesn't match the words. Restructure: developers approve tests, QA pairs on design; gates only for cross-cutting concerns (security, contract).
Workshops without engineering attendance QA team holds a BVA workshop; no developers come; the practice doesn't spread. Engineering manager makes attendance the bar; tie the workshop to a current sprint's work so it's directly relevant.
Metrics hidden from the team Quality dashboard exists only on the QA wiki; rest of the team operates on intuition. Dashboard is public on the team page; metrics reviewed in sprint reviews; cost-of-defect named at retros.
Cost of defects never surfaced Escapes happen, get fixed, move on; the org-wide cost is never quantified; prevention never gets prioritised. Every escape retro names cost in engineer-days + support hours; leadership sees the number; prevention becomes a budget item.
QA evangelism mistaken for advocacy QA talks about quality but doesn't do the structural work (workshops, pairing, three-amigos); becomes performative. Advocacy is concrete actions: published workshop, paired PRs, attended design meetings; not slogans.

Worked example — Study App QA advocacy program, year 1

Quarter 1: QA mission published in the team charter: "We improve the team's quality capability." Quality dashboard moved from the QA wiki to the main team dashboard. Escape rate, coverage, and flakiness reviewed in the weekly sprint review (5 min slot). Initial baseline: 22% escape rate, 65% coverage, 18% flakiness.

Quarter 2: First workshop — Boundary Value Analysis, hands-on with the team's quiz-scoring code. 5 of 6 developers attended; 4 contributed BVA tests to their next PRs. Pair-on-tests started for auth and payment PRs. Cost-of-defect surfaced at retros: April retro highlighted the 5-day cost of the cron-job race-condition escape; PM agreed to fund the scheduled-job test layer (next quarter's investment).

Quarter 3: Workshops: Equivalence Partitioning (Q2), Contract Testing with Schemathesis (Q3). Coverage rose to 78%, flakiness to 9%, escape rate to 12%. Three-amigos sessions launched for new features (Lessons V2, Recommendations); the QA edge-case contributions caught 4 design gaps before implementation. Pair-on-tests covered ~30% of PRs.

Quarter 4: Coverage 87%, flakiness 4%, escape rate 9%. Developers writing BVA tests by default on new features (visible in PR reviews — no QA prompting). Engineering attendance at workshops at 100%. Cost-of-defect data: cumulative escapes prevented ≈ 14 (per RCA, escapes that would have happened without the new layers), engineer-days saved ≈ 60. QA headcount unchanged at 3; engineering teams shipped 35% more features YoY thanks to lower rework.

Year 1 outcome: escape rate 22% → 9%, coverage 65% → 87%, developer quality-test capability up significantly (measured via PR review observations). The advocacy investment (≈ 15% of QA time on teaching, pairing, three-amigos) returned ≈ 60 engineer-days plus a cultural change that compounds in year 2+.

Quality bar

Senior bar

QA mission is "coach the team's quality capability"; metrics are team-public, in sprint reviews, in retros. One test-technique workshop per quarter with full engineering attendance. Three-amigos for every new feature; pair-on-tests for every high-risk PR. Cost-of-defect quantified at every retro; prevention is a budget line. Cultural-shift leading indicators (BVA coverage, "testability" sections, workshop attendance) tracked and rising.

Mid bar

QA dashboard visible to the team; metrics in sprint review; quarterly workshop; three-amigos for high-risk features; cost-of-defect mentioned at retros.

Junior bar

Can prepare and present one test-technique workshop on a familiar topic (BVA, EP, exploratory) to engineers in 60–90 minutes; can run a three-amigos session given an agenda.
