16 Group D · Defect & Metrics

Defect Triage — severity × priority classification on every bug

Separates how bad the defect is (severity) from how urgently it must be fixed (priority) — and the rest of triage is what flows from that distinction.

TL;DR

Defect triage assigns every bug two independent dimensions: severity (how bad the failure is — blocker, critical, major, minor, trivial) and priority (how urgently it must be fixed — immediate, high, medium, low). High severity does not imply high priority and vice versa: a blocker in a deprecated feature can be low priority; a cosmetic glitch on the sign-up page can be high priority for brand reasons. Triage also assigns owner, target release, and decision (fix-now / fix-later / won't-fix / duplicate / not-a-bug). The standard practice is a daily 30-minute triage meeting; every new defect is classified within 24 hours of filing, blockers are assigned immediately. Google, Microsoft, Mozilla, and Atlassian all run severity-priority triage with documented rubrics; conflating the two dimensions is the most common failure mode.

What it is

Defect triage is the recurring activity of classifying, prioritising, and assigning newly reported defects so engineering effort is directed where it matters. The triage outcome for every defect is a tuple: severity (the technical badness of the failure), priority (the business urgency of fixing it), owner (the engineer or team accountable), target (release or milestone), and decision (fix-now, fix-later, won't-fix, duplicate, not-a-bug, need-more-info).

The standard cadence is a daily 30-minute meeting attended by QA, engineering tech lead, and product owner. Every new defect filed in the last 24 hours is reviewed; existing high-severity defects without progress are re-triaged. The output is recorded in the tracker (Jira, Linear, GitHub Issues) on the defect itself — never in meeting minutes that drift.

Why we need it

Without triage, defects accumulate untriaged and engineering picks what to work on by visibility or convenience. High-severity defects in invisible features sit while a noisy cosmetic bug is fixed first. Triage makes the prioritisation explicit and reviewable: the team commits to a treatment plan within 24 hours of filing, so the backlog is signal, not noise. The cost of triage (30 minutes daily, two or three people) is dwarfed by the cost of a P0 defect that sat for a week because no one classified it.

How others do it

Google · severity definitions in the bug tracker

Google's bug-tracker (Buganizer / internal) ships with strict severity definitions and priority rubrics: S0 = product unusable for all users, S1 = product unusable for a significant subset, S2 = major feature broken, S3 = minor feature broken, S4 = cosmetic. Priority P0–P4 maps to SLAs: P0 has a 1-hour response SLA, P1 a 24-hour SLA, etc. Severity is set by QA/reporter (technical), priority is set by triage (business). The Buganizer schema enforces the two-dimension split.

What we take: codify the severity and priority rubrics in the tracker schema, not in a wiki page; the tracker enforces the language and the language enforces the practice.

Source: Software Engineering at Google — Larger Testing

Microsoft · daily triage with stakeholders

Microsoft's "ship room" or "triage board" tradition has been documented in Debugging the Development Process (Maguire, 1994) and Showstopper! (Zachary, 1994): daily 30-minute meeting, QA + engineering lead + program manager present, every new bug reviewed, every untriaged bug from prior days re-reviewed. The decisions are recorded in the tracker and broadcast to the team daily. Microsoft credits this practice with the ability to ship complex products like Office and Windows on schedule.

What we take: daily cadence beats weekly — once a backlog of untriaged defects forms, the meeting becomes a marathon and decisions become reactive.

Source: Microsoft — Work Item Management

Mozilla · public triage queue

Mozilla's Firefox triage is public: anyone can file a bug, the per-component triage queue is visible at bugzilla.mozilla.org, and decisions are visible with rationale. Mozilla's "Triage Workflow" docs describe a per-component triage owner who reviews the queue weekly (daily for high-traffic components) and assigns severity/priority + a "triage decision" tag. The transparency invests trust in the process — external contributors can see and challenge classifications.

What we take: publishing triage decisions (even internally) holds the triage team accountable and makes patterns visible (e.g. "we systematically downgrade UI bugs").

Source: Mozilla — BMO Triage

Atlassian · escalation policy + SLA

Atlassian's "Jira best practices" docs describe their triage rubric: every priority level has a documented SLA (P1 = 4h response, 24h resolution; P2 = 1d response, 1w resolution; P3 = 1w response, 1mo resolution). SLA breaches escalate automatically to the engineering manager. Customer-impact severity feeds priority directly via a published mapping table, removing case-by-case argumentation.

What we take: tie priority levels to SLAs; the SLA is the operational meaning of the priority and makes "P1" a contract not a label.

Source: Atlassian — Incident Management

Anatomy of a triage decision

Severity

Blocker / Critical / Major / Minor / Trivial — set by QA from the report; describes what is broken independent of business context.

Priority

P0 / P1 / P2 / P3 — set by triage from severity + business context; describes how urgently the team must respond.

Owner

Engineer or team accountable for the fix; never "unassigned" past triage. A team-level owner is acceptable; the team's lead routes internally.

Target

Release, sprint, or milestone the fix lands in; explicit (not "soon"). Aligned with the priority SLA.

Decision

Fix-now / fix-later / won't-fix / duplicate / not-a-bug / need-more-info. Recorded as a status on the ticket, not in chat.

Rationale

A one-line note on the decision (especially for won't-fix and downgrades); future you will need to defend it during a postmortem.

How to run a triage

  1. Define the rubrics first. Severity and priority levels live in a short doc; every level has a one-line definition and two examples. Without rubrics, triage devolves into arguments about labels.
  2. Schedule a daily 30-minute slot. QA lead, engineering tech lead, product owner; calendar block, agenda is the untriaged queue. Daily beats weekly; weekly beats nothing.
  3. Walk the untriaged queue. Open each defect in turn; reporter or QA summarises in 30 seconds; the room sets severity, priority, owner, target, decision.
  4. Record on the ticket. Not in chat, not in meeting minutes. Severity and priority are dropdown fields; the decision is the status; the rationale goes in a comment. The ticket is the truth.
  5. Re-triage stale defects. Any high-severity defect without progress for more than its SLA window is re-opened in the meeting — even if it has an owner.
  6. Track SLA breaches. A weekly dashboard shows P0/P1 defects past SLA; breaches escalate to the engineering manager automatically.
  7. Review the rubrics quarterly. As the product matures, what counts as "Critical" shifts — review the rubric definitions and recalibrate.

Deep dive

The core conceptual move is the severity/priority split. Severity is technical: a button that doesn't work for 5% of users is Critical regardless of who those users are. Priority is contextual: that same button might be P0 if those 5% are enterprise customers paying $500/mo, or P3 if they are on a deprecated browser the product is dropping support for next quarter. Conflating the dimensions causes one of two failure modes: either every important defect is labelled "Critical" (severity inflation) or every nasty bug is labelled "Low" because no one important reported it (priority deflation). Keeping the dimensions separate keeps the labels honest.

Severity rubrics need precise definitions and worked examples. "Blocker = product is unusable for all users in normal conditions" is precise; "Blocker = a really bad bug" is not. Each rubric level should have at least two real examples from the project's history. Without examples, the rubric is theory and triage becomes argument. Atlassian, Google, and Mozilla all publish their rubrics with examples; the act of writing them is itself a useful exercise because it forces the team to commit to definitions before the heat of triage.

Priority rubrics need SLAs. P0 = response within 1 hour, fix within 24 hours; P1 = response within 4 hours, fix within 1 week; P2 = response within 1 day, fix within 1 month; P3 = backlog, no SLA. The SLA is the operational meaning of the priority; without it, "P0" is decorative. Atlassian's product-management discipline ties every priority level to an SLA contract and tracks breach rates as an engineering-health metric. Tying priority to SLA also forces the discussion: "we can't have 50 P0s a week" because the team cannot honour the SLA on 50 P0s.

Daily versus weekly cadence is non-negotiable. With daily triage, the queue is at most yesterday's intake; the meeting is 30 minutes; decisions are timely. With weekly triage, the queue is 7 days of intake (often 30+ defects); the meeting is 2 hours; decisions slip toward "fix later" because the room is exhausted. Microsoft, Atlassian, and Google all converged on daily cadence for active products. The exception is steady-state maintenance (a stable mature product), where weekly can work because intake is low.

The four-axis triage decision — severity, priority, owner, decision — is the minimum. Some teams add labels (regression, customer-reported, security, accessibility) to enable slicing. Others add a "found-in-version" field for trend analysis. The label set should be small enough that triage doesn't grind on labelling; the right test is "would I slice the data by this field in the dashboard?" If not, drop the field.

Won't-fix decisions deserve special attention. A bug closed as won't-fix is a public statement of policy: "we know this is broken; we choose not to fix it." The rationale must be recorded — "edge case affecting 2 users on EoL browser, mitigation is the upgrade prompt." Without rationale, future-you (or your replacement) will reopen the bug and the team will re-litigate. Mozilla, Google, and Atlassian all require a rationale comment on won't-fix decisions.

Escape-rate feedback closes the loop. Defects that reach production despite triage are "escapes"; per-month escape rate is the triage team's accuracy metric. High escape rate suggests rubrics are too lenient (severity systematically under-set) or triage is missing categories. Combining escape-rate analysis with the triage meeting (one slot per week on "escapes from last month") tunes rubrics over time. Without this feedback loop, rubrics drift and triage becomes performative.

Lifecycle

1

Filed

Reporter (QA, dev, support, customer) creates a defect; severity set by reporter using the rubric; state = untriaged.

2

Triaged

Within 24h: severity confirmed, priority set, owner assigned, target chosen, decision recorded. State → fix-now / fix-later / won't-fix / duplicate / not-a-bug.

3

In progress

Owner picks up the fix; status updates land on the ticket; SLA timer runs.

4

Fix verified

QA verifies the fix in the target environment; regression test added; ticket → resolved.

5

Released & closed

Fix ships in target release; ticket → closed with the release version recorded.

6

Retrospective

SLA breaches and escapes are reviewed monthly; rubrics tuned; recurring root-cause patterns feed shift-left investments.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Conflating severity and priority Reporter sets both to "high" because the bug felt important; the dimensions stop being independent. Schema enforces the split: severity is a technical dropdown (blocker–trivial), priority is a business dropdown (P0–P3). Triage sets priority, not the reporter.
Severity inflation Everyone marks their bug "Critical" because they want attention; the label loses signal. Publish severity rubrics with examples; triage downgrades over-set severities and explains why; track severity-distribution as a metric.
Weekly triage with daily intake "Once a week is enough" — but Monday's queue is 30 defects deep and triage becomes a 2-hour marathon. Daily 30-min slot for active products; weekly only for steady-state maintenance.
Decisions in chat, not on ticket "We discussed it in Slack" — the decision lives in a thread no one can find next month. Every triage decision (severity, priority, owner, target, rationale) lands on the ticket; the ticket is the truth.
No SLA, no escalation P0 means "important" but nothing happens if it sits for a week; the priority is decorative. Each priority has a response and resolution SLA; breaches escalate automatically; SLA breach rate is an engineering metric.
Won't-fix without rationale Bug closed as won't-fix with no comment; six months later the same bug is filed again. Every won't-fix has a rationale comment; future filings of the same bug get linked and dispositioned with reference to the original rationale.

Worked example — Study App week-12 triage

In Study App week 12, the daily triage handled three illustrative defects:

  • QA-412 — "Submit button unresponsive on quiz page (Safari iOS 17)". Severity = Critical (core flow broken for a significant subset). Triage discovered iOS Safari accounts for 18% of mobile traffic; priority = P0; owner = frontend team; target = same-day hotfix; SLA = 24h. Hotfix shipped same-day.
  • QA-417 — "Footer link to Terms of Service has wrong colour contrast (#9aa vs #fff)". Severity = Minor (cosmetic + WCAG AA failure). Priority debate: contrast failures matter for compliance; the brand cares about legal posture. Triage set priority = P1 (legal compliance), target = next release, owner = design system team. SLA = 1 week. Fix shipped 4 days later.
  • QA-419 — "Quiz score 100 displays as 'You scored 100' but spec says 'perfect score'". Severity = Trivial (cosmetic copy mismatch). Triage discussed: this affects ~0.3% of attempts (perfect scores are rare), brand reviewed the copy and confirmed the new wording is acceptable. Decision = won't-fix, rationale = "spec updated to match the implementation rather than vice versa; copy was changed in product review meeting on 2026-05-22." Spec PR linked.

Daily triage runs from 10:00 to 10:30. Severity rubric (1 page) and priority rubric (with SLAs) are pinned in the QA handbook. Severity-distribution dashboard shows current quarter: 4% Blocker, 11% Critical, 38% Major, 35% Minor, 12% Trivial — flat trend, no severity inflation detected.

SLA breach rate (last 12 weeks): P0 0%, P1 6%, P2 14%, P3 not tracked. Two P1 breaches in week 9 (Lighthouse-CI deflake work) escalated to engineering manager; root cause was infrastructure not capacity; remediation closed in week 10. Escape rate (defects reaching production after the release where a regression test should have caught them): 2 escapes in 12 weeks, both downgraded from P1 to P2 in triage; root-cause analysis fed the priority rubric tuning.

Quality bar

Senior bar

Severity and priority rubrics are published with worked examples and reviewed quarterly. Triage is daily, 30 minutes, with QA + engineering + product. Every priority has a documented SLA; breach rate is tracked and escalates automatically. Won't-fix decisions always carry a rationale. Severity-distribution and escape-rate dashboards inform rubric tuning. Triage decisions are recorded on tickets, not chat.

Mid bar

Severity and priority dimensions are separated in the tracker schema. Daily or weekly triage with documented rubric. Each priority has a target SLA, even if not auto-escalated. Decisions recorded on tickets.

Junior bar

Can classify a defect into severity using the rubric, distinguish it from priority, and write a triage-ready report (steps, expected, actual, severity).
