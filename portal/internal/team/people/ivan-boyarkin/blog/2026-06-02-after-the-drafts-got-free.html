After the drafts got free.

In the IT world, the documentation layer is being commoditised by generative AI faster than anyone predicted. The most relevant transition for a System Analyst (SA) in the era of the new SDLC is to become a Project Manager (PM) on steroids. Or it can be more technical switch - toward architecture and system design. But there are some nuances here that I'd like to reveal.

Generative models — the current generation, not the imagined next generation — do a large chunk of the systems-analyst job credibly. Сredibly enough that the documentation layer of the SA position — drafting user stories, transcribing stakeholder interviews, sketching first-pass process maps, dictating an OpenAPI endpoint design — has stopped being a viable career identity. The work itself still has to happen. But owning it as your distinctive contribution stopped being interesting at some point in 2025. And the rest of the market is now realizing what that means.

The implication is not «the systems analyst disappears». No serious source in the literature claims that, and the consensus across PwC, McKinsey, Gartner, IIBA and the academic requirements-engineering community is the opposite — the role splits. It splits into two surfaces that the documentation layer used to mask, because writing the document was the visible output and the two surfaces hid behind it. One surface faces upward — toward product intent, stakeholder politics, AI product surfaces, evaluation loops. The other surface faces downward — toward architecture, non-functional forces, contracts, integration, threat models. The documentation layer was the bridge between them. Take the bridge away — the way generative AI is taking it away — and you can see the two surfaces clearly for the first time.

What generative AI actually does in this work

Let's start with small audit of the empirical shape of the tooling. By my opinion the real picture is more nuanced than either «AI replaces analysts» or «AI is hype». It is task-specific. Some tasks the models do at a level that makes hiring a junior analyst to do them look strange. Other tasks they do badly enough that putting the output into a production specification would be a small disaster. The honest grading sits across eight task families.

Legend

A

Strong. Current models produce output that is comparable to a competent junior, sometimes better. Human role collapses from author to reviewer.

B

Mixed. Useful as a first draft, but the draft systematically misses on a known dimension. Human role is to repair that dimension, not to rewrite.

C

Weak. Models produce surface-plausible output that fails on substance. Human author still does the work. AI is at best a typing accelerator.

1 — User-story drafting · A

The task. Take a paragraph of intent — «we want to let teachers reuse questions across courses» — and produce a set of well-formed user stories, in the canonical «as a / I want / so that» shape, covering the obvious branches.

A

Strong

The 2025 study by Bombarda et al. compared LLM-generated user stories against human-authored stories on coverage and stylistic quality and found them comparable. My own use confirms it — for breadth and form, the draft is essentially free. The analyst's contribution collapses to selection, prioritisation, and the rationale that AI does not produce well (see below).

What blocks it now. Diversity and creativity sit one rung below human authors. Models default to the canonical «as a [role], I want [thing], so that [benefit]» template and reach for the most-common-in-corpus phrasing of intent. The stories you get are correct, well-formed, and slightly bland. Persona-specific voice — the difference between «as a teacher» and «as the teacher running a hybrid cohort with mid-semester transfers» — is the part that thins.

When this changes. Largely already resolved at A-level. The remaining diversity gap closes on the input side, not the model side: feed the prompt a persona library and a RAG handle over the team's previous stories and the output snaps to the team's voice. Tech is available today; closing the gap is a tooling decision, not a model-generation upgrade. The corollary is that you do not need to wait for GPT-5 to see this; you need to wire up your retrieval.

Detail. Empirically, GPT-4o-class models match a competent junior analyst on INVEST-style form and the SBES 2024 follow-up reports a ~70% drop in analyst review time when the LLM produces the first draft. The practical implication is sharp: hiring an analyst whose distinctive skill is «writes good user stories» stopped being a viable hiring story in 2026. The story-author role is now a story-editor role, and the editor bills at a higher rate because the value is in selection, not production.

2 — Acceptance criteria · B

The task. Turn each story into testable acceptance criteria — preconditions, behaviour, postconditions, edge cases.

B

Mixed

The same study found that LLM-generated stories meet acceptance quality criteria less often than human-authored ones, regardless of model scale. The shape is right. The substance is shallow. The model misses domain edges it has never seen — concurrent-edit cases in collaborative editing, rate-limit interactions with batch endpoints, the long tail of «what happens when X is empty / null / partially valid». Analyst role: repair the substance, do not rewrite the shape.

What blocks it now. Acceptance-quality miss rate is constant across model sizes in the Bombarda study — scaling parameters does not move it. The gap is not «the model is small»; the gap is «the model has never been bitten by your systems». Generic acceptance criteria are easy. The criteria that matter — race condition on transaction retries, currency rounding at the third decimal, partial-state recovery after a half-applied migration — are the ones written after the incident. Those incidents are not in the training corpus.

When this changes. Moves to A when a team wires up its incident log, test history and bug tracker as RAG context for the analyst's prompt. Realistic horizon: 12–24 months for orgs with disciplined incident hygiene (postmortems closed, tests labelled with the bug they were born from); longer or never for orgs whose incident knowledge lives in tribal memory. The blocker is org data plumbing, not model capability.

Detail. The gap widens in safety-critical and regulated domains — medical, finance, identity — where the «weird» criteria are the load-bearing ones. In those domains the model is at low B; in CRUD-feature domains it is at high B and close to A with prompt engineering. Worth knowing for hiring decisions: an analyst whose comparative advantage is «catches the criterion the model misses» is more valuable in regulated domains, full stop.

3 — Process maps and as-is / to-be flows · B

B

Mixed

First-pass BPMN-ish narratives and PlantUML sequences come out clean. Where models break is on the actual failure modes inside a real org — the manual workaround the finance team has been doing for two years, the «we never use that endpoint because of an incident in 2024». Models do not know the organisational unwritten rules. Analyst role: layer the lived flow over the modelled flow.

What blocks it now. Tacit organisational knowledge. The model produces the canonical happy-path flow but does not know which steps are de facto skipped, which manual workarounds the team has built, which approvals are theatre and which are real. The result is a process map that reads cleanly and is wrong in the way that matters — the «shadow path» that 30% of the team actually takes is invisible.

When this changes. Improves on a 12–36 month horizon as orgs feed AI access to Slack archives, ticket history, change logs and postmortems. The technology is ready today — long-context models handle the input volume. The blocker is data plumbing and access control. McKinsey's 2026 «State of Organizations» explicitly names «organisational tacit knowledge integration» as the next-12-months frontier for AI in process-discovery work. Mature orgs (incident hygiene + open Slack archives + maintained changelogs) get to A first.

Detail. A pragmatic mitigation today: pair the model output with one senior who knows where the bodies are buried. The review costs roughly 30 minutes on a flow that would have taken the analyst 4 hours unaided. Net win is sevenfold, even before any RAG plumbing. This is the cheapest win-pattern in the bunch and the one I lean on most.

4 — Natural-language to formal spec · A

A

Strong

This is the cleanest win. Translating an ambiguous English requirement into an OpenAPI envelope, a JSON Schema, an SQL DDL, a Protobuf message, a state machine — models are reliably good at this, especially with chain-of-thought prompting or retrieval over the rest of the codebase. The IEEE Software «From Specifications to Prompts» piece argues the same. Analyst role: pick the envelope, supply the constraints; the formal text is free.

What blocks it now. Nothing fundamental at the model level. The remaining failure mode is prompt-side constraint completeness — whatever you forgot to specify, the model fills with a defensible default that does not survive review (loose nullability, missing pagination, error schemas that do not match the rest of the surface). The bug is not in the model; the bug is in the brief.

When this changes. Already at A; widens to near-perfect over the next 12 months as structured-output modes (constrained decoding, JSON-Schema-guaranteed output, OpenAPI validators wired in-loop) become the default. Newer envelopes (AsyncAPI 3.0, gRPC streaming contracts, GraphQL federation schemas) follow within months of training-corpus coverage. The ceiling here is essentially «the contract is as good as the specifier» — the typing has been free for a year already.

Detail. IEEE Software 2024 calls this «the cleanest single win for GenAI in requirements engineering» and I agree. With chain-of-thought + repo-RAG, GPT-4o-class models produce review-ready OpenAPI 3.1 envelopes with realistic examples, error schemas and security clauses. The practical consequence is the strongest single argument in this whole post: the cost of writing a specification is collapsing to near zero. The cost of deciding the specification — which envelope, which versioning policy, which error contract, which side of a CAP trade-off — stays human. That gap is the role.

5 — Stakeholder-interview summarisation · A

A

Strong

Transcribe-and-summarise on a sixty-minute interview, with thematic clustering, contradiction flagging, action-item extraction — fully credible at current model scale. IIBA's 2026 «AI Wednesdays» series documents this as the single most common AI-assist BAs are actually using. Analyst role: run the interview, ask the questions only a human can ask, hand the rest to the tool.

What blocks it now. Three failure modes show up reliably. Over-clustering — the model merges nuanced positions into a single bullet, smoothing out the contradictions that are precisely what you want to surface. Action-item hallucination — at ~5% rate across published evals, items appear that nobody actually committed to. Recency / primacy bias — the opening and closing minutes of the transcript get over-weighted relative to the middle. None of these are fatal; all of them are predictable.

When this changes. Largely solved already with three moves: role-aware prompting («distinguish Product-PM voice from Engineering-Lead voice; flag contradictions; mark low-confidence claims»), 1M-context models that eliminate the «lost in the middle» failure on hour-long transcripts, and confidence flags on extracted action items. By mid-2026 the standard workflow is Whisper-3-class transcription + a role-aware summariser + a verification pass that the analyst signs off in five minutes.

Detail. The IIBA 2026 trend report ranks transcribe-and-summarise as the most-used BA assist by a 4× margin over the next category. The role shift is sharp: the analyst's leverage in interviews moves from the note-taking to the question-asking. Question quality becomes the entire contribution. A good interviewer with a mediocre summariser beats a great summariser with no questions; the reverse is not true.

6 — Decision rationale and trade-offs · C

C

Weak

Asking a model to write the «why we chose X over Y» section gives you a paragraph that is fluent, plausible, and almost always generic. The trade-off naming is shallow because the actual trade-off lives in costs the model has never paid — vendor lock-in your CFO has a scar from, deployment latency your SRE has been allergic to since the 2024 rollback, the political weight of «the data team owns it» versus «the platform team owns it». Analyst role: still the author.

What blocks it now. Three layered problems that compound. Cost asymmetry — the model has never paid the costs it is supposed to weigh, so its weights are uniform and its trade-off prose reads as a textbook. Local history — the failed-migration scar, the team's allergy to a specific vendor, the unwritten ownership boundary that decides which team carries the on-call — none of this is in training corpus, even with RAG. Accountability — writing «we chose X over Y» is not a writing act; it is a commitment with a name on it. The model cannot carry a name.

When this changes. Realistically, this plateaus. With persistent organisational memory plus incident RAG plus codified ADR history the model can rise to «competent summariser of trade-offs the analyst named». It does not rise to «author of the call». The accountability part stays human under any model regime I can foresee. Useful planning horizon: C → C+ over 3–5 years; B is not realistic before 2030, and arguably not at all. This is the dimension where seniority shows.

Detail. McKinsey, IIBA and Salesforce converge on this in 2026, using almost the same vocabulary — «accountability remains non-delegable», «professional judgement essential», «human-led, AI-powered». Salesforce's «Architectural Decisions» piece is the cleanest framing: AI excels at exploring the decision space; humans excel at closing it. A senior analyst names trade-offs the model never proposed and the junior never recognised. That gap is the role's longest moat.

7 — Cross-stakeholder ambiguity resolution · C

C

Weak

«Product wants free-text search, legal wants no PII in indices, platform wants sub-200ms p95, finance wants the bill flat.» Resolving that is not a writing task. It is a negotiation across people who do not share a frame, conducted under unwritten organisational pressure, with consequences that get assigned to a name on a calendar. McKinsey and Gartner converge on the same word — irreducibly human. Models can map options; they cannot carry the ownership.

What blocks it now. The work is not production of text; it is convergence of people. The model can enumerate the four stakeholders' positions and produce a «possible compromise» paragraph. What it cannot do is read the room when Legal softens after the third meeting because their Director has a quiet incentive to close this quarter, or notice that Product's «free-text search» actually means «search-as-you-type with autocomplete» when you press them. The carrying — owning the negotiation and the consequences in front of named people — is the work.

When this changes. Not in the foreseeable horizon at the carrying level. Multimodal models that read tone, body language, escalation cues will improve the mapping of stakeholder dynamics. They will not improve the owning of the call. The accountability part — that the outcome is assigned to your name on the calendar — stays human under any regime I can foresee. This is the role's deepest moat and the surest long-term leverage.

Detail. Every analyst-survival paper from 2024–2026 converges on stakeholder disambiguation as the work AI cannot absorb. Gartner's 2026 «autonomous business» forecast carves out «high-stakes, trust-dependent consumer moments» as where human talent remains central; cross-stakeholder disambiguation is the internal analogue of those moments. The practical consequence is unambiguous: time spent in rooms with humans is now the highest-leverage time on an analyst's calendar. The work AI cannot do is also the work that compounds in reputation.

8 — Architectural design under named forces · C

C

Weak

Sketching an architecture under specific, named constraints — «p99 under 300ms at 20× growth, RPO five minutes, three regions, no cross-region writes, regulatory residency in two of three regions» — is where current models produce textbook patterns and miss the actual binding constraint. The binding constraint is the one that does not generalise. Salesforce's «human-led, AI-powered» framing is precise here: the architect provides the forces, the model executes within them; you cannot delegate the forces.

What blocks it now. Models are generalisation-shaped; binding constraints are project-specific. Ask for an architecture and you get a textbook pattern that is internally coherent and wrong because it does not know that your write QPS is bounded by a specific Kafka cluster you do not want to scale yet, or that your compliance officer reads the EU residency rule strictly while the US one tolerates a soft interpretation. The forces — the actual ones — are organisation-shaped, not pattern-shaped.

When this changes. Slow uplift over the next 2–3 years as agentic SDLC + RAG over architecture history + structured ADR corpora let the model carry more context. Expect C → B by 2027–2028, consistent with the Gartner agentic timeline. Strong upper bound at named-force level: the forces will always be project-specific, and naming them stays a human act. The model can execute under the forces faster than a human can; it cannot take the forces from you.

Detail. PwC's 2026 Agentic SDLC report names architecture-under-constraint as «the human ceiling above the agent layer» — the layer below it (implementation, refactoring, even spec drafting) is compressed by agents; the layer above it (force naming, trade-off binding) is not. The practical consequence for Path B is positive: agentic systems will accelerate the exploration of architectural alternatives — five candidate designs in fifteen minutes instead of two designs in three days — but the binding of the decision stays with the analyst-architect. Path B leverage strengthens over time, not weakens.

The irreducible core

Read the grades together and a pattern shows up that I did not see before grading them. The A-grade tasks share a property — they are tasks where the inputs are present and the outputs have a known shape. The C-grade tasks share a different property — they are tasks where the inputs are unstable, the shape of the output is contested, and the consequences are owned by a name. The B-grade tasks are in the middle: shape is known, inputs partially unstable.

That is the principle that survives every framing of the AI-in-SDLC conversation. McKinsey calls it «accountability that remains non-delegable». IIBA calls it «professional judgement». Salesforce calls it «human-led architecture». They are pointing at the same thing. The work that cannot be done by a model alone is the work where someone has to carry the decision — under named pressure, with consequences that come due, in front of people who will remember who made the call.

Naming this matters because it tells you where the role is going to be valuable on a five-year horizon. Not in the typing. Not in the drafting. In the carrying. And the carrying is asymmetric — it leans either toward the product surface (whose call is «what should this product be?») or toward the architecture surface (whose call is «what shape can hold these forces?»). Almost no senior carries both at once. That is the bifurcation.

The bifurcation

Two roles, not one. The empirical evidence in the labour-market data backs this up — ResumeWorded's 2026 skill-overlap maps put Product Owner against Business System Analyst at 71% shared skills, and Solution Architect against System Analyst at 64%. Both transitions are short walks. They are short walks in opposite directions.

Path A — the product analyst on steroids

The bet. The analyst evolves toward product. The work becomes: figure out what should exist, validate it against real users, design AI-augmented product surfaces (RAG, agents, eval loops, content pipelines), shape the backlog, run the stakeholder negotiation. The documentation layer collapses into a tool — the analyst orchestrates AI to produce the artefacts but does not author them by hand. Output volume per analyst goes up by an order of magnitude. The bottleneck moves to judgment about what to build.

What gets added. Product-sense skills. Evaluation literacy for AI-powered features (how do you measure a RAG pipeline's quality, what is the harness, what regressions are you blind to). Comfort with experimentation. Stakeholder orchestration at a level where you are not the scribe but the person who runs the room. Familiarity with the product surfaces that AI itself enables — agents, copilots, content workflows.

What gets dropped. The hand-authored BRD. The transcription. The «I wrote 84 user stories last sprint» metric. The mental model where the analyst is the typist for the product manager's intent.

Who this path fits. People whose comparative advantage is read on humans, not systems. People who finish a stakeholder meeting with a sharper sense of what is actually being asked for than anyone else in the room. People who want to be in the conversation about what to build rather than the conversation about how to build it. The 2026 Medium essays by Bandaru and others land this label as «AI product owner»; IIBA's trend report calls it «AI-augmented strategist». Same role, different vocabulary.

Path B — the system designer

The bet. The analyst evolves toward architecture. The work becomes: name the forces a system is operating under, design contracts that survive scale and change, carry the non-functional decisions (capacity, security, observability, integration), own the decision records, hold the design honest against the operational reality. The documentation layer collapses into a tool here too — but the analyst's contribution is not faster documents, it is better designed systems that the documents describe.

What gets added. Architecture-decision discipline (ADRs that name constraint, alternatives, accepted cost). NFR engineering — latency budgets, capacity projections, threat modelling with named adversary tiers. Contract design across services with versioning and deprecation policies. Integration patterns. The kind of trade-off muscle that PwC's 2026 Agentic SDLC report describes as «the human ceiling above the agent layer».

What gets dropped. The pretence that the analyst is a neutral translator between business and engineering. On this path the analyst owns engineering forces and is accountable for them. The output is not a specification handed off — it is a design that has been argued for and survives review.

Who this path fits. People whose comparative advantage is read on systems, not humans. People who finish an architecture review with a sharper sense of the binding constraint than anyone else in the room. People who want to be in the conversation about how to build it at the level where the design choices are still load-bearing — before the sprint, not during it. The literature calls this trajectory the «solution architect with BA roots», and the industry data shows it is the higher-paying of the two paths today by a noticeable margin (the AI-skilled-architect premium that Robert Half tracks sits at 25–50% over comparable non-architect roles).

The squeezed middle

The middle road — «the analyst who writes good specs» — is where the squeeze is. It is squeezed from above (AI drafts the specs cheaper), from below (AI translates the specs into formal artefacts cheaper), and from the side (the demand for hand-written specs is falling because nobody reads them end-to-end anymore). PwC's 2026 «Agentic SDLC» report puts the time saving on the documentation-spec loop at 50%+ when AI is in the loop, and the trajectory of that number is not toward zero. It is toward the documentation loop being a low-cost background process owned by whoever runs the tooling.

I do not think the middle disappears in 2026. I do think it becomes economically uncompetitive as a career identity by 2028. The Gartner prediction that autonomous business is a net-positive job creator by 2028–29 reads, on close reading, as a forecast about this exact shift: the role surface goes up and the role surface goes down, but the role surface in the middle thins.

A rubric — which path is yours

The question is not which path you find more attractive. Both look attractive in PowerPoint. The question is where your leverage is — the place where you do better work than the next person at the same hours of effort, and where AI does not do that work cheaper than you. Six questions I have been asking myself.

1. Where do you finish a meeting sharper than the room? Stakeholder meeting, you are Path A. Architecture review, you are Path B. If neither, the leverage is still latent and the next year's worth of work should be about discovering it.

2. What do you do better when the stakeholder is in the room? Path A leverage is highest under live human pressure — reading the unsaid, naming the contradiction, holding the negotiation. Path B leverage is highest when the stakeholder is not in the room and you are alone with the constraints.

3. When you read a system, where do your questions go first? «Who asked for this and what were they really after?» — Path A. «What happens at 10× the current load and how will we know?» — Path B.

4. What do you take notes on? Path A people take notes on people and motives. Path B people take notes on constraints and trade-offs. Look at your own notebook from the last quarter. The pattern is unambiguous.

5. Where does your reading habit pull you? Product books, behavioural-economics essays, growth case studies — Path A. Distributed-systems papers, capacity-planning posts, incident-review transcripts — Path B. Your reading list is a compass, even when you have not noticed it pointing.

6. What does AI do worse than you, and would still do worse than you after another generation of improvement? That is the long-term leverage question. If your answer involves reading rooms, carrying political weight, owning «what to build» decisions — Path A. If it involves carrying the binding constraint, naming the design force, owning «what shape can hold» decisions — Path B.

Where I sit on the rubric

I have been writing this portal for a few months and the answers have become unambiguous. The work I find compounds — the work that is still worth doing on the third pass — is on the architectural surface. The reference layer, ADR discipline, error-contract governance, observability hooks, capacity framing, threat modelling, IA scaling. The work where I am relieved when AI does the typing is the documentation layer around all of that. The work where I am uncomfortable when AI does the typing is decision-rationale and trade-off naming, and the discomfort is the diagnostic. That is where my leverage lives. Path B.

The reason this matters for me specifically — and the reason I am writing it down rather than just deciding it quietly — is that the study plan I am operating on through May 2026 was already pointed this direction, before I had named the bifurcation. The Senior Systems Analyst track I am training against, on close reading, is mostly a Path-B track with a Path-A onboarding layer. The architectural foundations section (handbook/), the ADR series, the testing strategy, the error-contract governance, the IA work — these are the artefacts of a Path-B trajectory. Naming the path makes the trajectory legible, and legibility is what I have been chasing for nine months.

What might break this thesis

The honest part. Three ways this map could turn out wrong.

One. The agent layer eats further up the stack than I have priced in. The current grade-C tasks (decision rationale, ambiguity resolution, architecture under named forces) are where I am betting human leverage stays. If models with persistent memory, better calibration, and access to the organisation's own history close that gap inside three years, the bifurcation flattens out and both paths get sucked higher. I do not believe this, but I cannot rule it out, and the probability is not zero. The hedge is that both paths are bets on judgment under accountability, and judgment under accountability is the last thing models touch — so even a partial hedge holds.

Two. «Product analyst on steroids» turns out to not be a separate role. It may be that what I am calling Path A is just «the new product manager», absorbed into the PM job family rather than evolving from the analyst job family. If so, the analyst-to-PM transition becomes the formal career path, and the analyst role itself dissolves into Path B over time. The labour-market data is ambiguous on this — the 71% skill overlap cuts both ways. This does not change my conclusion (which is to train toward Path B), but it does change the conclusion for analysts whose leverage is on the product surface.

Three. The bifurcation is a phase, not a stable configuration. It may be that 2026 is the awkward middle of a longer transition in which the role temporarily splits and then re-converges into a single «AI-native analyst» job family. Possible. I think the more likely shape is the one the data suggests — a stable bifurcation in which both paths remain distinct because the human leverage on each is different — but the alternative is on the table.

Closing

If I had to compress this post into one sentence: the documentation layer of the systems-analyst role is being commoditised, and the right response is not to defend it but to pick a side and train for the role that sits underneath it. The role above is product. The role below is architecture. The role that was documentation is being absorbed into the tooling. Pick the side where your leverage is real and where AI does not do it cheaper than you. Train for the carrying, not the typing. The typing has already gotten free.
