Designing DX. My experience.

Several months of building a documentation portal for the ETR Study App API — what I thought the work was, what it actually turned out to be, and a rubric for what mature documentation includes at each level.

I have been building a documentation portal for the ETR Study App API for several months. The premise was supposed to be modest. I had a backend service that deserved a credible front door, I knew Markdown well enough, and I had read enough portals to think I knew what good ones do. That was the premise. It turned out to be wrong in a specific way, and the wrongness is what this post is about.

The shorter version: documentation is not a visual problem with an editorial postscript. It is closer to the opposite. The visible portal — typography, navigation, search, breadcrumbs — is the easy half. The hard half is the substance the portal ships. The architectural narrative. The trade-offs decisions actually paid. The runbooks that have been used. The paths that route different readers to different first pages. I spent months on the first half before I noticed the second half existed.

What follows is the rubric I arrived at after that detour. Ten dimensions, each one a question a mature portal answers and most portals do not. For each dimension I describe what three levels of maturity — A, B, C — actually include. The rubric exists to keep the bar visible. It is descriptive, not aspirational; every level is something portals in the field actually look like.

The vocabulary

Three levels per dimension. The same three levels mean the same thing across all ten:

A

Mature. The artefact exists, is complete, and has been pressure-tested by reality — incidents, audits, onboarding, scale.

B

In progress. The artefact exists in the right shape, but parts of its substance are still partial; the bones are honest, the meat is patchy.

C

Absent or aspirational. The artefact is missing, templated-but-empty, or so sparse that a senior reader cannot tell whether the work behind it has been done.

Ten dimensions of mature documentation

The dimensions below are not invented. They are read off the portals experienced readers describe as useful rather than polished. Each is a question. Each has a discoverable answer in a mature portal.

1 — Architectural narrative

The question. Can the reader reconstruct why the system is shaped this way and not some other way from a single page? Does the portal carry a story arc that connects decisions to the constraints they were taken under?

A

Mature

A single top-level narrative page tells the story of how the system came to its current shape, in the order the constraints arrived. Per-domain narratives extend the arc into each major module. Every meaningful decision is tied to the constraint that forced it.

B

In progress

Top-level architectural narrative exists and reads cleanly, but per-domain stories are partial or missing. Constraints are often implicit — the reader infers the «why» rather than being told.

C

Absent

Decisions are scattered across ADRs and READMEs with no connecting story. The reader has to assemble the arc themselves from twenty disjoint pages — and so usually does not.

2 — Trade-offs exposure

The question. Are rejected alternatives visible on the page? Are their costs honestly named? Does the reader see the path that was not taken and the reason it wasn't — or only the conclusion?

A

Mature

Every decision page names the alternatives that were considered, attaches an honest cost to each (latency, complexity, vendor lock-in, ops burden), and accepts the specific loss the chosen path carries. A reader six months later can revisit the decision without re-running the analysis.

B

In progress

Alternatives are named, but more as gestures than as honest cost analyses. The losses of the chosen path are softened or omitted. Reader can guess at the trades, not verify them.

C

Absent

Decisions presented as conclusions. Alternatives invisible. The decision page reads as «we chose X», not as «we chose X over Y because Z, and accepted cost W».

3 — Diátaxis discipline

The question. Are the four content quadrants — tutorial, how-to, reference, explanation — actually separated? Does every page belong to one of them and not drift into the others? Are cross-quadrant loops closed (every tutorial cites the relevant references; every reference cites the relevant how-tos)?

A

Mature

Every page declares its quadrant explicitly. Per-quadrant templates exist and are used. Cross-quadrant loops are closed — references point to the how-tos that use them, tutorials cite the explanations they assume. Drift between quadrants is caught by review.

B

In progress

The team knows the four quadrants and most pages belong to one of them, but the assignment is implicit. Templates exist but are not always followed. A few pages mix quadrants — reference pages that contain how-to steps, tutorials that drift into explanation.

C

Absent

Pages mix quadrants without acknowledgement. No template discipline. The reader cannot tell whether they are reading a tutorial they should follow, a how-to they should reach for under pressure, or an explanation they should understand.

4 — Operational maturity

The question. Are there real runbooks, real postmortems, and real burn-rate history on the SLOs? Or are runbooks aspirational templates and SLOs aspirational targets that no incident has yet tested?

A

Mature

Runbooks have been used during real incidents and refined afterward. A library of closed postmortems exists and is reachable from the runbook pages they were forged in. SLO dashboards show burn-rate history, not just targets. The reader can tell, in a glance, that the team has been on call.

B

In progress

Runbooks exist in the right shape — preconditions, actions, recovery, escalation — and some have been touched in practice. A handful of postmortems are started. SLOs are defined; burn-rate dashboards exist but the history is thin.

C

Absent

Runbooks are aspirational templates. Zero closed postmortems on file. SLOs are targets without measurement history. A senior reader cannot tell whether the team has ever taken a page, and assumes not.

5 — Contracts as first-class

The question. Are API specs complete in their full envelope — endpoints, error contract, observability hooks, test plan? Are data models explicit? Where multiple services interact, is the cross-service contract carried on the page?

A

Mature

Every endpoint carries the full envelope: request/response schema, error contract, observability hooks (which log events, which metrics, which alerts fire), and a test-plan section (unit, integration, contract, E2E). Data models are explicit. Cross-service contracts have their own page, with versioning and deprecation policy.

B

In progress

The OpenAPI envelope is in place and the data model is documented, but a chunk of endpoints have empty observability and test-plan sections. Cross-service contracts implied but not consolidated.

C

Absent

An endpoint list, maybe a Swagger dump, no error contract, no observability hooks, no test plan. Data models implicit in the code. Contracts between services live in tribal knowledge.

6 — Quality of decision records

The question. Do ADRs and RFCs read as decisions made under named pressure — or as conclusions presented after the fact? Can a reader revisit a decision six months later without re-running the analysis?

A

Mature

Every ADR opens with the constraint that forced the decision and the alternatives considered, with honest costs attached to each. Consequences are traced over time — the «six months later» follow-up is on the page. Each ADR is dated, owned, and reachable from the architecture it shaped.

B

In progress

ADRs are written and dated, the structure is canonical, but alternative sections are thin and consequences not always traced. The frame is solid; the centre needs honesty.

C

Absent

ADRs present in shape but read as conclusions. Constraints absent. Alternatives missing. Consequences unrecorded. A reader cannot tell why this and not that.

7 — Performance · capacity · security as design forces

The question. Are non-functional concerns visible on the design pages — not as a sidebar but as forces that shaped the design? Capacity projections, security defaults, threat models?

A

Mature

Non-functional concerns are forces, not appendices. Capacity plans exist — «at what scale does the current schema break, and what is the next step». A threat model with named adversary tiers (script-kiddie, motivated insider, nation-state-equivalent) shapes the security pages. SLOs cite specific drills.

B

In progress

SLO numbers exist (latency, error budget). Security defaults are captured in ADRs and runbooks. What is missing is a capacity plan and a named threat model — the posture lives in defaults, not in analysis.

C

Absent

Non-functional concerns appear as a checklist of «we use TLS, we have rate limits», disconnected from how the system was actually designed. No capacity projections. No threat model.

8 — Tooling-as-documentation

The question. Does the tooling document itself? Is the Make-target surface self-describing? Can someone discover the workflow without reading external docs? Does the same tooling catch documentation drift?

A

Mature

make help is the entry point and every target is self-describing. Workflows are discoverable through tooling, not READMEs. A docs-check-style gate runs on every PR and refuses merges with stale or autogen-drifted pages. Once written, the tooling stays correct because drift is caught by the same tooling.

B

In progress

Some self-documentation. Most workflows reachable through Make. A few scripts still require reading source to understand what they do. Drift-catching gate exists but is partial.

C

Absent

Tooling is opaque. Workflows live in tribal knowledge or in scattered READMEs. Documentation drift is caught — if at all — by reviewers, not by CI.

9 — IA scalability

The question. Does the information architecture survive at ten times its current size? Is there a per-service spine that won't need re-organising when a second service arrives? Are taxonomies explicit and bounded?

A

Mature

IA scales to 10× current size without reorganisation. Per-service spine factored against common patterns. Taxonomies (page-type, ownership, lifecycle) explicit and bounded. New services and new domains slot in without touching the top level.

B

In progress

IA holds at current scale and is explicitly known to need reorganisation at the next scale step. The team has a plan for the reorganisation and the trigger that fires it.

C

Absent

Ad-hoc IA. Taxonomies unbounded. Every new service adds noise to the top level. Reorganisation happens reactively, under pressure.

10 — Cold-start productivity

The question. Can a new senior reader become productive in one day reading only the portal? Is there an explicit curated path — «read these N pages, in this order, with this time budget» — for at least the central personas?

A

Mature

Explicit curated paths per persona: senior architect doing cold-start due diligence, SWE shipping a first endpoint, SRE triaging an SLO breach, newcomer doing their first week. Each path is N pages in a defined order with a time budget and a one-line «what you'll be able to do after».

B

In progress

Onboarding paths exist for some personas — usually the newcomer. Senior-architect or oncall paths are missing. A new senior reader can find their way, but the portal does not budget the journey for them.

C

Absent

No curated paths. Reader is dropped on the homepage and has to build their own route through the portal. The single most common silent failure mode: the senior reader closes the tab.

The page that made the bar visible

The dimensions above did not arrive as a list. They arrived one at a time, over months, each in the same form: a question I could not have answered the day before, and could not stop seeing the day after. The intervention that turned them from a scattered set of corrections into a working rubric was building one section of the portal: handbook/.

That section is, in effect, the authoring contract for the whole portal. Principles. Substance contract. Style guide. Page skeleton. Authoring procedures. Templates per quadrant. A meta-layer — documentation about how to write the documentation. Writing it forced me to name, in one place, the dimensions I had been correcting myself against without ever stating them out loud. Once they were named, I could grade against them rather than feel my way page by page. It also made the contract legible to a future me, or any future author — in a way habit alone could not.

Before that page existed, the work on the portal was self-improvement conducted in private: corrections I made without ever publishing the rule that triggered them. Afterwards, the corrections had a home. A weak page could be diagnosed against a specific principle and fixed. A new page could open the right template and inherit the right shape. The rubric became external to my mood.

None of this is finished. Most of the ten dimensions are not at A. Most are not even at B. But the fact that the levels are named at all — that there is a vocabulary for the gap between a portal that looks impressive and a portal that earns trust — is what the past few months produced. It is slower than polish work, by a factor I keep underestimating. It is also the only work that compounds.

If I had to compress this post into one sentence: mature documentation is not a writing problem, it is a willingness to be legible about what you chose, what you didn't choose, what you tried that failed, what you have not yet measured. The ten dimensions are ten different ways of being legible. The authoring-reference page is the surface on which the rubric lives, so I can keep it in front of me. The rest is doing the work.
