Documentation authoring model

How we organize, write, and review the docs that ship with Study App — based on the Diátaxis quadrants, adapted to our two-portal setup.

Introduction

Documentation is a first-class product surface. The portal you are reading sits next to the source code in the same repository, builds on every push, and is reviewed alongside features. This page describes the four archetypes we use, when each applies, and how authors should pick the right one.

The model is consolidated in RFC 0003. If you ever feel torn between two formats, default to the one that matches the reader's need, not the author's intent.

Why it matters

Without a shared model, every author invents their own structure. Readers then have to re-learn the layout of every page. Worse, important information ends up in the wrong place: tutorials sprout reference tables, references grow chatty prose, and explanations turn into how-tos halfway through.

Reader cost

Time spent reorienting is time not spent learning. A consistent layout means the reader's eye lands in the right place: code on the right of how-tos, glossary at the top of references, a clear narrative through explanations.

Author risk

Without templates, every page is a fresh design problem. Templates reduce risk by giving authors known-good structures that already pass review.

How it works

The four archetypes — tutorial, how-to, reference, and explanation — map cleanly to four templates in this kit. Pick by answering one question:

  1. Is the reader learning by doing? → tutorial.
  2. Is the reader solving a specific problem? → how-to.
  3. Is the reader looking up a fact? → reference.
  4. Is the reader building a mental model? → explanation.

If you find yourself wanting to mix two, split the page in half and link.

Trade-offs

The model is opinionated. Strict separation can feel pedantic when a topic is naturally cross-cutting. We accept the friction because the alternative — drifting back to "every page is a snowflake" — is measurably worse for readers.

Where the four-way split is genuinely too tight, we fall back to the article template (this one) — a flexible prose surface that still imposes the right header, breadcrumbs, TOC, and footer-history conventions.
